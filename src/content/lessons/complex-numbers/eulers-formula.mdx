## The hook: the most beautiful equation in mathematics

Last lesson we found that multiplying complex numbers *adds* their angles. That is the fingerprint
of an exponential — because $e^{x}\,e^{y} = e^{x+y}$ turns multiplication into addition too. Euler
chased that clue and landed on one of the most astonishing identities ever written:
$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$. Plug in $\theta = \pi$ and you get
$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$ — a single line tying together $e$, $i$, $\pi$, $1$, and $0$. It is not a trick;
it is the engine behind every Fourier transform your phone runs.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
$e^{i\theta}$ is the point on the unit circle at angle $\theta$ — so raising $e$ to an imaginary
power is just *rotation*.
</Callout>

## Intuition: why an exponential becomes a circle

You already saw two clues. First, polar multiplication **adds angles**, exactly like exponents add.
Second, the unit circle is described by $\cos\theta + i\sin\theta$. Euler's formula says these are
the *same object*:

$$
e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta.
$$

A quick way to feel why: differentiate $f(\theta) = e^{i\theta}$ and you get
$f'(\theta) = i\,e^{i\theta}$. Multiplying by $i$ rotates a vector $90°$, so the velocity is always
perpendicular to the position — and motion that is always at right angles to your radius is exactly
motion around a circle at constant speed. The point sweeps the unit circle, never speeding up,
never leaving radius 1.

This means **polar form gets a clean new outfit**: instead of $r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$ we
simply write $r\,e^{i\theta}$. Every rule from last lesson — multiply lengths, add angles — now reads
off the exponent automatically.

## The formal definition

**Euler's formula** states that for any real $\theta$,

$$
e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta.
$$

It follows from matching the Taylor series of $e^x$, $\cos x$, and $\sin x$:

$$
e^{i\theta} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(i\theta)^n}{n!}
= \underbrace{\left(1 - \tfrac{\theta^2}{2!} + \cdots\right)}_{\cos\theta}
+ i\underbrace{\left(\theta - \tfrac{\theta^3}{3!} + \cdots\right)}_{\sin\theta}.
$$

Setting $\theta = \pi$ gives **Euler's identity**:

$$
e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0.
$$

Polar form becomes wonderfully compact, $z = r\,e^{i\theta}$, and we recover $\cos$ and $\sin$ as
combinations of rotations:

$$
\cos\theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}}{2}, \qquad
\sin\theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}}{2i}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
The Fourier transform — the single most used tool in audio, image, and time-series ML — is built
from $e^{-i 2\pi f t}$, a family of these rotating exponentials. Each one "probes" a signal for a
particular frequency. Euler's formula is the foundation the next track stands on.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Signal processing" icon="📻">
A pure tone is modeled as $e^{i\omega t}$: a phasor rotating at angular frequency $\omega$. Real
audio is a sum of many such rotations, and decomposing a sound into them is exactly what a Fourier
transform does. Speed up the rotation and the pitch rises.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Quantum mechanics" icon="🔬">
A quantum state's time evolution multiplies it by a phase factor $e^{-iEt/\hbar}$ — a rotation in
the complex plane whose speed is set by the energy $E$. Interference patterns, the essence of quantum
behavior, are these rotating phases adding up or cancelling.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Electrical power" icon="🔋">
Grid engineers model the wall-socket voltage as the real part of $V\,e^{i\omega t}$, a phasor
spinning 50 or 60 times a second. Writing it as a rotating exponential makes the calculus of AC
power turn into simple algebra on the exponent.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Computer graphics" icon="🌐">
To spin an object smoothly, animate $e^{i\theta}$ with $\theta$ increasing each frame and multiply
your 2D points by it. Because the modulus stays exactly 1, rotations never distort or drift — a
numerically clean alternative to accumulating rotation matrices.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
One identity, two readings: algebraically $e^{i\theta}$ converts multiplication into angle addition;
geometrically it is steady rotation on the unit circle. Holding both pictures at once is what makes
the Fourier world click.
</Callout>
