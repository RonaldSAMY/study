## The hook: why does multiplying feel like spinning?

In rectangular form, multiplying $(a + bi)(c + di)$ is a fiddly FOIL with a sign flip. It works, but
it hides something magical. Rewrite the same numbers by their **length and angle**, and the messy
product collapses into one sentence: *multiply the lengths, add the angles.* That is why complex
multiplication is the cleanest way ever invented to rotate things — robot joints, radio beams, and
spiraling fractals all ride on it.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Writing $z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$, multiplying two complex numbers **multiplies their moduli
and adds their arguments** — a scale and a rotation in one stroke.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the angle does the work

From the complex-plane lesson, every $z$ has a length $r = \lvert z \rvert$ and an angle
$\theta = \arg(z)$. Using the unit-circle definitions of $\cos$ and $\sin$ you already know, the
horizontal part is $r\cos\theta$ and the vertical part is $r\sin\theta$. So

$$
z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta).
$$

Now multiply two of these and group the trig with the angle-addition formulas (the same identities
from trigonometry):

$$
r_1(\cos\theta_1 + i\sin\theta_1)\cdot r_2(\cos\theta_2 + i\sin\theta_2)
= r_1 r_2\bigl(\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2) + i\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)\bigr).
$$

Read it off: the new length is $r_1 r_2$, the new angle is $\theta_1 + \theta_2$. Multiplying by a
number of length 1 is a **pure rotation**; multiplying by a real number (angle 0) is a **pure
scaling**. Everything else is a blend of both.

## The formal definition

The **polar form** of a nonzero complex number is

$$
z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta), \qquad r = \lvert z \rvert,\ \ \theta = \arg(z).
$$

The product and quotient rules are

$$
z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2\,\bigl(\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2) + i\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)\bigr),
$$

$$
\frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{r_1}{r_2}\,\bigl(\cos(\theta_1-\theta_2) + i\sin(\theta_1-\theta_2)\bigr).
$$

Raising to a power repeats the rotation, giving **De Moivre's theorem**:

$$
z^n = r^n\bigl(\cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta)\bigr).
$$

This is also how you find roots: the $n$ solutions of $w^n = z$ are spaced evenly by
$360°/n$ around a circle — the **roots of unity** when $z = 1$.

<Callout type="why">
This rotate-and-scale view is the bridge to the Fourier transform at the heart of modern ML for
audio and images: a signal is decomposed into rotating complex exponentials, and multiplying by one
shifts its phase. Polar form makes that phase arithmetic trivial.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="AC circuits" icon="⚡">
Electrical engineers represent voltages and impedances as phasors in polar form. To combine them
they just multiply moduli and add the phase angles — no calculus, no FOIL. The dreaded "phase shift"
of a circuit is literally an added argument.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Robotics" icon="🤖">
A planar robot arm that turns $25°$ and then another $40°$ has rotated $65°$ total. Encode each turn
as a unit complex number and *multiply* them — the arguments add automatically. Composing rotations
becomes one multiplication, with no risk of the gimbal headaches that plague other methods.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Radio & antennas" icon="📡">
In a phased-array antenna, each element's signal is multiplied by a unit complex number to nudge its
phase. Choosing those angles steers the combined beam across the sky — all electronically, with no
moving parts. The "steering" is addition of arguments.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Fractal art" icon="🎆">
Repeatedly multiplying a point by a fixed complex number with modulus slightly above 1 traces a
**logarithmic spiral**: each step scales by the same factor and turns by the same angle. The
hypnotic spirals in generative art are just De Moivre's theorem on loop.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Addition lives in rectangular form; multiplication lives in polar form. Choosing the right
representation for the job is half of being fluent with complex numbers.
</Callout>
