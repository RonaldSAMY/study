## The hook: the equation with no answer

Try to solve $x^2 = -1$. Every real number you square comes out positive (or zero), so no
real number works. For centuries mathematicians shrugged and called such equations
"impossible." Then someone made a bold move: *what if we just invent a number whose square is
$-1$ and see what happens?* That invented number turned out to be no fantasy at all — it quietly
runs the math behind your phone charger, every digital photo's compression, and the qubits inside
a quantum computer.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The **imaginary unit** $i$ is defined by the single rule $i^2 = -1$, and a **complex number**
$a + bi$ simply bundles a familiar real part with a new imaginary part.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a new direction for numbers

You already know how to multiply powers and roots. The trouble is that $\sqrt{-1}$ has no home on
the number line — squaring never produces a negative. So instead of forcing it onto the line, we
give it a brand-new direction, perpendicular to the reals, and label one step in that direction
$i$.

Once $i$ exists, a complex number is just a pair: a **real part** $a$ and an **imaginary part** $b$,
written $a + bi$. You can add and multiply these exactly like the algebra you already know — treat
$i$ as an ordinary symbol — with one extra simplification rule: every time you see $i^2$, replace it
with $-1$.

That single substitution is the whole trick. Powers of $i$ even cycle:
$i^1 = i$, $i^2 = -1$, $i^3 = -i$, $i^4 = 1$, and then it repeats.

## The formal definition

The imaginary unit satisfies

$$
i^2 = -1, \qquad \text{equivalently} \qquad i = \sqrt{-1}.
$$

A **complex number** is any expression

$$
z = a + bi, \qquad a, b \in \mathbb{R},
$$

where $a = \operatorname{Re}(z)$ is the real part and $b = \operatorname{Im}(z)$ is the imaginary
part. **Addition** works component by component:

$$
(a + bi) + (c + di) = (a + c) + (b + d)\,i.
$$

**Multiplication** is ordinary distribution (FOIL), then apply $i^2 = -1$:

$$
(a + bi)(c + di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)\,i.
$$

One more essential idea: the **complex conjugate** flips the sign of the imaginary part,
$\bar{z} = a - bi$, and multiplying a number by its conjugate clears the imaginary part entirely:
$z\bar{z} = a^2 + b^2$. That trick is how you divide complex numbers.

<Callout type="why">
Machine learning leans on complex numbers wherever signals and rotations appear: the Fourier
transforms inside audio and image models, the spectral view of graphs, and the linear algebra of
quantum machine learning all live in $\mathbb{C}$. Comfort with $a + bi$ now pays off everywhere
later.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Electronics" icon="🔌">
Engineers describe an AC capacitor's opposition to current as a purely imaginary quantity (they
write $j$ instead of $i$ to avoid clashing with current). Multiplying a signal by $j$ shifts it a
quarter cycle. The "impossible" number becomes the natural bookkeeping for *timing* in circuits.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Quantum computing" icon="⚛️">
A qubit's state carries complex **amplitudes** like $a + bi$. You never measure the amplitude
directly — you measure a probability, which is $\lvert a + bi \rvert^2 = a^2 + b^2$. That is exactly
the conjugate trick $z\bar{z}$ turning a complex number into a real, observable chance.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Game programming" icon="🎮">
Store a 2D sprite's position as the complex number $x + yi$. Multiplying by $i$ rotates it
$90°$ around the origin: the point $(x, y)$ becomes $(-y, x)$. One multiplication replaces a whole
rotation matrix — a favorite shortcut in 2D game engines.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Fractals" icon="🌀">
The Mandelbrot set is generated by the rule $z \mapsto z^2 + c$, repeated over and over with
complex numbers. Squaring a complex number is just the multiplication above. Whether a point's
sequence stays bounded or shoots off to infinity paints the famous swirling boundary.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the through-line: in every example the *interesting* action is **multiplication**. Adding
complex numbers is mundane; multiplying them rotates and scales — the superpower we unpack over the
next three lessons.
</Callout>
