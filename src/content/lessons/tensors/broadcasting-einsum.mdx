## The hook: adding a bonus to a million salaries without a loop

Suppose a spreadsheet holds a $1000 \times 12$ table of monthly salaries, and you want to give every
person a flat 200-unit raise each month. You could write a double loop over twelve million cells —
or you could just write `table + 200` and let the framework figure out that the single number
applies everywhere. That quiet stretching of a small thing to fit a big thing is **broadcasting**,
and it is what makes tensor code both fast and short.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Broadcasting** automatically stretches smaller shapes to match bigger ones (without copying data),
and **einsum** is a tiny notation that names exactly which axes to multiply and sum — so any tensor
operation fits on one line.

</Callout>

## Intuition: line shapes up from the right

You already know a tensor's **shape** is its tuple of axis lengths. When you combine two tensors
element-wise, the framework compares their shapes **axis by axis, starting from the right**. For each
aligned pair it asks one question: are they equal, or is one of them 1?

- Equal lengths → line up directly.
- One length is 1 → stretch that axis to match the other (a 1 acts like "repeat me").
- A missing axis on the shorter shape → treated as length 1, then stretched.
- Neither equal nor 1 → **error**, the shapes are incompatible.

So $(1000, 12)$ combined with a scalar works (the scalar is shape $()$, stretched everywhere), and
$(256, 256, 3)$ combined with $(3,)$ works (the 3 channels line up on the right). But $(3, 4)$ with
$(2, 4)$ fails: the leading 3 and 2 disagree and neither is 1.

## The formal definition

Two shapes broadcast if, after right-aligning them and padding the shorter with leading 1s, every
aligned pair $(s_d, t_d)$ satisfies

$$
s_d = t_d \quad\text{or}\quad s_d = 1 \quad\text{or}\quad t_d = 1,
$$

and the result axis is $\max(s_d, t_d)$.

For naming reductions there is **Einstein summation**. The rule: an index repeated on the input side
but dropped from the output is summed over. A matrix product is

$$
C_{ik} = \sum_{j} A_{ij}\, B_{jk},
$$

which in einsum is simply `'ij,jk->ik'`. Drop the `k` to get a matrix–vector product `'ij,j->i'`;
keep all indices to get a batched element-wise multiply.

```python
import numpy as np
A = np.random.randn(4, 3)
B = np.random.randn(3, 5)
np.einsum('ij,jk->ik', A, B)        # matrix product, shape (4, 5)
np.einsum('bij,bjk->bik', X, Y)     # the SAME idea, batched over b
```

<Callout type="why">
Attention, convolutions, and batched linear layers are all einsum one-liners over batch, head, token,
and feature axes. Broadcasting is how a bias vector adds to a whole batch, and how a per-channel scale
hits every pixel. Together they replace nested loops with a single readable expression that the GPU
runs in parallel.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Finance" icon="💹">
A returns matrix of shape $(\text{days}, \text{assets})$ times a weight vector of shape
$(\text{assets},)$, summed over the asset axis, gives daily portfolio returns —
`'da,a->d'`. The weights broadcast across every day automatically.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Audio" icon="🎧">
A stereo clip has shape $(2, \text{samples})$. Multiply by a per-channel gain of shape $(2, 1)$ and
broadcasting applies the left gain to every left sample and the right gain to every right sample —
no loop over the samples.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports analytics" icon="🏀">
A season's box scores sit in $(\text{players}, \text{stats})$. To z-score each stat, subtract a
mean of shape $(\text{stats},)$ and divide by a std of the same shape. Both broadcast down every
player row, normalizing the whole table at once.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Climate science" icon="🌡️">
A temperature field of shape $(\text{months}, \text{lat}, \text{lon})$ minus a baseline map of shape
$(\text{lat}, \text{lon})$ yields the monthly *anomaly*. The baseline lacks a month axis, so it is
treated as length 1 and broadcast across all twelve months.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Broadcasting never actually copies the small array — it just reads the same elements repeatedly. That
is why "stretching" a bias across a million rows costs almost no memory.
</Callout>
