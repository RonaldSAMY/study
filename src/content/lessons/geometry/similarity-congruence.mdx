## The hook: measuring a tree without leaving the ground

You want the height of a tall tree but you only have a tape measure. So you wait for sunshine, measure
the tree's **shadow** (say $12$ m), then stand a $1$ m ruler upright and measure *its* shadow ($0.8$ m).
The Sun's rays hit both at the same angle, so the tree-and-shadow triangle is a **scaled-up copy** of
the ruler-and-shadow triangle. The sides keep the same ratio, so the tree is
$1 \times \tfrac{12}{0.8} = 15$ m tall. You measured a giant using a stick — because the two shapes are
**similar**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Two shapes are **similar** if one is a scaled copy of the other: same angles, and all corresponding
sides in the **same ratio** (the **scale factor** $k$). If $k = 1$, they're identical — that special
case is **congruence**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the same shape, zoomed

In the last lesson, Pythagoras tied the *sides* of a right triangle together. Similarity tells you what
happens to *all* the sides at once when you zoom a whole shape in or out.

- **Congruent** shapes are perfect copies — same angles **and** same side lengths. You could slide,
  flip, or rotate one onto the other and it would land exactly. (Scale factor $k = 1$.)
- **Similar** shapes have identical **angles** but their sides are scaled by a single number $k$. A
  photo and its enlargement, a model car and the real car — same proportions, different size.

The crucial discipline is *corresponding* parts: the biggest side matches the biggest side, the angle
at one corner matches the angle at the matching corner. Once you line them up, every pair of
corresponding sides hides the **same** ratio $k$.

Slide the scale factor and watch the green copy grow and shrink. Notice that **every** side scales by
the same $k$, the perimeter scales by $k$ too — but the **area** scales by $k^2$.

most useful facts.

## The formal definition

Two polygons are **similar** (written $\sim$) when, for every pair of corresponding sides,

$$
\frac{a'}{a} = \frac{b'}{b} = \frac{c'}{c} = k,
$$

and all corresponding angles are equal. The shared number $k$ is the **scale factor**. From it:

$$
\text{Perimeter}' = k \cdot \text{Perimeter}, \qquad \text{Area}' = k^2 \cdot \text{Area}.
$$

**Congruence** (written $\cong$) is the case $k = 1$: corresponding sides *and* angles are all equal.
For triangles, you don't need to check everything — shortcuts like **SSS**, **SAS**, and **ASA**
guarantee congruence from just three matching pieces.

<Callout type="why">
Scale factor is the one-dimensional ancestor of **scalar multiplication** on vectors, which you'll meet
soon: multiplying a vector by $k$ stretches it by $k$ without changing its direction — exactly what $k$
does to a shape here. Normalizing data, resizing images, and adjusting learning rates are all "pick a
$k$ and scale."
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Architecture" icon="📐">
A blueprint at scale $1\!:\!50$ means every centimeter on paper is a similar copy of $50$ cm of real
building. A wall drawn $8$ cm long is truly $4$ m. Architects live in similarity: the drawing and the
building are the same shape, $k = 50$ apart.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Photography" icon="📷">
Enlarging a photo from 4×6 to 8×12 uses $k = 2$. The image stays similar — faces don't stretch — because
both dimensions scale together. Change only the width and you break similarity, and everyone looks
squashed.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Map making" icon="🗺️">
A map's scale bar (e.g. "1 cm = 2 km") is a scale factor. Real distances and map distances form similar
triangles, so you can read true distances straight off the paper — the navigator's everyday use of
$k$.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Pizza value" icon="🍕">
An 18-inch pizza isn't twice a 9-inch pizza — it's a similar shape with $k = 2$, so it has $k^2 = 4$
times the area (and feeds four times as many people). The $k^2$ rule is why the big pie is almost always
the better deal.
</ExampleCard>
