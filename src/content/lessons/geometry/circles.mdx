## The hook: why every pizza price hides a secret

A 10-inch pizza costs \$12; the 14-inch costs \$18. The big one is only $40\%$ wider — surely it's
overpriced? Not even close. A circle's area depends on the radius **squared**, so going from a 5-inch
radius to a 7-inch radius nearly *doubles* the pizza. The shop is quietly relying on a number that has
fascinated humans for four thousand years: $\pi$, the constant that links a circle's edge and its
interior to its radius.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A circle is the set of all points the same distance — the **radius** $r$ — from a center; and from that
single $r$, the constant $\pi \approx 3.14159$ produces both the distance around it
($C = 2\pi r$) and the space inside it ($A = \pi r^2$).
</Callout>

## Intuition: the most "similar" shape there is

In the last lesson, every shape had its own proportions. Circles are special: **all circles are
similar.** A coin and the Moon are the same shape scaled by some factor $k$. That's exactly why a single
constant can describe every circle at once.

Take **any** circle and divide its circumference (the distance once around) by its diameter (all the way
across). You always get the same number — about $3.14159$. We call it $\pi$. It never settles into a
repeating pattern and never ends; it is **irrational**. Because $\pi$ is fixed:

- the **circumference** is just a bit more than $3$ diameters: $C = \pi d = 2\pi r$;
- the **area** turns out to be $\pi r^2$ — radius squared, scaled by $\pi$.

That squared radius is why doubling a circle's size *quadruples* its area — the $k^2$ rule from
similarity, made famous.

## The formal definition

For a circle of radius $r$ (so diameter $d = 2r$):

$$
C = 2\pi r = \pi d, \qquad A = \pi r^2.
$$

The defining property of $\pi$ is the ratio itself:

$$
\pi = \frac{C}{d} \approx 3.14159265\ldots
$$

A useful relative: an **arc** is a slice of the circumference, and a **sector** is a slice of the area.
A fraction $\theta/360°$ of the full turn gives an arc of $\frac{\theta}{360°}\,C$ and a sector of
$\frac{\theta}{360°}\,A$.

<Callout type="why">
$\pi$ escapes geometry entirely. It shows up in the bell-curve formula at the heart of probability and
statistics, in every wave and oscillation, and in the math of rotations that powers computer graphics.
Meeting it here, as a humble ratio, is meeting one of the most important constants in all of science.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Pizza value" icon="🍕">
A 16-inch pizza has area $\pi \times 8^2 \approx 201$ in², while two 8-inch pizzas total only
$2 \times \pi \times 4^2 \approx 101$ in². One large genuinely beats two smalls — the $r^2$ in the area
formula is the deal-hunter's best friend.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="GPS & delivery" icon="📍">
A courier app promises delivery within a 3 km radius. The area it can serve is $\pi \times 3^2 \approx
28$ km². Bump the radius to 4 km and coverage jumps to $\approx 50$ km² — almost double the city for one
extra kilometer, thanks again to $r^2$.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Astronomy" icon="🪐">
Earth's radius is about $6{,}371$ km, so a trip around the equator is $2\pi r \approx 40{,}030$ km. The
same $C = 2\pi r$ that sizes a coin sizes a planet — circles don't care how big they get.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Athletics" icon="🏃">
A running track's inside lane hugs two semicircle ends. Outer lanes have a larger radius, so their
circumference is longer by $2\pi \,\Delta r$ per lane. That's precisely why sprinters in outer lanes get
a staggered head start — the math of $C = 2\pi r$ keeps the race fair.
</ExampleCard>
