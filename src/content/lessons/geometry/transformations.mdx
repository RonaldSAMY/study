## The hook: how a video game moves a thousand things at once

In a racing game, the car, the trees, the crowd, and the road signs are all just lists of corner
points. Every frame — sixty times a second — the engine **slides**, **turns**, and **resizes** those
points to make the world rush past. It never redraws anything from scratch; it just *transforms* the
coordinates it already has. The same three moves animate cartoons, rotate a CAD model, and let your
phone re-orient a photo. Master sliding, turning, and scaling, and you've learned the grammar of every
moving picture — and you've arrived at the doorstep of **vectors**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **transformation** takes every point of a shape and moves it by a rule: **translation** slides
(add the same amount to every point), **rotation** turns around a center, and **scaling** stretches or
shrinks — and a translation is described by a single **vector**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from measuring shapes to moving them

So far geometry has been about *static* figures — their angles, sides, areas. Coordinate geometry let us
pin a shape to the grid as a list of points. Transformations are what we do when we **change those
points by a rule** while keeping the shape recognizable.

The three rigid-or-similar moves:

- **Translation** — slide every point the same distance in the same direction. Shape, size, and
  orientation all stay the same; only position changes. The "how far and which way" is captured by a
  pair of numbers $[\,t_x, t_y\,]$ — a **vector**.
- **Rotation** — turn every point by an angle $\theta$ about a fixed center. Distances from the center
  are preserved, so the shape is congruent to the original, just spun.
- **Scaling** — multiply every coordinate by a factor $k$. This is the **similarity** move from earlier:
  same shape, new size.

Translations and rotations keep a shape **congruent**; scaling makes it **similar**. Notice how every
idea in this track is converging.

## The formal definition

Write a point as $(x, y)$. The three transformations act like this:

**Translation** by vector $\mathbf{t} = [\,t_x, t_y\,]$ — add it to every point:

$$
(x, y) \;\mapsto\; (x + t_x,\; y + t_y).
$$

**Scaling** by factor $k$ about the origin — multiply every coordinate:

$$
(x, y) \;\mapsto\; (k\,x,\; k\,y).
$$

**Rotation** by angle $\theta$ about the origin:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x' &= x\cos\theta - y\sin\theta, \\
y' &= x\sin\theta + y\cos\theta.
\end{aligned}
$$

<Callout type="why">
Look closely at translation: $(x,y) \mapsto (x + t_x, y + t_y)$ is **vector addition**, and scaling is
**scalar multiplication** — the two operations that *define* a vector space. Rotation, written compactly,
becomes a **matrix** multiplying a vector. In other words, the entire next track — vectors, then
matrices, then linear transformations — is this lesson, formalized. Transformations are where geometry
hands the baton to linear algebra.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Computer animation" icon="🎞️">
A 3D character is a mesh of thousands of points. To make it walk, the animator applies a translation
vector each frame and rotations at every joint. The figure never gets re-drawn — only transformed,
which is why animation is really just vector arithmetic at scale.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Architecture & CAD" icon="🏢">
A drafter designs one window, then <em>translates</em> copies across a facade and <em>rotates</em> the
whole floor plan to study the building from new angles. Each "copy and place" is a transformation acting
on the same base coordinates.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Robotics" icon="🤖">
A robot arm reaches a target by chaining rotations at its joints and translations along its links.
Computing where the gripper ends up is a sequence of transformations — the literal math that guides the
hand to the object.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Art & perspective" icon="🖼️">
A digital artist drags, spins, and resizes a layer with on-screen handles. Each handle move is a
translation, rotation, or scaling applied to the layer's pixels — the same three operations, wrapped in
a friendly interface.
</ExampleCard>
