## The hook: how carpenters check a square corner without a protractor

A carpenter framing a deck needs the corner to be a perfect $90°$ — but a tape measure can't read
angles. So they use a trick older than recorded history: measure $3$ feet along one edge, $4$ feet along
the other, and adjust until the diagonal between those marks is exactly $5$ feet. The instant it reads
$5$, the corner is dead square. Why those numbers? Because $3^2 + 4^2 = 5^2$. That is the
**Pythagorean theorem** doing real work on a job site.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
In any **right triangle**, the area of the square built on the longest side (the **hypotenuse**) equals
the combined areas of the squares built on the other two sides: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.
</Callout>

## Intuition: angles you just met, now with sides

In the last lesson you measured the **angles** where lines meet. A **right triangle** is a triangle
with exactly one of those special $90°$ angles. Its two shorter sides — the ones forming the right
angle — are the **legs** ($a$ and $b$). The side opposite the right angle, always the longest, is the
**hypotenuse** ($c$).

The theorem isn't about the sides directly — it's about the **squares** drawn on them. Picture a literal
square of tiles sitting on each side: $a \times a$ tiles, $b \times b$ tiles, and $c \times c$ tiles. The
claim is that the two smaller tile-squares can be rearranged to *exactly* fill the big one. No tiles left
over, none missing. That's why it's an equation about areas.

## The formal definition

For a right triangle with legs $a$ and $b$ and hypotenuse $c$:

$$
a^2 + b^2 = c^2.
$$

Solving for the hypotenuse, or for a missing leg:

$$
c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}, \qquad a = \sqrt{c^2 - b^2}.
$$

The theorem only holds when the angle between $a$ and $b$ is a right angle — and remarkably, the
**converse** is true too: if $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$, the triangle *must* be right-angled. That's exactly why
the carpenter's $3$-$4$-$5$ measurement proves the corner is square.

<Callout type="why">
This formula is the seed of **distance** everywhere in machine learning. The length of a vector,
$\lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots}$, is just Pythagoras extended to many
dimensions. Every time a model measures how "far apart" two data points are, it is running this
theorem under the hood.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Home theater" icon="📺">
A TV's size is its <em>diagonal</em>. A 55-inch screen that is 48 inches wide must be
$\sqrt{55^2 - 48^2} \approx 27$ inches tall. Pythagoras converts the advertised diagonal into the real
width and height you need to fit it on a shelf.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Architecture" icon="🏗️">
A roof rafter is the hypotenuse of a right triangle: the horizontal "run" is one leg, the vertical
"rise" the other. To span a 6 m run with a 2.5 m rise, the rafter must be
$\sqrt{6^2 + 2.5^2} \approx 6.5$ m — order it too short and it won't reach the ridge.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Baseball" icon="⚾">
A baseball diamond is a square 90 ft on a side. The catcher's throw from home plate to second base is
the diagonal: $\sqrt{90^2 + 90^2} \approx 127$ ft. That single number decides whether a runner can be
caught stealing.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Astronomy" icon="🔭">
Star catalogs give positions as coordinates. To find the true separation between two stars projected on
the sky at $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$, astronomers take $\sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}$ — the
distance formula, which is Pythagoras wearing a coordinate-geometry hat.
</ExampleCard>
