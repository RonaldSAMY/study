## The hook: how a skyscraper stays straight

Look up at a glass tower. Every floor is a horizontal line, and they are all **parallel** — perfectly
evenly spaced, never tilting toward each other. The steel braces that run diagonally across the
facade cut through those floors at an angle. Here is the surprising part: an engineer only has to
measure **one** of the angles where a brace meets a floor, and the geometry of parallel lines instantly
tells them *every other* angle in the whole pattern. That single trick keeps buildings square, bridges
true, and floor tiles aligned.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A point marks a place, a line joins places, and the **angle** between two lines measures how much you
turn from one to the other — and when a line crosses two **parallel** lines, the angles it makes repeat
in a fixed, predictable pattern.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from places to turns

You already plot **points** as $(x, y)$ pairs on the coordinate plane. Geometry adds two more atoms:

- A **line** is the straight path through points, extending forever in both directions. A piece of one
  with two endpoints is a **segment**.
- An **angle** is the amount of rotation between two lines (or segments) that meet at a point called the
  **vertex**. We measure it in **degrees**, where a full turn is $360°$.

A few names worth knowing, because the whole subject leans on them:

- **Right angle** — exactly $90°$, the corner of a square.
- **Acute** — less than $90°$ (a sharp corner). **Obtuse** — between $90°$ and $180°$ (a blunt one).
- **Straight angle** — $180°$, a flat line.
- Two angles are **complementary** if they add to $90°$, and **supplementary** if they add to $180°$.

The real fireworks start when a single line — a **transversal** — slices across two **parallel** lines.
Suddenly the angles come in matched families.

## The formal definition

When a transversal crosses two parallel lines, eight angles appear. They split into just two sizes —
one acute value $\alpha$ and its supplement $180° - \alpha$ — related by these rules:

$$
\text{Corresponding angles:} \quad \alpha_1 = \alpha_2
$$

$$
\text{Alternate (interior or exterior) angles:} \quad \alpha_1 = \alpha_2
$$

$$
\text{Co-interior (same-side) angles:} \quad \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = 180°
$$

And around any single intersection, angles on a straight line are supplementary while **vertically
opposite** angles (across the X) are equal:

$$
\angle\,\text{a} + \angle\,\text{b} = 180°, \qquad \angle\,\text{a} = \angle\,\text{(opposite)}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
This is your first taste of a recurring machine-learning theme: **one measured quantity propagating
into many through fixed rules.** It is exactly how a network "knows" countless values from a few — and
how the coordinate geometry behind vectors keeps directions consistent.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Architecture" icon="🏛️">
A staircase stringer is a transversal cutting across the parallel lines of each floor. Because
corresponding angles are equal, the carpenter cuts <em>every</em> step notch at the same angle — measure
once, repeat with confidence.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Art & perspective" icon="🎨">
In a one-point perspective drawing, the parallel edges of a tiled floor are crossed by your line of
sight. Renaissance painters used the equal-angle rule to keep tiles shrinking smoothly toward the
vanishing point so the room looks convincingly deep.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Carpentry & DIY" icon="🔨">
Cutting crown molding for a corner? The two walls act like a transversal meeting parallel ceiling and
floor lines. Knowing supplementary angles lets you set the miter saw to the matching angle so the joint
closes with no gap.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports fields" icon="🏟️">
The yard lines on a football field are parallel; a diagonal camera cable or a player's running line
crosses them as a transversal. Broadcasters use the equal corresponding angles to keep on-screen
graphics aligned with the field as the camera pans.
</ExampleCard>
