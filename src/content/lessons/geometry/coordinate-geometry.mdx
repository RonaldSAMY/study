## The hook: how your map app knows you're 2.3 km away

Tap a café in a map app and it instantly shows "$2.3$ km away." Behind that number, your phone has two
pairs of coordinates — your location and the café's — and it does a tiny calculation that turns *pure
algebra* into a *real distance on the ground*. The trick is to drop a right triangle between the two
points and let Pythagoras finish the job. This marriage of grid and formula is **coordinate
geometry**, and it's the bridge from the shapes of geometry to the vectors of machine learning.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Put two points on a coordinate grid and the straight-line **distance** between them is the hypotenuse of
a right triangle whose legs are the horizontal and vertical gaps: $d = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}$.
</Callout>

## Intuition: Pythagoras, now with coordinates

You've used the coordinate plane to plot points, and you've proved $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. Coordinate
geometry is what happens when those two ideas shake hands.

Take two points $P = (x_1, y_1)$ and $Q = (x_2, y_2)$. To get from $P$ to $Q$ you move $\Delta x =
x_2 - x_1$ across and $\Delta y = y_2 - y_1$ up. Those two moves are the **legs** of a right triangle,
and the direct line from $P$ to $Q$ is its **hypotenuse**. So the distance is just Pythagoras applied to
the coordinate gaps.

The **midpoint** — the exact center of the segment — is even simpler: average the $x$'s and average the
$y$'s. It's the "balance point" halfway along.

## The formal definition

For points $P = (x_1, y_1)$ and $Q = (x_2, y_2)$:

$$
d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}.
$$

The **midpoint** is the component-wise average:

$$
M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \; \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right).
$$

This extends seamlessly. In three dimensions you simply add a $z$-term,
$d = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2 + (\Delta z)^2}$, and the same pattern continues for any number of
coordinates.

<Callout type="why">
This formula is, almost word for word, the **length of a vector** and the **Euclidean distance** between
two data points — the workhorse of $k$-nearest-neighbors, clustering, and similarity search. When a
recommender asks "which users are closest to you?", it's running exactly this sum of squared
differences, just with hundreds of coordinates instead of two.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="GPS & maps" icon="🛰️">
On a small map, two pins at grid tiles $(12, 5)$ and $(15, 9)$ are
$\sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5$ tiles apart. Scale each tile to real meters and you have the straight-line
"as-the-crow-flies" distance your navigation app quotes.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Video games" icon="🎮">
A game checks whether an enemy is close enough to attack by computing the distance between the player's
$(x, y)$ and the enemy's $(x, y)$. To save CPU, engines often compare the <em>squared</em> distance
(skipping the square root) — the formula, optimized for 60 frames a second.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports analytics" icon="⚽">
Soccer trackers log every player's pitch coordinates 25 times a second. The distance formula turns those
dots into "kilometers run" and "pass length," the stats that fill match reports and scout databases.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Astronomy" icon="✨">
Two stars plotted at $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ on a sky chart have an angular separation found with
the very same $\sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}$. The midpoint, meanwhile, marks the center of a
constellation pattern.
</ExampleCard>
