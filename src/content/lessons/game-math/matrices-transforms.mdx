## The hook: one number-grid moves a whole sprite

A character sprite is hundreds of vertices. When the player turns, jumps onto a moving platform, and the
camera zooms, every one of those vertices must move *together* and *consistently*. You could write three
separate code paths — one to shift, one to spin, one to resize — but games instead fold all of it into a
single **transform matrix**. Multiply each vertex by that one grid of numbers and it is simultaneously
translated, rotated and scaled. Better still, matrices **compose**: parent a sword to a hand to an arm,
and you just multiply their matrices.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **transform matrix** is a single grid of numbers that, when multiplied by a point, moves, turns and
resizes it all at once — and chaining transforms is just **multiplying** their matrices.

</Callout>

## Intuition: why a 3×3 for a 2D world?

You already know matrix multiplication composes linear transformations. Rotation and scaling are linear
— they keep the origin fixed — so a $2 \times 2$ matrix handles them. But **translation** (sliding) is
*not* linear: it moves the origin. The fix is a famous trick: add a third coordinate that's always 1
("homogeneous coordinates"), so a 2D point becomes $(x, y, 1)$. Now a $3 \times 3$ matrix can sneak the
translation into its last column, and *all three* operations live in one matrix.

$$
\begin{bmatrix} x' \\ y' \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} =
\begin{bmatrix} a & b & t_x \\ c & d & t_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

The top-left $2\times2$ block does rotation and scaling; the top-right column $(t_x, t_y)$ does the
slide; the bottom row stays $[0\ 0\ 1]$ to keep the trick consistent.

## The formal definition

The three primitive 2D transforms as homogeneous matrices:

$$
T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & t_x \\ 0 & 1 & t_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
R = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
S = \begin{bmatrix} s_x & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & s_y & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

A combined transform is their product. The conventional **scale, then rotate, then translate** order is

$$
M = T \cdot R \cdot S.
$$

Because matrix multiplication is **not commutative**, $T \cdot R \neq R \cdot T$: rotating then sliding
sends an object somewhere different than sliding then rotating. Read the chain **right-to-left** — the
matrix nearest the point acts first.

```glsl
// A vertex shader's heart: one matrix-times-point per vertex
gl_Position = u_model * vec4(localPos, 1.0);  // local -> world in one multiply
```

<Callout type="why">
The GPU runs this exact multiply for <em>every vertex of every object every frame</em> — millions of
times. Folding T, R and S into one matrix means the GPU does a single multiply instead of three, which
is a big reason games push so much geometry.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="2D platformers" icon="🎮">
A spinning coin pickup has a matrix $T(\text{coinPos}) \cdot R(\text{spin}) \cdot S(\text{bob})$. The
engine rebuilds it each frame from three numbers and the coin translates, rotates and gently pulses with
one multiply per vertex.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="RPG equipment" icon="🗡️">
A sword's world matrix is the hand's matrix times the sword's local matrix. Parent the hand to the arm to
the torso and the chain of multiplications means the blade follows every joint automatically — no manual
position juggling. This is the scene graph.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Strategy games" icon="🏰">
Placing a hundred identical barracks reuses one mesh with a hundred different transform matrices — each
just a translate, a facing rotation and a scale. The GPU instances them, drawing the lot in essentially
one call.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="UI & menus" icon="🖱️">
A pop-up that slides in while growing from nothing animates a single matrix: translate from off-screen
and scale from 0 to 1 over a fraction of a second. The same matrix math that moves 3D worlds also drives
the buttons you click.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Multiplication order is the number-one transform bug. "Scale-rotate-translate" ($T\,R\,S$ applied to a
column point) is the safe default; flip it and your object orbits the origin instead of spinning in place.
</Callout>
