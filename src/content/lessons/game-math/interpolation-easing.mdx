## The hook: why does one menu feel cheap and another feel expensive?

Tap a button in a polished app and the panel slides in, slows as it arrives, and settles with a tiny
bounce. Tap one in a rough prototype and the panel just *teleports*. Same start, same end — but one feels
alive and the other feels broken. The difference is entirely in the **in-between frames**: how a value
travels from A to B over time. That traveling is **interpolation**, and shaping its speed along the way is
**easing**. It is the cheapest, highest-impact polish in all of game feel.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Interpolation** fills in the values between a start and an end as a fraction $t$ goes from 0 to 1, and
**easing** bends how fast $t$ moves so motion can accelerate, slow down, or overshoot.

</Callout>

## Intuition: the blend knob t

Every interpolation has one master control: a fraction $t$ that slides from 0 (fully at the start) to 1
(fully at the end). The simplest blend is the **linear interpolation**, or **lerp** — you already used it
without the name: walking from your position toward a target by a fraction each frame is a lerp.

A lerp moves at a *constant* speed, which often looks robotic. Real objects start slow, speed up, and
ease to a stop. **Easing** fixes this by remapping $t$ before you use it: feed in linear time, pass it
through a curve, and get back a value that lingers near 0, rushes through the middle, then settles near 1.
The classic in games is **smoothstep**, which has zero slope at both ends so motion glides in and out.

For richer paths you blend more than two points. A **Bézier curve** uses control points to define a
smooth path — the same curves type designers use for fonts and you used as easing presets. And when the
thing being blended is an *orientation*, plain lerp distorts it, so you reach for **slerp**, the
spherical interpolation you met with quaternions, which keeps a steady angular rate.

## The formal definition

**Linear interpolation** between two values $a$ and $b$:

$$
\operatorname{lerp}(a, b, t) = a + (b - a)\,t = (1 - t)\,a + t\,b, \qquad t \in [0, 1].
$$

It works component-wise on vectors, so positions and colors lerp the same way. **Easing** simply
replaces the straight $t$ with a shaped $f(t)$ where $f(0) = 0$ and $f(1) = 1$. A popular choice is
**smoothstep**:

$$
f(t) = 3t^2 - 2t^3.
$$

A **cubic Bézier** curve blends four control points with the Bernstein weights:

$$
\mathbf{B}(t) = (1-t)^3\,\mathbf{P}_0 + 3(1-t)^2 t\,\mathbf{P}_1 + 3(1-t)\,t^2\,\mathbf{P}_2 + t^3\,\mathbf{P}_3.
$$

For orientations, **slerp** replaces lerp so the angular speed stays constant along the arc (see the
quaternions lesson).

```csharp
// Frame-rate-aware smooth follow, plus an eased pop-in
camera.position = Vector3.Lerp(camera.position, target, 1f - Mathf.Exp(-k * dt));
float eased = t * t * (3f - 2f * t);   // smoothstep
```

<Callout type="why">
Easing is not just decoration. A smooth curve means <strong>no sudden jumps in velocity</strong>, which
reads to the eye as weight and intention. Game designers call the sum of these little touches "juice" —
and it is mostly well-chosen interpolation.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="UI & menus" icon="📱">
A settings panel slides in by lerping its position from off-screen to its resting spot, with an
ease-out curve so it decelerates as it arrives. Swap in a "back" curve and it overshoots slightly then
settles — the difference between functional and delightful.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Camera work" icon="🎥">
A follow camera does not snap to the player; it lerps toward them a fraction each frame, so quick
player moves produce a soft, trailing pursuit. Designers tune that fraction to make the camera feel
tight and responsive or loose and cinematic.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Cutscenes" icon="🎬">
A scripted camera glides along a Bézier path between handcrafted control points, sweeping past the hero
in one continuous arc. Bézier curves give artists smooth, editable trajectories without keyframing every
single frame.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="HUD feedback" icon="❤️">
When you take damage, the health bar does not jump — it tweens from the old value to the new one over a
fraction of a second with an easing curve, so your eye can track exactly how much you lost. Score and XP
bars use the identical trick.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
A subtle gotcha: repeatedly calling <strong>lerp(current, target, 0.1)</strong> each frame is
frame-rate dependent — it converges faster at higher frame rates. The exponential form in the snippet
above fixes it by folding $\Delta t$ into the blend.
</Callout>
