## The hook: can the guard see you?

A stealth game lives or dies on one question asked thousands of times a second: *is the player inside
the guard's vision cone?* The guard has a facing direction; you are somewhere relative to it. The engine
does not run a fancy AI to decide — it does **one multiplication and one comparison**. The dot product
tells it whether you're in front and roughly how far off-center; the cross product tells it whether
you're to the left or the right. Two tiny operations answer "can the guard see you," "is the wall
facing the light," and "did the car turn left or right" all at once.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The **dot product** measures how much two vectors agree (front/back and angle); the **cross product**
measures how much they differ in turn (left/right, and in 3D, the perpendicular direction).
</Callout>

## Intuition: agreement vs turning

You already used the dot product for similarity. In games it wears two hats:

- **Facing check.** With $\mathbf{f}$ the facing direction and $\mathbf{t}$ the direction to a target,
  the sign of $\mathbf{f} \cdot \mathbf{t}$ says **in front** (positive) or **behind** (negative). Zero
  means exactly side-on.
- **Angle / cone.** If both are unit vectors, $\mathbf{f} \cdot \mathbf{t} = \cos\theta$ directly — so
  comparing the dot to $\cos(\text{halfConeAngle})$ is the entire vision-cone test.

The **cross product** is about *rotation between* the vectors. In 2D it collapses to a single number
(the $z$ component): its **sign** tells you whether $\mathbf{t}$ is to the **left** ($+$) or **right**
($-$) of $\mathbf{f}$, and its **magnitude** is the area of the parallelogram they span. In 3D the cross
product returns a whole vector perpendicular to both — which is exactly how you compute a **surface
normal** for lighting.

## The formal definition

For $\mathbf{a} = (a_x, a_y)$ and $\mathbf{b} = (b_x, b_y)$, the **dot product** is

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_x b_x + a_y b_y = \lVert \mathbf{a} \rVert\,\lVert \mathbf{b} \rVert \cos\theta.
$$

So the angle between them is $\theta = \arccos\!\left(\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert}\right)$, and the **scalar projection** of $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\mathbf{a}$ is $\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert}$.

The **2D cross product** is a single scalar:

$$
\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = a_x b_y - a_y b_x = \lVert \mathbf{a} \rVert\,\lVert \mathbf{b} \rVert \sin\theta.
$$

In **3D** it is a vector perpendicular to both inputs:

$$
\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} a_y b_z - a_z b_y \\ a_z b_x - a_x b_z \\ a_x b_y - a_y b_x \end{bmatrix}.
$$

```glsl
// A triangle's surface normal, the building block of lighting
vec3 normal = normalize(cross(b - a, c - a));
float brightness = max(dot(normal, lightDir), 0.0);  // facing the light?
```

<Callout type="why">
The dot product is the most common single operation in graphics: <strong>every lit pixel</strong>
computes <code>dot(normal, lightDir)</code> to decide how bright it is. The cross product builds the
normals that dot product needs. The two are a team.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Stealth AI" icon="🥷">
A guard sees you when you're inside its cone: normalize the direction to the player, then check
$\mathbf{f} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{t}} > \cos(30°)$ for a 60°-wide cone. One dot product replaces any amount
of hand-tuned trigonometry.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Racing AI" icon="🏁">
To know if a rival should steer left or right toward the next checkpoint, take the cross product of the
car's forward vector with the direction to the checkpoint. Positive $z$? Turn left. Negative? Turn
right. The magnitude even tells you how hard.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="3D lighting" icon="💡">
A flat-shaded polygon's brightness is $\max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{l})$ — the dot of its surface
normal with the light direction. Faces pointing at the light glow; faces turned away (negative dot)
clamp to black. The normal $\mathbf{n}$ itself came from a cross product of two edges.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Physics: which side" icon="⚖️">
Collision code asks "is this point on the inside of that wall?" by crossing the wall's edge vector with
the vector to the point. The sign places the point left or right of the edge — the foundation of the
separating-axis test and 2D polygon containment.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Memory aid: <strong>dot → how aligned</strong> (a number that peaks when parallel), <strong>cross → how
perpendicular / which side</strong> (peaks when at right angles, sign gives handedness).
</Callout>
