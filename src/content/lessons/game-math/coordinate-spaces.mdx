## The hook: where is the dragon's nose, really?

Ask "where is the dragon's nose?" and there are five honest answers. It's at the tip of the model
(its own coordinates). It's hovering over the castle (world coordinates). It's slightly left of where
the camera looks (view coordinates). It's at pixel (812, 240) on your monitor (screen coordinates). Each
answer is correct *in its own space*, and rendering is nothing more than walking the nose through these
spaces one matrix at a time. Understand the journey and projection, cameras and screen-space UI all stop
being mysterious.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A point lives in many **coordinate spaces** at once; rendering carries it **local → world → view → clip
→ screen**, and each arrow is a single matrix multiplication.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a chain of frames

You already know a transform matrix moves a point from one frame to another. Coordinate spaces are just
the standard chain of those frames:

- **Local (model) space** — the object's own coordinates, authored around its center. The dragon's nose
  is always the same local point no matter where the dragon walks.
- **World space** — the shared stage. The **model matrix** $M$ places each object into it.
- **View (camera) space** — the world re-expressed *from the camera's point of view*. The **view matrix**
  $V$ is essentially the inverse of the camera's world transform, so the camera sits at the origin.
- **Clip / NDC space** — the **projection matrix** $P$ applies perspective and squashes everything into a
  cube from $-1$ to $1$, ready for clipping.
- **Screen space** — the **viewport transform** stretches that cube to actual pixels.

The whole pipeline for a vertex is one stacked product, $\mathbf{p}_{\text{clip}} = P\,V\,M\,\mathbf{p}_{\text{local}}$, famous in shader code as the MVP matrix.

## Play with it

Pick a stage and watch the same ship — and one tracked green nose vertex — drawn in that space as it
orbits. Notice how in **view** space the camera snaps to the origin, and in **screen** space everything
lands inside the viewport rectangle, measured in pixels.

## The formal definition

A local vertex becomes a clip-space vertex through the **Model–View–Projection** product:

$$
\mathbf{p}_{\text{clip}} = \underbrace{P}_{\text{projection}}\;\underbrace{V}_{\text{view}}\;\underbrace{M}_{\text{model}}\;\mathbf{p}_{\text{local}}.
$$

The view matrix is the inverse of the camera's world transform:

$$
V = M_{\text{camera}}^{-1}.
$$

After projection, the GPU divides by the $w$ component (the **perspective divide**) to reach normalized
device coordinates, then the viewport transform maps $[-1, 1]$ to pixels:

$$
x_{\text{screen}} = \frac{x_{\text{ndc}} + 1}{2}\,\text{width}, \qquad
y_{\text{screen}} = \frac{1 - y_{\text{ndc}}}{2}\,\text{height}.
$$

```glsl
// Standard vertex shader: the whole journey in one line
gl_Position = u_proj * u_view * u_model * vec4(localPos, 1.0);
```

<Callout type="why">
Separating the spaces is what makes engines modular: artists work in local space, level designers in
world space, the camera system owns the view matrix, and lighting is often done in view space because
the camera-at-origin frame makes the math cheaper. Each team touches one matrix.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Open-world games" icon="🗺️">
A tree is authored once in local space, then dropped thousands of times across the world with different
model matrices. The mesh data never changes — only the matrix that lifts it into world space does.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="First-person shooters" icon="🎯">
The view matrix <em>is</em> the camera. Mouse-look updates the camera's world transform; the engine
inverts it to get $V$, and suddenly the entire world is described relative to where you're looking —
which is why your gun (parented to the camera) appears glued to the screen.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Strategy / RTS UI" icon="🧮">
Click-to-select needs the reverse trip: take the mouse's <em>screen</em> pixel, unproject it back through
the inverse viewport, inverse projection and inverse view to get a <em>world</em> ray, then test what it
hits. Picking is the pipeline run backwards.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="AR / mobile games" icon="📱">
A creature pinned to your real table is placed in world space from the phone's tracked pose; the camera's
live position feeds the view matrix; the lens calibration feeds the projection matrix. The same five
spaces, with the camera driven by the real device.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Health bars and damage numbers float above enemies by taking the enemy's <strong>world</strong> position
all the way to <strong>screen</strong> space, then drawing 2D text there. That "3D world to 2D label"
trick is just reading off the screen-space coordinate.
</Callout>
