## The hook: why does the spaceship keep drifting?

Fire up any space shooter and let go of the controls. The ship keeps gliding. Tap a thruster and it
slowly curves. Nothing in that scene is a single number — the ship's **place** on screen, the way it's
**heading**, how **fast** it moves: every one of those is an arrow with a size and a direction. Games
are, underneath the art, a giant pile of arrows being added together sixty times a second. That arrow
is the **vector**, and once you see entities as vectors you can move, steer and chase anything.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Every moving thing in a game is described by **position** (where it is) and **velocity** (where it's
going and how fast) — and each frame the engine just does <strong>position += velocity</strong>.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from arrows to entities

You already met vectors as arrows with a length and direction, or equivalently a list of numbers. Game
code uses exactly that, with three jobs that come up constantly:

- **Position** $\mathbf{p}$ — an arrow from the world origin to the entity. In 2D it's $(x, y)$.
- **Velocity** $\mathbf{v}$ — how the position changes *per second*. Its length is the **speed**; its
  direction is where the entity travels.
- **Direction** $\hat{\mathbf{d}}$ — velocity with its length thrown away, kept at exactly 1. This is
  the **unit vector**, and it answers "which way is the player facing?" independent of speed.

The single most-used trick is **normalizing**: take any vector, divide by its length, and you get a
unit vector pointing the same way. That lets you separate *where* from *how fast* — aim with the unit
vector, then multiply by whatever speed you want.

## The formal definition

An entity in 2D carries a position and a velocity, both vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$:

$$
\mathbf{p} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}, \qquad
\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \end{bmatrix}.
$$

Each frame advances the position by the velocity scaled by the time step $\Delta t$ (seconds since the
last frame):

$$
\mathbf{p}_{\text{new}} = \mathbf{p} + \mathbf{v}\,\Delta t.
$$

The **speed** is the magnitude of the velocity, straight from Pythagoras:

$$
\lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}.
$$

And **normalizing** turns any nonzero vector into a unit-length direction:

$$
\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{\lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert}, \qquad \lVert \hat{\mathbf{v}} \rVert = 1.
$$

```csharp
// Move toward a target at a fixed speed (Unity-style, per frame)
Vector2 toTarget = target - position;       // direction arrow
Vector2 dir = toTarget.normalized;          // length 1
position += dir * speed * deltaTime;         // step along it
```

<Callout type="why">
Multiplying by $\Delta t$ is what makes movement **frame-rate independent**: a 144 Hz gaming PC and a
30 Hz phone take different-sized steps but cover the same distance per second. Forget it and your game
literally runs faster on faster hardware.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Platformers" icon="🍄">
A jumping plumber has velocity $\begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \end{bmatrix}$. Pressing right nudges $v_x$;
the jump sets $v_y$ upward and gravity subtracts from it every frame. The arc you see is just position
accumulating a velocity whose vertical part keeps shrinking.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Top-down shooters" icon="🔫">
A bullet is spawned at the gun's muzzle with velocity $\hat{\mathbf{d}} \cdot \text{muzzleSpeed}$, where
$\hat{\mathbf{d}}$ is the unit aim direction. Normalize first, then scale — otherwise diagonal shots
would travel faster than straight ones (a classic beginner bug).
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Racing games" icon="🏎️">
A car's **speedometer** literally shows $\lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert$, the magnitude of its velocity
vector. The direction of that vector is where the nose points; drifting is the moment velocity and
facing direction stop agreeing.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="RPG companions" icon="🧙">
A follower computes $\mathbf{p}_{\text{player}} - \mathbf{p}_{\text{self}}$ to get the arrow toward you,
normalizes it, and walks along it at its move speed. Subtracting two position vectors to get a "from A
to B" arrow is the most common operation in all of game AI.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Subtraction is the unsung hero: <strong>target − self</strong> is always the arrow pointing from you to
your goal. Normalize it to steer, measure its length to know how far away the goal is.
</Callout>
