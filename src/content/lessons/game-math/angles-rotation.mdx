## The hook: the turret that tracks you

Walk past a sentry turret in any action game and it swivels to follow you — not snapping instantly, but
turning at a believable speed, always taking the short way around. Behind that simple-looking behavior
are three small ideas: figure out the **direction** to the target, convert it to an **angle**, and rotate
toward that angle a little bit each frame without overshooting. Master this and you can make eyes follow
a cursor, a car steer into a corner, or a missile hunt its prey.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
To face a target, take the **angle** of the arrow pointing at it, then each frame rotate your current
heading **toward** that angle by at most your **turn rate** — always the short way around.

</Callout>

## Intuition: from an arrow to a heading

You already know that `target − self` gives the arrow pointing from you to your goal. A **heading** is
just that arrow boiled down to a single number: the angle it makes with the positive x-axis. The tool
that converts a 2D arrow into an angle is `atan2`.

Why `atan2` and not plain $\arctan$? Plain $\arctan(\Delta y / \Delta x)$ throws away which quadrant you
are in — it cannot tell "up-right" from "down-left," and it divides by zero when the target is straight
up. `atan2` takes the two components *separately*, so it returns the correct angle across the full circle
from $-180°$ to $180°$.

Once you have your current heading and the desired heading, the trick is the **difference**. Subtract
them and you might get something like $350°$ — but turning $350°$ is silly when you could turn $-10°$ the
other way. So you **wrap** the difference into the range $-180°$ to $180°$, which always picks the
shorter rotation. Finally, you only move by your **angular velocity** times the frame time, so the turn
is smooth and capped.

## The formal definition

The desired heading toward a target is the angle of the displacement arrow:

$$
\theta_{\text{target}} = \operatorname{atan2}(\Delta y,\ \Delta x), \qquad \Delta = \mathbf{p}_{\text{target}} - \mathbf{p}_{\text{self}}.
$$

The raw difference between desired and current heading must be **wrapped** to the shortest signed angle:

$$
\Delta\theta = \bigl((\theta_{\text{target}} - \theta) + \pi \bmod 2\pi\bigr) - \pi \in (-\pi,\ \pi].
$$

Then you turn by at most the angular velocity $\omega$ times the frame time $\Delta t$, clamping so you
never overshoot:

$$
\theta_{\text{new}} = \theta + \operatorname{clamp}\bigl(\Delta\theta,\ -\omega\,\Delta t,\ \omega\,\Delta t\bigr).
$$

```csharp
// Turn smoothly toward a target, capped at turnRate (rad/s)
float desired = Mathf.Atan2(target.y - pos.y, target.x - pos.x);
float maxStep = turnRate * Time.deltaTime;
heading = Mathf.MoveTowardsAngle(heading, desired, maxStep);
```

<Callout type="why">
Multiplying the turn by $\Delta t$ makes rotation <strong>frame-rate independent</strong>, just like
movement. And clamping to $\omega\,\Delta t$ is what separates a snappy arcade turret from a heavy tank
that swings its turret slowly — the same code, one different number.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Tower defense" icon="🏰">
Each tower picks the nearest enemy, computes the heading to it with `atan2`, and rotates its cannon at a
fixed turn rate. A slow turn rate becomes a balancing knob: heavy towers hit hard but cannot track fast
flyers, so you must place them where targets approach head-on.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Stealth games" icon="🕵️">
A guard's vision cone is centered on its heading. To check if it can see you, the guard takes the angle
to the player and asks whether the wrapped difference from its heading is smaller than half the cone
angle — pure angle math deciding whether the alarm sounds.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Twin-stick shooters" icon="🎮">
The character continuously faces the aim stick (or mouse). The render code sets the sprite's rotation to
`atan2` of the aim direction every frame, so the gun points exactly where you are pushing — instant,
because the turn rate here is effectively infinite.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Homing missiles" icon="🚀">
A seeking missile flies forward at constant speed but can only turn so fast. Each frame it nudges its
heading toward the target by a capped angle, which is exactly why a nimble player can out-juke a missile
with a sharp turn the missile cannot match.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The same wrap-and-clamp pattern drives <strong>angular velocity</strong> in general: a spinning coin, a
rotating platform, or a steering wheel all advance an angle by a rate times $\Delta t$ each frame.
</Callout>
