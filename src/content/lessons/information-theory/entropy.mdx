## The hook: which text message actually tells you something?

A friend sends two texts. The first: *"The sun came up this morning."* The second: *"It just snowed
in the Sahara."* Both are short sentences, but only one makes you sit up. The sunrise was a near
certainty — you learned almost nothing. The desert snow was wildly unlikely — it carries a flood of
**information**. Information theory makes this gut feeling exact: the **less probable** an outcome, the
**more surprise** it delivers when it happens, and the natural unit of that surprise is the **bit**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Entropy** is the average surprise of a random variable — how many yes/no questions, on average, you
need to pin down its outcome. It is largest when every outcome is equally likely and zero when the
result is already certain.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from surprise to bits

You already know that a random variable spreads probability across its values (the **pmf** from the
random-variables lesson). Information theory asks a new question about that spread: *how surprising is
a typical draw?*

Start with one outcome of probability $p$. Its **surprise** (or *information content*) is

$$
I(x) = \log_2 \frac{1}{p(x)} = -\log_2 p(x).
$$

Why a logarithm, and why base 2? Two reasons make this the *only* sensible choice:

- **Rarer means more surprising.** As $p \to 1$ the surprise drops to $0$ (no news); as $p \to 0$ it
  shoots up. A sure thing tells you nothing.
- **Independent surprises add.** If two independent things happen, probabilities multiply but surprise
  should *add*. The logarithm is exactly the function that turns multiplication into addition, so
  $I(x,y) = I(x) + I(y)$. Base $2$ makes the unit a **bit** — the answer to one yes/no question.

**Entropy** is then just the *expected* surprise: average $I(x)$ over the distribution, weighting each
outcome by how often it occurs. A coin you can predict perfectly has zero entropy; a fair coin has
exactly one bit; a fair die has $\log_2 6 \approx 2.585$ bits.

## The formal definition

For a discrete random variable $X$ with pmf $p(x)$, the **Shannon entropy** is the probability-weighted
average of the surprise:

$$
H(X) = \sum_x p(x)\, \log_2 \frac{1}{p(x)} = -\sum_x p(x)\, \log_2 p(x).
$$

Measured with $\log_2$ the unit is **bits**; with the natural log $\ln$ it is **nats**. A few anchors
worth memorizing:

$$
H_{\min} = 0 \;\text{(one outcome certain)}, \qquad H_{\max} = \log_2 n \;\text{(uniform over } n \text{ outcomes)}.
$$

The maximum-at-uniform fact is not an accident: among all distributions on $n$ outcomes, the flat one
spreads probability as evenly as possible, leaving the most to be resolved. For a two-outcome variable
with $P(X=1)=p$, entropy is the famous **binary entropy** curve, peaking at $p=\tfrac12$:

$$
H(p) = -p\log_2 p - (1-p)\log_2 (1-p).
$$

<Callout type="why">
Entropy is the floor on **lossless compression**: Shannon's source-coding theorem says you cannot
encode draws of $X$ in fewer than $H(X)$ bits each on average. Every ML loss built on log-probabilities
— the cross-entropy that trains classifiers, the perplexity that scores language models — is entropy
wearing a different hat. Master this and the next three lessons fall into place.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Surprise-measured-in-bits shows up anywhere a system must deal with uncertainty efficiently.

<ExampleCard domain="Telegraphy" icon="📻">
**Morse code** is entropy coding done by hand in 1840. The letter *E* (very common in English) gets the
shortest symbol, a single dot; rare *Q* gets a long dash-dash-dot-dash. Assigning short codes to
high-probability letters and long codes to rare ones is precisely how you push the average message
length down toward the entropy of the letter distribution.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Word games" icon="🎯">
In **20 Questions** each yes/no answer can at best halve the remaining possibilities — one bit. To
single out one item from a million you need about $\log_2(10^6) \approx 20$ questions, which is exactly
why the game is named for twenty. Wordle players chase the same goal: a good opening guess is the one
that carries the most **expected bits** of information.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="File compression" icon="🗜️">
A **ZIP** archive shrinks a file by replacing frequent byte patterns with short codes. A document that
is all one repeated character has near-zero entropy and compresses to almost nothing; an already-random
encrypted file has near-maximal entropy and barely shrinks at all. The compression ratio is a direct
readout of the data's entropy.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Security" icon="🔐">
**Password strength** is measured in bits of entropy. A truly random 4-digit PIN has $\log_2(10^4)
\approx 13.3$ bits; a random 12-character passphrase from a large alphabet has $70$-plus bits. Each
extra bit *doubles* the number of guesses an attacker must try — entropy literally counts the work
needed to break in.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Two of these — Morse and ZIP — use entropy to *spend fewer bits*; two — 20 Questions and passwords —
use it to *count the bits of uncertainty* to be resolved. Same number, opposite directions.
</Callout>
