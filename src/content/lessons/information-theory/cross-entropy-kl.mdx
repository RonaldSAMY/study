## The hook: the price of being confidently wrong

Imagine an app that predicts tomorrow's headline category and announces *"99% chance: sports."* If it's
right, fine. But on the day a political scandal breaks, that same overconfidence is expensive — the app
assigned almost no probability to what actually happened, and a good scoring rule should punish it
hard. Entropy told us the *irreducible* surprise baked into reality. Now we need a number for the
**extra** surprise we suffer when our **model** of reality is wrong. That number is **cross-entropy**,
and the wrongness alone is the **KL divergence**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Cross-entropy** is the average number of bits you spend encoding outcomes from the true distribution
$p$ using a code built for your predicted distribution $q$; the **KL divergence** is the *wasted* bits
above the unavoidable minimum — and it is zero exactly when $q = p$.
</Callout>

## Intuition: coding with the wrong codebook

From the last lesson, the best possible code spends $H(p)$ bits per symbol when the true distribution
is $p$ — short codes for likely outcomes, long codes for rare ones. But you never know $p$ exactly; you
have a **model** $q$. If you build your codebook around $q$ and reality follows $p$, you assign the
wrong code lengths, and your average cost rises to the **cross-entropy** $H(p, q)$.

The overspend has a name. It is the **Kullback–Leibler divergence** $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, q)$, the
gap between what you paid and the best you could have paid:

$$
\underbrace{H(p, q)}_{\text{bits you spend}} = \underbrace{H(p)}_{\text{unavoidable}} + \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, q)}_{\text{wasted by a wrong } q}.
$$

Two facts make this useful. First, $D_{\mathrm{KL}} \ge 0$ always (Gibbs' inequality), and it equals
$0$ **only** when $q = p$. So minimizing cross-entropy drives your model toward the truth. Second, KL
is **not symmetric**: $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, q) \ne D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q \,\|\, p)$ in general, so it is
a *divergence*, not a true distance.

## The formal definition

For distributions $p$ (true) and $q$ (model) over the same outcomes, the **cross-entropy** is

$$
H(p, q) = -\sum_x p(x)\, \log_2 q(x),
$$

and the **KL divergence** of $q$ from $p$ is

$$
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, q) = \sum_x p(x)\, \log_2 \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = H(p, q) - H(p) \;\ge\; 0.
$$

In supervised learning the true label is usually **one-hot**: $p$ puts all its mass on the correct
class $y$. Then $H(p) = 0$, the sum collapses to a single term, and cross-entropy becomes the
**negative log-likelihood** of the right answer:

$$
H(p, q) = -\log_2 q(y) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, q).
$$

This is the categorical cross-entropy loss, the workhorse objective behind logistic regression, image
classifiers, and language models alike. Predict the truth with high probability and the loss is tiny;
assign the correct class a sliver of probability and the loss explodes.

<Callout type="why">
Training a classifier by minimizing cross-entropy is *identical* to minimizing
$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, q)$ between the data distribution and the model — and that, by the equation
above, is the same as **maximum likelihood**. Three ideas you may have met separately — cross-entropy
loss, KL divergence, and maximum likelihood — are one and the same gradient.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Any time a model's probabilities are scored against what really happens, cross-entropy is the referee.

<ExampleCard domain="Language models" icon="💬">
A language model's quality is reported as **perplexity**, which is just $2$ raised to the cross-entropy
per token: $\text{PPL} = 2^{H(p,q)}$. A perplexity of $20$ means the model is, on average, as unsure as
if choosing uniformly among $20$ words. Lowering cross-entropy on held-out text is *the* objective of
training a model like this one.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Medical testing" icon="🩺">
A diagnostic model that outputs a probability of disease is scored by **log loss** — cross-entropy
against the eventual ground truth. A doctor's tool that says *"5% risk"* about a patient who is in fact
sick pays a huge penalty, which is why calibration matters: a well-calibrated $q$ minimizes expected
cross-entropy and earns trust.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports betting" icon="🎰">
A bookmaker's odds are an implied distribution $q$ over outcomes. A bettor with a better model $p$
profits in proportion to the **KL divergence** between them — Kelly betting grows your bankroll at a
rate equal to $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, q)$. Being right where the market is wrong is literally measured
in bits.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Neural networks" icon="🤖">
The final softmax layer of a classifier emits a distribution $q$ over labels; the training label is the
one-hot $p$. Backpropagation minimizes $-\log q(y)$ for every example. The elegant payoff: the gradient
of softmax-plus-cross-entropy reduces to the clean residual $q - p$, which is why this pairing is the
default in deep learning.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="warning">
KL is directional. Minimizing $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, q)$ (used in classification) makes $q$ cover
every outcome $p$ allows — "mean-seeking." Minimizing the *reverse*, $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q \,\|\, p)$ (used
in variational inference), lets $q$ lock onto one mode and ignore the rest. Same symbols, very different
behavior.
</Callout>
