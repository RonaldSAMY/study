## The hook: two very different models, one currency

A deep neural network and a humble decision tree look nothing alike. One is a million-parameter stack of
matrix multiplies; the other is a flowchart of yes/no questions. Yet both run on the same currency:
**bits of information**. The network learns by minimizing **cross-entropy**; the tree grows by greedily
maximizing **information gain**, which is just mutual information in disguise. The three measures from
the last three lessons aren't abstractions — they are the engines inside the algorithms you actually
deploy.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Training a classifier means **minimizing cross-entropy** between its predictions and the truth, and
splitting a decision tree means **maximizing the information gain** — the mutual information — between a
feature and the label.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the two great appearances

**Appearance one — the loss function.** Every probabilistic classifier outputs a distribution $q$ over
labels; the data hands you the true label $p$ (one-hot). From the cross-entropy lesson, the cost of
that prediction is $-\log q(y)$. Average it over the dataset and you have the **cross-entropy loss**,
the single most common objective in supervised learning. Gradient descent simply slides the model's
parameters downhill on this surface.

**Appearance two — the split rule.** A decision tree must choose *which question to ask*. Before a
split, the labels in a node have entropy $H(S)$. After splitting on a feature, each child is purer, with
lower average entropy. The **information gain** is the drop:

$$
\mathrm{IG}(S, A) = H(S) - \sum_{v}\frac{|S_v|}{|S|}\, H(S_v).
$$

Here is the beautiful part: that expression is exactly the **mutual information** $I(\text{label};A)$
between the label and the feature you split on. The tree, asking *"which question tells me the most
about the answer?"*, is literally doing greedy mutual-information maximization — the same idea as the
20-questions game, run automatically.

## The formal definition

**Cross-entropy loss** over a dataset of $N$ examples with $C$ classes, true one-hot labels $y_{ic}$
and predicted probabilities $q_{ic}$:

$$
\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{c=1}^{C} y_{ic}\, \log q_{ic}.
$$

Minimizing $\mathcal{L}$ is identical to **maximum likelihood** and to minimizing the KL divergence to
the data distribution — the chain of equalities from two lessons ago. For language models the same loss
is reported as **perplexity** $= e^{\mathcal{L}}$ (using nats), the effective branching factor of the
model's predictions.

**Information gain** for a tree split is mutual information between the label $Y$ and the split outcome
$A$:

$$
\mathrm{IG} = H(Y) - H(Y \mid A) = I(Y; A) \;\ge\; 0.
$$

A greedy learner (ID3, C4.5, and the entropy criterion in modern libraries) evaluates every candidate
feature and threshold, computes $\mathrm{IG}$, and keeps the largest — then recurses on each child.

<Callout type="why">
This is the punchline of the whole track. The "loss" you minimize and the "criterion" a tree splits on
are not arbitrary engineering choices — they are forced by information theory. Cross-entropy is the only
loss consistent with treating outputs as probabilities, and information gain is the only split rule that
measures bits of label uncertainty removed. The math you learned *is* the algorithm.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

From flowcharts to deep space, the same bits do the work.

<ExampleCard domain="Lending" icon="🏦">
A credit model that approves loans is often a **gradient-boosted tree**. At every node it asks the
question with the highest information gain — *"is income above a threshold?"*, *"more than two missed
payments?"* — ranking applicants by how sharply each split separates *repaid* from *defaulted*. The tree
is choosing the most informative questions, in order.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Deep-space communication" icon="🛰️">
A probe near Saturn whispers data across billions of kilometers through noise. **Shannon's channel
capacity** $C = \max I(X;Y)$ sets the hard limit on reliable bits per second; **error-correcting codes**
add just enough redundancy to approach it. The same mutual information that picks tree splits here caps
how fast the universe lets you talk.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Field biology" icon="🦋">
A naturalist's **identification key** is a hand-built decision tree: *wings or no wings? spotted or
plain?* A well-designed key orders its questions by information gain so the species is named in the
fewest steps. Long before computers, biologists were minimizing expected questions — entropy in a field
notebook.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Online advertising" icon="📣">
Click-through-rate models predict the probability you tap an ad and are trained with **log loss** —
cross-entropy against whether the click actually happened. A model that confidently predicts a click
that never comes is penalized hard, so the system learns calibrated probabilities that decide, in
milliseconds, which ad to show.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="warning">
Information gain is biased toward features with **many distinct values** (a unique ID would "perfectly"
split the data with maximal gain yet generalize terribly). Real learners fix this with the **gain
ratio** or by penalizing high-cardinality splits — a reminder that maximizing bits on the training set
is not the same as learning.
</Callout>
