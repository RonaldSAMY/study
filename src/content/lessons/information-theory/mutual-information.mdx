## The hook: how much does height tell you about shoe size?

If I tell you a stranger is unusually tall, you'd confidently bet their shoes are large too — not
because height *causes* a shoe size in any simple way, but because the two travel together. Knowing one
**shrinks your uncertainty** about the other. Plain correlation captures part of this, but only the
straight-line part. **Mutual information** captures *all* of it: every way two variables share fate,
linear or curved, smooth or lumpy, measured in honest bits.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The **mutual information** $I(X;Y)$ is the number of bits of uncertainty about $Y$ that vanish once you
learn $X$ (and vice versa — it's symmetric). It is zero exactly when the two variables are independent.
</Callout>

## Intuition: uncertainty removed

Recall entropy $H(Y)$ — your uncertainty about $Y$ before you know anything. Now suppose you get to see
$X$ first. Your *remaining* uncertainty about $Y$ is the **conditional entropy** $H(Y \mid X)$: the
average entropy of $Y$ once $X$ is pinned down. The drop between the two is the information that $X$
carried about $Y$:

$$
I(X;Y) = H(Y) - H(Y \mid X).
$$

If $X$ tells you nothing, $H(Y \mid X) = H(Y)$ and the information is $0$. If $X$ determines $Y$
completely, $H(Y \mid X) = 0$ and the information equals the entire $H(Y)$. Everything useful lives in
between.

There is a second, deeper way to read the same quantity. Two variables are **independent** exactly when
their joint distribution factorizes, $p(x,y) = p(x)\,p(y)$. Mutual information is the **KL divergence**
(from the last lesson) between the real joint and that idealized independent version — a measurement of
*how far from independent* the variables actually are:

$$
I(X;Y) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}\big(p(x,y)\,\|\,p(x)\,p(y)\big).
$$

Because KL is never negative, $I(X;Y) \ge 0$, with equality **only** under independence.

## The formal definition

For a joint pmf $p(x,y)$ with marginals $p(x)$ and $p(y)$, mutual information is

$$
I(X;Y) = \sum_{x}\sum_{y} p(x,y)\, \log_2 \frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)\,p(y)}.
$$

It can be rewritten in several equivalent and illuminating ways:

$$
I(X;Y) = H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y) = H(X) - H(X \mid Y) = H(Y) - H(Y \mid X).
$$

Key properties, all flowing from these forms:

- **Non-negative and symmetric:** $I(X;Y) = I(Y;X) \ge 0$. Shared information has no preferred direction.
- **Zero iff independent:** $I(X;Y) = 0 \iff p(x,y) = p(x)\,p(y)$.
- **Bounded:** $I(X;Y) \le \min\big(H(X), H(Y)\big)$ — you can't learn more about $Y$ than $Y$'s own
  uncertainty.
- **Catches nonlinearity:** unlike Pearson correlation, MI sees curved or non-monotone dependence;
  $Y = X^2$ has zero correlation but plenty of mutual information.

<Callout type="why">
Mutual information is the principled answer to *"which feature is worth keeping?"* A feature with high
$I(\text{feature};\text{label})$ carries real signal about the target; one with $I \approx 0$ is noise,
no matter how complex it looks. It also underlies the **information bottleneck** view of deep learning,
where a good representation maximizes information about the label while compressing away the input.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Wherever two quantities might share information, MI puts a number on it.

<ExampleCard domain="Feature selection" icon="🧪">
Building a churn model, you score each candidate feature by its mutual information with the
"will-cancel" label. *Months-since-last-login* might carry $0.4$ bits while *zodiac sign* carries
$\approx 0$. Ranking features by $I(\text{feature};\text{label})$ — called the **information gain
filter** — drops the dead weight before any model is trained.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Genetics" icon="🧬">
Two genes that sit close on a chromosome are **co-inherited**, so their variants are statistically
linked. Geneticists quantify this *linkage disequilibrium* with mutual information between the two loci:
high MI flags genes that travel together through generations, helping map which stretch of DNA carries a
trait.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Language" icon="📰">
Why do *"strong tea"* and *"powerful engine"* sound right but *"powerful tea"* sound off? Linguists
measure **pointwise mutual information** between word pairs: collocations like *San–Francisco* or
*machine–learning* have high PMI, meaning the words co-occur far more than chance. Search engines and
language models lean on exactly this signal.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Computer vision" icon="📸">
To align two photos of the same scene from different cameras, software slides one image over the other
and **maximizes the mutual information** between their pixel intensities. Because MI doesn't assume the
brightness scales match, it aligns even images with wildly different contrast — the standard trick in
multi-sensor image registration.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Correlation asks *"do they rise and fall together in a straight line?"* Mutual information asks the more
honest question: *"does knowing one reduce my uncertainty about the other — in any way at all?"* That
generality is why MI shows up far beyond linear statistics.
</Callout>
