## The hook: how much rain fell on the valley?

A storm drops rain unevenly across a watershed — a downpour over the ridge, a drizzle in the basin. The
rainfall *rate* is a function of location, $r(x, y)$. To get the **total volume** of water collected, you
cannot just multiply one number by the area; the rate changes everywhere. Instead you chop the valley into
tiny patches, multiply each patch's area by the rainfall right there, and add it all up. Shrink the patches
to nothing and that sum becomes a **double integral** — the same "accumulate over a region" move that, in
probability, turns a density into a probability.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **multiple integral** adds up the value of a function over every point of a region — geometrically, the
**volume trapped between the surface $z = f(x, y)$ and the ground**.

</Callout>

## Intuition: a Riemann sum in two dimensions

You already know the single integral $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ as the **area** under a curve, built from thin
rectangles. A double integral repeats the idea one dimension up. Instead of thin rectangles under a curve we
stack thin **boxes** under a surface: each box sits on a small tile of the region, its height is the
function's value there, and its volume is

$$
\text{height} \times \text{area} = f(x_i, y_j)\,\Delta x \,\Delta y.
$$

Sum the boxes and you get a **Riemann sum** — a blocky, staircase approximation of the volume. Make the
tiles smaller and the staircase hugs the smooth surface ever more tightly. In the limit of infinitely many
infinitely small tiles, the approximation becomes exact:

$$
\iint_R f(x, y)\,dA = \lim_{\Delta x, \Delta y \to 0} \sum_{i, j} f(x_i, y_j)\,\Delta x \,\Delta y.
$$

The deep point: integration is just **summation taken to the continuous limit**. That is why expectations,
total probability, and average loss over a data distribution are all integrals in disguise.

<Callout type="tip">
At $n = 1$ the entire region is one giant box — a wild over- or under-estimate. Each time you double the
resolution the error roughly quarters, because you are refining in <em>both</em> directions at once. That is
the 2D version of "more rectangles, better integral."
</Callout>

## The formal definition

Over a rectangular region $R = [a, b] \times [c, d]$, a double integral can be computed as an **iterated
integral** — integrate one variable at a time, holding the other fixed (the reverse of a partial
derivative):

$$
\iint_R f(x, y)\,dA = \int_a^b \!\left( \int_c^d f(x, y)\,dy \right) dx.
$$

**Fubini's theorem** says the order does not matter for nice functions: you may integrate $x$ first or $y$
first and get the same answer. When you change to better-suited coordinates (say polar), the **Jacobian
determinant** from the chain-rule lesson reappears as the area-scaling factor:

$$
\iint f \, dx\,dy = \iint f \,\lvert \det J \rvert \, du\,dv .
$$

The idea extends to any dimension — triple integrals for mass and charge, and $n$-fold integrals over a
probability space:

$$
\mathbb{E}[g(\mathbf{x})] = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} g(\mathbf{x})\, p(\mathbf{x})\, d\mathbf{x}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
Machine learning is awash in integrals. The "true" loss is the **expected** loss over the whole data
distribution — an integral $\int \ell(\mathbf{x})\,p(\mathbf{x})\,d\mathbf{x}$. We can't compute it exactly,
so training estimates it with a finite sum over a mini-batch: a Riemann-sum-style sample of the integral.
The grid above is a picture of exactly that approximation.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Hydrology" icon="🌧️">
The demo's watershed is the real use case: total runoff is the double integral of rainfall rate over the
basin's area. Flood planners integrate predicted rainfall maps to size reservoirs and storm drains before
the next storm arrives.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="3D printing" icon="🧱">
The amount of plastic in a printed part is the integral of its height profile over the base — volume under a
surface, literally. Slicing software sums material layer by layer, a 3D Riemann sum that predicts spool
usage and print time.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Medical imaging" icon="🩻">
A CT scanner reconstructs tissue density $\rho(x, y)$ across a slice. The total mass or contrast within an
organ is the double integral of density over that organ's region — accumulating a function over an area to
get a clinically meaningful total.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Astronomy" icon="🌌">
A galaxy's total brightness is the integral of its surface-brightness map over the sky. Astronomers sum
light across every pixel of the image — a Riemann sum over the telescope's grid — to measure how much energy
the galaxy pours out.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="warning">
For non-rectangular regions the inner integral's limits can depend on the outer variable (e.g. integrating
over a triangle or disk). Sketch the region first and read the limits off the picture — swapping the order
of integration then often turns a hard integral into an easy one.
</Callout>
