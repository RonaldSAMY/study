## The hook: what's the one number that captures a face?

A photo of a face is tens of thousands of pixels, yet faces are not random — they vary in a few
coordinated ways: lighting, the shape of the jaw, the set of the eyes. Researchers found you can rebuild
a recognizable face from a small handful of template faces called **eigenfaces**, each a direction of
greatest variation across a database of portraits. Storing a face becomes storing a few numbers — *how
much of each template*. That trick of replacing thousands of correlated measurements with a few
meaningful ones is **Principal Component Analysis**, and it is where every idea in this track finally
joins forces.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**PCA** rotates your data to line up with its directions of greatest spread, then keeps only the top
few — compressing many correlated features into a handful that capture most of the variation.
</Callout>

## Intuition: turn the cloud until it's widest

Picture a 2-D cloud of data points stretched into a tilted oval. The two original axes (feature 1,
feature 2) are an arbitrary choice — the cloud doesn't care about them. PCA asks a better question:
*along which direction does the data spread the most?* That widest direction is the **first principal
component (PC1)**; the next-widest direction perpendicular to it is **PC2**, and so on.

Here's how every earlier lesson shows up at once:

- The spread of the data is summarized by its **covariance matrix** — a symmetric matrix.
- From the eigenvector lesson, a symmetric matrix has **perpendicular eigenvectors** and real
  eigenvalues. Those eigenvectors *are* the principal components, and each **eigenvalue is the variance
  along that direction**.
- Keeping the top components and **projecting** the data onto them (the projection lesson) gives a
  faithful low-dimensional summary — and this is precisely the **SVD's** best low-rank approximation
  from the last lesson, applied to centered data.

So PCA is not a new trick; it's the eigen/SVD machinery pointed at the question "where is the
information?" The answer: in the directions of largest variance.

the cloud onto PC1 loses almost nothing — the points were really 1-D all along. When both spreads are
equal there is no preferred direction, and PCA can't compress.
</Callout>

## The formal definition

Given data points stacked as rows of a matrix $X$, first **center** them by subtracting the mean of
each column. The **covariance matrix** is

$$
C = \frac{1}{n}\, X^{\top} X \quad (\text{centered}),
$$

a symmetric $d \times d$ matrix. PCA finds its **eigen-decomposition**

$$
C = V \Lambda V^{\top},
$$

where the columns of $V$ are the orthonormal **principal components** $\mathbf{v}_1, \dots,
\mathbf{v}_d$ and $\Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1 \ge \cdots \ge \lambda_d)$ holds the
**variance** along each. Equivalently, run the **SVD** $X = U\Sigma V^{\top}$: the same $V$ appears, and
$\lambda_i = \sigma_i^2 / n$. To reduce to $k$ dimensions, **project** onto the top $k$ components:

$$
Z = X V_k, \qquad V_k = [\,\mathbf{v}_1 \;\cdots\; \mathbf{v}_k\,].
$$

The fraction of variance kept is $\dfrac{\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_k}{\lambda_1 + \cdots +
\lambda_d}$ — your "how much did I preserve?" dial.

<Callout type="why">
PCA is the most-used dimensionality reducer in ML: it speeds up models, removes correlated noise,
fights overfitting, and makes high-dimensional data drawable in 2-D. It is the direct payoff of vectors,
bases, eigenvectors, projections, and the SVD — the entire track in one technique.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Face recognition" icon="🙂">
**Eigenfaces** are the principal components of a database of portraits. Any face is approximated as a
mean face plus a few eigenfaces with the right weights, so recognition compares short coefficient
vectors instead of raw pixels — faster and far more robust to lighting.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Population genetics" icon="🧬">
Run PCA on genetic markers across many people and the top two components reproduce a **map of
geography** — individuals cluster by ancestral region. The dominant directions of genetic variation
turn out to encode where populations historically lived and mixed.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Finance" icon="💹">
The daily moves of hundreds of stocks are highly correlated. PCA reveals that the first component is
essentially "the whole market goes up or down," and the next few are sector rotations — a few factors
explain most of the chaos, which is how risk models compress thousands of assets.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports science" icon="🏃">
Wearable sensors log dozens of correlated gait measurements per stride. PCA collapses them into a few
**movement signatures** — overall intensity, asymmetry, fatigue — letting coaches track an athlete's
form with a handful of interpretable numbers instead of raw sensor floods.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
PCA keeps directions of greatest <em>variance</em>, which is great for compression but not always for
prediction — sometimes a low-variance direction is the one that matters. That tension is exactly what
the next tracks, regression and optimization, take on.
</Callout>
