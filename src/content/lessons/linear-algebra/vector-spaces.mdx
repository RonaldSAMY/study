## The hook: how many LEGO bricks do you actually need?

Imagine a giant tub of LEGO. To rebuild *any* model in the set, you don't need every brick — you
need a small, well-chosen **kit** of brick types. From that kit you can combine and stack to reach
every shape the set allows. Pick a kit that's too small and some models are impossible; throw in a
brick that's just two smaller bricks glued together and you've wasted a slot — it adds nothing new.

Linear algebra has exactly this idea. A handful of vectors that can build *everything* in a space,
with **no redundancy**, is called a **basis**. The size of that kit is the **dimension**, and the
number of genuinely independent directions a set of vectors gives you is its **rank**. These three
words describe the skeleton of every dataset, image, and model you will ever touch.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **basis** is a minimal set of building-block vectors whose combinations reach every point in a
space; the **rank** counts how many truly independent directions you actually have.
</Callout>

## Intuition: building blocks, span, and waste

You already know two moves from earlier lessons: **adding** vectors and **scaling** them. Chaining
those moves together — $c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots$ — is a **linear combination**, and
the set of *all* points you can reach this way is the **span** (the idea behind the earlier span
lesson).

Now ask two sharper questions about a collection of vectors:

1. **Do they reach everything?** If every point in the plane is some combination of them, they
   **span** the space.
2. **Is any one of them wasted?** If one vector is already a combination of the others, it adds no new
   direction — the set is **linearly dependent**. If none is redundant, the set is **independent**.

A set that is *both* spanning *and* independent is a **basis** — just enough, with nothing wasted. In
2D you need exactly two independent vectors. The moment your two vectors line up (become parallel),
you lose a direction: the **rank** drops from 2 to 1, and the span collapses from the whole plane down
to a single line. That collapse is the same event you met as a **zero determinant** in the previous
lesson.

## The formal definition

A **vector space** $V$ is a set you can add and scale within, where the results stay inside and obey
the familiar rules (associativity, a zero vector, distributivity, and so on). A **linear combination**
of vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \dots, \mathbf{v}_k$ is

$$
c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k, \qquad c_i \in \mathbb{R},
$$

and their **span** is the set of *all* such combinations. The vectors are **linearly independent** if
the only way to get the zero vector is the trivial one:

$$
c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad c_1 = \cdots = c_k = 0.
$$

A **basis** of $V$ is a set that is both linearly independent and spans $V$. Every basis of a given
space has the same number of vectors — that number is the **dimension**, $\dim V$. For a matrix, the
**rank** is the dimension of the span of its columns:

$$
\operatorname{rank}(A) = \dim\big(\operatorname{span}\{\text{columns of } A\}\big).
$$

<Callout type="why">
Rank is the number of *independent* features your data really carries. If a 100-column dataset has
rank 7, then 93 columns are combinations of the other 7 — the data secretly lives in a 7-dimensional
space. Spotting that is the first step toward compression, denoising, and PCA later in this track.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Genetics" icon="🧬">
Human genomes have hundreds of thousands of markers, yet when researchers look for **independent
directions** of variation, only a handful matter — and astonishingly, the top two often reconstruct a
map of geographic ancestry. The genome's effective **rank** is tiny compared to its raw size; a small
**basis** of variation patterns explains most of the differences between people.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Music production" icon="🎚️">
A mixing board has many faders, but a finished track is a **linear combination** of a few stems —
drums, bass, vocals, synth. Those stems form a **basis** for the song: blend them with different gains
and you span every mix. If two stems are identical, one fader is redundant — dependent — and your
effective control **rank** drops.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Cooking" icon="🧂">
A pantry of five base sauces (a *mother-sauce* kit) lets a chef build dozens of dishes by combining
and scaling them. That kit **spans** the menu. Add a sixth sauce that's just two others mixed together
and it's **dependent** — it expands nothing. The smallest kit that still covers the menu is the
**basis**.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Robotics" icon="🤖">
A robot arm's reachable poses are spanned by its independent **joints**. Each joint that moves the
hand in a genuinely new direction adds to the arm's **rank** (its degrees of freedom). If two joints
happen to produce the same motion, the arm loses a degree of freedom — a *singular configuration*,
exactly a rank collapse.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
"Dimension," "rank," and "degrees of freedom" are the same idea wearing three coats: how many
independent knobs you can turn. The rest of the track is about finding the *best* knobs to turn.
</Callout>
