## The hook: how does your phone know which way to go?

When a map app says *"in 200 meters, turn left,"* it is doing arithmetic with **arrows**. Your
position, your speed, the direction to the next turn — each is a quantity that needs **both a size and
a direction**. A single number like "200" can't capture "200 meters *to the northeast*." We need a
richer object. That object is the **vector**, and it is the single most important idea in all of
machine learning.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **vector** is an arrow: it has a **length** (how much) and a **direction** (which way). Equivalently,
it's just an ordered **list of numbers** — and that second view is what lets us describe data,
images, words, and users as vectors.
</Callout>

## Intuition: two faces of the same thing

There are two ways to picture a vector, and fluency means switching between them effortlessly:

1. **The geometric view** — an arrow from the origin to a point. The arrow $\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$
   means *"go 3 right and 1 up."*
2. **The list-of-numbers view** — the components $(3, 1)$. This view doesn't care about the picture;
   it scales to 3, 300, or 3-million dimensions, which is exactly what data needs.

A 2D vector is written $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{bmatrix}$. The little arrow (or bold
type) signals "this is a vector, not a single number."

## The formal definition

A vector in $n$ dimensions is an ordered list of $n$ real numbers:

$$
\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n.
$$

Two operations make vectors useful, and **everything** in linear algebra is built from them:

**Addition** (component by component):

$$
\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 + b_1 \\ a_2 + b_2 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

**Scalar multiplication** (stretch or shrink by a number $k$):

$$
k\,\mathbf{a} = \begin{bmatrix} k\,a_1 \\ k\,a_2 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

The **length** (or *magnitude* / *norm*) comes straight from the Pythagorean theorem:

$$
\lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots + v_n^2}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
These two operations are the whole game. "A space where you can add things and scale them" is the
definition of a **vector space** — and neural-network weights, images, and word embeddings all live in
one.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Vectors aren't only arrows on graph paper. Here is the *same* mathematical object showing up in
completely different places.

<ExampleCard domain="Navigation" icon="🧭">
A drone's **displacement** is the vector $\begin{bmatrix} 400 \\ 300 \end{bmatrix}$ meters (east, north).
Its length $\sqrt{400^2 + 300^2} = 500$ m is the straight-line distance, and the direction is the bearing
it must fly. Add the wind's velocity vector and you get the *true* path over the ground.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Physics" icon="🍎">
Two ropes pull a crate. Each pull is a **force vector**. The crate doesn't feel them separately — it
feels their **vector sum**. If the forces are equal and opposite, the sum is the zero vector and nothing
moves. This is exactly the *tip-to-tail* addition from the demo.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Digital color" icon="🎨">
Every pixel on your screen is a vector $\begin{bmatrix} R \\ G \\ B \end{bmatrix}$ with three numbers from
0–255. Pure red is $\begin{bmatrix} 255 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$. Averaging two color vectors blends the
colors — addition and scaling, doing real work.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Recommender systems" icon="🎬">
A streaming service represents your taste as a vector — maybe
$[\,\text{comedy}, \text{action}, \text{romance}, \dots\,]$. Each movie is a vector too. When your vector
and a movie's vector **point the same way**, you get a recommendation. (That "same way" test is the
dot product — the next lesson.)
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the leap: the first two examples are 2D arrows you can draw; the last lives in *hundreds* of
dimensions you can't picture. The algebra is identical. That's the power of the list-of-numbers view.
</Callout>
