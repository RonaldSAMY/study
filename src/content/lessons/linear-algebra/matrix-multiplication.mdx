## The hook: rotate, then scale — in one step

A game character spins to face a new direction, then the camera zooms in. That's two transformations
applied in order. The astonishing thing about matrices is that you can **pre-combine** them: multiply
the rotation matrix by the scale matrix once, and the single result does *both* moves at the same
cost as one. Graphics cards, robot controllers, and neural networks all rely on this — chaining
transformations by multiplying their matrices instead of redoing the work every frame.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Matrix multiplication is function composition**: the product $AB$ is the single matrix that does
"apply $B$ first, then $A$."
</Callout>

## Intuition: a matrix is a verb

In the matrices lesson, a matrix's columns told you **where the basis vectors land**. So a matrix is an
*action* — a verb that relocates every point in space.

What if you want to do two actions in a row? Apply $B$, then apply $A$. The combined effect is itself a
linear transformation, so it must be *some* matrix — and that matrix is exactly the product $AB$.

The mechanical rule for one entry: the entry in row $i$, column $j$ of $AB$ is the **dot product of
row $i$ of $A$ with column $j$ of $B$**. The dot product from two lessons ago is the atom of matrix
multiplication.

A vector is just a one-column matrix, so applying a matrix $A$ to a vector $\mathbf{x}$ (written
$A\mathbf{x}$) is the same operation: each output coordinate is a **row of $A$ dotted with $\mathbf{x}$**.

Two warnings that surprise everyone:

- **Order matters.** $AB \neq BA$ in general. Rotating then scaling differs from scaling then rotating.
- **Shapes must line up.** An $m\times n$ times an $n\times p$ gives $m\times p$; the inner dimensions
  must match or the product doesn't exist.

## The formal definition

If $A$ is $m\times n$ and $B$ is $n\times p$, then $C = AB$ is $m\times p$ with entries

$$
c_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{ik}\,b_{kj}
= (\text{row } i \text{ of } A) \cdot (\text{column } j \text{ of } B).
$$

For a $2\times 2$ worked out in full:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} e & f \\ g & h \end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix} ae+bg & af+bh \\ ce+dg & cf+dh \end{bmatrix}.
$$

Key properties:

- **Associative:** $(AB)C = A(BC)$ — you can regroup a chain of transformations freely.
- **Not commutative:** $AB \neq BA$ in general — order is meaning.
- **Identity:** $AI = IA = A$ — multiplying by $I$ changes nothing.

<Callout type="why">
A deep neural network is a stack of matrix multiplies separated by nonlinear functions. Each layer
transforms its input; composing them is repeated matrix multiplication. The entire forward pass of a
model is, at heart, this one operation done billions of times.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="3D game graphics" icon="🎮">
To draw a model the GPU multiplies a chain of matrices — model → world → camera → projection. Each is
a transformation; their product is one matrix that maps a 3D vertex straight to a screen pixel. Order
is sacred: swap two and the character ends up inside the floor.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Robotics" icon="🦾">
A robot arm's joints chain together. Each joint contributes a transformation matrix from one link to
the next; multiplying them down the arm gives a single matrix locating the gripper in world space —
the heart of **forward kinematics**.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Web search" icon="🔗">
Page-ranking models the web as a transition matrix: where a random surfer goes next. Multiplying that
matrix by the current distribution of surfers gives the next step's distribution; repeating the
multiply settles into the importance scores that rank pages.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Image processing" icon="🎞️">
A photo pipeline might rotate, then crop, then color-correct. Each stage is a matrix; the GPU folds
them into one product so the whole pipeline is a single pass over the pixels instead of three.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Sanity-check shapes before you multiply: writing the dimensions as (m × <strong>n</strong>)(<strong>n</strong> × p)
makes the rule obvious — the inner numbers must match and they cancel, leaving m × p.
</Callout>
