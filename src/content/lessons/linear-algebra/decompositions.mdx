## The hook: how does a streaming service store a million ratings cheaply?

A movie service has millions of users and thousands of films, but the giant table of "who rated what"
is mostly empty and deeply repetitive — taste really comes down to a few hidden themes like *gritty
crime*, *feel-good rom-com*, or *space opera*. Instead of storing the whole table, the service
**factors** it into two skinny matrices: one placing each user along those few themes, one placing each
movie. Multiply them back and you approximately recover every rating — and can even *predict* the
missing ones. Breaking a matrix into simpler factors like this is **matrix decomposition**, and it is
how linear algebra turns enormous problems into small, fast, interpretable ones.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **matrix decomposition** rewrites a matrix as a product of simpler matrices — triangular, orthogonal,
or diagonal — so that solving, projecting, or compressing becomes easy.
</Callout>

## Intuition: factor a number, factor a matrix

You factor $60 = 2^2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5$ to see its structure at a glance. Matrices factor too, and each
classic factorization exposes a different truth and powers a different task:

- **LU** — split a matrix into a **lower**-triangular $L$ times an **upper**-triangular $U$. This is
  just Gaussian elimination, recorded. Triangular systems solve in one sweep, so LU is the workhorse
  for solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ fast and repeatedly.
- **QR** — split into an **orthonormal** $Q$ (perpendicular unit columns, from the last lesson) times an
  upper-triangular $R$. Because $Q$'s columns are an orthonormal basis, QR is the clean, stable way to
  do **least-squares** projections.
- **SVD** — the crown jewel: **any** matrix becomes $U\Sigma V^{\top}$, a **rotation**, then a pure
  **scaling**, then another **rotation**. It works for any shape of matrix and reveals exactly how much
  the matrix stretches space and in which directions.

The SVD is the one to hold in your mind's eye: every linear map, no matter how tangled, is secretly
just *rotate → stretch → rotate*. Watching that is the best way to feel it.

## Play with it

The unit circle is the "before." Step through $A = U\Sigma V^{\top}$ one factor at a time and watch the
circle rotate (by $V^{\top}$), stretch into an ellipse (by the singular values $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$),
then rotate again (by $U$). The semi-axes of the final ellipse are the singular values.

<SvdEllipseStudio client:visible />

<Callout type="tip">
Slide $\sigma_2$ toward zero: the ellipse squashes to a sliver. A tiny singular value means the matrix
barely uses that direction — so you can <strong>drop</strong> it and lose almost nothing. That single
observation is the whole idea of low-rank compression (and PCA, next lesson).
</Callout>

## The formal definition

For an invertible $n\times n$ matrix, **LU** (with row pivoting) is

$$
PA = LU,
$$

with $P$ a permutation, $L$ lower-triangular, $U$ upper-triangular. The **QR** decomposition of an
$m\times n$ matrix with independent columns is

$$
A = QR, \qquad Q^{\top}Q = I,\;\; R \text{ upper-triangular},
$$

where $Q$'s columns are an orthonormal basis for the column space. The **singular value
decomposition** of *any* $m\times n$ matrix $A$ is

$$
A = U\,\Sigma\,V^{\top},
$$

where $U$ ($m\times m$) and $V$ ($n\times n$) are **orthogonal** (rotations/reflections) and $\Sigma$
is diagonal with the **singular values** $\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \cdots \ge 0$. Keeping only the
largest $k$ singular values gives the **best rank-$k$ approximation** of $A$ (the Eckart–Young
theorem) — the optimal compression for a given size.

<Callout type="why">
The SVD ties this whole track together. The singular values squared are the eigenvalues of $A^{\top}A$;
the right singular vectors are eigenvectors of that symmetric matrix; truncating the small ones is
exactly **PCA** and the basis of image compression, recommender systems, and noise reduction. One
factorization, a dozen applications.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Image compression" icon="🖼️">
A grayscale photo is a matrix of pixel intensities. Its **SVD** usually has a few large singular values
and a long tail of tiny ones. Keep the top, say, 30 and you reconstruct a near-perfect image from a
fraction of the numbers — low-rank approximation as lossy compression you can dial up or down.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Recommender systems" icon="🎞️">
Factoring the user–item rating matrix (a truncated SVD / latent-factor model) discovers hidden
"taste dimensions." Each user and each movie becomes a short vector in that shared space, and their dot
product predicts a rating — even for films the user has never seen.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Natural language" icon="📚">
Latent Semantic Analysis runs an SVD on a word–document matrix. The top singular directions capture
**topics**: documents about the same subject line up, and synonyms end up close together, all from the
geometry of the decomposition.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Weather simulation" icon="🌦️">
Solving the huge linear systems inside a forecast model relies on **LU**-style factorizations to crank
through millions of equations efficiently, reusing the factors as the simulation steps forward in time
instead of re-solving from scratch.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
LU is for solving, QR is for projecting, SVD is for understanding and compressing. When in doubt about
"what does this matrix really do?", the SVD answers it: rotate, stretch by the singular values, rotate.
</Callout>
