## The hook: where's the nearest point on the road?

You're standing in a field and want to reach a long straight road as fast as possible. You don't walk
to some random spot on it — you walk **straight toward it**, meeting the road at a perfect right angle.
That shortest path is the **perpendicular**, and the point where you hit the road is your
**projection** onto it. This single picture — drop a perpendicular to find the closest point — is how
machines fit lines to data, compress signals, and answer "what's the best approximation I can make
inside a limited space?"

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The **projection** of a point onto a subspace is its closest point there, and the leftover **residual**
always meets the subspace at a **right angle**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the shadow and the leftover

You already know the **dot product** measures how much two vectors align, and that it's zero exactly
when they're **perpendicular** (orthogonal). Projection puts that to work.

Take a point $\mathbf{p}$ and a line through the origin in direction $\mathbf{d}$. The **projection**
$\hat{\mathbf{p}}$ is the shadow $\mathbf{p}$ casts straight down onto the line. Split $\mathbf{p}$ into
two pieces:

1. the part **along** the line — that's the projection $\hat{\mathbf{p}}$, and
2. the part **left over**, $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{p} - \hat{\mathbf{p}}$ — the **residual**.

The key fact: the residual is **perpendicular** to the line. And perpendicular is precisely what makes
the projection the *closest* point — any other point on the line forms a right triangle with
$\mathbf{p}$, and the hypotenuse (the distance to that other point) is always longer than the leg
(the residual). Minimizing distance and demanding a right angle are the *same condition*. That is the
whole idea of **least squares**: the best fit is the one whose errors are orthogonal to everything you
could have adjusted.

## The formal definition

The **projection** of a vector $\mathbf{p}$ onto the line spanned by $\mathbf{d}$ is

$$
\hat{\mathbf{p}} = \frac{\mathbf{p} \cdot \mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{d}}\, \mathbf{d}.
$$

The residual $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{p} - \hat{\mathbf{p}}$ satisfies the **orthogonality condition**

$$
\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{d} = 0.
$$

For a whole subspace spanned by the columns of a matrix $A$, projecting a target $\mathbf{b}$ leads to
the **normal equations**

$$
A^{\top} A\, \hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^{\top} \mathbf{b},
$$

whose solution $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ gives the **least-squares** best fit — the coefficients that make the
residual $\mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ orthogonal to every column of $A$. A set of vectors that are
mutually perpendicular *and* unit length is called **orthonormal**; with an orthonormal basis,
projecting is just a sequence of dot products — no matrix inverse needed.

<Callout type="why">
Linear regression *is* a projection. Your data targets live in a high-dimensional space, your model can
only reach the subspace spanned by its features, and the best fit is the projection of the targets onto
that subspace. The "least" in least squares is literally the squared length of the orthogonal residual.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="GPS positioning" icon="🛰️">
A receiver hears noisy distance readings from several satellites — more equations than unknowns, none
perfectly consistent. It solves a **least-squares** problem, projecting the contradictory measurements
onto the set of physically possible positions, landing on the spot whose residual error is smallest.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Audio engineering" icon="🎧">
Noise-cancelling headphones estimate the component of incoming sound that lies **along** the unwanted
noise pattern and subtract it. What's left — the residual, orthogonal to the noise model — is the music
you actually want to hear.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports analytics" icon="⚽">
To rate teams from lopsided schedules, analysts fit a rating vector so predicted score differences best
match real ones. It's a least-squares **projection**: ratings whose residual errors are orthogonal to
the schedule structure, giving the fairest fit the data allows.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Medical imaging" icon="🩻">
A CT scanner collects far more X-ray readings than unknown tissue densities. Reconstruction projects
those measurements onto the space of consistent images, choosing the density map whose residual
mismatch is minimized — orthogonality turning a flood of rays into a clean cross-section.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Orthonormal bases make everything easy: dot products become coordinates, and projections become
addition. The next lesson's QR decomposition is exactly the machine that <em>builds</em> such a basis
from any set of vectors.
</Callout>
