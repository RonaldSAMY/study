## The hook: every smoothie from two scoops

A juice bar has two base blends: a green scoop and a berry scoop. By choosing **how many** of each
you want — two greens and half a berry, say — you can hit a huge range of flavors and nutrition
profiles. Each recipe is just *"this much of one plus that much of the other."* The full menu of
everything you can make from those two scoops is their **span**. Change the two base blends and the
reachable menu changes with them. That single idea — building new vectors by scaling and adding a
fixed set — is the backbone of linear algebra.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **linear combination** is "scale each vector by a weight, then add them up." The **span** is the set
of **every** point you can reach by trying all possible weights.
</Callout>

## Intuition: building blocks and the territory they cover

You already add vectors and scale them by numbers. Do both at once to a *collection* of vectors and
you get a **linear combination**:

$$
c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k .
$$

The weights $c_1, c_2, \dots$ are dials you can turn to any real number. As you turn them, the result
sweeps across space. The **span** is the complete territory you can cover.

How much territory depends on whether the vectors point in genuinely different directions:

- Two vectors pointing **different ways** → their span is the **whole 2D plane**. Any point is some mix.
- Two vectors that are **parallel** (one is a multiple of the other) → the second adds nothing new, so
  the span collapses to a single **line**. Most of the plane becomes unreachable.

That distinction — does a new vector add a new direction, or is it redundant? — is exactly what we'll
later call **linear independence**.

In <strong>parallel</strong> mode, try as hard as you like — the point never leaves the line. Adding a
vector that's just a stretched copy of one you already have grows the span by <strong>nothing</strong>.
</Callout>

## The formal definition

Given vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \dots, \mathbf{v}_k$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$, their **span** is the set of all
linear combinations:

$$
\operatorname{span}\{\mathbf{v}_1, \dots, \mathbf{v}_k\}
= \left\{\, c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k \;\middle|\; c_1, \dots, c_k \in \mathbb{R} \,\right\}.
$$

A few facts worth holding onto:

- The span **always contains the origin** (set every weight to 0).
- In $\mathbb{R}^2$, two vectors span the whole plane **exactly when** they aren't parallel.
- "Is $\mathbf{b}$ in the span of these vectors?" is the same question as "do weights exist that build
  $\mathbf{b}$?" — a system of equations, which the Gaussian-elimination lesson will solve.

<Callout type="why">
Span is the geometry behind what a model **can express**. The reachable outputs of a linear layer are
the span of its columns. If your building blocks don't span the space your target lives in, no choice
of weights can ever reach it — the model is blind to that direction by construction.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Nutrition" icon="🥤">
Two base smoothies, each a vector of (protein, sugar, calories). Every blend $c_1\mathbf{s}_1 +
c_2\mathbf{s}_2$ is a linear combination. The span is the full set of nutrition profiles the bar can
serve — and if the two bases are nutritionally proportional, that menu shrinks to a single line.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Audio synthesis" icon="🎚️">
A synth builds tones by mixing a few basic waveforms. Sliding the volume fader on each is scaling;
summing them is adding. The set of all sounds you can produce is the span of those waveforms — add a
truly new waveform and you unlock timbres the others could never reach.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Investing" icon="📈">
A portfolio is a weighted mix of assets: $c_1$ shares of one, $c_2$ of another. The span of your
chosen assets is every risk-return position you can hold. Two assets that always move together (highly
correlated) span barely more than one — you've added no real diversification.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="3D animation" icon="🎭">
Character faces are animated with **blendshapes**: a neutral face plus weighted "smile," "frown," and
"blink" vectors. Every expression the rig can show is a linear combination of those shapes — their
span is the character's entire emotional range.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Counting directions: in $\mathbb{R}^2$ you need **two** non-parallel vectors to span everything; a
third is always redundant. In $\mathbb{R}^3$ you need three. That minimum count is the dimension — the
star of a later lesson.
</Callout>
