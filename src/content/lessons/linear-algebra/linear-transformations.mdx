## The hook: how does a photo filter tilt a whole image at once?

When an app skews a photo into a 3-D-looking card, or a game leans a sprite as it turns, it isn't
nudging pixels one by one. It applies a single rule to **every point in the plane simultaneously**.
Crucially, the rule keeps the image *coherent*: straight edges stay straight, parallel borders stay
parallel, and the spacing stays even. That special, well-behaved kind of transformation — the only
kind that a matrix can perform — is a **linear transformation**, and it is the verb to the matrix's
noun.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **linear transformation** is a function that moves all of space at once while keeping the origin
fixed and grid lines straight, parallel, and evenly spaced — and it is completely described by where
it sends the basis vectors.
</Callout>

## Intuition: track the basis, and you've tracked everything

Here's the beautiful shortcut. You met a **basis** in the last lesson — say $\hat{\imath} = [1,0]$ and
$\hat{\jmath} = [0,1]$. Every vector is a combination of these two: $[3,2] = 3\hat{\imath} +
2\hat{\jmath}$.

A transformation is **linear** when it respects combinations — it doesn't matter whether you combine
first and then transform, or transform first and then combine. So if you know only *two* things —
where $\hat{\imath}$ lands and where $\hat{\jmath}$ lands — you know where **every** point lands:

$$
T([3, 2]) = 3\,T(\hat{\imath}) + 2\,T(\hat{\jmath}).
$$

That is why a 2×2 **matrix** is enough to describe the whole warp: its two columns are simply the new
homes of $\hat{\imath}$ and $\hat{\jmath}$. Reshaping the basis reshapes all of space, and because the
combination rule is preserved, grid lines can only stay straight and evenly spaced. (This builds
directly on matrix multiplication from earlier in the track — multiplying a matrix by a vector *is*
applying the transformation.)

## The formal definition

A function $T : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is a **linear transformation** if it preserves addition
and scalar multiplication:

$$
T(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = T(\mathbf{u}) + T(\mathbf{v}),
\qquad
T(c\,\mathbf{v}) = c\,T(\mathbf{v}).
$$

These two rules force $T(\mathbf{0}) = \mathbf{0}$ and guarantee the "straight, even grid" behavior.
Every such $T$ is a matrix multiplication. In 2-D, with $T(\hat{\imath}) = \begin{bmatrix} a \\ c
\end{bmatrix}$ and $T(\hat{\jmath}) = \begin{bmatrix} b \\ d \end{bmatrix}$,

$$
T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}, \qquad
A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}, \qquad
A\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}
= \begin{bmatrix} ax + by \\ cx + dy \end{bmatrix}.
$$

Composing transformations corresponds to **multiplying** their matrices, and the **determinant** of
$A$ tells you the factor by which areas scale (negative = orientation flipped, zero = collapsed).

<Callout type="why">
Every layer of a neural network does an *affine* step — a linear transformation $A\mathbf{x}$ plus a
shift — before a nonlinear squashing function. Understanding what a matrix does geometrically (rotate,
stretch, shear, project) is understanding the literal machinery inside deep learning.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Computer graphics" icon="🎮">
Every rotation, scale, and shear of a 3-D model is a matrix applied to its vertices. A spinning
character is just its vertex vectors multiplied by a rotation matrix each frame — straight edges stay
straight, which is why polygons never crumple.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Economics" icon="📈">
An input–output model multiplies a vector of this year's production by a matrix of inter-industry
demands to get next year's. Doubling demand doubles the result, and combined demands add — the system
is **linear**, so a single matrix forecasts the whole economy's next state.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Seismology" icon="🌐">
A strain matrix describes how the ground deforms during an earthquake: it maps each tiny
displacement vector to its stretched, sheared version. Because rock deforms linearly for small
motions, one 3×3 matrix captures the whole local distortion field.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Typography" icon="✍️">
Italic fonts are often a plain font run through a **shear** matrix $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0.2 \\ 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}$ — it slides each point sideways in proportion to its height. Verticals tilt, but every
straight stroke stays straight and parallel, which is exactly why the slanted text still reads
cleanly.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Rotate, scale, shear, reflect, project — these are the verbs of linear algebra. The next lessons ask a
deeper question: for a given transformation, are there special directions that barely change at all?
</Callout>
