## The hook: how does a video game know a wall is lit?

In a 3D game, a surface looks bright when it **faces** the light and dark when it turns away. The
renderer doesn't "see" anything — it computes a single number from two vectors: the direction the
surface points and the direction to the light. When those two arrows agree, the number is large and
the pixel is bright. When they're perpendicular, the number is zero and the surface goes dark. That
"how much do these agree?" number is the **dot product**, and it runs millions of times per frame.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The **dot product** takes two vectors and returns one number that says **how much they point the same
way** — large and positive when aligned, zero when perpendicular, negative when opposed.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from "two lists" to "one verdict"

You already know a vector is an arrow *and* a list of numbers. Addition and scaling combine vectors
into new vectors. The dot product is different: it eats two vectors and spits out a **single number**
(a *scalar*). That number compresses everything about their relationship into one verdict.

There are two equivalent ways to read it:

1. **Algebraically** — multiply matching components and add them up. Pure arithmetic.
2. **Geometrically** — it equals $\lVert\mathbf{u}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert\cos\theta$, where
   $\theta$ is the angle between the arrows. So the dot product secretly knows the angle.

Setting those two views equal is what lets a computer find the angle between two arrows **without ever
drawing them** — just from the numbers.

A close cousin is **projection**: the "shadow" one vector casts along another. If you shine a light
straight down onto vector $\mathbf{u}$, the length of $\mathbf{v}$'s shadow tells you how much of
$\mathbf{v}$ goes in the $\mathbf{u}$ direction.

## The formal definition

For two vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$, the **dot product** is the sum of component-wise products:

$$
\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + \cdots + u_n v_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i v_i .
$$

The geometric identity ties it to length and angle:

$$
\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \lVert \mathbf{u} \rVert \, \lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert \cos\theta .
$$

Solving for the angle gives **cosine similarity**, the workhorse of comparing vectors:

$$
\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\lVert \mathbf{u} \rVert \, \lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert} .
$$

And the **projection** of $\mathbf{v}$ onto $\mathbf{u}$ — the shadow vector — is

$$
\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{u}} \mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{u}}\; \mathbf{u} .
$$

<Callout type="why">
Cosine similarity is *the* default way machine-learning systems ask "are these two things alike?" —
two documents, two faces, two users. Because it divides out length, it compares **direction only**, so
a long document and a short one about the same topic still score as similar.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="3D game graphics" icon="🎮">
A flat surface has a **normal** vector pointing straight out of it. To shade a pixel, the engine takes
the dot product of that normal with the direction to the light. Facing the light → near 1 → fully lit.
Edge-on → 0 → dark. This one multiply-and-add is the heart of real-time lighting.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Social networks" icon="🕸️">
Represent each person by a vector of the groups they belong to (1 = member, 0 = not). The dot product
of two people's vectors **counts the groups they share** — an instant "how connected are you?" score
that suggestion engines use to recommend new friends.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Image filters" icon="🖼️">
A blur or edge-detector slides a small grid of weights (a *kernel*) over the image. At each spot it
flattens the pixels and the kernel into vectors and takes their dot product to make one output pixel.
A whole filtered photo is millions of dot products.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Robotics" icon="🤖">
A robot arm wants to know how much of its motor's push actually moves the gripper toward a target.
Project the motion vector onto the target direction: the dot product gives the **useful component**,
while the leftover is wasted sideways motion the controller can trim.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Sign tells the story: positive dot product = acute angle = "broadly agree," negative = obtuse angle =
"work against each other," zero = perpendicular = "independent." You'll see those three cases
everywhere from physics to recommendation.
</Callout>
