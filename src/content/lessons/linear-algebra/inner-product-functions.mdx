## The hook: how a radio finds one station in the noise

A radio antenna picks up a chaotic sum of every broadcast at once. To lock onto a single station, the
receiver **multiplies** the incoming signal by a reference wave at that station's frequency and **adds up
the result over time**. If the signal contains that frequency, the products reinforce and the sum is
large; if it doesn't, the products cancel to nearly zero. That "multiply, then sum" move is doing
something you already know in disguise — it's a **dot product**, but between *waves* instead of arrows.

You learned that the dot product of two vectors, $\sum_i a_i b_i$, measures how much they agree, and that
a zero dot product means **orthogonal** — perpendicular, sharing nothing. The leap of this lesson is that
a **function is just a vector with infinitely many components** (one per input value), and the sum
becomes an **integral**. With that single move, every tool from orthogonality and projections carries
over to functions — and opens the door to Fourier analysis.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Treat a function as a vector whose components are its values; then the **inner product** is
$\langle f, g\rangle = \int f(x)\,g(x)\,dx$, and two functions are **orthogonal** when that integral is
zero.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from sums to integrals

For vectors $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the dot product pairs up matching components and
sums them:

$$
\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i\,b_i.
$$

Now imagine the index $i$ getting finer and finer until it becomes a continuous input $x$. The list of
components $a_i$ turns into a function $f(x)$, the list $b_i$ into $g(x)$, and the discrete sum becomes a
continuous one — an **integral**:

$$
\langle f, g\rangle = \int_a^b f(x)\,g(x)\,dx.
$$

This is a genuine **inner product**: it's symmetric, linear in each slot, and $\langle f, f\rangle =
\int f(x)^2\,dx \ge 0$ gives a notion of *length* (the function's energy). Everything you know about
right angles and shadows now applies. The **norm** of a function is $\lVert f\rVert = \sqrt{\langle f,
f\rangle}$, and the **angle** between two functions is defined through the same cosine formula as for
arrows. Best of all, $\langle f, g\rangle = 0$ means $f$ and $g$ are **orthogonal** — they overlap to
nothing across the interval, even though both may be wildly nonzero pointwise.

The crown jewel: on $[-\pi, \pi]$, the sines and cosines of different frequencies are **mutually
orthogonal**. For integers $m \ne n$,

$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin(mx)\sin(nx)\,dx = 0,
\qquad
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin(mx)\cos(nx)\,dx = 0.
$$

Positive lobes of the product exactly cancel negative ones. That orthogonal family is the basis of the
**Fourier series**.

## Play with it

Pick two functions and watch their product $f(x)\,g(x)$ shaded beneath them — **green** where positive,
**rose** where negative. The inner product is the signed area: when green and rose cancel, you get
$\langle f, g\rangle = 0$ and the functions are **orthogonal**.

## The formal definition

A real **inner product space** of functions on $[a, b]$ equips them with

$$
\langle f, g\rangle = \int_a^b f(x)\,g(x)\,w(x)\,dx,
$$

often with a weight $w(x) \ge 0$ (take $w \equiv 1$ for the plain case). From it follow length and
orthogonality just as in finite dimensions:

$$
\lVert f\rVert = \sqrt{\langle f, f\rangle},
\qquad
f \perp g \iff \langle f, g\rangle = 0.
$$

Given an **orthogonal basis** of functions $\{\phi_k\}$, you can **project** any function onto it — the
exact analog of dropping a vector onto coordinate axes. The coefficient along $\phi_k$ is

$$
c_k = \frac{\langle f, \phi_k\rangle}{\langle \phi_k, \phi_k\rangle},
\qquad
f(x) \approx \sum_k c_k\,\phi_k(x).
$$

Each coefficient is computed **independently** precisely because the basis is orthogonal — no
coupled system to solve, just one integral per coefficient. With $\phi_k$ the sines and cosines, these
$c_k$ are the **Fourier coefficients**, and the sum is the Fourier series.

<Callout type="why">
This is where linear algebra and analysis fuse into the toolkit of machine learning and signal
processing. <strong>Fourier and wavelet transforms</strong>, the <strong>kernel trick</strong> (a kernel
is an inner product in a function space), <strong>Gaussian processes</strong>, and spectral methods for
graphs all rest on inner products of functions. "Project onto an orthogonal basis" is the same idea that
gives you <strong>PCA</strong> for data — coming next in matrix decompositions.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Once functions are vectors, "multiply and integrate" measures overlap everywhere.

<ExampleCard domain="Signal processing" icon="📡">
A <strong>matched filter</strong> detects a known pulse buried in noise by computing the inner product of
the incoming signal with a stored copy of the pulse. A large value means strong overlap — the pulse is
present. This same correlation integral underlies radar, GPS lock-on, and sonar.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Music & acoustics" icon="🎵">
The <strong>harmonics</strong> of a vibrating string — its fundamental and overtones — are sine waves at
multiples of one frequency, and they are mutually <strong>orthogonal</strong>. That independence is why a
single note decomposes cleanly into a pure spectrum, and why a tuner can read off one harmonic's strength
without the others bleeding in.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Numerical approximation" icon="📐">
To approximate a complicated function, project it onto an orthogonal family — <strong>Legendre</strong> or
<strong>Chebyshev</strong> polynomials, or sines and cosines. Because the basis is orthogonal, each
coefficient is one independent integral, and truncating the sum gives the best low-order fit in the
energy ($L^2$) sense.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Image compression" icon="🖼️">
JPEG slices an image into blocks and projects each onto an orthogonal basis of <strong>cosine
patterns</strong> (the discrete cosine transform). Most coefficients turn out tiny and are thrown away —
orthogonality guarantees that discarding one barely disturbs the rest, so the image survives with a
fraction of the data.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Detection, music, approximation, compression — all are "compute $\langle f, \phi\rangle$ against an
orthogonal family." The function-as-vector view is what unifies them.
</Callout>
