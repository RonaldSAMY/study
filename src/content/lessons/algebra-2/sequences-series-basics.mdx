## The hook: counting the seats in a stadium

The front row of a stadium section has 20 seats. Each row behind it adds 4 more: 20, 24, 28, 32… That
orderly list is a **sequence**. Now the practical question: how many seats are in the whole section of 30
rows? Adding them one by one is tedious — but the running total of a sequence, called a **series**, has a
beautiful shortcut. Patterns and their totals are everywhere: savings plans, loan payments, viral shares,
even the pixels of a fractal.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **sequence** is an ordered list of numbers following a rule; a **series** is what you get when you add
those numbers up — and two patterns, *adding* and *multiplying*, cover an astonishing amount of the world.
</Callout>

## Intuition: two ways to make a pattern

Almost every simple number pattern grows in one of two ways:

- **Arithmetic** — you **add** the same amount, the *common difference* $d$, each step. The stadium rows
  ($+4$ each time) are arithmetic. Plotted, the terms march up in a straight line.
- **Geometric** — you **multiply** by the same factor, the *common ratio* $r$, each step. A rumor that
  doubles, or a savings account that grows by 5% a year, is geometric. This is the discrete cousin of the
  exponential growth you met earlier — the same "multiply every step" engine.

The distinction is exactly addition versus multiplication, the two moves you've been building on all
track. Arithmetic sequences relate to **lines**; geometric ones relate to **exponentials**.

A **series** is the cumulative sum: $S_n = a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n$. Watching it grow tells you the *total
so far* — total seats, total saved, total reach.

## The formal definition

An **arithmetic sequence** has a constant difference $d$:

$$
a_n = a_1 + (n-1)\,d.
$$

Its series — the sum of the first $n$ terms — pairs the first and last term ($n$ terms, average
$\frac{a_1 + a_n}{2}$ each):

$$
S_n = \frac{n}{2}\,(a_1 + a_n).
$$

A **geometric sequence** has a constant ratio $r$:

$$
a_n = a_1\,r^{\,n-1}, \qquad S_n = a_1\,\frac{1 - r^{\,n}}{1 - r}\quad (r \neq 1).
$$

And the showstopper: when $|r| < 1$, the terms shrink fast enough that even an **infinite** sum converges:

$$
S_\infty = \frac{a_1}{1 - r}.
$$

So $1 + \tfrac12 + \tfrac14 + \tfrac18 + \cdots = \dfrac{1}{1 - \frac12} = 2$ — infinitely many positive
numbers adding to a finite total.

<Callout type="why">
Series are how the discrete becomes the continuous. The infinite geometric sum is the simplest
**convergent series**, and the same idea — adding infinitely many shrinking pieces — defines integrals,
Taylor approximations of functions, and the discounted future rewards a reinforcement-learning agent sums
when it plans ahead.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Stadium design" icon="🏟️">
Rows of 20, 24, 28, … (an arithmetic sequence with $d = 4$). For 30 rows the last row has
$20 + 29\cdot4 = 136$ seats, and the section total is $S_{30} = \tfrac{30}{2}(20 + 136) = 2340$ seats —
counted in one line instead of thirty.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Saving up" icon="🐖">
You stash \$50 the first month and add \$25 more each month than the last (\$50, \$75, \$100, …) — an
arithmetic plan. The series tells you the **total saved** after a year at a glance, so you know exactly
when you'll hit your goal.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Social media" icon="📣">
You share a post with 3 friends; each shares with 3 more, and so on — a geometric sequence with $r = 3$.
The series $3 + 9 + 27 + \cdots$ is the **total reach**, exploding so fast that a handful of rounds can
blanket a whole network. This is the discrete twin of viral exponential growth.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Physics of play" icon="🏀">
A dropped ball rebounds to 70% of its previous height each bounce: $h, 0.7h, 0.49h, \ldots$ — geometric
with $r = 0.7$. Because $|r| < 1$, the **total vertical distance** it ever travels is a finite
$S_\infty$, even though it technically bounces forever.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Adding the same amount (stadium, savings) is arithmetic and grows like a line; multiplying by the same
factor (shares, bounces) is geometric and grows — or shrinks — like an exponential. Recognizing which one
you have tells you instantly which sum formula to reach for.
</Callout>
