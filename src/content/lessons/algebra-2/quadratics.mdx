## The hook: the perfect free throw

Watch a basketball leave a shooter's hand. It doesn't travel in a straight line — it rises,
slows, curves over, and drops cleanly through the hoop. That graceful, symmetric arc has a name:
a **parabola**. The same shape catches water in a fountain, holds up a suspension bridge, and
focuses sunlight in a solar cooker. Straight lines were the story of Algebra 1; the moment something
**curves**, you've left lines behind and entered the world of the **quadratic**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **quadratic** is any expression with a squared term, $ax^2 + bx + c$, and its graph is always a
**parabola** — a single smooth U-shaped curve with one lowest (or highest) point.
</Callout>

## Intuition: what the squared term does

In Algebra 1 a linear function $y = mx + b$ had a constant slope — every step right changed $y$ by the
same amount. A quadratic breaks that rule. The $x^2$ term makes the slope *itself* change as you move,
which is exactly what bends the line into a curve.

Three numbers control everything:

- **$a$** — how sharply it curves and which way it opens. Positive $a$ opens **upward** (a valley);
  negative $a$ opens **downward** (a hill, like the basketball).
- **$b$** — slides the curve left/right and tilts where the turning point sits.
- **$c$** — the **y-intercept**, where the curve crosses the vertical axis ($x = 0$).

The single most important point on a parabola is its **vertex** — the bottom of the valley or the top
of the hill. Because a parabola is perfectly symmetric, the vertex sits exactly halfway between the two
points where it crosses the x-axis. Those crossing points are the **roots** (also called *zeros* or
*solutions*): the $x$-values that make the whole expression equal zero.

## The formal definition

A **quadratic function** has the standard form

$$
f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c, \qquad a \neq 0.
$$

To find its **roots** we solve $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$. There are two classic tools.

**Factoring** rewrites the expression as a product. If

$$
ax^2 + bx + c = a\,(x - r_1)(x - r_2),
$$

then the roots are simply $r_1$ and $r_2$, because a product is zero only when one of its factors is
zero. For example $x^2 - x - 6 = (x-3)(x+2)$, so the roots are $3$ and $-2$.

When factoring isn't obvious, the **quadratic formula** always works:

$$
x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}.
$$

The piece under the square root, $b^2 - 4ac$, is the **discriminant**. It decides the fate of the roots:
positive gives **two** real roots, zero gives **one** (a repeated root, where the vertex touches the
axis), and negative gives **none** that are real.

**Completing the square** rewrites the quadratic to expose its vertex directly:

$$
f(x) = a\left(x + \tfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2 + \left(c - \tfrac{b^2}{4a}\right),
$$

so the vertex sits at $x = -\dfrac{b}{2a}$. This *vertex form* is how you read off the peak of an arc at
a glance.

<Callout type="why">
Quadratics are the simplest curves that have a **minimum**. Machine learning is, at its heart, the
search for the minimum of an error function — and the friendliest error functions (like
mean-squared error) are bowl-shaped quadratics. Finding the vertex *is* learning.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Sports" icon="🏀">
A free throw leaves the hand at $4.2$ m high and follows $h(x) = -0.45x^2 + 1.9x + 4.2$, where $x$ is the
horizontal distance in meters. The negative $a$ makes it arc downward; the **vertex** is the peak of the
shot, and a **root** marks where the ball would hit the floor if it missed the rim.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Architecture" icon="🌉">
The cables of a suspension bridge hang in a parabola. Engineers write the curve as $y = ax^2$ with the
**vertex** at the lowest point mid-span. Knowing $a$ tells them exactly how tall each support tower must
be at any distance from the center — pure quadratic geometry holding up tons of traffic.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Business" icon="💰">
A food truck finds its daily profit follows $P(p) = -8p^2 + 240p - 1000$, where $p$ is the price of a meal.
Because $a < 0$ the profit curve is a hill: the **vertex** reveals the single price that maximizes profit,
and the **roots** are the break-even prices where profit hits zero.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Engineering" icon="⛲">
Water shooting from a fountain nozzle traces a parabola before gravity pulls it back down. Designers tune
the launch angle and speed — effectively $b$ and $c$ — so the **roots** (where the water lands) drop the
stream neatly back into the basin instead of onto passers-by.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Every one of these is the *same* equation $ax^2 + bx + c$ wearing a different costume. Learn to read the
vertex and the roots, and you can describe any arc in nature or engineering.
</Callout>
