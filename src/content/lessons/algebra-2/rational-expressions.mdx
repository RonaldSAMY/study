## The hook: splitting the bill on a road trip

Five friends rent a beach house for \$600. Each pays \$120. But if more friends join, the cost *per
person* drops — \$60 with ten, \$30 with twenty. The formula is simply $\text{cost} = 600/n$. Notice two
strange things: you can't split the bill among **zero** people, and no matter how many friends pile in,
the per-person cost never quite reaches **zero**. Those two oddities — a forbidden input and a floor the
curve approaches but never touches — are the signature of a **rational expression**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **rational expression** is one polynomial divided by another, $\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}$ — and because you can
never divide by zero, it comes with forbidden inputs and invisible walls called **asymptotes**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: what division forbids

You've built polynomials by adding and multiplying. Now we **divide** one by another. The moment a
denominator can equal zero, the whole expression breaks down there — division by zero is undefined. So
every rational expression carries a **domain restriction**: the set of $x$-values that are simply not
allowed.

Near a forbidden input the denominator gets tiny, so the fraction blows up toward $\pm\infty$. The curve
hugs a vertical line it can never cross — a **vertical asymptote**. Meanwhile, as $x$ runs far out toward
$\pm\infty$, the expression often settles toward a steady value, tracing a **horizontal asymptote**.

A quick rule for horizontal asymptotes of $\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$, comparing the **degrees**:

- denominator degree **bigger** → the curve flattens to $y = 0$;
- degrees **equal** → it flattens to the ratio of the leading coefficients;
- numerator degree **bigger** → no horizontal asymptote (it keeps growing).

## Play with it

Slide $a$, $b$, and $c$ in $y = \dfrac{ax+b}{x-c}$. The **vertical asymptote** sits at $x = c$ (the
forbidden input) and the **horizontal asymptote** at $y = a$. Watch the curve race toward both green
walls without ever touching them.

## The formal definition

A **rational expression** is a quotient of two polynomials,

$$
R(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}, \qquad q(x) \neq 0.
$$

Its **domain** is all real numbers *except* the roots of $q(x)$. Those excluded roots produce **vertical
asymptotes** (unless the same factor also cancels from the top, which instead leaves a removable "hole").

Rational expressions simplify and combine much like ordinary fractions. **Factor, then cancel** common
factors:

$$
\frac{x^2 - 9}{x^2 - x - 6} = \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{(x-3)(x+2)} = \frac{x+3}{x+2}, \quad x \neq 3.
$$

To **add or subtract**, use a common denominator; to **multiply**, multiply across; to **divide**, flip
and multiply — exactly the rules from arithmetic fractions, now with polynomials in the slots.

<Callout type="why">
Ratios are the natural language of *rates* and *normalized scores*. In machine learning the softmax that
turns raw scores into probabilities is a rational expression (each score's exponential over the sum), and
metrics like precision and the F1-score are ratios whose behavior near zero — their asymptotes — decides
how a model is judged.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Pharmacology" icon="💊">
After an IV drip starts, drug concentration follows something like $C(t) = \dfrac{20t}{t + 4}$. There's a
**horizontal asymptote** at $C = 20$: the concentration climbs but levels off at a steady therapeutic
plateau, never quite exceeding it no matter how long the drip runs.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Shared travel" icon="🚗">
Splitting a \$600 rental among $n$ people costs $\dfrac{600}{n}$ each. The **vertical asymptote** at
$n = 0$ reflects the impossible "zero people," and the **horizontal asymptote** at \$0 means adding riders
keeps shrinking each share but can never make it free.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Optics" icon="📷">
A camera lens obeys $\dfrac{1}{f} = \dfrac{1}{d_o} + \dfrac{1}{d_i}$. Solving for the image distance gives
a rational expression $d_i = \dfrac{f\,d_o}{d_o - f}$, whose **vertical asymptote** at $d_o = f$ is the
focusing limit: place the subject exactly one focal length away and the image races to infinity — it
won't focus.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Travel planning" icon="⏱️">
Average speed for a fixed 120-mile trip is $\bar v = \dfrac{120}{t}$, a rational function of time. Halving
your time doubles the required speed, and the **vertical asymptote** at $t = 0$ shows the obvious: you
can't cover the distance in no time at all.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
In each case the math flags the impossible (dividing by zero) and the unreachable (the horizontal floor or
ceiling). Reading the asymptotes tells you the limits of the situation before you compute a single value.
</Callout>
