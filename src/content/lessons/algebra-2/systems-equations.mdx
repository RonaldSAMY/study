## The hook: which phone plan should you pick?

Plan A charges \$30 a month flat. Plan B charges \$10 plus \$0.20 per GB of data. Which is cheaper?
It *depends* on how much data you use — and there is one exact usage where the two plans cost the same.
Below that you'd pick one plan, above it the other. Finding that break-even point means solving **two
equations at the same time**, demanding a single answer that makes *both* true. That's a **system of
equations**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **system of equations** is two (or more) equations that must all hold at once; its **solution** is the
set of values satisfying every equation simultaneously — graphically, the point where the lines cross.
</Callout>

## Intuition: one equation, many answers; two equations, one answer

A single line like $y = 2x + 1$ has *infinitely* many solutions — every point on the line works. Pin a
second equation onto it, and you suddenly demand a point that lies on **both** lines at once. Two
different straight lines can only do that in **one** place: their intersection.

There are three possible outcomes, and the graph tells you which instantly:

- **One solution** — the lines cross at a single point (the usual case).
- **No solution** — the lines are **parallel** (same slope, different intercepts) and never meet.
- **Infinitely many** — the two equations are secretly the *same* line, lying exactly on top of each other.

Graphing is the picture, but you also need exact answers. Two algebraic methods deliver them:

- **Substitution** — solve one equation for a variable, then plug that expression into the other.
- **Elimination** — add or subtract the equations so one variable cancels, leaving one to solve directly.

## The formal definition

A **linear system in two variables** looks like

$$
\begin{aligned}
a_1 x + b_1 y &= c_1 \\
a_2 x + b_2 y &= c_2
\end{aligned}
$$

A **solution** is a pair $(x, y)$ that satisfies **both** equations.

**Substitution** — from the first equation express one variable, say $y = \tfrac{c_1 - a_1 x}{b_1}$, then
substitute it into the second so only $x$ remains.

**Elimination** — scale the equations so a variable's coefficients match, then subtract. For example:

$$
\begin{aligned}
2x + 3y &= 12 \\
2x -\ \ y &= 4
\end{aligned}
\quad\xrightarrow{\ \text{subtract}\ }\quad 4y = 8 \ \Rightarrow\ y = 2,\ \ x = 3.
$$

The graph of each equation is a line; the solution is exactly where they intersect. Whether a system has
one, none, or infinitely many solutions is decided by the slopes and intercepts — the same picture you
explored in the demo.

<Callout type="why">
Systems of equations are the foundation of **linear algebra**, the engine room of machine learning.
Fitting a model means solving a system for the best parameters; with thousands of variables we no longer
draw lines but use matrices and elimination at scale. Every "least-squares" fit is a giant, organized
version of the crossing point you just dragged.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Consumer choice" icon="📱">
Plan A: $y = 30$. Plan B: $y = 10 + 0.2x$ for $x$ gigabytes. Setting them equal, $30 = 10 + 0.2x$ gives
$x = 100$ GB — the **break-even** usage. Use less and the flat plan loses; use more and it wins. One
intersection settles the whole decision.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Economics" icon="📊">
Markets balance where **supply meets demand**. Demand falls with price, $q = 100 - 2p$; supply rises,
$q = 10 + 3p$. Solving the system gives the **equilibrium** price and quantity — the single $(p, q)$ where
buyers and sellers agree. That crossing point is the market price.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Chemistry" icon="🧪">
Mixing a 20% saline solution with a 50% one to make 10 L of 30% solution sets up two equations: total
volume $x + y = 10$ and total salt $0.2x + 0.5y = 3$. Elimination reveals exactly how many liters of each
to pour — a system solved in the lab every day.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Travel" icon="🚆">
A freight train leaves at 40 mph; two hours later an express chases at 60 mph. Writing each train's
distance as a function of time gives two equations; their **intersection** is the moment and place the
express catches up — the classic "when do they meet?" puzzle, solved by a system.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Phones, markets, beakers, trains — each becomes two equations that must hold together. The solution is
always the one set of values where every condition is satisfied at once.
</Callout>
