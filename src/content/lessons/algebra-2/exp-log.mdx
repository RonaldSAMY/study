## The hook: the rumor that ate the school

One person tells two friends a rumor at 8am. Each of them tells two more by 9am, and so on. By lunchtime
the whole school knows. Nothing was added at a steady rate — instead the *number of new tellers
multiplied* every hour. That multiplying-not-adding behavior is **exponential growth**, and it is both
the most powerful and the most deceptive pattern in mathematics. Its inverse, the **logarithm**, is how
we measure and tame it.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
An **exponential function** puts the variable *in the exponent* ($y = b^x$) so the output multiplies at a
steady rate, and a **logarithm** answers the reverse question: *"what exponent produces this number?"*
</Callout>

## Intuition: when the exponent becomes the variable

In the last lesson the exponent was a fixed number — $x^2$, $x^3$. Now we flip it. In $y = 2^x$ the
**base is fixed** and the **exponent varies**. That single change transforms the behavior completely:

- A polynomial like $x^2$ adds bigger and bigger *amounts* as $x$ grows.
- An exponential like $2^x$ *multiplies* by the same factor with every step. Each unit of $x$ doubles the
  output, then doubles it again.

If the base is bigger than 1, you get **growth** (the rumor). If it's between 0 and 1 — or equivalently
the exponent is negative — you get **decay**, where the quantity halves repeatedly toward zero
(radioactivity, a cooling coffee, a fading echo).

The **logarithm** is exponentiation run backwards. Since $2^3 = 8$, we say $\log_2 8 = 3$: *"the power you
raise 2 to in order to get 8 is 3."* This is exactly the inverse relationship you'll see again and again —
a log undoes an exponential the way subtraction undoes addition.

## The formal definition

An **exponential function** has the form

$$
f(x) = A\,b^{x}, \qquad b > 0,\ b \neq 1,
$$

where $A$ is the starting value and $b$ the **base** (growth factor per step). Scientists often write it
with the natural base $e \approx 2.718$ as $A\,e^{rx}$, where $r$ is the continuous **growth rate**
($r>0$ grows, $r<0$ decays).

The **logarithm** is its inverse. By definition,

$$
y = \log_b x \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad b^{y} = x.
$$

Because logs undo exponentials, they convert multiplication into addition — captured in three rules that
mirror the power rules exactly:

$$
\log_b(xy) = \log_b x + \log_b y, \quad \log_b\!\frac{x}{y} = \log_b x - \log_b y, \quad \log_b(x^k) = k\,\log_b x.
$$

A handy consequence is the **doubling time / half-life**: for $A\,e^{rt}$, the quantity doubles every
$\dfrac{\ln 2}{r}$ units of time (and halves every $\dfrac{\ln 2}{|r|}$ when decaying).

<Callout type="why">
Exponentials and logs are the heartbeat of machine learning. Probabilities are multiplied together over
huge datasets, so we work with their **logs** to turn impossible products into manageable sums (the
"log-likelihood"). Learning rates **decay exponentially**, and the sigmoid and softmax that produce model
outputs are built directly from $e^x$.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Personal finance" icon="🏦">
Money in a savings account at 6% compounded annually grows as $A(t) = 1000 \cdot 1.06^{t}$. It doesn't add
a fixed amount each year — it multiplies by 1.06, so the interest itself earns interest. The
**doubling time** is about $\ln 2 / \ln 1.06 \approx 12$ years, regardless of how much you start with.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Microbiology" icon="🦠">
A bacterial colony that splits every 20 minutes follows $N(t) = N_0 \cdot 2^{t/20}$ (with $t$ in minutes).
Starting from a single cell, you pass a billion in under 10 hours — the same multiply-every-step engine as
the rumor, which is why infections and yeast cultures explode so suddenly.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Seismology" icon="🌍">
The Richter scale is a **logarithm** of ground motion. Each whole number up means **ten times** the
amplitude, so a magnitude 7 quake shakes 100× harder than a magnitude 5. Logs compress an enormous range
of energies into a tidy 1–10 dial — exactly the log axis from the demo.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Audio" icon="🔊">
Loudness in **decibels** is logarithmic too: $L = 10\log_{10}(I/I_0)$. Every +10 dB is ten times the sound
intensity, even though it only sounds "about twice as loud." That's why 110 dB at a concert is genuinely
dangerous while 70 dB conversation is fine — the scale hides a thousand-fold gap.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Growth examples (money, bacteria) use the exponential going *forward*; measurement scales (Richter,
decibels) use the logarithm going *backward* to squeeze huge ranges into readable numbers. Same
relationship, opposite directions.
</Callout>
