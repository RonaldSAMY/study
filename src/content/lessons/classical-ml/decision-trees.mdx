## The hook: twenty questions, but the computer is asking

Think of the game *20 Questions*. "Is it alive? Is it bigger than a breadbox? Can it fly?" A good
player doesn't ask random things — they ask the question that **splits the remaining possibilities most
evenly into useful groups**, ruling out as much as possible with each answer. A nurse at an emergency
room does the same: *Are you breathing? Is there chest pain? How high is the fever?* Each yes/no answer
routes you down a branch until the right action is obvious.

A **decision tree** is exactly this game, learned automatically from data. It is a flowchart of
yes/no questions, and at every fork it picks the single question that teaches it the most.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A decision tree asks a sequence of yes/no questions, and at each step it chooses the question that
**reduces uncertainty the most** — splitting a messy mix of classes into purer and purer groups.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a good question kills uncertainty

In the **entropy** lesson you saw that uncertainty has a number. A group that is a 50/50 mix of two
classes is maximally uncertain — you learn a full bit by being told which class a point is. A group
that is *all one class* has zero uncertainty: it is **pure**.

A decision tree builds on that single idea. Imagine a pile of labeled points, some sky-blue and some
indigo, jumbled together. The tree looks for a **threshold** — "is feature x below 5?" — that slices
the pile into two smaller piles. A *good* slice leaves each pile much purer than the original; a *bad*
slice leaves both piles just as mixed as before.

The amount of uncertainty removed by a split is the **information gain**. The tree is greedy: at every
fork it tries many thresholds, measures the gain of each, and keeps the best one. Then it repeats on
each child pile, and again, growing branches until the leaves are pure (or nearly so). Reaching a pure
leaf means the questions so far were enough to decide the class.

## The formal definition

At a node holding $n$ points, let $p_k$ be the fraction belonging to class $k$. We measure how mixed
the node is with an **impurity** score. Two common choices:

**Gini impurity:**

$$
G = 1 - \sum_{k} p_k^2.
$$

**Entropy** (in bits, the quantity from the previous lesson):

$$
H = - \sum_{k} p_k \log_2 p_k.
$$

Both are $0$ for a pure node and largest for an even mix. For two classes at 50/50, $G = 0.5$ and
$H = 1$ bit.

A split sends $n_{\text{L}}$ points left and $n_{\text{R}}$ points right. The quality of the split is
the **information gain** — the parent's impurity minus the size-weighted impurity of the children:

$$
\text{Gain} = H(\text{parent}) - \sum_{c \in \{\text{L}, \text{R}\}} \frac{n_c}{n}\, H(c).
$$

The tree is built by **recursive, greedy splitting**: at each node, try every feature and threshold,
pick the split with the highest gain, then recurse on the left and right children. It stops when a node
is pure, too small to split, or a maximum depth is reached.

```python
# impurity of a node, and the gain of one split (two classes)
gini   = 1 - sum((counts / counts.sum())**2)        # parent impurity
parent = gini
gain   = parent - (nL/n) * giniL - (nR/n) * giniR   # impurity drop
```

That depth limit matters: an unconstrained tree will keep asking questions until **every** training
point sits in its own pure leaf — memorizing noise. That is **overfitting**, and we fight it by
limiting depth, requiring a minimum number of points per leaf, or pruning weak branches afterward.

<Callout type="why">
Decision trees are the rare ML model a human can read like a flowchart — they are **interpretable**,
need no feature scaling, and capture **nonlinear** boundaries by stacking simple cuts. A single tree is
also the building block for the most powerful tabular models around: **random forests** and **gradient
boosting**, which combine many trees and are the subject of the next lesson.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

A "choose the question that reduces uncertainty most, then branch" recipe shows up far beyond data
science.

<ExampleCard domain="Emergency medicine" icon="🚑">
ER **triage** is a decision tree drawn on a laminated card. *Is the airway clear? Is breathing labored?
Is the patient responsive?* Each answer routes the patient toward a severity level. The first questions
are deliberately the ones that most sharply separate "needs care this second" from "can wait" — exactly
the highest-information-gain splits at the top of the tree.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Wildlife biology" icon="🦋">
Field guides identify a species with a **dichotomous key**: *Are the wings scaled or clear? Are the
antennae clubbed or feathered?* Each couplet is a yes/no split that halves the candidate species. A
well-designed key orders its questions so that each one eliminates the most possibilities — a tree
chosen to maximize information gain at every fork.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Aviation" icon="✈️">
Predicting whether a flight will be **delayed** branches naturally: *Is there a thunderstorm at the
hub? Is it a tight evening connection? Is the inbound aircraft already late?* Weather, time of day, and
route each carve the data into groups with very different delay rates — and the tree learns which
question to ask first by measuring which one purifies "on time" versus "delayed" the most.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Gaming" icon="🎮">
Enemy AI in many games is literally a hand-built **behavior tree**: *Can I see the player? Is my health
low? Is cover nearby?* Each check routes the agent to flee, attack, or heal. Designers order the checks
so the most decisive question — usually "is the player visible?" — sits at the root, the same instinct
as putting the highest-gain split first.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the pattern in all four: the *order* of questions is everything. Asking the most informative
question first is what makes a key short, a triage fast, and a learned tree shallow and accurate.
</Callout>
