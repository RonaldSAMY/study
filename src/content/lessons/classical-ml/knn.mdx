## The hook: you are who your neighbors are

Imagine you move to a brand-new town and want to guess what genre of music the new family next door
likes. You don't have their playlist — but you *do* know that the three closest houses are all into
jazz. A pretty good guess for the newcomers? Jazz. You just performed **k-nearest neighbors** in your
head: to label something unknown, look at the things nearest to it and let them vote. No equations, no
training — just *closeness*. This humble idea turns out to be one of the most intuitive classifiers in
all of machine learning.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
To classify a new point, find the **k** training points closest to it and let them **vote** — the
majority class wins.
</Callout>

## Intuition: closeness is a measurement

In the **vectors** and **dot-product** lessons you learned to treat any
object — a song, a house, a flower — as a **point in feature space**: an ordered list of numbers. A
song might become $(\text{tempo}, \text{loudness}, \text{energy})$; a house might become
$(\text{size}, \text{bedrooms}, \text{age})$. Once everything is a point, **distance** between points
becomes a precise stand-in for *similarity*: points that sit near each other are alike, points far
apart are different.

k-NN takes that idea to its simplest conclusion. To label a mystery point:

1. Measure the distance from it to **every** known (labeled) point.
2. Keep the $k$ closest ones — its **neighborhood**.
3. Count the classes in that neighborhood and pick the **majority**. That vote is your prediction.

That is the entire algorithm. The only choices you make are *how big* the neighborhood is (the number
$k$) and *how* you measure distance.

## The formal definition

Let the training set be pairs $(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)$, where each $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is a
feature vector and $y_i$ is its class label. For a new query point $\mathbf{q}$ we need a distance.
The usual choice is **Euclidean distance** (straight-line):

$$
d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{q}) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - q_i)^2}.
$$

A common alternative is the **Manhattan distance** (city-block — sum of axis-aligned steps):

$$
d_1(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{q}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lvert x_i - q_i \rvert.
$$

Let $N_k(\mathbf{q})$ be the set of the $k$ training points with the **smallest** distances to
$\mathbf{q}$. The prediction is the class that appears most often among them — the **argmax of the
class counts**:

$$
\hat{y}(\mathbf{q}) = \arg\max_{c} \sum_{\mathbf{x}_i \in N_k(\mathbf{q})} \mathbf{1}[\,y_i = c\,].
$$

Here $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ is $1$ when the neighbor's label equals class $c$ and $0$ otherwise — so the
sum is simply the vote count for class $c$.

Two practical notes the formula hides. First, you must **scale (normalize) your features**: if "salary"
ranges in the tens of thousands and "age" in the tens, salary will dominate every distance and age will
be ignored — so standardize each feature first. Second, $k$ controls the **bias-variance tradeoff**:
tiny $k$ means low bias but high variance (overfits noise), large $k$ means high bias but low variance
(oversmooths).

```python
# k-NN classification of one query point q
d = np.linalg.norm(X - q, axis=1)   # distance to every training point
idx = d.argsort()[:k]               # indices of the k smallest distances
pred = mode(y[idx])                 # majority vote among those neighbors
```

<Callout type="why">
k-NN is the textbook example of **lazy learning**: there is no training phase and no model parameters
to fit — it just memorizes the data and does all the work at prediction time. Yet it implicitly carves
the feature space into a **decision boundary** defined entirely by where the data sits. It's a perfect
mental anchor for everything that follows: more advanced classifiers are largely clever ways to draw
that same boundary faster and more smoothly.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The "ask your neighbors" move shows up far beyond toy graphs. Here is the *same* algorithm doing real
work.

<ExampleCard domain="Music streaming" icon="🎵">
Describe each track by audio features — $(\text{tempo}, \text{energy}, \text{danceability},
\text{acousticness})$ — so every song is a point. To guess the genre of a freshly uploaded track, find
the nearest songs already in the library and let their genre tags vote. Close in feature space tends to
mean close in sound.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Botany" icon="🌾">
Measuring a seed or flower — petal length, petal width, sepal length — places each specimen in feature
space. A new sample is labeled by the variety of the nearest known specimens. This is exactly how the
classic iris/seed-classification tasks work: distinct varieties form tight clusters, so neighbors
agree.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Computer vision" icon="✍️">
Flatten a small handwritten-digit image into a long pixel vector. To recognize a new digit, compare it
to thousands of labeled examples and take the majority label of the closest ones. Two images of the
same digit have similar pixel vectors, so the nearest neighbors usually share the correct answer — a
surprisingly strong baseline for digit recognition.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Real estate" icon="🏠">
To estimate a home's value, find the nearest **comparable** homes by $(\text{size}, \text{bedrooms},
\text{location}, \text{age})$ and **average** their sale prices. This is k-NN doing *regression* rather
than classification: instead of voting on a label, the neighbors average a number — exactly the
"comps" appraisers have used for decades.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the last example: when the answer is a number rather than a category, you swap the majority vote
for an **average** of the neighbors' values. Same neighborhood, different aggregation — that's k-NN
regression.
</Callout>
