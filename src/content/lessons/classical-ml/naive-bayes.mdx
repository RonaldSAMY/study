## The hook: the guard standing at your inbox

Every email that arrives gets a split-second trial. A quiet little classifier reads the words —
*"free", "winner", "invoice", "meeting"* — and decides, before you ever look, whether the message
belongs in your inbox or the spam folder. It is wrong rarely enough that you forget it exists. The
astonishing part is how *simple* the math behind it is: it is little more than counting words and
multiplying probabilities. That classifier is **Naive Bayes**, and once you see how it works you can
build one by hand.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Start with how likely spam is in general, then let each word in the email nudge that belief up or
down — and **multiply** all the nudges together because we pretend the words are independent.
</Callout>

## Intuition: collecting clues, one word at a time

You already know **Bayes' rule**: it tells you how to update a belief after seeing evidence. Here the
belief is *"this email is spam"* and the evidence is *the words in it*. Before reading anything, you
have a hunch — maybe a third of mail is spam. That is the **prior**.

Then you read the words, treating each one as a **clue**. The word *"viagra"* is far more common in
spam than in real mail, so it shoves your belief hard toward spam. The word *"meeting"* does the
opposite. Each clue multiplies your odds by how much more (or less) spammy it makes the message.

The catch: properly accounting for how words appear *together* is enormously complicated. So Naive
Bayes makes one bold simplification — it pretends every word is **independent** of the others given
the class. That assumption is almost never literally true (the words "machine" and "learning" clearly
travel together), which is exactly why it is called **naive**. But it lets us replace a hopeless joint
calculation with a simple **product** of one-word probabilities — and in practice it works
remarkably well.

## The formal definition

We want the probability of a class $C$ (say, spam) given the observed words $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n$.
Bayes' rule says:

$$
P(C \mid x_1, \dots, x_n) = \frac{P(C)\, P(x_1, \dots, x_n \mid C)}{P(x_1, \dots, x_n)}.
$$

The hard term is $P(x_1, \dots, x_n \mid C)$ — the chance of seeing *that exact combination* of
words. The **naive (conditional-independence) assumption** says that, once we fix the class, the
words don't influence each other:

$$
P(x_1, \dots, x_n \mid C) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid C).
$$

Substituting gives the Naive Bayes posterior:

$$
P(C \mid x_1, \dots, x_n) = \frac{P(C) \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid C)}{P(x_1, \dots, x_n)}.
$$

To **classify**, we don't even need the denominator: it is the same for every class, so we just pick
the class with the largest numerator:

$$
\hat{C} = \arg\max_{C} \; P(C) \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid C).
$$

Multiplying hundreds of tiny probabilities underflows to zero, so in practice we work in **log
space**, turning the product into a sum:

$$
\log P(C) + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log P(x_i \mid C).
$$

One more fix: if a word never appeared with a class in training, its probability is $0$ and wipes out
the whole product. **Laplace smoothing** adds a small count to every word so no probability is ever
exactly zero:

$$
P(x_i \mid C) = \frac{\text{count}(x_i, C) + 1}{\text{count}(C) + V},
$$

where $V$ is the size of the vocabulary.

```python
# log-space scoring: rows = classes, cols = words
logp = np.log(prior) + np.log(likelihood[:, present]).sum(axis=1)
pred = logp.argmax()   # most probable class — no denominator needed
```

<Callout type="why">
Naive Bayes is the classic **baseline** in machine learning: it trains in a single pass over the
data, needs almost no tuning, and handles thousands of features (words) effortlessly. For text
problems it is often shockingly hard to beat — which is why "try Naive Bayes first" is standard
advice before reaching for anything heavier.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The "multiply clue probabilities, pick the biggest" recipe is not about email at all. It shows up
anywhere you classify something from a bundle of features.

<ExampleCard domain="Email / NLP" icon="📧">
The spam filter from the demo: each word has a probability under "spam" and under "ham". Multiply the
prior by the likelihood of every word that appears, compare the two classes, and route the message.
This is the original killer app for Naive Bayes.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Medicine" icon="🩺">
Given a patient's symptoms — fever, cough, loss of smell — estimate the probability of each disease.
Start from how common each disease is (the prior), then multiply in how likely each symptom is for
that disease. The diagnosis with the highest score is the leading suspect for further testing.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="News / NLP" icon="📰">
An article lands in the newsroom system and must be filed under *sports*, *politics*, or *tech*. The
word "election" is likely under politics, "quarterback" under sports. Multiply each category's prior
by the likelihood of the article's words and tag it with the winner — automatic topic sorting.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Social media" icon="💬">
Sentiment analysis of a post: is it positive or negative? Words like "love" and "amazing" are far
likelier in positive posts; "terrible" and "broken" in negative ones. The same product of word
likelihoods scores each sentiment and flags the post's mood.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the pattern is identical every time: a <strong>prior</strong> per class, a
<strong>likelihood</strong> per feature, a <strong>product</strong> to combine them, and an
<strong>argmax</strong> to decide. Swap "words" for "symptoms" and email for patients — the algebra
never changes.
</Callout>
