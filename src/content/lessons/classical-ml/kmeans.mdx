## The hook: the customers nobody labeled

A shop has ten thousand customers and not a single tag describing any of them. Nobody wrote down
"this is a bargain hunter" or "this one is a weekend splurger." All you have is what each person *did*:
how often they visit, how much they spend, how late at night they browse. Plot every customer as a
point and a striking thing happens — they don't smear out evenly. They **clump**. Some clumps spend
big and rarely; others spend little but constantly. You never told the data what the groups were, yet
the groups are sitting right there. **k-means** is the algorithm that finds them.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
k-means hunts for **k centers** so that every point sits as close as possible to its nearest center,
by repeatedly assigning points to the closest center and then sliding each center to the middle of the
points it caught.
</Callout>

## Intuition: clumps as points near a shared center

You already know how to turn anything into a **vector** and how to measure the **distance** between two
vectors. k-means is built entirely on those two skills. Picture each data item as a point in space. A
**cluster** is just a bunch of points huddled near a common location — and that location is the
cluster's **center** (its *centroid*).

The catch that makes this interesting: this is **unsupervised** learning. Nobody handed us the right
answers — there are no labels to learn from. The algorithm must propose the groups itself. It does so
by guessing some centers and then improving them through two steps that take turns:

1. **Assign** — color each point by whichever center is nearest to it. (Pure distance, the dot-product
   neighbor of the last lesson.)
2. **Update** — move each center to the *average position* of all the points that just chose it.

Re-assign, re-center, re-assign, re-center. Each round the centers slide toward the dense heart of
their group, and the grouping gets cleaner. After a few passes nothing moves — the centers have settled
and the clusters are found.

## The formal definition

We have $n$ data points $x_1, \dots, x_n$, each a vector in $\mathbb{R}^d$. We want $k$ centroids
$\mu_1, \dots, \mu_k$ and an assignment $c_i$ that says which centroid point $x_i$ belongs to. k-means
chooses them to minimize the **within-cluster sum of squares** (also called the *inertia*):

$$
J = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl\lVert x_i - \mu_{c_i} \bigr\rVert^2 .
$$

This is a chicken-and-egg problem: the best assignments depend on the centers, and the best centers
depend on the assignments. **Lloyd's algorithm** breaks the loop by fixing one and optimizing the
other, in turn.

**Assignment step** — with the centers held fixed, give each point to its nearest center:

$$
c_i = \arg\min_{j} \, \bigl\lVert x_i - \mu_j \bigr\rVert^2 .
$$

**Update step** — with the assignments held fixed, move each center to the mean of its members. If
$C_j$ is the set of points assigned to center $j$, then

$$
\mu_j = \frac{1}{\lvert C_j \rvert} \sum_{i \in C_j} x_i .
$$

Each step can only lower $J$ (or leave it unchanged), so the algorithm **converges** — but only to a
**local minimum**, not necessarily the best possible grouping. That is exactly the "different seeds,
different answer" effect from the demo. In practice we run it several times from different random starts
and keep the lowest-inertia result, and smarter seeding (**k-means++**) spreads the initial centers out
to avoid bad starts. Choosing $k$ itself is a judgment call: a common trick is the **elbow method** —
plot inertia against $k$ and look for the bend where adding more clusters stops helping much.

The whole update loop is just a few lines of array math:

```python
# X: (n, d) points, mu: (k, d) centers
labels = np.argmin(((X[:, None] - mu) ** 2).sum(2), axis=1)   # assign
mu = np.array([X[labels == j].mean(0) for j in range(k)])      # update
```

<Callout type="why">
k-means is the gateway to **unsupervised learning**: finding structure with no labels at all. The same
"compress data to k representative vectors" move is **vector quantization** — replacing many vectors
with their nearest centroid to save space. And the assign-then-update rhythm is the hard-edged cousin of
the **EM algorithm** behind Gaussian mixture models, where points belong to clusters *softly* by
probability instead of all-or-nothing.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The "group points around k centers" move shows up far beyond customer data.

<ExampleCard domain="Marketing" icon="🛍️">
A retailer clusters shoppers by spend, frequency, and recency. k-means surfaces segments like
"loyal regulars," "big rare spenders," and "one-time browsers" — none of which anyone defined in
advance. Each centroid is the *average profile* of a segment, and campaigns get tailored to it.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Computer vision" icon="🖼️">
To shrink an image to $k$ colors, treat every pixel as an RGB vector and run k-means with, say,
$k = 16$. Each pixel is then redrawn as its nearest centroid color. The photo still looks right but now
uses just 16 colors — that's **color quantization**, classic vector quantization at work.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Astronomy" icon="✨">
Survey telescopes measure brightness, color, and size for millions of objects. k-means groups them by
these properties, separating, say, red giant stars from young blue ones, or galaxies of different types
— letting astronomers triage a flood of objects without inspecting each by hand.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Logistics" icon="🚚">
Where should a company place $k$ warehouses to serve thousands of delivery addresses? Treat each address
as a point and run k-means: the centroids land at the spots that minimize total squared distance to
customers — exactly the warehouse locations that keep deliveries short.
</ExampleCard>
