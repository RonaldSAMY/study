## The hook: stop taking limits the slow way

You already know what a derivative *is*: the limit of $\frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}$ as $h \to 0$. Beautiful —
and exhausting. Grinding that limit by hand for every function would make calculus unusable, like doing
long multiplication when a times-table exists. Mathematicians did the painful limits once, spotted the
patterns, and bottled them into a handful of **rules**. Learn four of them and you can differentiate
almost anything you'll meet — in seconds, no limit required.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The **differentiation rules** are shortcut recipes — power, product, quotient, and chain — that turn a
function into its derivative directly, replacing the limit definition with arithmetic.
</Callout>

## Intuition: four moves cover almost everything

Every elaborate function is built by *combining* simpler ones, and each way of combining has its own rule:

- **Power rule** — for a plain power $x^n$, slide the exponent down front and reduce it by one.
- **Product rule** — for two things *multiplied*, the rate is "first's rate times second, plus first times
  second's rate." Both factors take turns changing.
- **Quotient rule** — for a *ratio*, a tidy combination of top and bottom rates, all over the bottom squared.
- **Chain rule** — for a function *inside* another function, multiply the **outer rate** by the **inner
  rate**. Think nested gears: a fast inner gear spins through a slow outer one, and the rates multiply.

The chain rule is the star. It is the one that powers backpropagation in neural networks, and the one
worth slowing down for: it says *rates of nested processes multiply.*

## The formal definition

Let $u$ and $v$ be functions of $x$, and $c, n$ constants. The four workhorse rules:

**Power rule.**
$$
\frac{d}{dx}\,x^{n} = n\,x^{\,n-1}.
$$

**Product rule.**
$$
(u\,v)' = u'\,v + u\,v'.
$$

**Quotient rule.**
$$
\left(\frac{u}{v}\right)' = \frac{u'\,v - u\,v'}{v^{2}}.
$$

**Chain rule.** If $y = f(g(x))$, write $u = g(x)$; then
$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx} = f'\!\big(g(x)\big)\cdot g'(x).
$$

Two helpers make these fly: the derivative of a constant is $0$, and constants slide outside,
$(c\,u)' = c\,u'$. With $d(t) = \sqrt{4t+1} = (4t+1)^{1/2}$, the chain rule gives outer rate
$\tfrac{1}{2}(4t+1)^{-1/2}$ times inner rate $4$, i.e. $d'(t) = \dfrac{2}{\sqrt{4t+1}}$ — exactly the
emerald curve in the demo.

<Callout type="why">
**Backpropagation is the chain rule, full stop.** A neural network is functions nested dozens of layers
deep, and training needs the derivative of the final loss with respect to each early weight. The chain
rule says: multiply the local rate at every layer along the path. Every modern model is trained by chaining
these rates from output back to input.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Each rule earns its keep in a different real situation.

<ExampleCard domain="Road safety" icon="🚗">
A car's braking distance grows like $d(v) = 0.01\,v^2$. The **power rule** gives $d'(v) = 0.02\,v$: the
distance needed climbs *twice as fast* as your speed. Doubling speed quadruples the stopping distance —
which is why a small speed increase is a big safety deal.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Retail revenue" icon="🛒">
Revenue is price times units sold, $R(p) = p\,(10 - p)$. The **product rule** (or expanding first) gives
$R'(p) = 10 - 2p$. Revenue stops rising at $p = 5$, where raising the price loses more customers than the
extra dollar gains — the product rule splits those two competing effects apart.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Pharmacology" icon="💊">
A drug's concentration follows $C(t) = \dfrac{5t}{t^2 + 1}$. The **quotient rule** yields
$C'(t) = \dfrac{5(1 - t^2)}{(t^2+1)^2}$, which hits zero at $t = 1$ — the exact moment the drug peaks in the
bloodstream before the body clears it faster than it absorbs.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Water systems" icon="🚰">
A tank's depth is $d(t) = \sqrt{4t + 1}$ as water streams in. The **chain rule** nests a square root around
a steady inflow: $d'(t) = \dfrac{2}{\sqrt{4t+1}}$. The depth rises quickly when shallow and creeps once
deep — the inner rate is constant, but the outer square root tames it.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Spot the structure first: a lone power? a product? a ratio? something nested? Name the shape and the right
rule is automatic. Real functions often stack several rules — peel them from the outside in.
</Callout>
