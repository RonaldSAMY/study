## The hook: how much water is in the tank?

A pipe pours water into a tank, but the flow keeps changing — a surge here, a trickle there. You don't
have a meter on the tank itself, only a gauge on the *rate* of inflow, litres per minute, wobbling
moment to moment. How many litres ended up inside? If the rate were constant you'd just multiply rate by
time. It isn't, so you chop the time into thin slices, treat the rate as roughly constant across each
sliver, multiply, and add up all the pieces. Slice finer and finer and that running total sharpens into
the exact volume. That summing-of-slices is the **integral** — and it turns out to be the derivative run
in reverse.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
An **integral** adds up infinitely many infinitely thin slices to give a total — the **area under a
curve** — and the Fundamental Theorem says integrating is the *inverse* of differentiating.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from a staircase to the smooth truth

To find the area under a rate curve, approximate it with **rectangles**. Each rectangle's height is the
rate at some sample point and its width is a small slice of time, so its area (height × width) is the
amount delivered during that slice. Add the rectangles and you get a **Riemann sum** — a staircase
estimate of the total.

Here's the magic: with just a few fat rectangles the staircase is crude, overshooting on the way up and
undershooting on the way down. But pack in more, thinner rectangles and the staircase hugs the curve ever
more tightly. In the limit of infinitely many infinitely thin rectangles, the error vanishes and the sum
becomes the *exact* area. That limit is the **definite integral**.

And now the punchline that ties this whole track together. The **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus** says
the two great operations — slicing-and-summing (integration) and finding-the-slope (differentiation) — are
inverses. Accumulating a rate gives back the original total, because the rate *was* the derivative of the
total all along. Derivatives take totals apart; integrals put them back together.

## The formal definition

Split the interval $[a, b]$ into $n$ slices of width $\Delta x = \frac{b - a}{n}$, sample the function at a
point $x_i^{*}$ in each, and sum the rectangle areas. The **definite integral** is the limit of that
Riemann sum:

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^{*})\,\Delta x.
$$

It is the signed area between the curve and the axis from $a$ to $b$. Computing it from the limit every
time would be brutal — so the **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus** hands us a shortcut. If $F$ is an
**antiderivative** of $f$ (meaning $F'(x) = f(x)$), then

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a).
$$

To integrate, just *undo* a derivative and subtract the endpoints. For the demo, the inflow rate is
$r(t) = 2 + \sin t$. An antiderivative is $R(t) = 2t - \cos t$, so the total over $[0, 8]$ is

$$
\int_0^8 (2 + \sin t)\,dt = R(8) - R(0) = (16 - \cos 8) - (0 - 1) \approx 17.15 \text{ litres},
$$

exactly the green "true area" the staircase converges to.

<Callout type="why">
Probability lives on integrals. The area under a probability density curve **is** the probability, and it
must total $1$ across all outcomes. Expected values, the normalizing constant of a distribution, the area
under an ROC curve grading a classifier — each is an integral. Accumulating a rate into a total is one of
the most reused moves in all of machine learning.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Integration is "add up a rate over an interval to get the total." Watch it gather quantities everywhere.

<ExampleCard domain="Water systems" icon="🚰">
A pump's inflow rate $r(t)$ litres/min wobbles over time. The water collected is
$\int_0^T r(t)\,dt$ — the area under the rate curve. No tank meter needed: integrate the flow gauge and you
recover the exact volume, the same accumulation the demo animates.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Energy billing" icon="🔌">
Your home draws power $P(t)$ in kilowatts, swinging as appliances switch on and off. The **energy** the
meter bills you for is $\int P(t)\,dt$ in kilowatt-hours — the area under the power curve. Your electric
bill is literally a definite integral.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Travel" icon="🚙">
On a road trip your speed $v(t)$ rises and falls. The **distance covered** is $\int_a^b v(t)\,dt$, the area
under the speed graph. Since position is the antiderivative of speed, the Fundamental Theorem says total
distance is just the change in odometer reading — end minus start.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Hydrology" icon="🌧️">
A storm dumps rain at a varying intensity $i(t)$ mm/hour. Total rainfall is $\int i(t)\,dt$ — accumulate
the intensity over the storm's hours. Flood models integrate these rates across whole catchments to predict
how high the river will crest.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Every example is "rate × time, summed up." Whatever you have the *rate* of — water, energy, distance,
rainfall — integrating recovers the *total*. And by the Fundamental Theorem, that total is just an
antiderivative evaluated end minus start.
</Callout>
