## The hook: where does the value *want* to go?

A patient takes a steady dose of medicine, and the drug concentration in their blood climbs hour
after hour — fast at first, then leveling off. It never quite reaches the so-called *steady-state*
level, but it edges closer and closer, as near as you like. Ask "what is the concentration **at**
infinite time?" and you get a shrug. Ask "what value is it **heading toward**?" and there is a crisp,
single answer. That second question — *what value does a quantity approach?* — is the **limit**, and it
is the foundation every other idea in calculus is built on.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **limit** is the value a function *approaches* as its input creeps toward some point — even if the
function never actually arrives, or isn't defined there at all.
</Callout>

## Intuition: approaching from both sides

You met limits informally already: you watched a function's output settle toward a number as the input
slid toward a target. The key word is *approach*. We don't care what happens **at** the point — we care
what happens **near** it.

To pin a limit down we sneak up from **two directions**:

- The **left-hand limit** — slide in from smaller inputs.
- The **right-hand limit** — slide in from larger inputs.

If both sides head to the *same* number, that shared destination is **the limit**. If the two sides
disagree, the limit **does not exist** — the curve is torn at that point.

A function is **continuous** at a point when three things line up: the function is defined there, the
limit exists there, and the two are *equal*. Plain-language version: you can draw the curve through that
point without lifting your pen. When that fails, you get a **removable hole** (a single missing point you
could patch) or a **jump** (the curve leaps to a new height).

## The formal definition

We write the limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $a$ like this:

$$
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L.
$$

It says: as $x$ gets arbitrarily close to $a$ (without necessarily equaling $a$), $f(x)$ gets
arbitrarily close to $L$. The two **one-sided limits** are written

$$
\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \quad \text{(from the left)}, \qquad \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) \quad \text{(from the right)}.
$$

The full limit exists exactly when these agree:

$$
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \iff \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L.
$$

Finally, $f$ is **continuous at $a$** when the limit not only exists but matches the actual value:

$$
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a).
$$

The demo's first curve, $f(x) = \dfrac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}$, is undefined at $x = 1$ (it reads $\tfrac{0}{0}$),
yet it simplifies to $x + 1$ everywhere else, so $\lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = 2$. The limit happily exists; the
function just has a hole we could fill by *defining* $f(1) = 2$.

<Callout type="why">
That $\tfrac{0}{0}$ form is not a dead end — it is the engine of the next lesson. The **derivative** is
exactly a limit of the shape $\frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}$, which is $\tfrac{0}{0}$ at $h = 0$. Limits are
what let calculus divide "nothing by nothing" and still get a sharp answer.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

A limit is "the value a process is heading toward," and continuity is "no surprises, no tears." Watch them
appear far from any math class.

<ExampleCard domain="Medicine" icon="💊">
With repeated doses, blood drug concentration approaches a **steady-state** ceiling it never exactly hits:
$\lim_{t \to \infty} C(t) = C_{\text{ss}}$. Doctors dose against that limiting value, not the messy hour-by-hour
numbers, because the limit is the stable target the body settles into.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Logistics" icon="📦">
A courier charges \$4 for parcels up to 1 kg and \$7 the instant you tip over 1 kg. At exactly 1 kg the
price **jumps** — the left limit is \$4, the right limit is \$7. The cost function is *discontinuous*
there, which is why that last gram can cost you three dollars.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Climate science" icon="🧊">
Heat a block of ice and its temperature climbs, then **plateaus at 0 °C** while it melts — energy goes into
the phase change, not the thermometer. The temperature-vs-energy curve stays continuous (no jump) but
flattens, a vivid picture of a value that momentarily refuses to budge.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Streaming video" icon="📶">
Adaptive players nudge quality up step by step as bandwidth improves, *approaching* the source resolution
without overshooting it. "Best achievable quality" is a limit the stream homes in on — closer and closer,
yet capped by what the file actually contains.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Same two questions everywhere: *what value is this heading toward* (limit), and *can I get there without a
sudden break* (continuity). A plateau is a calm limit; a price jump is a torn one.
</Callout>
