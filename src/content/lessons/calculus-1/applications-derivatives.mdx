## The hook: what price makes the most money?

You run a lemonade stand. Charge too little and you barely cover your lemons; charge too much and the
line vanishes. Somewhere in between sits a sweet spot — the price that squeezes out maximum profit. You
could guess prices all afternoon, or you could notice something: at the very top of the profit curve, the
slope is **flat**. Nudge the price either way from the peak and profit barely moves. That flatness — a
derivative equal to zero — is calculus's universal fingerprint for "best possible," and it turns
guesswork into a one-line equation.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
At a maximum or minimum, the tangent is **horizontal**, so the derivative is **zero** — solving
$f'(x) = 0$ finds the peaks and valleys.
</Callout>

## Intuition: flat tangents mark the extremes

You already know $f'(x)$ is the slope of the tangent line. Walk along a curve and read the sign of that
slope:

- $f'(x) > 0$: the curve is **rising** — you can still climb higher.
- $f'(x) < 0$: the curve is **falling** — you have overshot.
- $f'(x) = 0$: the tangent is **flat** — a possible peak (max) or valley (min).

The points where $f'(x) = 0$ are called **critical points**, and the maxima and minima hide among them. To
tell a peak from a valley, check whether the slope switches from $+$ to $-$ (a peak) or $-$ to $+$ (a
valley) — or peek at the second derivative $f''$: concave-down ($f'' < 0$) is a peak, concave-up
($f'' > 0$) is a valley.

There's a second power move: **related rates**. When two quantities are tied by an equation, the chain
rule links their *rates* of change. A balloon's radius and its volume are connected, so the rate the
radius grows is locked to the rate you pump air in. Differentiate the relationship with respect to time
and the unknown rate falls out.

## The formal definition

To find the extreme values of a smooth function $f$ on an interval:

1. Compute the derivative $f'(x)$.
2. Solve for the **critical points** where it vanishes:
$$
f'(x) = 0.
$$
3. Classify each with the **second-derivative test**:
$$
f''(x) < 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{local maximum}, \qquad f''(x) > 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{local minimum}.
$$
4. On a closed interval, also check the endpoints — the global extreme may sit at a boundary.

For the lemonade stand, profit is $P(p) = (p-1)(12 - 2p) = -2p^2 + 14p - 12$. Then $P'(p) = -4p + 14$, and
$P'(p) = 0$ gives the optimal price $p = 3.5$. Since $P''(p) = -4 < 0$, it's a maximum — exactly the peak in
the demo.

For **related rates**, differentiate a relationship with respect to time. If a quantity $V$ depends on a
quantity $r$ that depends on time, the chain rule chains the rates:

$$
\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{dV}{dr}\cdot\frac{dr}{dt}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
Training a model *is* optimization. Gradient descent hunts for the spot where the loss is smallest, and
that minimum is where the gradient — the multivariable cousin of $f'(x) = 0$ — vanishes. "Set the
derivative to zero" is the same instinct, whether you're pricing lemonade or fitting a billion-parameter
network.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Find the flat tangent, or chain the rates — here's that single move across very different problems.

<ExampleCard domain="Small business" icon="🍋">
Lemonade profit $P(p) = -2p^2 + 14p - 12$ peaks where $P'(p) = -4p + 14 = 0$, at \$3.50 a cup. Below it
you're leaving money on the table; above it you scare off customers. One derivative pins the perfect price.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Construction" icon="🚧">
With 40 m of fencing for a rectangular pen, the area is $A(w) = w(20 - w)$. Setting $A'(w) = 20 - 2w = 0$
gives $w = 10$ — a square encloses the most space. The same logic sizes packaging, storage yards, and
solar-panel layouts.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Aviation fuel" icon="✈️">
Fuel burn per kilometre is U-shaped in airspeed: too slow wastes time aloft, too fast fights drag. The
**most efficient cruise speed** is the bottom of that curve, exactly where the derivative of cost-per-km is
zero. Airlines fly that critical point to shave millions off fuel bills.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Hot-air balloon" icon="🎈">
A balloon's volume is $V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3$, so its radius and volume rates are linked:
$\frac{dV}{dt} = 4\pi r^2 \frac{dr}{dt}$. Pump air at a steady $\frac{dV}{dt}$ and the skin expands slower as
$r$ grows — a textbook **related-rates** chain, the same one that tracks spreading oil spills and rising
floodwaters.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Two flavours of the same tool: <strong>optimization</strong> asks "where is the slope zero?" to find best
and worst; <strong>related rates</strong> asks "how do linked rates connect?" via the chain rule. Both are
just the derivative, put to work.
</Callout>
