## The hook: expanding a power without the grind

Multiplying $(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$ is easy. But $(a+b)^5$? Multiplying it out term by term is
tedious and error-prone. There's a pattern hiding in the coefficients ŌĆö $1, 5, 10, 10, 5, 1$ ŌĆö and it is
exactly the **combinations** you just learned. The binomial theorem turns "expand this power" into "count
how many ways each term appears", and arranges every answer into the elegant staircase of **Pascal's
triangle**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
In $(a+b)^n$, the coefficient of the term with $r$ copies of $b$ is $\binom{n}{r}$ ŌĆö the number of ways to
choose which $r$ of the $n$ factors hand over a $b$.

</Callout>

## Intuition: every term is a counting question

Write $(a+b)^n$ as $n$ identical factors multiplied together:

$$
(a+b)^n = \underbrace{(a+b)(a+b)\cdots(a+b)}_{n \text{ factors}}.
$$

To build a term, each factor donates either an $a$ or a $b$. A term like $a^{n-r}b^r$ comes from choosing
$r$ of the factors to give a $b$ (the rest give $a$). **How many ways** can you choose those $r$ factors?
Exactly $\binom{n}{r}$ ŌĆö a combination. So that's the coefficient.

Stacking these coefficients row by row builds **Pascal's triangle**: each row $n$ is the list
$\binom{n}{0}, \binom{n}{1}, \dots, \binom{n}{n}$, and ŌĆö beautifully ŌĆö every interior number is the sum of
the two directly above it.

## The formal definition

The **binomial theorem** states that for any non-negative integer $n$,

$$
(a+b)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} a^{\,n-r} b^{\,r}
= \binom{n}{0}a^n + \binom{n}{1}a^{n-1}b + \cdots + \binom{n}{n}b^n.
$$

The coefficients $\binom{n}{r}$ are called **binomial coefficients**, and Pascal's triangle tabulates them.
Its growth rule is **Pascal's rule**:

$$
\binom{n}{r} = \binom{n-1}{r-1} + \binom{n-1}{r}.
$$

For example, row 4 reads $1, 4, 6, 4, 1$, so

$$
(a+b)^4 = a^4 + 4a^3b + 6a^2b^2 + 4ab^3 + b^4.
$$

<Callout type="why">
Set $a = b = 1$ and the theorem gives $\sum_r \binom{n}{r} = 2^n$ ŌĆö the count of all subsets of an
$n$-element set. Set $a$ and $b$ to a success/failure probability and the same expansion becomes the
**binomial distribution**, the bridge from counting into probability and the next track.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Probability" icon="­¤¬Ö">
Flip a fair coin 4 times. The number of ways to get exactly 2 heads is $\binom{4}{2} = 6$ ŌĆö row 4's
middle coefficient. The whole row $1, 4, 6, 4, 1$ counts the ways to get $0,1,2,3,4$ heads, and they sum
to $2^4 = 16$ equally likely sequences.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Climate" icon="­¤ī¦’ĖÅ">
A forecaster models 5 independent days, each "rain" or "dry". The number of ways exactly 3 of the 5 days
are rainy is $\binom{5}{3} = 10$ ŌĆö straight from row 5. Expanding $(\text{rain} + \text{dry})^5$ organizes
every possible weather week at once.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Manufacturing" icon="­¤ÅŁ">
On a line where each item is "pass" or "fail", the chance of exactly $k$ failures in $n$ items is weighted
by $\binom{n}{k}$. Quality engineers lean on these coefficients to predict how often a given number of
defects slips through a batch.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Product testing" icon="­¤¦¬">
Running an A/B test on 6 users, each either "converts" or "doesn't", the number of ways to see exactly 4
conversions is $\binom{6}{4} = 15$. The binomial expansion lays out the full spread of outcomes a small
experiment can produce.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The pattern $a^{n-r}b^r$ means exponents always sum to $n$: as the power of $b$ climbs $0,1,2,\dots,n$, the
power of $a$ falls $n, n-1, \dots, 0$. If a term's exponents don't add to $n$, something went wrong.
</Callout>
