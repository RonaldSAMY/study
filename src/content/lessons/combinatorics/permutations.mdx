## The hook: who stands on the podium?

Eight sprinters line up for a 100 m final. Only three will take gold, silver, and bronze — and the
*order* is the whole story. Gold-then-silver for two runners is a different result than silver-then-gold.
How many possible podiums are there? The rule of product gets us started, but there's a twist: once a
runner takes gold, they can't also take silver. Each choice **removes** an option. That shrinking-choice
pattern is a **permutation**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **permutation** counts **ordered** arrangements where items aren't reused: the first slot has all
options, the next has one fewer, and so on.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the choices count down

From **counting principles** you know that independent steps multiply. Permutations are the same rule of
product — with one new wrinkle: the steps aren't independent, because each pick uses up an item.

Filling the podium: gold has 8 candidates, then silver has only 7 left, then bronze has 6. So

$$
8 \times 7 \times 6 = 336 \text{ podiums.}
$$

If instead you arranged **all** $n$ items in a row, you'd multiply every count down to 1:

$$
n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 2 \times 1 = n! \quad (\text{``}n\text{ factorial''}).
$$

Factorials are the natural language of "in how many orders?" — and they grow shockingly fast.

## The formal definition

The number of **permutations of $n$ distinct items taken $r$ at a time** — ordered, no repeats — is

$$
P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} = \underbrace{n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times (n-r+1)}_{r \text{ factors}}.
$$

The factorial form just writes the shrinking product compactly: the $(n-r)!$ in the denominator cancels
all the tail factors you never used. Special cases worth memorizing:

$$
P(n, n) = n!, \qquad P(n, 1) = n, \qquad P(n, 0) = 1, \qquad 0! = 1.
$$

<Callout type="why">
Ordering matters constantly in computation: the number of ways to sequence tasks, to order layers, or to
arrange a dataset all scale like factorials. That explosive growth is exactly why brute-forcing every
ordering (for example, every route in the traveling-salesman problem) becomes impossible even for modest
$n$.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Live events" icon="🎤">
A music festival must order 6 bands into 6 time slots for one stage. Every distinct line-up is an
arrangement of all 6, so there are $6! = 720$ possible schedules. Swap any two bands' slots and you have
a genuinely different evening.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Hospitality" icon="🪑">
Seating 5 guests along one side of a dinner table is a permutation of all 5: $5! = 120$ seating charts.
If only the 3 chairs nearest the host matter, you instead arrange 5 people into 3 ordered seats:
$P(5,3) = 5 \times 4 \times 3 = 60$.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Security" icon="🔑">
A 4-character code drawn from 26 letters **with no repeats** is a permutation, not a free product:
$P(26, 4) = 26 \times 25 \times 24 \times 23 = 358{,}800$. Forbidding repeats actually *reduces* the count
versus the $26^4$ you'd get if letters could repeat.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Language" icon="🔤">
The word "LISTEN" has 6 distinct letters, so it has $6! = 720$ rearrangements (anagrams), including
"SILENT" and "TINSEL". Order is the entire point of a word — the same letters in a new order make a new
string.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Two flags tell you it's a permutation: <strong>order matters</strong> and <strong>no repeats</strong>.
If repeats are allowed (like a PIN), go back to the plain rule of product instead.
</Callout>
