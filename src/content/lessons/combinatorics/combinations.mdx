## The hook: does the lottery care what order you picked?

You fill in 6 numbers on a lottery ticket. When the balls are drawn, nobody checks the *order* you wrote
them — a winning set is a winning set. That's the opposite of the race podium, where order was
everything. When order **doesn't** matter, counting "ordered line-ups" overcounts every group many
times over. Fixing that overcount gives us **combinations**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **combination** counts **unordered** selections: take the ordered count and divide out the $r!$
different orderings of each chosen group, since they're all the same selection.
</Callout>

## Intuition: divide away the duplicates

In **permutations**, choosing 3 people for ranked roles (President, VP, Treasurer) from 6 gave
$P(6,3) = 6 \times 5 \times 4 = 120$ ordered line-ups. But a *committee* of 3 has no roles — only
membership matters. The trio of Ann, Bo, and Cy got counted once for each of its orderings:

$$
3! = 6 \text{ orderings of the same three people.}
$$

So the number of genuine committees is $120 / 6 = 20$. That's the core move of a combination: **count
ordered, then divide by $r!$** to collapse every reordering of a group into one.

## Play with it

with the **unordered** count ($C(n,r)$). The readout shows the divisor $r!$ — exactly how many times each
selection was double-counted. Small cases list every committee.

<Callout type="tip">
Notice the symmetry: $C(n, r) = C(n, n-r)$. Choosing 4 players to put <em>on</em> the field from 11 is
the same count as choosing the 7 to leave <em>off</em>. Picking who's in also decides who's out.
</Callout>

## The formal definition

The number of **combinations of $n$ items taken $r$ at a time** — unordered, no repeats — is

$$
C(n, r) = \binom{n}{r} = \frac{P(n, r)}{r!} = \frac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}.
$$

The notation $\binom{n}{r}$ is read "$n$ choose $r$". Useful values:

$$
\binom{n}{0} = 1, \qquad \binom{n}{n} = 1, \qquad \binom{n}{1} = n, \qquad \binom{n}{r} = \binom{n}{n-r}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
"Choose $r$ from $n$" is everywhere in machine learning: how many ways to pick a subset of features, to
sample a mini-batch from a dataset, or to form pairs of points to compare. The binomial coefficient also
becomes the engine of the binomial distribution — counting how many ways $k$ successes can land among $n$
trials.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Lottery & gambling" icon="🎰">
In a 6-from-49 lottery, the order of the drawn balls is irrelevant, so the number of possible tickets is
$\binom{49}{6} = 13{,}983{,}816$. Your single ticket is one of nearly 14 million equally likely sets —
that one division by $6!$ is what makes the odds so steep.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Card games" icon="🃏">
A 5-card poker hand is a selection from 52 cards where order doesn't matter:
$\binom{52}{5} = 2{,}598{,}960$ possible hands. Every probability in poker — flush, full house, the lot —
starts from this single count of total hands.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Team sports" icon="🏀">
A coach with 10 available players must field a starting 5. With no positions assigned, that's
$\binom{10}{5} = 252$ possible line-ups. Choosing the 5 who start automatically chooses the 5 on the
bench — the $C(n,r) = C(n,n-r)$ symmetry in action.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Food & dining" icon="🍕">
A pizza shop has 8 toppings and you want any 3 (no doubling). The number of distinct topping sets is
$\binom{8}{3} = 56$. Mushroom-pepper-onion is the same pizza however you list the toppings, so order is
ignored.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="warning">
The make-or-break question is <strong>does order matter?</strong> Ranked roles, finishing places, and
sequences are permutations. Committees, hands, teams, and topping sets are combinations. Same items —
very different counts.
</Callout>
