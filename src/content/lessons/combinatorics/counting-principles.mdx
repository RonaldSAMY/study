## The hook: how many outfits do you really own?

Open your closet: say 5 shirts, 3 pairs of trousers, and 2 pairs of shoes. It *feels* like a small
wardrobe — but how many distinct outfits can you build? Not 10. Not 5. The honest answer is
$5 \times 3 \times 2 = 30$. Counting "how many ways" sounds trivial until the numbers explode, and
almost every probability you will ever compute starts by counting the possibilities. Two simple rules
do nearly all of the work.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
When choices **combine** (one of these **and** one of those), you **multiply**; when choices are
**alternatives** (this **or** that), you **add**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: "and" multiplies, "or" adds

You already met **sets** — collections of distinct things. Counting principles are about how big the
set of *possible outcomes* becomes when you make a sequence of decisions.

- **Rule of product (AND).** If a first decision has $m$ options and a second *independent* decision has
  $n$ options, then making both has $m \times n$ outcomes. Each of the $m$ first choices opens up a full
  fan of $n$ second choices. Draw it as a tree and the leaves multiply.
- **Rule of sum (OR).** If you must pick exactly one item from one of two *separate* groups (of sizes
  $m$ and $n$ that don't overlap), there are $m + n$ choices in total.

The whole skill is reading a problem and asking: *are these steps combined (and) or alternatives (or)?*

## The formal definition

Suppose a procedure is built from $k$ successive independent steps, where step $i$ can be done in $n_i$
ways. By the **rule of product**, the number of ways to do the whole procedure is

$$
N = n_1 \times n_2 \times \cdots \times n_k = \prod_{i=1}^{k} n_i .
$$

If instead you must choose exactly one outcome from $k$ **disjoint** (non-overlapping) groups of sizes
$n_1, n_2, \dots, n_k$, the **rule of sum** gives

$$
N = n_1 + n_2 + \cdots + n_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} n_i .
$$

<Callout type="why">
Counting is the floor that probability is built on. A probability is often
$\frac{\text{favorable outcomes}}{\text{total outcomes}}$ — and both counts come from exactly these two
rules. In machine learning, the same arithmetic sizes a model's hypothesis space and the number of
possible feature combinations.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Food & dining" icon="🍔">
A build-your-own-burger stall offers 3 buns, 4 patties, and 6 toppings (choose one of each). That is
$3 \times 4 \times 6 = 72$ different burgers — the rule of product. If they *also* sell 5 ready-made
salads as an alternative meal, the menu offers $72 + 5 = 77$ distinct orders (product, then a sum).
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Cybersecurity" icon="🔐">
A 4-digit PIN allows each position to be any digit 0–9, independently. That is
$10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10 = 10^4 = 10{,}000$ possible PINs. Add one more digit and you multiply
by 10 again — which is exactly why longer codes are so much harder to guess.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Genetics" icon="🧬">
DNA is written with 4 bases (A, C, G, T). A **codon** is a sequence of 3 bases, and order matters, so
there are $4 \times 4 \times 4 = 64$ possible codons. Those 64 combinations are more than enough to
encode the 20 amino acids life uses.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Travel" icon="✈️">
To fly from city A to city C through hub B, suppose there are 4 morning flights A→B and 3 afternoon
flights B→C. Each itinerary pairs one of each, so there are $4 \times 3 = 12$ routes. A direct A→C
flight would be a separate alternative you'd *add* on.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The trap is mixing the rules. "How many outfits?" is a product (you wear a top <em>and</em> a bottom).
"How many single garments could I grab?" is a sum (a top <em>or</em> a bottom). Read the connective.
</Callout>
