## The hook: why does the dragon sound like it's *behind* you?

You hear a low rumble. It is quieter in your left ear, and it fades as you sprint away. Without
looking, you *know* the dragon is to your right and far off. Your game never told you "dragon, right,
40 metres" in words — it shaped a **wave of air pressure** so precisely that your brain reconstructed
the scene. Game audio is the craft of turning positions and events in a virtual world into those
waves, thousands of times a second.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Sound is a wave of pressure over time; a computer stores it as a stream of numbers (samples), and
**DSP** — digital signal processing — is just arithmetic on those numbers to mix, shape, and place
the sound in space.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from a wiggling wave to a list of numbers

You already met the **Fourier transform**: any sound is a sum of pure sine waves, each with a
**frequency** (pitch) and **amplitude** (loudness). To store such a wave, the computer measures its
height at evenly spaced instants — this is **sampling**. The number of measurements per second is the
**sample rate**; CD-quality audio uses 44,100 samples per second.

Two knobs control a single tone:

- **Frequency** — how many cycles per second (hertz). Higher frequency, higher pitch.
- **Amplitude** — how tall the wave is. Higher amplitude, louder sound.

To place a sound in 3D, two more effects matter. **Attenuation** makes distant sources quieter, and
**panning** sends more signal to the ear that faces the source. Together they are most of what "3D
audio" means.

## The formal definition

A continuous sound $x(t)$ is sampled at rate $f_s$ to give a sequence $x[n] = x(n / f_s)$. A single
sampled tone is

$$
x[n] = A \, \sin\!\left( 2\pi f \, \frac{n}{f_s} + \varphi \right),
$$

where $A$ is amplitude, $f$ is frequency in hertz, and $\varphi$ is the phase. **Mixing** several
sources is simply adding their samples:

$$
y[n] = \sum_{i} g_i \, x_i[n].
$$

The per-source gain $g_i$ is where 3D audio lives. A common **distance attenuation** model uses an
inverse rolloff from a reference distance $d_0$:

$$
g(d) = \frac{d_0}{\max(d_0,\; d)}.
$$

Stereo **panning** with angle parameter $p \in [-1, 1]$ splits one source into two channels using the
constant-power law:

```python
# one mixed stereo frame from many positioned sources
theta = (pan + 1) * (math.pi / 4)        # 0..pi/2
gain  = ref_dist / max(ref_dist, dist)   # distance attenuation
left  += sample * gain * math.cos(theta) # constant-power pan
right += sample * gain * math.sin(theta)
```

<Callout type="why">
The Fourier view tells you *what* a sound is made of; DSP is how you *act* on it. Filtering out
rumble, reverb in a cave, the muffling of a wall — each is a small arithmetic operation on the sample
stream, applied to thousands of samples per frame.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Rhythm games" icon="🎵">
A rhythm game lives or dies on timing. It schedules notes against the audio clock — the running
**sample count** — not the frame clock, because frames stutter but samples never do. Hit windows are
measured in milliseconds, which at 48 kHz is just a count of samples since the song began.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Open-world survival" icon="🏔️">
A campfire emits a looping crackle. As you walk away its gain follows the rolloff curve, and panning
swings it from your right ear to your left as you circle it. Dozens of such emitters are summed into
one stereo mix every frame — addition of samples, exactly the formula above.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Horror" icon="🕯️">
A whisper is run through a **low-pass filter** when it comes from behind a door: the high frequencies
are attenuated, leaving a muffled hush. That filter is a handful of multiply-adds per sample — DSP
turning geometry (a wall between you and the source) into a felt sense of space.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Mobile" icon="📱">
A phone game can't mix 200 voices — the CPU and battery won't allow it. It uses **voice limiting**:
only the loudest few sources (after attenuation) are mixed; the rest are dropped. The attenuation math
decides *which sounds are worth computing at all*.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Every one of these is the same pipeline: sample the wave, scale by a gain, add the results, ship the
buffer to the speakers — repeated tens of thousands of times per second.
</Callout>
