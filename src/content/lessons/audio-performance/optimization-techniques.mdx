## The hook: how does one frame draw a million blades of grass?

A modern open world fills the screen with grass, trees, rocks and crowds — far more than any GPU could
draw "one object at a time" inside 16.6 ms. The trick is never to do the obvious thing. The fastest
work is the work you **skip**, and the cheapest object is the one you **don't draw in full**. Every
high-end engine is a stack of clever ways to do *less* while looking like *more*.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The way to fit a huge scene inside the frame budget is not to compute faster but to **compute less**:
skip what can't be seen, merge what's similar, and simplify what's far away.
</Callout>

## Intuition: four ways to do less

You learned the **frame budget** and you learned **data-oriented design** — laying data out so the CPU
streams through it cache-friendly. Optimization techniques put that mindset to work on rendering. Four
ideas carry most of the weight:

- **Culling** — don't draw what the camera can't see. Objects outside the view frustum, or hidden
  behind others, are skipped entirely. The cheapest object costs nothing.
- **Batching** — combine many objects that share a material into a single **draw call**, because the
  CPU cost of *telling* the GPU to draw is often higher than the drawing itself.
- **Instancing** — when thousands of objects share one mesh (grass, trees, bullets), send the mesh
  **once** plus a list of positions. One call draws them all.
- **Level of detail (LOD)** — a distant object covers a few pixels, so swap its high-poly mesh for a
  cheap one. Nobody can tell, and the savings are enormous.

The unifying theme is **data-oriented**: store like with like so you can process whole groups at once,
rather than touching scattered objects one at a time.

## The formal definition

Model the CPU cost of a frame as a fixed overhead plus a per-draw-call cost plus per-object shading:

$$
T = T_0 + N_{\text{calls}} \cdot c_{\text{call}} + \sum_{j} c_j .
$$

Each technique attacks a different term. **Culling** shrinks the set of objects, cutting the sum and
the calls. **Instancing** collapses many calls into one, so $N_{\text{calls}}$ drops from "one per
object" to "one per mesh type":

$$
N_{\text{calls}} = M \quad (\text{mesh types}) \;\; \text{instead of} \;\; N \quad (\text{objects}).
$$

**LOD** cuts each far object's shading cost $c_j$ by using a mesh with far fewer triangles. The payoff
is biggest where you'd expect: a scene with many copies of few meshes (a forest) gains enormously from
instancing, while a scene that is mostly off-screen gains from culling.

```python
visible  = [o for o in scene if in_frustum(o, camera)]  # culling
groups   = group_by(visible, key=lambda o: o.mesh)      # batch / instance
for mesh, objs in groups.items():
    lod = choose_lod(mesh, distance(objs, camera))      # LOD
    draw_instanced(lod, [o.transform for o in objs])    # ONE call per group
```

<Callout type="why">
This is data-oriented design paying off. Grouping objects by mesh and storing their transforms
contiguously means the CPU streams through tight arrays and issues one call per group — cache-friendly
and call-light at the same time. The math and the memory layout reinforce each other.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Open-world RPGs" icon="🌳">
A forest is thousands of copies of a dozen tree meshes. <strong>Instancing</strong> draws each species
in one call, and <strong>LOD</strong> turns distant trees into flat billboards. Without both, the view
distance you take for granted would be impossible.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Real-time strategy" icon="⚔️">
An RTS may field thousands of units. <strong>Culling</strong> ignores everyone off the mini-map view,
and units sharing a model are <strong>batched</strong> so the CPU isn't drowned issuing one draw call
per soldier — the bottleneck in big battles is call count, not pixels.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Mobile" icon="📱">
Phone GPUs are tiny and power-limited, so doing less is survival. Aggressive <strong>LOD</strong> and
texture atlases (a form of batching) keep draw calls low; many mobile engines cap calls per frame as a
hard rule, because each one costs precious battery.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Racing" icon="🏎️">
At 200 km/h the roadside whips past, so far scenery can be brutally simplified — players never focus on
it. <strong>LOD</strong> plus <strong>occlusion culling</strong> (skipping what's hidden behind the car
and barriers) keeps the frame locked at 60 while the track stays sharp where the eye actually looks.
</ExampleCard>
