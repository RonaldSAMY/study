## The hook: who are your customers, really?

You run an online store. Nobody labeled your shoppers — there is no column that says "bargain hunter"
or "premium loyalist." Yet plotting spending against visit frequency, you *see* clumps. Some buyers
sit firmly in one clump; others hover on a boundary, half one type and half another. You want to
discover these groups **and** express that in-between feeling honestly, rather than forcing every
person into a single box.

That honest, soft grouping is what a **Gaussian Mixture Model** delivers — and it is our first taste
of *unsupervised* learning, where the data has no labels at all.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A Gaussian Mixture Model assumes your data was produced by a few overlapping bell-shaped clusters, and
it learns each cluster's center, shape, and weight — giving every point a **probability** of belonging
to each group rather than a hard label.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a recipe with hidden choices

Picture how each data point was *born*. First nature secretly rolls a weighted die to pick a cluster
$k$ (cluster $k$ comes up with probability $\pi_k$). Then it draws the point from that cluster's
**Gaussian** — the bell curve you met in continuous distributions, now in 2D with a center
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ and a spread described by a covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$.

We see the points but **not** the die rolls. Those hidden choices are *latent variables*. The whole
challenge is a chicken-and-egg loop:

- If we knew which cluster each point came from, estimating each Gaussian would be easy (just average
  its points).
- If we knew the Gaussians, guessing each point's cluster would be easy (whichever bell is tallest
  there).

We know neither. The **EM algorithm** breaks the loop by alternating: guess the assignments, update
the Gaussians, repeat — and it provably never makes things worse.

Three blobs of customers, three Gaussians that start badly placed. Press **E-step**, then **M-step**,
and repeat (or use **E + M**). Watch the points recolor as they share themselves between clusters, and
the ellipses glide into place.

## The formal definition

A GMM models the probability of a point $\mathbf{x}$ as a weighted sum of $K$ Gaussian densities:

$$
p(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k), \qquad \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k = 1, \quad \pi_k \ge 0.
$$

The **mixing weights** $\pi_k$ are how often each cluster is chosen; each $\mathcal{N}$ is one bell.
We fit the parameters by maximizing the (log-)likelihood of the data — the same MLE principle from
estimation, now with a sum *inside* the log that has no closed-form solution. EM solves it by
iterating two steps.

**E-step (Expectation).** Given the current parameters, compute the **responsibility** of cluster $k$
for point $\mathbf{x}_n$ — the posterior probability it came from that cluster:

$$
r_{nk} = \frac{\pi_k \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} \pi_j \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_j, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j)}.
$$

**M-step (Maximization).** Treat those soft responsibilities as fractional memberships and re-estimate
each Gaussian. With $N_k = \sum_{n} r_{nk}$ the effective number of points in cluster $k$:

$$
\boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \frac{1}{N_k} \sum_{n=1}^{N} r_{nk}\, \mathbf{x}_n, \qquad
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k = \frac{1}{N_k} \sum_{n=1}^{N} r_{nk}\, (\mathbf{x}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k)(\mathbf{x}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k)^{\top},
$$

$$
\pi_k = \frac{N_k}{N}.
$$

Alternate E and M until the log-likelihood stops climbing. Each round can only **increase** (or hold)
the likelihood, so EM is guaranteed to converge — though to a *local* optimum, which is why the
starting guess and re-runs matter.

<Callout type="why">
GMMs are the soft cousin of $k$-means. In fact $k$-means is the special case where you force each
responsibility to 0 or 1 and make every Gaussian a perfect circle. The mixture model keeps the
probabilities, so it can capture stretched, tilted, overlapping clusters — which is what real data
actually looks like.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Customer segmentation" icon="🛍️">
Plot each shopper by spend and frequency, fit a GMM, and out fall groups like "occasional splurgers"
and "frequent savers." A shopper on the edge gets, say, 0.6 to one segment and 0.4 to another — so
marketing can target the *blend* instead of pretending everyone is one type.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Astronomy" icon="🔭">
A telescope survey records stars' colors and brightness. Different stellar populations form
overlapping clouds in that space. A GMM separates them softly, and a star straddling two clouds keeps a
fair probability for each — exactly the uncertainty astronomers want to preserve.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Speech & audio" icon="🎙️">
Classic speaker-recognition systems modeled each voice as a mixture of Gaussians over sound features.
A new clip is scored under every speaker's GMM, and the highest density wins. The mixture captures that
one voice produces many different sounds.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Anomaly detection" icon="🚨">
Fit a GMM to normal sensor readings from a machine. Because the model gives a *density* everywhere, a
new reading that lands where every bell is tiny — low $p(\mathbf{x})$ — is flagged as an anomaly. The
mixture defines "normal" as a smooth landscape, not a hard boundary.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The recurring power is the word <strong>soft</strong>: GMMs hand back probabilities, not verdicts. That
lets the model say "I am 70% sure" — priceless when the cost of a confident mistake is high.
</Callout>
