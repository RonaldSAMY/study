## The hook: why does your shot "miss" a player who's already dead?

You line up the perfect headshot, click, and... nothing. A heartbeat later the enemy crumples, or worse, the
game decides you missed. The bullet was never the problem. The problem is that your computer and the game
server are separated by hundreds of kilometres of cable, and information takes *time* to cross it. Online
games live or die by how they tame that delay. Before we can hide latency, we have to understand the raw
materials: **bits grouped into packets**, the two ways to ship them (**TCP** and **UDP**), and the timing
numbers — **latency, jitter, and bandwidth** — that describe the pipe between you and everyone else.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The network breaks your data into small **packets** and ships them across a wire that has a **speed limit**
(bandwidth), a **travel time** (latency), and an annoying habit of being **inconsistent** (jitter) and
sometimes **losing** packets entirely.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from bits to packets to a felt experience

You already know that everything is **bits grouped into bytes**. A network doesn't send your game state as
one giant blob — it chops it into **packets**: small, self-contained chunks (often a few hundred to ~1500
bytes) each wearing a header that says where it came from and where it's going, like an envelope around a
short letter.

Three numbers describe the journey of those envelopes:

- **Latency** — how long *one* packet takes to travel one way. The round trip (there and back) is the **RTT**.
- **Bandwidth** — how *many* bits the wire can carry per second. Think width of the pipe, not speed of a drop.
- **Jitter** — how much the latency *wobbles* from packet to packet. Steady 80 ms feels far better than a
  chaotic 40–160 ms.

And then there's **packet loss**: envelopes that simply never arrive. How you react to loss is the whole
difference between the two transport protocols below.

## Play with it

Send packets across a link. Drag the **latency** up and watch every packet take longer to cross. Crank the
**loss** and switch between **UDP** (fire-and-forget — lost packets are just gone) and **TCP** (lost packets
are detected and **resent**, in amber). Notice the trade: TCP delivers everything, but a resend costs you an
extra round trip.

## The formal definition

A packet's total one-way delay is a sum of pieces. The two that matter most for games are the fixed
**propagation delay** (distance over signal speed) plus the **transmission delay** (size over bandwidth):

$$
t_{\text{latency}} = \underbrace{\frac{d}{s}}_{\text{propagation}} + \underbrace{\frac{B}{R}}_{\text{transmission}} + t_{\text{queue}}
$$

where $d$ is distance, $s$ the signal speed in the medium, $B$ the packet size in bits, and $R$ the link rate
(bandwidth) in bits per second. The round-trip time is

$$
\text{RTT} \approx 2 \, t_{\text{latency}}.
$$

**Jitter** is the variability of that latency — concretely, the standard deviation (or mean absolute change)
of successive one-way delays:

$$
J = \operatorname{stddev}(t_1, t_2, \dots, t_n).
$$

A handy sanity check is the **bandwidth-delay product**, the amount of data "in flight" on the wire at once:

$$
\text{BDP} = R \times \text{RTT}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
Games almost always pick **UDP**. A late packet in a shooter is *useless* — by the time TCP resends a 30 ms-old
position, the world has moved on. UDP lets the game say "drop it, I'll send a fresher update next tick."
Reliability, when needed (chat, loot pickups), is rebuilt *selectively* on top of UDP rather than forced on
everything.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Competitive FPS" icon="🎯">
At 64-tick, a server updates the world about every 16 ms. If your RTT is 60 ms, your click is seen ~30 ms
late and the reply takes ~30 ms more. That gap is why the next lessons exist: prediction and lag
compensation paper over an RTT you can never fully remove.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Voice chat (VoIP)" icon="🎙️">
A teammate's voice is split into tiny audio packets sent over UDP. Lose one and you hear a brief glitch —
acceptable. *Resend* it and you'd hear that syllable arrive a full RTT late, after the conversation moved on.
Freshness beats completeness, the same reason games avoid TCP.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Mobile gaming on the move" icon="📱">
On a train switching cell towers, your bandwidth is fine but **jitter** spikes wildly — 40 ms one moment,
200 ms the next. Steady high latency is playable; unpredictable latency makes prediction guess wrong, which
is why mobile titles buffer aggressively.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Live sports streaming" icon="📺">
A stream cares about **bandwidth** (enough bits per second for 4K) and tolerates latency by buffering several
seconds ahead. A game is the opposite: tiny bandwidth, but every millisecond of latency is felt. Same wire,
opposite priorities.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Bandwidth and latency are independent. A fat 1 Gbps satellite link can still have a 600 ms RTT — a very
*wide* pipe that is also very *long*. Buying a faster plan rarely lowers your ping.
</Callout>
