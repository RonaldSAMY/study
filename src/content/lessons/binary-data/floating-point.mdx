## The hook: the wall you can almost walk through

In countless games, a player who walks slowly into a wall sometimes jitters, clips, or sinks a hair into
it. A big reason is that the position `x = 10.0` a game *thinks* it stored is really `10.000000476...`.
Integers from the last lessons can't hold a position like 10.3 or a velocity of −0.5 at all. For that we
need **floating-point** numbers — and they trade exactness for enormous range. Understanding that trade
explains physics glitches, money-rounding bugs, and why training a neural net uses "float32."

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **floating-point** number is binary **scientific notation**: a sign, a fraction (the *mantissa*), and an
*exponent* that slides the binary point — giving huge range at the cost of tiny rounding errors.
</Callout>

## Intuition: scientific notation, in binary

You already know scientific notation: $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ is a fraction (6.022) times a power of ten.
Floating-point is the exact same idea in **base 2**. A number is split into three parts packed into bits:

1. **Sign** (1 bit) — positive or negative, just like the sign bit from two's complement.
2. **Exponent** — *where* the binary point floats. This is what gives floats their gigantic range, from
   subatomic to astronomical.
3. **Mantissa** (also called the *fraction* or *significand*) — the actual digits, the precision. More
   mantissa bits means more decimal places of accuracy.

The name "floating" point is literal: the exponent lets the point slide, so the same handful of bits can
represent 0.00001 or 1,000,000. The catch is that the mantissa has a **fixed** number of bits. Just as you
can't write one-third exactly in decimal (0.3333...), most everyday decimals can't be written exactly in
binary — so the computer stores the **nearest** value it can and the rest is rounding error.

float, just small enough to see. Slide each part and watch the value. Notice how nudging the
**exponent** scales the number by powers of two, while the **mantissa** fine-tunes in between.

## The formal definition

A normalized floating-point value is built from a sign $s$, an exponent field $e$ with a fixed **bias**,
and a mantissa fraction $m$:

$$
x = (-1)^{s} \times 2^{\,e - \text{bias}} \times \left(1 + \frac{m}{2^{p}}\right),
$$

where $p$ is the number of mantissa bits. The leading $1$ is "free" (always assumed), which buys one extra
bit of precision. The **IEEE-754** standard fixes the field sizes:

$$
\textbf{float32:}\ 1 + 8 + 23 \text{ bits}, \qquad \textbf{float64:}\ 1 + 11 + 52 \text{ bits}.
$$

The decimal 0.1 has no exact finite binary form (it repeats forever in base 2), so it is rounded. The same
for 0.2. Add the two rounded values and the small errors survive:

```python
print(0.1 + 0.2)          # 0.30000000000000004
print(0.1 + 0.2 == 0.3)   # False
```

<Callout type="why">
Neural networks store weights and activations as float32 (or even 16-bit floats) precisely because of this
trade: floats give the gigantic dynamic range training needs, and a little rounding error per number is
acceptable. Knowing floats are <em>approximate</em> is why you compare them with a tolerance, never with ==.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Game physics" icon="🎮">
A character's position is three floats (x, y, z). Far from the world origin, the gaps between
representable floats grow, so objects visibly <em>jitter</em>. Big open-world games "re-center" the world
around the player to keep coordinates small — a direct workaround for floating-point precision loss.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Money & banking" icon="💵">
Never store currency as a float: 0.10 + 0.20 not landing on 0.30 means cents quietly vanish or appear.
Banks store amounts as <strong>integers</strong> (counts of cents) instead, sidestepping the rounding
problem entirely. The float bug is a real accounting hazard.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Space navigation" icon="🛰️">
A probe must represent both tiny thruster nudges and distances of billions of kilometers. Floating-point's
exponent gives that range from one compact format — the same reason scientists write 6.022 × 10²³ instead
of a number with 24 digits.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Audio synthesis" icon="🎚️">
Digital audio samples are often floats between −1.0 and +1.0. The mantissa's precision sets how finely a
waveform's amplitude is captured; too few bits and you hear quantization "hiss." More mantissa bits buy a
cleaner signal — precision you can literally hear.
</ExampleCard>
