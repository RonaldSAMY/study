## The hook: what is a saved game made of?

When you hit *"Save"* in a game, your hero's health, position, level, and inventory all get written to
a file on disk. But that disk has no idea what "health" or "sword" means. It only knows how to store
one thing, billions of times over: a tiny switch that is either **on** or **off**. Every character,
every color, every score in every game you have ever played is built from nothing but these switches.
That switch is the **bit**, and learning to think in bits is the foundation of understanding computers.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **bit** is a single on/off switch (1 or 0); group **8 bits** together and you get a **byte**, which
can represent 256 different values — enough for a number, a letter, or one color channel.
</Callout>

## Intuition: counting with switches

A single bit isn't very exciting — it holds one of two states, like a light switch. The magic happens
when you line several up. With 2 bits you can make 4 combinations: `00`, `01`, `10`, `11`. Add a third
bit and you double again to 8 combinations. Each bit you add **doubles** the number of things you can
represent.

That doubling is why you keep seeing the same numbers in computing: 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256.
They are the **powers of two**. A group of 8 bits — a **byte** — gives $2^8 = 256$ possibilities,
which we usually read as the whole numbers from 0 up to 255.

Inside a byte, each bit has a **place value**, just like the columns in ordinary decimal numbers. The
rightmost bit is worth 1, the next is worth 2, then 4, 8, and so on up to 128 on the far left. To find
the byte's value, you switch on the bits you want and add up their place values.

## The formal definition

A **bit** holds one binary digit: $b \in \{0, 1\}$. A **byte** is an ordered group of 8 bits. Reading
those bits as a number, each bit $b_i$ is multiplied by its place value $2^i$:

$$
\text{value} = \sum_{i=0}^{7} b_i \cdot 2^i = b_7 \cdot 128 + b_6 \cdot 64 + \cdots + b_1 \cdot 2 + b_0 \cdot 1.
$$

With $n$ bits you can represent $2^n$ distinct values, spanning $0$ to $2^n - 1$:

$$
n = 8 \;\Rightarrow\; 2^8 = 256 \text{ values}, \quad \text{range } 0 \text{ to } 255.
$$

Larger units are just stacks of bytes. Historically these used powers of two, and the precise (binary)
units make that explicit:

$$
1\ \text{KiB} = 2^{10}\ \text{bytes} = 1024, \qquad 1\ \text{MiB} = 2^{20}\ \text{bytes} = 1{,}048{,}576.
$$

```text
1 bit   = one on/off switch
8 bits  = 1 byte        (0 .. 255)
1024 B  = 1 KiB
1024 KiB= 1 MiB
```

<Callout type="why">
Everything a computer touches — a model's weights, a texture, a chat message — is ultimately a pile of
bytes. Knowing that a byte tops out at 255 explains a thousand real bugs: color channels that wrap, save
counters that reset, and characters that turn into garbage.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Game inventory" icon="🎒">
A character's status effects — *poisoned, shielded, invisible, on-fire* — are often packed into a single
byte as a **bit flag**: one bit per effect. Bit on means "active." One byte tracks up to 8 effects at
once, and checking "is the player poisoned?" is just reading one bit. Compact and lightning fast.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Pixel color" icon="🎨">
A standard pixel stores red, green, and blue as **one byte each** (0–255). That is why color pickers go
up to 255: pure red is 255 for red and 0 for the others. Three bytes (24 bits) give over 16 million
colors — the "true color" you see on screen.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="File sizes" icon="💾">
A retro sprite sheet might be 64 KiB; a modern game install is tens of GiB. Each step up the ladder —
byte, kilobyte, megabyte, gigabyte — multiplies by about 1024. When a download "stalls at 99%," it is
still shuffling the last few thousand bytes into place.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Networking" icon="📡">
Internet speeds are measured in **bits** per second (Mbps), but files are measured in **bytes**. Because
1 byte = 8 bits, a "100 Mbps" connection downloads only about 12.5 megabytes per second. Mixing up bits
and bytes is the classic reason a download feels 8× slower than the advertised speed.
</ExampleCard>
