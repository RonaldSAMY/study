## The hook: the number after the poll

"Candidate Reyes leads with **54%**, *plus or minus 3 points*." That little "± 3" is doing heavy lifting.
A single poll surveyed maybe 1,000 of millions of voters; the 54% is an estimate that would wobble if you
re-ran the poll tomorrow. The margin of error is an admission: *we are not sure of the exact number, but
we are fairly sure the truth lives in this band.*

In the last lesson we produced a single best estimate. But a point with no uncertainty is dangerous — it
hides how much it could be wrong. A **confidence interval** attaches honesty to the estimate.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **confidence interval** is a range built from your sample that, if you repeated the whole study many
times, would contain the true value about 95% of the time.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the interval is the random thing

Here is the mental flip that trips everyone up. The true value (Reyes's real support) is a **fixed
number** — it isn't random. What *is* random is your **sample**, and therefore the interval you compute
from it. Each new poll gives a slightly different center and a slightly different band.

So "95% confidence" is **not** "there's a 95% chance the truth is in *this* interval." The truth either is
or isn't in your one interval. Rather, it's a statement about the **procedure**: the recipe you used
produces intervals that capture the truth 95% of the time across many repetitions.

Why 95%, and why does it work? Because of the **Central Limit Theorem** from the probability track: sample
means and proportions are approximately **normally distributed** around the truth, and 95% of a normal
distribution lies within about $1.96$ standard errors of its center. That fixed multiplier is where the
band's width comes from.

<Callout type="tip">
Want a tighter interval? Collect more data. The width shrinks like $1/\sqrt{n}$ — so to **halve** your
margin of error you need **four times** the sample size. Precision is expensive.
</Callout>

## The formal definition

Suppose we estimate a quantity with a statistic $\hat{\theta}$ whose **standard error** (its standard
deviation across samples) is $\text{SE}$. By the CLT, $\hat{\theta}$ is approximately normal around the
true $\theta$, so a confidence interval at level $1-\alpha$ is

$$
\hat{\theta} \;\pm\; z_{\alpha/2}\,\cdot\,\text{SE},
$$

where $z_{\alpha/2}$ is the normal critical value — $z_{0.025} = 1.96$ for 95% confidence. For a
proportion $\hat{p}$ from $n$ observations the standard error is

$$
\text{SE} = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}\,(1-\hat{p})}{n}}, \qquad \text{CI}_{95\%} = \hat{p} \pm 1.96\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}.
$$

The interpretation is the subtle part:

$$
P\big(\,\text{the random interval covers } \theta\,\big) = 0.95,\quad\text{not}\quad P(\theta \in [\text{this interval}]) = 0.95.
$$

<Callout type="why">
In machine learning you never report a single accuracy number from one test set without a confidence
interval — a model scoring 91% on 100 examples versus 91% on 100,000 examples carry very different
certainty. Confidence intervals are how you tell whether a leaderboard improvement is real or noise.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Election polling" icon="🗳️">
The "margin of error" printed beside every poll *is* a 95% confidence interval half-width. A poll of 1,000
voters has a margin around ±3 points; that's why a 51%–49% "lead" is often called a statistical tie — the
intervals overlap heavily.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Clinical trials" icon="💊">
A drug lowers blood pressure by "8 mmHg, 95% CI [5, 11]." Because the entire interval sits above zero,
regulators can be confident the effect is real and positive. If the interval were [−1, 17], the same point
estimate would be far less convincing.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Quality control" icon="🏭">
A factory samples 200 chips and finds a 2% defect rate with a 95% CI of [0.4%, 3.6%]. If the contract
demands "under 5% defects," the whole interval clears the bar — strong evidence the batch is acceptable
without inspecting all 50,000 chips.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Crop science" icon="🌾">
A new seed variety yields "0.6 t/ha more, 95% CI [0.1, 1.1]." The lower bound is just above zero, so the
gain is statistically supported but possibly small — telling a farmer both the *best guess* and the
*plausible worst case* for the season.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
A confidence interval reports three things at once: the **estimate** (center), the **uncertainty**
(width), and a **plausible range**. Whether the interval excludes a meaningful value — like zero — is often
the whole point, which leads straight to hypothesis testing.
</Callout>
