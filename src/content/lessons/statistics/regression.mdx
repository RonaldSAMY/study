## The hook: how much yield per bag of fertilizer?

A farmer plots ten fields: on the x-axis how much fertilizer each got, on the y-axis the harvest. The dots
trend upward but they scatter — weather, soil, luck. She wants one clean line through the cloud so she can
answer "*if I add one more bag, how many extra tonnes can I expect?*" and predict next year's yield.

That line is **regression**. It is the moment statistics stops summarizing and starts **predicting**: turn
a relationship between two quantities into a formula you can extrapolate.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Regression finds the line (or curve) that comes **closest** to all the data at once — closest meaning the
**sum of squared vertical gaps** is as small as possible.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the line that disappoints everyone equally

No straight line can pass through a scattered cloud of points. Every line leaves each point a little
above or below it — that leftover gap is a **residual**. A good fit is the line that keeps the residuals
*collectively* small.

But small in what sense? We square each residual before adding them up, for two reasons: squaring makes
every gap positive (so misses above and below don't cancel), and it **punishes big misses far more than
small ones** — a point twice as far off contributes four times the penalty. So least-squares regression
chases the line that avoids large errors above all.

Now connect it to the calculus you know. The total squared error is a function of the line's slope and
intercept. Minimizing it is exactly **finding where the derivative is zero** — the optimization move from
*Applications of Derivatives*. Set the partial derivatives with respect to slope and intercept to zero and
solve; out pops a unique best line.

## The formal definition

We model the relationship as a line with noise,

$$
y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \varepsilon_i,
$$

and choose $\beta_0$ (intercept) and $\beta_1$ (slope) to minimize the **sum of squared residuals**:

$$
\text{SSE}(\beta_0, \beta_1) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \big(y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i\big)^2.
$$

Setting both partial derivatives to zero, $\partial \text{SSE}/\partial \beta_0 = 0$ and $\partial
\text{SSE}/\partial \beta_1 = 0$, and solving gives the closed-form **least-squares estimates**:

$$
\hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}, \qquad \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}.
$$

The slope is the **covariance of $x$ and $y$ over the variance of $x$** — the relationship you saw in the
probability track, now doing predictive work. Quality of fit is summarized by $R^2$, the fraction of the
variance in $y$ the line explains.

<Callout type="why">
Linear regression is the "hello world" of machine learning — and its loss function, the mean squared
error, is the template for nearly all of supervised learning. Swap the line for a neural network and the
squared-error idea is unchanged; only the way we minimize it has to change.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Crop science" icon="🌾">
Regressing yield on fertilizer gives a slope of, say, 0.7 t/ha per bag — a direct, actionable prediction.
But extrapolate too far and the model lies: real yield curves bend and eventually fall as over-fertilizing
poisons the soil. Lines are local truths.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Salary data" icon="💼">
HR fits salary against years of experience to spot pay anomalies. An employee sitting far **below** the
regression line — a large negative residual — may be underpaid for their experience, flagging a fairness
review. The residuals, not the line, are the story.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Real estate" icon="🏠">
Zillow-style price models regress sale price on square footage, bedrooms, and location at once — that's
**multiple regression**, the same least-squares idea with several slopes. The fitted coefficients say how
many dollars each extra bedroom is worth, holding the rest fixed.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports analytics" icon="🏀">
A team regresses wins on payroll to ask whether spending buys victories. A near-flat slope with a small
$R^2$ would say money explains little — the scatter around the line is where the real competitive story
(coaching, chemistry, luck) lives.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The recurring caution: regression captures **association, not causation**, and only over the range of data
you have. A great fit on past data can still mislead if you push it beyond where the line was learned.
</Callout>
