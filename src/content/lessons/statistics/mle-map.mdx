## The hook: what's the *real* click rate?

You launch a new "Buy now" button and watch the first 20 visitors: **7 of them click**. So... what is the
button's true click-through rate? "35%," you say — and you're right, but *why* is 35% the best answer and
not 30% or 40%? What makes one guess better than another when the truth is hidden?

Descriptive statistics summarized the data we *saw*. **Estimation** takes the next step: it reaches past
the sample to name the hidden **parameter** of the process that generated it. The guiding principle is
beautifully simple.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The best estimate of a parameter is the value that makes the **data you actually observed** as likely as
possible — that's **Maximum Likelihood Estimation**. **MAP** adds a prior belief to the mix.
</Callout>

## Intuition: run the world backwards

A probability model runs *forwards*: given a parameter $p$ (the true click rate), it tells you how likely
any particular outcome is. Estimation runs it *backwards*: given the outcome you saw, which $p$ would have
made that outcome least surprising?

Try every candidate $p$ and score it by **how probable our 7-out-of-20 result is under that $p$**:

- If $p = 0.05$, seeing 7 clicks would be a freak event — low score.
- If $p = 0.9$, seeing *only* 7 clicks would also be strange — low score.
- Somewhere in between is a $p$ that makes 7-out-of-20 most ordinary. That peak is the **MLE**.

This "score as a function of the parameter" is the **likelihood**. We slide the parameter until the
likelihood is maximized — hence *maximum likelihood*.

## Play with it

The data is fixed: **7 conversions out of 20 visitors**. Slide the parameter $p$ and watch the likelihood
of *that* data rise and fall. Find the peak — it lands exactly at $7/20 = 0.35$. Then toggle **add a
prior**: a skeptical prior (we doubt rates are that high) shifts the best estimate to the **MAP**, a
compromise between the data and the belief.

<Callout type="tip">
With lots of data the likelihood curve grows tall and narrow, so the prior barely nudges it — MAP and MLE
nearly agree. With little data the curve is flat and the prior does real steering. <strong>More data,
weaker prior.</strong>
</Callout>

## The formal definition

For independent observations $x_1, \dots, x_n$ from a model with parameter $\theta$, the **likelihood** is
the probability of the data viewed as a function of $\theta$:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} p(x_i \mid \theta).
$$

The **maximum likelihood estimate** is the maximizer

$$
\hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}} = \arg\max_{\theta}\, \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \arg\max_{\theta}\, \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log p(x_i \mid \theta),
$$

where we take the **log** because it turns the fragile product into a friendly sum (and the maximizer is
unchanged). For our button, with $k$ clicks in $n$ visitors, this gives the satisfying $\hat{p} = k/n$.

**MAP** estimation multiplies in a **prior** $p(\theta)$ and maximizes the posterior $p(\theta \mid x) \propto
\mathcal{L}(\theta)\,p(\theta)$:

$$
\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_{\theta}\, \Big[ \log \mathcal{L}(\theta) + \log p(\theta) \Big].
$$

<Callout type="why">
This *is* how models train. Fitting a neural network by minimizing **cross-entropy loss** is exactly
maximum likelihood in disguise (minimizing negative log-likelihood). Adding **L2 weight regularization**
is exactly MAP with a Gaussian prior on the weights. Estimation is the engine under the hood of learning.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="A/B testing" icon="🧪">
You compare two checkout pages. Each conversion count is binomial data, and the MLE click rate is simply
$k/n$ for each page. The whole experiment is two likelihood peaks — and deciding whether they truly differ
is the hypothesis test two lessons from now.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Epidemiology" icon="🦠">
To estimate a disease's transmission rate, scientists pick the parameter under which the observed case
curve is most probable — maximum likelihood. Early in an outbreak, data is scarce, so a **prior** from past
epidemics (MAP) stabilizes the estimate until more cases arrive.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Manufacturing" icon="🏭">
A new machine produces 3 defects in a batch of 500. The MLE defect rate is $3/500 = 0.6\%$. But the
factory has decades of history saying rates hover near $1\%$; folding that in as a prior gives a MAP
estimate that won't overreact to one lucky batch.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports analytics" icon="🏀">
A rookie sinks 4 of his first 5 free throws. Pure MLE says he's an 80% shooter — absurd from 5 attempts.
A MAP estimate with a prior centered on the league average (~75%) pulls that wild number back toward
reality. This shrinkage is exactly why analysts trust priors with small samples.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The recurring lesson: **MLE listens only to the data; MAP also listens to prior knowledge.** When data is
plentiful they converge. When data is scarce, a good prior is the difference between a sensible estimate
and a ridiculous one.
</Callout>
