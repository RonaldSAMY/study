## The hook: did the new page actually win?

Your website's old checkout page converts at 10%. You redesign it, run an A/B test, and the new version
converts at 12%. Champagne? Not yet. With only a few hundred visitors, **random luck alone** could produce
a 12% just by the toss of the coin. Before you ship the change, you need to answer one question: *is this
gap too big to be a fluke?*

Hypothesis testing is the disciplined way to answer it. It assumes "nothing changed" and then checks
whether your data is **too surprising** for that assumption to survive.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Assume there is **no effect**, then ask: *if that were true, how often would I see data at least this
extreme by pure chance?* If the answer is "rarely," the no-effect story is implausible.
</Callout>

## Intuition: innocent until proven surprising

A hypothesis test is a courtroom for ideas. It starts from a presumption of innocence:

- The **null hypothesis** $H_0$ — the boring default: *no difference, no effect, nothing's going on.*
- The **alternative** $H_1$ — what you suspect: *the new page really is better.*

We give the null every benefit of the doubt and ask: *if the null were true, what range of results would
chance alone produce?* That range is the **sampling distribution under the null** — a bell curve (thanks
again to the Central Limit Theorem) centered on "no effect."

Then we drop our actual observed result onto that curve. If it lands in the fat middle, it's an ordinary
fluctuation — the null survives. If it lands far out in a thin tail, then *either* an extraordinary
coincidence happened *or* the null is wrong. The **p-value** measures exactly how far out we are: the
probability, under the null, of a result at least this extreme.

<Callout type="warning">
A small p-value does <strong>not</strong> mean the effect is large or important — only that it's unlikely
to be pure chance. With a huge sample, a trivial 0.1% lift can be "statistically significant" yet
commercially worthless. Always read the <strong>effect size</strong> alongside the p-value.
</Callout>

## The formal definition

We compute a **test statistic** that measures how far the data sits from the null, in units of standard
error. For comparing an observed proportion $\hat{p}$ to a null value $p_0$:

$$
z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}.
$$

The **p-value** is the probability, *assuming $H_0$*, of a statistic at least as extreme as the one
observed:

$$
p = P\big(\,|Z| \ge |z| \;\big|\; H_0\,\big) \quad\text{(two-tailed)}.
$$

We fix a **significance level** $\alpha$ (usually $0.05$) in advance and decide:

$$
p \le \alpha \;\Rightarrow\; \text{reject } H_0, \qquad p > \alpha \;\Rightarrow\; \text{fail to reject } H_0.
$$

Two ways to be wrong: a **Type I error** (false positive — reject a true null, rate $\alpha$) and a
**Type II error** (false negative — miss a real effect, rate $\beta$). Lowering one tends to raise the
other.

<Callout type="why">
Every claim that a new model "beats the baseline" is a hypothesis test in spirit. Is the accuracy gain
real or did it ride one lucky test split? The same logic guards against fooling yourself — the single most
important skill in empirical machine learning.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="A/B testing" icon="🖱️">
The null is "both checkout pages convert equally." You compute a $z$ from the two conversion counts; if its
p-value drops below 0.05 you ship the new page. Run enough tests, though, and some will look "significant"
by chance — which is why teams correct for multiple comparisons.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Clinical trials" icon="💊">
The null is "the drug works no better than placebo." A small p-value is the evidence regulators demand
before approval. Here a **Type I error** approves a useless drug and a **Type II error** buries a working
one — so trials are sized to keep both rates low.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Quality control" icon="🏭">
The null is "this machine is still calibrated to 500 g." Each shift, a sample of boxes yields a $z$; a
significant result triggers a halt-and-inspect. A two-tailed test is right here — overfilling and
underfilling are both faults.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports analytics" icon="🎾">
A coach claims a new serve technique boosts ace rate. The null says "no change." With only 40 serves the
test may lack the **power** to detect a real improvement — a Type II error — reminding us that "not
significant" means *not enough evidence*, not *proven false*.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
A confidence interval and a hypothesis test are two views of one idea: if the 95% CI for an effect
**excludes** the null value, the two-tailed test rejects $H_0$ at $\alpha = 0.05$. Same evidence, two
languages.
</Callout>
