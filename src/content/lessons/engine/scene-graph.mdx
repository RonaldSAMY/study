## The hook: the sword that must follow the hand

Your hero swings a sword. The blade has to ride in the hand, the hand rides on the
forearm, the forearm swings from the elbow, and the whole arm pivots at the shoulder —
which itself sits on a body that is running across the map. If you tried to track the
sword's screen position with one giant equation, every animation frame would be a
nightmare of bookkeeping. Game engines refuse to do that. Instead they arrange the world
as a **tree** and let each part describe its motion *relative to its parent*. Move the
shoulder and the sword comes along for free. That tree is called a **scene graph**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Give every object a **local** transform measured relative to its parent, then a child's
place in the world is just its parent's world transform **composed with** its own local
one — so moving a parent automatically carries all of its children.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a tree of relative motions

You already met two ideas that this lesson simply clicks together. From
**matrices &amp; transforms** you know that a translate, rotate, or scale can be packed into
a single matrix, and that applying one transform *after* another is the same as
**multiplying** their matrices. From **trees** you know how to model "things inside things":
a node with a parent and some children.

A scene graph is exactly that tree, where every node carries one **local transform** — its
position, rotation, and scale *as seen by its parent*. The shoulder doesn't need to know
where it is on the map; it only knows "I am rotated 30 degrees from the body." To find
where anything truly is, you walk from the root down to the node, multiplying the local
transforms together as you go. Nothing new — it is trees plus matrix multiplication.

## The formal definition

Give each node a **local transform** built from the familiar pieces. In 2D homogeneous
coordinates a rotate-then-translate local matrix looks like this:

$$
M_{local} =
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta & -\sin\theta & t_x \\
\sin\theta & \cos\theta & t_y \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

A general local transform is the product of translate, rotate, and scale,
$M_{local} = T \cdot R \cdot S$. The key rule of a scene graph connects a node to its
parent:

$$
M_{world} = M_{parent}^{world} \cdot M_{local}.
$$

The root has no parent, so its world transform is just its own local one. Every other node
multiplies its parent's already-computed world matrix by its local matrix. Expanding the
chain for a node $n$ levels deep gives a product running from the root downward:

$$
M_{world} = M_{0} \cdot M_{1} \cdot M_{2} \cdots M_{n}.
$$

Because matrix multiplication is **not commutative**, order matters: parent on the left,
child on the right. You compute it with one pass down the tree, reusing each parent's
result for its children:

```js
function computeWorld(node, parentWorld) {
  node.world = multiply(parentWorld, node.local);
  for (const child of node.children) {
    computeWorld(child, node.world);
  }
}
```

<Callout type="why">
This is why moving a parent is free: you never touch the children's local data, yet their
world matrices change the next frame because each one is rebuilt from the parent's new
world matrix. One edit at the top ripples correctly to the whole subtree.
</Callout>

## The same idea, in four different worlds

The parent-times-local rule is everywhere objects live inside moving objects.

<ExampleCard domain="Astronomy sim" icon="🪐">
A moon orbits a planet that orbits a sun. The moon's local transform only says "circle my
planet"; the planet's only says "circle the sun." Multiply up the chain and the moon
traces its real looping path through space — without anyone ever writing that path down.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Puppetry" icon="🎭">
A marionette is a scene graph you can hold. The control bar is the root; strings lead to
the head, arms, and legs as children. Tilt the bar (the parent) and every limb swings
together; twitch a single string and only that one body part moves. Same math, made of
wood and thread.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Construction vehicle" icon="🏗️">
An excavator's cab rotates on its tracks, the boom pivots on the cab, the stick pivots on
the boom, and the bucket pivots on the stick. Each operator lever changes one local angle;
the bucket's world position falls out of the composed transforms down the whole arm.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Game UI" icon="🖼️">
A heads-up display nests panels: a window holds a row, the row holds buttons, a button
holds its icon and label. Slide the parent window and every nested child slides with it,
because each child is positioned relative to its parent — a scene graph for 2D interfaces.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Different materials, identical math. Whether it is moons, marionettes, or menus, "world
equals parent times local" is the single rule doing all the work.
</Callout>
