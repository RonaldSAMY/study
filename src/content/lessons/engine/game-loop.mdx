## The hook: why the boss is unbeatable on your friend's laptop

You finally beat the level. Your friend tries it on an older laptop and the boss moves in
*slow motion*, jumps half as high, and sometimes phases straight through the floor. Same code, same
boss, completely different physics. The culprit isn't the GPU or the art — it's the **game loop**: how
the engine decides *how much time has passed* between one frame and the next, and what it does with that
time. Get this wrong and your game literally plays differently on every machine.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A game loop repeats **update, then render** forever — and if you advance the physics in **fixed-size
time steps** instead of "however long the last frame took," the simulation behaves identically on a fast
PC, a slow laptop, or a phone.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a flipbook that has to keep perfect time

In the previous lesson on **numerical integration** you saw that stepping physics forward means taking
the current velocity and position and nudging them by a small amount of time $\Delta t$. Euler and
semi-implicit Euler are only *approximations*: the bigger the step, the more error you accumulate. A
ball under gravity stepped once per second looks nothing like the same ball stepped 60 times per second.

Now connect that to a real game. Each loop iteration does two jobs:

1. **Update** — move the world forward by some amount of time (run physics, AI, input).
2. **Render** — draw the current state to the screen.

The naive approach is to measure how long the last frame took — the **delta time** $\Delta t$ — and feed
*that* straight into the physics. It seems reasonable: a frame took 33 ms, so advance the world 33 ms.
But $\Delta t$ now depends on the hardware, the GPU load, even a background download. A frame-time
**spike** hands your integrator one giant step, and (just like the integration lesson warned) the
approximation blows up: the ball tunnels through the floor or gains energy from nowhere.

The fix is to *decouple* the size of the physics step from the frame rate. We always advance physics in
small, **fixed** chunks of $\Delta t$, and we use an **accumulator** to bank up real elapsed time and
spend it one fixed chunk at a time.

## The formal definition

Let real wall-clock time advance by a measured **delta time** each frame:

$$
\Delta t_{\text{frame}} = t_{\text{now}} - t_{\text{prev}}.
$$

A **variable timestep** loop integrates with that value directly, so the state update is

$$
\mathbf{s}_{n+1} = \mathbf{s}_n + \Delta t_{\text{frame}} \; f(\mathbf{s}_n),
$$

where $f$ is the derivative (velocity, acceleration) from the integration lesson. Because
$\Delta t_{\text{frame}}$ changes every frame and can spike, the trajectory is **not reproducible**.

A **fixed timestep** loop instead chooses one constant step $\Delta t$ (say $\tfrac{1}{120}\,\text{s}$)
and keeps an **accumulator** $A$ of unspent real time. Each frame it adds the frame's elapsed time and
then drains the accumulator in whole steps:

$$
A \mathrel{+}= \Delta t_{\text{frame}}, \qquad
\text{while } A \ge \Delta t: \quad \mathbf{s} \leftarrow \mathbf{s} + \Delta t \, f(\mathbf{s}), \;\; A \mathrel{-}= \Delta t.
$$

```js
accumulator += frameDt;            // bank the real time that passed
while (accumulator >= DT) {         // spend it in equal fixed chunks
  prev = state;                     // remember last state for interpolation
  state = integrate(state, DT);     // every step is exactly DT seconds
  accumulator -= DT;
}
const alpha = accumulator / DT;     // leftover < DT, as a fraction
render(lerp(prev, state, alpha));   // smooth between the two latest steps
```

Whatever real time is left over is the remainder $A < \Delta t$. Rather than waste it, we render a blend
between the previous and current physics states using the **interpolation factor**

$$
\alpha = \frac{A}{\Delta t}, \qquad \mathbf{s}_{\text{render}} = (1 - \alpha)\,\mathbf{s}_{\text{prev}} + \alpha\,\mathbf{s}_{\text{cur}},
$$

with $\alpha \in [0, 1)$. This keeps physics perfectly deterministic while rendering stays buttery smooth
even when the frame rate and the step rate don't line up.

<Callout type="why">
The fixed loop is the same total amount of integration as the variable loop — it just refuses to take
one big inaccurate step when it could take several small accurate ones. That single discipline is what
makes a replay recorded on one machine play back identically on another.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The "advance in fixed chunks, keep the leftover" trick shows up far beyond game physics.

<ExampleCard domain="Rhythm games" icon="🎵">
A rhythm game must judge taps against a song at exact beats. If timing were tied to the frame rate, a
dropped frame would shift every note and "Perfect" hits would become "Misses." Instead the audio clock
is the fixed master step; the renderer interpolates note positions toward the next beat. The music never
drifts even when the graphics stutter.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Film & animation" icon="🎬">
Movies are a fixed timestep made physical: 24 frames per second, every frame exactly
$\tfrac{1}{24}\,\text{s}$ of story time. Slow motion is just rendering many fixed steps between two
moments; "pulldown" to show 24 fps film on 60 Hz TVs is literally an accumulator deciding when to repeat
a frame. Consistent step size is why a film looks the same in every cinema.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Networked multiplayer" icon="🌐">
Competitive games run **deterministic lockstep**: every client advances the simulation in identical
fixed ticks (e.g. 60 per second) and exchanges only inputs. Because the step is fixed, two machines that
start equal stay equal, so a tiny input log can reproduce an entire match. A variable step would diverge
within seconds, and rewind-and-replay netcode would be impossible.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Factory automation" icon="🏭">
A conveyor-line controller (a PLC) runs a fixed **scan cycle**: read all sensors, update logic, drive
all actuators, repeat on a guaranteed interval. The fixed period is what makes the line behave the same
shift after shift; if the cycle time wandered with CPU load, a packer arm would grab at the wrong moment
and parts would scatter.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the split each time: a **fixed master clock** does the real work, while a faster, looser layer
just *presents* it (render, display, draw the note). Update and render are different jobs running at
different rates on purpose.
</Callout>
