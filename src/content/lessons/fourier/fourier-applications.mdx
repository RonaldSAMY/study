## The hook: the picture a machine "sees" when it hears

Show a modern speech model a one-second clip and it almost never looks at the raw waveform. It looks at a
**spectrogram** — a heat-map of *which frequencies are loud at which moments*. That image is built by
sliding the FFT across short windows of audio, and it is the input to systems that transcribe your voice,
flag a cough as pneumonia, or tag a song by its hook. This final lesson is the victory lap: every piece —
sinusoids, series, transform, FFT, convolution — comes together in the tools that shape audio, images, and
even the attention mechanism inside large language models.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Real systems rarely work on raw signals; they work on **frequency content over time** — and machine
learning increasingly bakes sinusoids straight into its models.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from one spectrum to a moving picture

A single Fourier transform tells you which frequencies are in a signal — but not **when**. For music or
speech, *when* is everything: a melody is a sequence of changing pitches. The fix is the **Short-Time
Fourier Transform (STFT)**: chop the signal into short overlapping windows and take the FFT of each. Stack
those spectra side by side, color them by energy, and you get a **spectrogram** — time across, frequency
up, brightness = loudness.

This is the same trade-off the transform first taught us: a short window pins down *time* but blurs
*frequency*; a long window sharpens *frequency* but smears *time*. You cannot have both perfectly — that's
the time–frequency uncertainty principle, now a practical knob you tune.

Three big application families fall out of this:

- **Audio:** equalizers boost or cut frequency bands; spectrograms feed speech and music models.
- **Images:** a 2-D Fourier transform exposes spatial frequencies, so filtering, compression (JPEG), and
  denoising all happen in frequency space.
- **Sequence models:** Transformers can't tell token order from attention alone, so they add **sinusoidal
  positional encodings** — a bank of sines and cosines of many frequencies — to stamp each position with a
  unique, smoothly varying signature.

## The formal definition

The **Short-Time Fourier Transform** of a signal $x$, using a window $w$, is

$$
\text{STFT}\{x\}(\tau, f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)\,w(t-\tau)\,e^{-i 2\pi f t}\,dt,
$$

and the **spectrogram** is its squared magnitude $|\text{STFT}(\tau,f)|^2$ — energy as a function of time
$\tau$ and frequency $f$. In machine learning, two Fourier-flavored constructions are worth naming.

**Transformer positional encodings.** For a token at position $\text{pos}$ and embedding dimension $i$,

$$
PE(\text{pos}, 2i) = \sin\!\Big(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{\,2i/d}}\Big), \qquad
PE(\text{pos}, 2i+1) = \cos\!\Big(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{\,2i/d}}\Big).
$$

This is a stack of sinusoids whose frequencies range from very fast to very slow — exactly a Fourier basis
for position. It lets the model compute relative offsets through phase differences, the same shift property
you met in the transform.

**Random Fourier features.** To let simple models capture wiggly patterns, map an input $\mathbf{x}$
through $\cos(\boldsymbol{\omega}^\top \mathbf{x} + b)$ for random frequencies $\boldsymbol{\omega}$; this
approximates a powerful kernel and is what lets small networks represent fine detail in images and 3-D
scenes (as in NeRF).

<Callout type="why">
The thread of this whole track lands here: sinusoids are not just for audio engineers. They are a
<strong>universal coordinate system</strong>. Encoding position, approximating kernels, building model
inputs, compressing data — modern ML reaches for Fourier ideas because frequency is often where structure
is simplest.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Music apps (Shazam)" icon="🎵">
Song-ID apps don't compare raw audio — they build a spectrogram, pick out the loudest time-frequency peaks
as a robust "constellation" fingerprint, and match that against a database. Because peaks survive noise and
compression, it works even in a loud café.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Medical diagnostics" icon="🩺">
A baby's heartbeat, an EEG, or a lung recording is routinely turned into a spectrogram so a model can spot
abnormal rhythms — atrial fibrillation, a seizure's signature, a wheeze. The diagnostic clues live in the
frequency pattern, not the raw squiggle.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Large language models" icon="🤖">
The original Transformer adds **sinusoidal positional encodings** so the model knows token order; newer
schemes like RoPE *rotate* query and key vectors by position-dependent angles — pure Euler-formula
rotations. Either way, the math you learned for waves is what tells an LLM where each word sits.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Astronomy & exoplanets" icon="🪐">
To find a planet, astronomers take years of a star's brightness and run a periodicity search (a Fourier /
Lomb–Scargle analysis) to detect the faint, regular dip of a transit. A peak at one period is a candidate
new world — Fourier analysis as planet-hunting.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice how the role of Fourier shifts from "analysis tool" to "model ingredient." In Shazam and medicine
the spectrogram is a feature; in Transformers and NeRF the sinusoids are *built into* the architecture.
Same waves, deeper integration.
</Callout>
