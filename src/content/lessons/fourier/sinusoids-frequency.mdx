## The hook: the one shape nature can't stop drawing

Pluck a guitar string, watch a buoy bob on the swell, measure the voltage coming out of a wall socket,
or trace the daylight hours across a year — and you keep meeting the **same curve**. It rises, eases off,
falls, and comes back, perfectly smooth and perfectly repeating. That curve is the **sinusoid**, and it
is the single atom out of which Fourier's whole world is built. Before we can break complicated signals
into waves, we need to know a wave inside out — and a wave is described by just **three numbers**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Every pure wave is $A\sin(2\pi f t + \varphi)$: **amplitude** $A$ says how tall it is, **frequency** $f$
says how fast it repeats, and **phase** $\varphi$ says where in its cycle it starts.
</Callout>

## Intuition: three knobs, one wave

You already know from the unit circle that a point spinning around a circle traces out a sine wave. Now
we put **three control knobs** on that wave:

1. **Amplitude $A$** — the radius of the circle. Bigger $A$ means a taller wave: a louder sound, a
   brighter light, a bigger swing. It stretches the curve **vertically**.
2. **Frequency $f$** — how many full turns happen each second, measured in **hertz** (Hz). High $f$ packs
   more cycles into the same time: a higher musical pitch, a "faster" wiggle. It squeezes the curve
   **horizontally**.
3. **Phase $\varphi$** — where on the circle you started the clock. Changing $\varphi$ slides the whole
   wave **left or right** without changing its shape. A sine and a cosine are the very same wave, just a
   quarter-cycle apart in phase.

The factor of $2\pi$ is the bridge between "cycles" and "radians": one full cycle is $2\pi$ radians, so
multiplying the frequency $f$ by $2\pi$ converts turns-per-second into radians-per-second. That combined
quantity $\omega = 2\pi f$ is called the **angular frequency**.

## The formal definition

A **sinusoid** is any function of time of the form

$$
x(t) = A\,\sin(2\pi f t + \varphi) = A\,\sin(\omega t + \varphi),
$$

where $A$ is the amplitude, $f$ the frequency in hertz, $\omega = 2\pi f$ the angular frequency in
radians per second, and $\varphi$ the phase in radians. The **period** — the time for one full cycle — is

$$
T = \frac{1}{f},
$$

so a $440$ Hz tone repeats every $1/440$ of a second. A phase shift and an amplitude can always be
repackaged as a **sum of a sine and a cosine** at the same frequency, using the angle-addition identity:

$$
A\sin(\omega t + \varphi) = \underbrace{A\cos\varphi}_{a}\,\sin(\omega t) + \underbrace{A\sin\varphi}_{b}\,\cos(\omega t).
$$

This "sine-plus-cosine" form is the one Fourier analysis loves. Even cleaner is **Euler's formula**,
$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$, which lets us write a sinusoid as the imaginary part of a single
rotating complex exponential $A\,e^{i(\omega t + \varphi)}$ — one tidy object carrying amplitude, frequency,
and phase all at once.

<Callout type="why">
Machine-learning models almost never see a raw waveform. They see **features**: the amplitude and phase
of each frequency. A speech model works on the energy in frequency bands; a Transformer encodes token
positions with sinusoids of many frequencies. Knowing that any wave is just $(A, f, \varphi)$ is what
makes those representations make sense.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The triple $(A, f, \varphi)$ shows up under different names wherever something oscillates.

<ExampleCard domain="Music & audio" icon="🎶">
A tuning fork produces a near-perfect sinusoid. Its **frequency** is the note (A above middle C is
$440$ Hz), its **amplitude** is the loudness, and its **phase** is usually inaudible on its own — yet phase
between two speakers is exactly what stereo and noise-cancelling headphones manipulate.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Electrical power" icon="🔌">
The mains supply is a sinusoid: $230$ V at $50$ Hz in Europe, $120$ V at $60$ Hz in North America. The
three-phase power that runs factories is literally **three sinusoids** of equal amplitude and frequency,
offset in **phase** by $120°$ each, so the total delivered power stays smooth.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Radio & Wi-Fi" icon="📡">
A radio station "at $98.5$" broadcasts a carrier sinusoid at $98.5$ MHz. **FM** radio encodes sound by
nudging the **frequency**; **AM** radio encodes it by nudging the **amplitude**; modern Wi-Fi and 5G pack
data into the **phase** of many carriers at once. Same three knobs, trillions of times a second.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Ocean & climate" icon="🌊">
Ocean swell arriving at a beach is well modeled as a sinusoid whose **period** $T = 1/f$ (often $8$–$15$
seconds) sets how far apart the crests are. Tides are sinusoids too, with periods locked to the moon —
which is why tide tables are essentially a short list of amplitudes, frequencies, and phases.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The thread tying these together: nothing here is "approximately" a wave by coincidence. Anything driven
by steady rotation or a restoring force <em>is</em> a sinusoid, and is pinned down completely by how tall,
how fast, and when it starts.
</Callout>
