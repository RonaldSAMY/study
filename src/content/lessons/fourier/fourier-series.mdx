## The hook: how can smooth waves make a sharp corner?

A square wave is all hard edges — it sits flat, then jumps, then sits flat again. A sine wave is the
smoothest curve there is. So here is something that should feel impossible: if you add up enough sine
waves, of just the right sizes, those gentle curves conspire to build the square wave's vertical cliffs.
This was Joseph Fourier's outrageous claim in 1807 — that *any* repeating shape, however jagged, is
secretly a stack of pure tones. He was right, and that idea now underlies how a synthesizer makes a buzzy
sawtooth, how an organ pipe gets its timbre, and how MP3 throws away what you can't hear.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A periodic signal is a sum of a **fundamental** sine wave plus its **harmonics** — sinusoids at $2\times$,
$3\times$, $4\times$ the base frequency — each weighted by its own amount.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a recipe of harmonics

In the last lesson you summed *two* waves. A Fourier series just keeps going. Pick a signal that repeats
with period $T$, so its **fundamental frequency** is $f_1 = 1/T$. The only sinusoids allowed in the recipe
are the fundamental and its **harmonics**: $2f_1, 3f_1, 4f_1, \dots$ — whole-number multiples. Each
harmonic gets a weight (its amplitude), and the right set of weights reproduces the signal exactly.

Why only whole-number multiples? Because those are precisely the waves that *also* repeat over the same
period $T$ — a wave at $2.3 f_1$ would drift out of step and ruin the periodicity. The harmonics are the
exact set of building blocks that "fit" cleanly into one period.

The shape of a signal is encoded entirely in **how much of each harmonic** it contains:

- A **square wave** needs only the odd harmonics ($1f, 3f, 5f, \dots$), each shrinking like $1/k$.
- A **sawtooth** uses *every* harmonic, shrinking like $1/k$.
- A **triangle** uses odd harmonics shrinking much faster, like $1/k^2$, which is why it looks so smooth.

That list of weights is the signal's **spectrum** — its fingerprint in frequency.

## The formal definition

A periodic function $x(t)$ with period $T$ (fundamental angular frequency $\omega_0 = 2\pi/T$) can be
written as a **Fourier series**:

$$
x(t) = a_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \Big[\, a_k \cos(k\omega_0 t) + b_k \sin(k\omega_0 t) \,\Big].
$$

The constant $a_0$ is the signal's average (its "DC" level). The weights are recovered by **integrating
the signal against each harmonic** over one period — projecting the signal onto that wave, just like a dot
product, but for functions:

$$
a_k = \frac{2}{T}\int_0^T x(t)\cos(k\omega_0 t)\,dt, \qquad
b_k = \frac{2}{T}\int_0^T x(t)\sin(k\omega_0 t)\,dt.
$$

This works because the harmonics are **orthogonal**: the integral of $\sin(k\omega_0 t)\cos(m\omega_0 t)$
over a period is zero unless the frequencies match. Each integral therefore picks out one harmonic's
weight and ignores all the others. In the cleaner **complex form**, every harmonic is a rotating
exponential and the pair $(a_k, b_k)$ collapses into one complex coefficient:

$$
x(t) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} c_k\, e^{i k \omega_0 t}, \qquad
c_k = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T x(t)\, e^{-i k \omega_0 t}\,dt.
$$

<Callout type="why">
That integral-against-a-basis-wave is *exactly* the inner product of functions you met in linear algebra.
Fourier series are nothing more than expressing a signal in an **orthonormal basis** of sinusoids — the
same move as writing a vector in coordinates. The coefficients $c_k$ are the coordinates, and this is the
mental model that makes the Fourier transform and even attention's positional encodings click.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Musical timbre" icon="🎺">
Why does a trumpet sound different from a flute playing the same note? Same fundamental, **different
harmonic recipe**. A trumpet is rich in high harmonics (bright, buzzy); a flute is nearly a pure
fundamental (mellow). The list of harmonic amplitudes *is* the instrument's tone color.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Synthesizers" icon="🎛️">
Classic analog synths offer exactly the waveshapes from the demo — sawtooth, square, triangle — because
each is a known harmonic series. A "low-pass filter" sweep then removes the upper harmonics one by one,
which is literally undoing the sum you just built up. Subtractive synthesis is Fourier series in reverse.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Mechanical engineering" icon="⚙️">
A spinning engine or pump produces vibration that repeats every revolution. Engineers expand that
vibration into a Fourier series; a spike at the $2\times$ harmonic might mean a misaligned shaft, a spike
at the blade-count harmonic a damaged fan. The harmonic fingerprint diagnoses the fault.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Power grids" icon="🔋">
Ideal grid power is a single $50/60$ Hz sine, but cheap electronics inject **harmonic distortion** —
extra energy at $3\times$, $5\times$, $7\times$. Utilities measure "total harmonic distortion" (a Fourier
series in action) because those harmonics overheat transformers and must be filtered out.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Across all four, the *frequency* is shared and the *harmonic weights* tell the story — the instrument, the
fault, the distortion. Fourier series turns "what does this repeating signal look like?" into "how much
of each harmonic does it contain?"
</Callout>
