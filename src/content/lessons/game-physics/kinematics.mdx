## The hook: why does Mario's jump look so *right*?

Press jump in any good platformer and the hero leaps up, slows at the top, hangs for a heartbeat, then
falls faster and faster until they land. Nobody hand-drew that arc. The engine knows three numbers about
Mario — where he is, how fast he is going, and how gravity is changing that speed — and it just updates
them sixty times a second. That trio is **position, velocity, and acceleration**, and the rule that
links them is the beating heart of every physics engine ever shipped.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Acceleration changes velocity, velocity changes position, and a game just repeats <strong>v += a·Δt;
p += v·Δt</strong> every single frame.
</Callout>

## Intuition

You already know from calculus that the **derivative** measures a rate of change. Kinematics is that idea
wearing a costume:

- **Velocity is the derivative of position** — how fast your location is changing right now.
- **Acceleration is the derivative of velocity** — how fast your speed-and-direction is changing.

A game runs this backwards. It does not differentiate; it **integrates**, one tiny step at a time. Given
the current acceleration, it adds a little to the velocity; given the new velocity, it adds a little to
the position. Each step covers $\Delta t$ seconds (the time since the last frame). Stack thousands of
these micro-steps together and a smooth, believable arc falls out — exactly the curve a derivative would
have predicted, rebuilt from the bottom up.

And because position and velocity are **vectors** (from the previous lesson), the very same update runs
independently on $x$ and $y$. Horizontal motion coasts along untouched while gravity quietly drains the
vertical velocity — which is precisely why a jump traces a parabola.

## The formal definition

Track an entity with a position vector $\mathbf{p}$, a velocity vector $\mathbf{v}$, and an acceleration
vector $\mathbf{a}$. Calculus defines velocity and acceleration as derivatives:

$$
\mathbf{v} = \frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt}, \qquad \mathbf{a} = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = \frac{d^2\mathbf{p}}{dt^2}.
$$

A game cannot take a true derivative mid-play, so it approximates the integral with small steps of size
$\Delta t$. The workhorse is **semi-implicit Euler** — update velocity first, then use that fresh velocity
to move position:

$$
\mathbf{v}_{\text{new}} = \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{a}\,\Delta t, \qquad
\mathbf{p}_{\text{new}} = \mathbf{p} + \mathbf{v}_{\text{new}}\,\Delta t.
$$

For the special case of *constant* acceleration (like gravity), calculus even gives a closed form you can
check the simulation against:

$$
\mathbf{p}(t) = \mathbf{p}_0 + \mathbf{v}_0\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{a}\,t^2.
$$

```js
// One physics step (per frame). dt = seconds since last frame.
function integrate(body, dt) {
  body.vx += body.ax * dt;   // velocity gets nudged by acceleration
  body.vy += body.ay * dt;
  body.x  += body.vx * dt;   // position gets nudged by the new velocity
  body.y  += body.vy * dt;
}
```

<Callout type="why">
This step-by-step integration is the same trick machine learning uses in gradient descent: you can't jump
to the answer, so you take many small steps in the direction of change. Games integrate motion; optimizers
integrate down a loss surface. Master the loop once and both click into place.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Platformers" icon="🍄">
A jump sets vertical velocity to a big upward value, then every frame gravity subtracts from it. The hero
rises while $v_y$ is positive, freezes for one frame as $v_y$ crosses zero (the famous "hang time"), then
falls as $v_y$ goes negative. Tuning gravity and jump speed is literally tuning $\mathbf{a}$ and
$\mathbf{v}_0$.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Basketball free throw" icon="🏀">
A shooting game launches the ball with an initial speed and angle, then only gravity acts. Whether it
swishes is decided entirely by $\mathbf{p}(t) = \mathbf{p}_0 + \mathbf{v}_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}t^2$
passing through the hoop's position at some time $t$. Aim is just solving that equation.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Artillery games" icon="💣">
In Worms or a tank-duel game, you pick power (initial speed) and angle; the shell then free-falls under a
constant downward acceleration. Wind can be modeled as a small *horizontal* acceleration, so $a_x$ is no
longer zero and the horizontal velocity slowly drifts — a tiny change to the same update loop.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Drag racing" icon="🏎️">
A dragster sim holds a large *forward* acceleration while the throttle is down, so velocity climbs every
frame and the distance covered grows like $\tfrac{1}{2}a t^2$. The speedometer reads $\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert$,
and the quarter-mile time is just asking when accumulated position hits the finish line.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Same four lines of code, four genres. The only thing that changes between a jump, a free throw, an
artillery shell, and a dragster is the <strong>acceleration vector</strong> you feed the loop.
</Callout>
