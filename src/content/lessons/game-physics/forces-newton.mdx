## The hook: the spaceship that obeys you, not you it

In the last lesson you *told* the entity its acceleration directly. But a spaceship does not know its
acceleration — it knows that an engine is pushing it, that nothing slows it in the vacuum, and that it is
heavy. Real game feel comes from flipping the control around: you apply **forces** — a thruster, gravity,
drag, a sudden collision kick — pile them all up, and let the body *figure out* its own acceleration from
how massive it is. That flip is Newton's second law, and it is the rule that makes a feather flutter and a
boulder lumber using the exact same code.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Add up every force on a body into one net force, divide by its mass to get acceleration
(<strong>a = F / m</strong>), and feed that into the kinematics loop you already know.

</Callout>

## Intuition

Kinematics gave you the engine: `v += a·Δt; p += v·Δt`. The missing piece was *where does $\mathbf{a}$ come
from?* Newton's answer is wonderfully mechanical:

- **First law (inertia):** with zero net force, velocity never changes — a drifting ship coasts forever.
- **Second law:** the net force equals mass times acceleration, $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$. Rearranged for
  a game, $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{F}/m$.
- **Third law:** every push has an equal and opposite push back (the recoil when a cannon fires).

The practical trick is the **force accumulator**. Each frame you start a running total
$\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$, then *add* every force acting this instant: gravity pulls down, drag pushes back
against velocity, a thruster shoves forward. Because forces are **vectors**, "adding them up" is just the
tip-to-tail vector addition from earlier — heavier bodies simply respond less to the same total, because
you divide by a bigger $m$.

## The formal definition

Sum the forces acting on a body into a single net force vector, then convert to acceleration with the
second law:

$$
\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_i \mathbf{F}_i, \qquad \mathbf{a} = \frac{\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}}{m}.
$$

Three forces cover most games. **Gravity** is proportional to mass (so all bodies fall together):

$$
\mathbf{F}_g = m\,\mathbf{g}.
$$

**Linear drag** opposes velocity, draining energy over time:

$$
\mathbf{F}_d = -k\,\mathbf{v}.
$$

An **impulse** is a force applied over a tiny time — a collision or a kick. It changes momentum instantly,
so rather than feed it through acceleration you add it straight to velocity:

$$
\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{F}\,\Delta t = m\,\Delta \mathbf{v} \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \Delta\mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{J}}{m}.
$$

```js
// Per frame: accumulate forces, then integrate.
let F = { x: 0, y: 0 };
F.y += body.m * g;          // gravity (down, if g is negative)
F.x += -k * body.vx;        // drag opposes velocity
F.y += -k * body.vy;
if (thrusting) F.y += THRUST;          // held force
const ax = F.x / body.m, ay = F.y / body.m;   // a = F / m
body.vx += ax * dt; body.vy += ay * dt;       // then kinematics
body.x  += body.vx * dt; body.y += body.vy * dt;
```

<Callout type="why">
The force accumulator is the same pattern as summing gradients in a neural network: many independent
contributions are added into one vector before a single update step. Whether it is forces on a rigid body
or gradients on a weight, "sum the pushes, then take one step" is everywhere.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Space-sim thruster" icon="🚀">
In a vacuum the only force is your engine, so the ship obeys the first law: cut the throttle and it coasts
at constant velocity forever. Thrust adds a force in the facing direction; net force divided by the ship's
mass gives the acceleration that slowly builds speed. Bigger freighters accelerate lazily — same engine,
larger $m$.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Skydiver + parachute" icon="🪂">
Free-fall stacks gravity (down) against drag (up, growing with speed). The skydiver speeds up until drag
balances weight and the net force hits zero — **terminal velocity**. Popping the chute spikes the drag
coefficient $k$, so net force flips upward and they decelerate hard before drifting down gently.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Pinball flipper" icon="🔮">
A flipper barely touches the ball, yet it launches across the table. That is an **impulse**: a big force
over a few milliseconds. Games skip the messy contact and just add $\mathbf{J}/m$ straight to the ball's
velocity, instantly changing its momentum without ever computing the contact acceleration.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Hot-air balloon" icon="🎈">
Buoyancy is an upward force that beats gravity when the air inside is hot enough, so the net force points
up and the balloon accelerates skyward. Cool the air and weight wins again. It is the accumulator with one
extra upward term — gravity down, buoyancy up, drag resisting whichever way it moves.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the recurring shape: <strong>gravity + drag + something special</strong>. Swap the "something"
(thrust, buoyancy, an impulse) and one accumulator powers wildly different feels.
</Callout>
