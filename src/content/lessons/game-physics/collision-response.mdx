## The hook: why does the ball stop bouncing?

Drop a basketball in your game and it should bounce — high at first, then lower, then a flurry of little
taps until it rests. Drop a lump of clay and it just *splats*. Same fall, same floor, wildly different
endings. Your collision detector already told you **that** two things touched and **where**. But it says
nothing about what happens next: does the ball rocket back up, slide along, or die on the spot? That is
**collision response**, and it is the moment a contact turns into new velocities.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
At a contact, fire a single **impulse** along the surface normal that reverses the approaching speed —
multiply that reversal by **restitution** to set the bounciness, and rub off sideways speed with
**friction**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: detection found the touch, now respond

Collision *detection* hands you two facts: a contact point and a unit **normal** $\mathbf{n}$ pointing
out of the surface. Newton's laws (forces and $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$) told you how velocity builds up
over a frame. Response stitches them together at the instant of contact.

Split the incoming velocity into two pieces relative to the surface:

- The **normal** part — how fast the body drives *into* the surface. This is the part that must turn
  around, or the body would tunnel straight through.
- The **tangential** part — how fast it slides *along* the surface. Friction acts here.

Restitution $e$ is a single dial from $0$ to $1$. At $e = 1$ the normal speed comes back untouched (a
perfect, lossless bounce); at $e = 0$ none returns (the body sticks, like clay). Anything in between
keeps a fraction, so each bounce is a little weaker than the last and the ball eventually settles. That
shrinking is energy leaving the system — exactly what you watched the spring lose to damping, now packed
into one number per collision.

## The formal definition

Let two bodies approach with relative velocity $\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}}$ at the contact, and let
$\mathbf{n}$ be the unit contact normal. The speed of approach along the normal is the projection
$\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \cdot \mathbf{n}$. The scalar **impulse** that resolves the collision is

$$
j = \frac{-(1 + e)\,(\mathbf{v}_{\text{rel}} \cdot \mathbf{n})}{\frac{1}{m_1} + \frac{1}{m_2}},
$$

where $e$ is the restitution and $m_1, m_2$ are the masses. Each body then updates its velocity by that
impulse, split by its own mass and pushed in opposite directions along $\mathbf{n}$:

$$
\mathbf{v}_1' = \mathbf{v}_1 + \frac{j}{m_1}\,\mathbf{n}, \qquad
\mathbf{v}_2' = \mathbf{v}_2 - \frac{j}{m_2}\,\mathbf{n}.
$$

For the common case of a ball hitting an immovable floor, take $m_2 \to \infty$ and the whole thing
collapses to flipping and scaling the normal speed: $v_n' = -e\,v_n$. Friction then shaves the
tangential speed $v_t$ toward zero by some fraction $\mu$, so $v_t' = (1 - \mu)\,v_t$.

```js
// Ball vs static floor: reflect the normal speed, scale by e.
const vn = v.x * n.x + v.y * n.y;        // speed into the surface
if (vn < 0) {                            // only if actually approaching
  v.x -= (1 + e) * vn * n.x;             // v_n' = -e * v_n  (when |n| = 1)
  v.y -= (1 + e) * vn * n.y;
}
```

<Callout type="why">
The factor $(1 + e)$ is doing two jobs at once: the $1$ cancels the incoming normal speed (stop the
penetration), and the $e$ adds back the rebound. So $e = 0$ just halts the approach, while $e = 1$ both
halts it and launches an equal speed back out.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four arcades

<ExampleCard domain="Pinball bumpers" icon="🎯">
A pop bumper is restitution turned up past 1 in spirit: it doesn't just reflect the ball, it injects
extra impulse along the normal so the ball leaves *faster* than it arrived. Drop $e$ to a normal value
and the same bumper feels dead — the ball dribbles off instead of snapping away.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Breakout / Arkanoid" icon="🧱">
The ball-vs-paddle hit is a near-perfect bounce ($e \approx 1$) so the rally never loses energy. Many
versions cheat the normal based on *where* the ball strikes the paddle, steering the rebound angle — a
deliberate lie on top of the honest reflection.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Pool break" icon="🎱">
Racking balls have high restitution, so the cue ball's impulse barely fades as it scatters the pack
through chains of contacts. The break looks explosive precisely because $e$ is close to 1 and almost no
energy is lost at each click.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Bouncy platformer" icon="🦘">
A trampoline tile sets a high restitution just for the player's feet, so landing reverses the downward
speed into a bigger upward launch. Tune $e$ per surface and the same jump button gives a soft mat, a
springy pad, or a sticky tar pit.
</ExampleCard>
