## The hook: the rope that just works

Your hero fires a grappling hook. The rope arcs out, snaps taut, and they swing across the chasm — the line
bending, whipping, and settling exactly like real rope. You did not animate a single frame of that. You also
did not solve a scary system of differential equations for a flexible cable. You did something almost
embarrassingly simple: you took a string of points, let them fall under gravity, and then kept *yanking
neighbors back to a fixed distance apart* until they gave up and behaved.

That trick — declaring a rule the simulation must obey and then repeatedly forcing it true — is the whole
idea of **constraints**. Joints, ropes, cloth, and ragdolls are all just clever piles of constraints.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A constraint is a geometric promise (these two points stay a fixed distance apart); you enforce it by gently
shoving the points back onto the promise every frame until the whole assembly is consistent.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from free particles to chained ones

In rigid-body dynamics you integrated each body independently — gravity in, new position out. **Verlet
integration** made that especially tidy: instead of storing velocity, you store the *previous* position, and
velocity is implied by the gap between "where I was" and "where I am." That tiny detail is the secret weapon
here, because it means you can teleport a point to fix a constraint and its velocity updates *for free*.

Now connect two points with a rule: stay exactly $L$ apart. After integrating gravity they will have drifted
too close or too far. So you measure the current distance, find the error, and slide both points along the
line between them until the gap is $L$ again. Do that for every link in a chain and you get a rope — but
fixing link 3 nudges link 4 out of place, which nudges link 5, and so on. The fix is delightfully blunt:
just **repeat the whole sweep several times per frame**. More repetitions means a stiffer, more rope-like
result; fewer means a stretchy, elastic one. This relax-until-consistent loop is the heart of *Position-Based
Dynamics*, the technique behind most game cloth and rope.

## The formal definition

A point mass under **Verlet integration** advances using only its current and previous positions:

$$
\mathbf{x}_{n+1} = 2\,\mathbf{x}_n - \mathbf{x}_{n-1} + \mathbf{a}\,\Delta t^2,
$$

where the implied velocity is $\mathbf{v}_n \approx (\mathbf{x}_n - \mathbf{x}_{n-1})/\Delta t$.

A **distance constraint** between points $\mathbf{p}_i$ and $\mathbf{p}_j$ with rest length $L$ asks the
scalar function $C$ to vanish:

$$
C(\mathbf{p}_i, \mathbf{p}_j) = \lVert \mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{p}_j \rVert - L = 0.
$$

If the current separation is $d = \lVert \mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{p}_j \rVert$, you split the error and move
each free point along the connecting direction $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = (\mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{p}_j)/d$:

$$
\mathbf{p}_i \mathrel{-}= \tfrac{1}{2}(d - L)\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \qquad
\mathbf{p}_j \mathrel{+}= \tfrac{1}{2}(d - L)\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}.
$$

A pinned point takes zero share, so its neighbor absorbs the full correction. A softer alternative is a
**spring** (Hooke's law), pulling with force $\mathbf{F} = -k\,(d - L)\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ instead of snapping
the distance exactly.

```js
// one Verlet step, then relax the constraints a few times
const vx = p.x - p.px;                 // implicit velocity
p.px = p.x; p.x += vx + gravity * dt * dt;
for (let k = 0; k < iters; k++)        // more iters -> stiffer
  for (const c of links) project(c);   // pull each pair to rest length
```

<Callout type="why">
Treating joints as constraints instead of forces is what makes them stable. Stiff springs explode under large
time steps, but position projection can never overshoot — it only ever moves points *toward* the answer, so
the simulation stays calm even at 60 frames per second with chunky steps.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Ragdoll characters" icon="🦴">
When an enemy is defeated, its skeleton becomes a graph of point masses joined by distance constraints (bone
lengths) plus angle limits (so elbows do not bend backward). Gravity plus relaxation gives that signature
flailing, tumbling collapse down the stairs — no keyframes required.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Rope bridges" icon="🌉">
A wobbly plank bridge is two pinned ropes with planks slung between them. As the player runs across, each
footstep displaces a node; the constraints propagate the sag and bounce down the span, and the bridge keeps
trying to snap back to its rest shape.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Cloth flags and banners" icon="🚩">
A flag is a grid of points with distance constraints to neighbors (structural) and diagonals (shear). Pin one
edge to the pole, push the rest with a wind force, and relax: the fabric ripples and folds. Game cloth is
quite literally a 2D mesh of the same links you just dragged.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Grappling-hook swing" icon="🕸️">
Spider-Man-style traversal is a single distance constraint between the player and an anchor point on a
building. While airborne, gravity accelerates the player and the rope constraint converts that fall into a
smooth pendulum arc — release at the right moment to launch.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Real engines stack constraint <strong>types</strong>: distance for length, angle for joint limits, and contact
for collisions. Solving them together in the same relaxation loop is exactly how PhysX and Box2D build
believable mechanisms from simple parts.
</Callout>
