## The hook: why does the crate tumble when you shoot the corner?

You fire a rocket at a wooden crate. Hit it dead-center and it slides straight back. Clip the
top corner instead and it slews around, tumbling end over end as it flies away. Same crate, same
shot strength — the only thing that changed was **where** the hit landed. Until now your physics
engine has treated bodies as single points that only *move*. Real objects also *turn*, and the
mathematics of turning is what separates a floaty arcade toy from a world that feels solid.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A force applied **off-center** does two jobs at once — it pushes the whole body (linear motion)
**and** twists it around its center of mass (rotation). The twisting power is called **torque**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: turning is just translation's twin

You already know the linear story from **Forces & Newton's Laws** and **Collision Response**: an
impulse changes velocity, and heavier things resist that change. Rotation is the *exact same story*
told with rotating words. Every linear idea has a spinning twin:

- **force** becomes **torque** — the twisting effort around the center.
- **mass** becomes **moment of inertia** — how stubbornly a shape resists being spun.
- **velocity** becomes **angular velocity** — how fast it turns, in radians per second.

Two facts make rotation click. First, torque depends on the **lever arm**: the farther from the
center you push, the more twist you get — the reason a long wrench loosens a stubborn bolt. Second,
moment of inertia depends on how the mass is *spread out*: a shape with mass flung far from the
center is harder to spin than a compact one of the same weight. A pull aimed straight **through** the
center has no lever arm at all, so it makes zero torque and the body only slides.

## The formal definition

In 2D, a rigid body has three "linear" numbers (position, velocity, mass) plus three rotational
twins (angle $\theta$, angular velocity $\omega$, moment of inertia $I$).

**Torque** from a force $\mathbf{F}$ applied at offset $\mathbf{r}$ from the center of mass is the 2D
cross product — a single signed number:

$$
\tau = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F} = r_x F_y - r_y F_x.
$$

Its sign tells you the spin direction (counter-clockwise is positive), and it vanishes exactly when
$\mathbf{F}$ points along $\mathbf{r}$. Rotation then obeys Newton's law with the twins swapped in:

$$
\tau = I\,\alpha, \qquad \alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt}.
$$

For a sudden hit we use an **impulse** $\mathbf{J}$ instead of a sustained force. It delivers both a
linear and an angular kick in one instant:

$$
\Delta \mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{J}}{m}, \qquad \Delta \omega = \frac{\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{J}}{I}.
$$

The moment of inertia depends on the shape. For a solid rectangle of width $w$ and height $h$ spun
about its center,

$$
I = \frac{m\,(w^2 + h^2)}{12}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
This is the engine of every believable collision. When two bodies smash together, the contact point
is almost never on the line between their centers — so the same impulse that bounces them apart also
sets them spinning. Get $\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{J}$ right and debris tumbles, cars fishtail, and
ragdolls flop convincingly. Get it wrong and everything slides like air-hockey pucks.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Pool & billiards" icon="🎱">
Strike the cue ball dead-center and it rolls straight. Tap it off-center — low, high, or to the side
— and you put "english" on it: the lever arm $\mathbf{r}$ creates torque, so the ball curves, draws
back, or follows through. Trick-shot artists are solving $\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{J}$ by feel.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Figure skating" icon="⛸️">
A skater spinning with arms out pulls them in and suddenly whirls faster. No torque was added — they
shrank their **moment of inertia** $I$. Because angular momentum $I\,\omega$ is conserved, a smaller
$I$ forces a larger $\omega$. Game engines lean on the same conserved quantity to keep spins stable.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Spaceflight sim" icon="🚀">
A spacecraft with no wheels still turns using small **RCS thrusters** mounted off-center. Fire one
and its push, applied far from the center of mass, is almost pure torque — the ship pivots in place.
Pair two opposite thrusters and you cancel the drift to rotate without moving, a classic Asteroids
move.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Playground" icon="🎠">
A merry-go-round is hard to start from rest but, once spinning, hard to stop — that stubbornness *is*
moment of inertia. Pushing on the outer rail (big $\mathbf{r}$) works far better than shoving near
the center pole, which is exactly why the handles are on the edge.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the pattern across all four: the *same* force does wildly different things depending on
**where** it lands and **how the mass is arranged**. That "where times what" is the cross product,
and "how it's arranged" is the moment of inertia.
</Callout>
