## The hook: why does my moon slingshot into deep space?

You code up a tiny space sim. A planet sits at the center, a moon starts in a clean circular orbit, and
gravity pulls it inward every frame. You hit play... and over a minute the moon's circle quietly grows
into a spiral, swings wider and wider, and eventually escapes off-screen forever. You didn't add energy.
Newton's law is exact. So where did the extra speed come from? The culprit is not your physics — it's the
*way you stepped time forward*. The most obvious update rule, **explicit Euler**, silently injects energy
on every frame. Choosing a smarter stepper fixes it for free.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Forces tell you acceleration, but turning acceleration into motion requires *stepping time in discrete
chunks* — and the order in which you update velocity and position decides whether energy stays put or
leaks in and blows your world apart.
</Callout>

## Intuition

You already know Newton's second law gives acceleration from force: $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{F}/m$. And you
saw the plain **Euler step** in differential equations: take the current slope and march forward by a
little time $\Delta t$. A physics engine does exactly that, sixty times a second, to push every entity's
velocity and position forward.

The subtlety is that you have *two* coupled quantities. Acceleration changes velocity, and velocity
changes position. Plain (explicit) Euler updates both using only **old** values: it nudges position with
the old velocity, and the old velocity with the old acceleration. For anything that curves — an orbit, a
spring, a pendulum — those old values are always slightly stale, and the staleness consistently points
*outward*. Frame after frame the error adds up in the same direction, so total energy creeps upward and
the motion winds outward.

The fix is almost insultingly small. **Semi-implicit (symplectic) Euler** updates velocity first, then
moves position with the *new* velocity. **Verlet** is built so that a step and its time-reverse are
symmetric. Both keep energy bounded forever, even though each individual step is no more accurate than
Euler's. The takeaway: for games, *long-term stability* matters far more than per-step precision.

## The formal definition

Write the state as position $\mathbf{x}$ and velocity $\mathbf{v}$, with acceleration
$\mathbf{a}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x})/m$. Over one timestep $\Delta t$:

**Explicit (forward) Euler** — everything uses old values:

$$
\mathbf{v}_{n+1} = \mathbf{v}_n + \mathbf{a}(\mathbf{x}_n)\,\Delta t, \qquad
\mathbf{x}_{n+1} = \mathbf{x}_n + \mathbf{v}_n\,\Delta t.
$$

**Semi-implicit (symplectic) Euler** — velocity first, then move with the *new* velocity:

$$
\mathbf{v}_{n+1} = \mathbf{v}_n + \mathbf{a}(\mathbf{x}_n)\,\Delta t, \qquad
\mathbf{x}_{n+1} = \mathbf{x}_n + \mathbf{v}_{n+1}\,\Delta t.
$$

**Velocity Verlet** — symmetric, second-order accurate, averages start and end accelerations:

$$
\mathbf{x}_{n+1} = \mathbf{x}_n + \mathbf{v}_n\,\Delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{a}_n\,\Delta t^2, \qquad
\mathbf{v}_{n+1} = \mathbf{v}_n + \tfrac{1}{2}\,(\mathbf{a}_n + \mathbf{a}_{n+1})\,\Delta t.
$$

The single line that flips explicit into semi-implicit Euler is using $\mathbf{v}_{n+1}$ instead of
$\mathbf{v}_n$ in the position update:

```js
// semi-implicit (symplectic) Euler — one body, one step
v.x += a(x).x * dt;   // update velocity FIRST
v.y += a(x).y * dt;
x.x += v.x * dt;      // then move with the NEW velocity
x.y += v.y * dt;
```

<Callout type="why">
Semi-implicit Euler and Verlet are <em>symplectic</em>: they conserve a quantity extremely close to the
true energy, so error oscillates around a bound instead of marching off to infinity. That is why almost
every shipping game uses one of them — a stable, slightly-wrong simulation beats an accurate one that
explodes after thirty seconds.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Space simulation" icon="🪐">
An orbital sandbox like a Kerbal-style game must keep planets circling for *hours* of play. Explicit
Euler would let every moon spiral outward and escape; symplectic integrators keep orbits closed so your
solar system is still recognizable after a long session.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Vehicle suspension" icon="🏎️">
A car's wheels ride stiff spring-dampers. Stiff springs mean large accelerations, and explicit Euler on a
stiff spring gains energy until the chassis vibrates itself off the track. Semi-implicit Euler with a
small fixed timestep keeps the suspension planted and the ride believable.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Cloth and rope" icon="🧵">
A cape or flag is a mesh of point masses joined by springs. Verlet is the cloth simulator's favorite: it
stores only positions (velocity is implied by the previous position), is trivially stable, and makes
distance constraints — "these two points stay 5 cm apart" — easy to enforce by just nudging positions.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Pendulum clocks" icon="🕰️">
A swinging pendulum should keep the *same* amplitude forever with no friction. Under explicit Euler the
swing grows wider every cycle as phantom energy accumulates; under a symplectic stepper the amplitude
holds steady, which is exactly the energy-conserving behavior real physics demands.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Whatever stepper you pick, use a <strong>fixed</strong> timestep for physics (accumulate real frame time
and run, say, 60 Hz steps). Variable timesteps make collisions and stiff springs nondeterministic, which
breaks replays, networking and "feel".
</Callout>
