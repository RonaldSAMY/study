## The hook: a million sparks from one rule

A barrel explodes. Glowing embers blast outward, arc through the air, and wink out one by one. It looks like
chaos you could never hand-author — and yet there is no choreography here at all. There is one **emitter** and
one tiny rule applied to thousands of nearly identical specks: be born somewhere with a random velocity, fall
under gravity, fade as you age, and disappear. Run that rule on a crowd and emergent beauty falls out for
free.

Particles are how games fake everything that is too wispy, too numerous, or too fluid to model as solid
bodies: smoke, fire, dust, rain, magic, blood, foam. The math is gentle; the payoff is enormous.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A particle system is one emitter spawning many independent, short-lived points — each just integrates a
simple motion equation and fades out over its lifetime.
</Callout>

## Intuition: numerical integration, but a thousand times

You already know how to push one point forward in time: take its velocity, add gravity, step the position.
That is plain **numerical integration** — the same explicit Euler step you used for a single projectile. A
particle system is that step copied across a whole array, with two new ingredients bolted on.

First, **randomization at birth**. If every spark launched identically you would see one boring line. Instead
the emitter samples each new particle's velocity, lifetime, size, and color from a range, so the crowd fans
out into a natural-looking spray. Second, a **lifetime**. Each particle carries an age that ticks up every
frame; when age passes its lifetime the particle dies. Along the way you fade its transparency from 1 to 0 so
it dissolves instead of popping out.

The last practical trick is **recycling**. Allocating and freeing thousands of objects per second would
hammer the garbage collector, so engines keep a fixed **pool** and simply mark dead particles as available,
reusing those slots for the next batch. Same memory, forever.

## The formal definition

Each particle stores a position $\mathbf{p}$, velocity $\mathbf{v}$, an age $a$, and a lifetime $\tau$. Every
frame it takes one explicit Euler step under acceleration $\mathbf{g}$:

$$
\mathbf{v} \leftarrow \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{g}\,\Delta t, \qquad
\mathbf{p} \leftarrow \mathbf{p} + \mathbf{v}\,\Delta t, \qquad
a \leftarrow a + \Delta t.
$$

The particle is alive while $a < \tau$, and its opacity fades linearly with the fraction of life elapsed:

$$
\alpha = 1 - \frac{a}{\tau}.
$$

At birth the emitter samples a random launch from a base speed $s$ and an angle range, for example
$\mathbf{v}_0 = s\,(\cos\theta,\ \sin\theta)$ with $\theta$ drawn around straight up. The expected number of
live particles in steady state is approximately $\text{rate} \times \bar{\tau}$.

```js
for (const p of pool) {
  if (!p.active) continue;
  p.vy += gravity * dt;          // accelerate
  p.x += p.vx * dt;              // integrate position
  p.y += p.vy * dt;
  p.age += dt;
  p.alpha = 1 - p.age / p.life;  // fade out
  if (p.age >= p.life) p.active = false;  // recycle the slot
}
```

<Callout type="why">
Particles are the bridge from physics to <strong>rendering</strong>. Once you have positions and alphas, the
GPU draws each particle as a textured, additively blended quad (a billboard). Thousands of these stacked with
"lighter" blending are what give fire and magic their glow — which is exactly where the next track picks up.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Explosion VFX" icon="💥">
An explosion is a one-shot <em>burst</em>: emit a few hundred particles in a single frame with high outward
speed and short lifetimes, layering fast bright sparks over slower dark smoke. The randomized velocities give
that ragged, no-two-alike blast shape.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Campfire flame" icon="🔥">
A flame is a continuous emitter with low spawn speed and *negative* gravity (buoyancy) so embers drift upward.
Color shifts from white-hot at birth to deep red as each particle ages, and the alpha fade makes the tongue of
fire taper to nothing at the top.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Chimney smoke" icon="🏭">
Smoke uses long lifetimes, large soft particles, gentle upward drift, and a sideways wind force. As particles
age they grow and fade, so a thin stream from the chimney swells into a dispersing, billowing cloud.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Fireworks" icon="🎆">
A firework chains two systems: a rocket particle climbs, and on death it triggers a radial burst of hundreds
of glowing sparks with gravity and a slow fade, producing the classic falling-willow trails against the night
sky.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The same machinery scales from cosmetic sparkles to <strong>simulation</strong>: GPU particle systems push
millions of points for snow, rain, and even crude fluids, all driven by this one born-move-fade-recycle loop.
</Callout>
