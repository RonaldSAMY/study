## The hook: how many people will actually show up?

An airline sells 180 seats but books 190 passengers, betting that some won't show. A call center staffs
for "about 7 calls a minute." A vaccine trial counts how many of 1,000 volunteers catch the disease.
None of these can be predicted exactly — but each follows a **named pattern** so reliable it has a
formula. Learn three of these patterns and you can model a huge share of the counting problems in the
world. They are the **Bernoulli**, **Binomial**, and **Poisson** distributions.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **discrete distribution** is a reusable recipe for the pmf $p(k) = P(X = k)$ — once you recognize
which recipe your situation matches, a couple of parameters tell you *everything*.
</Callout>

## Intuition: build them up from one coin flip

Every distribution in this lesson grows from a single yes/no trial.

1. **Bernoulli($p$)** — one trial with success probability $p$. $X$ is $1$ (success) or $0$ (failure).
   It's the atom: $\mathbb{E}[X] = p$.
2. **Binomial($n, p$)** — repeat that *same* trial $n$ **independent** times and count the successes.
   Now $X \in \{0, 1, \dots, n\}$, and the shape is a hump centered near $np$.
3. **Poisson($\lambda$)** — what the Binomial *becomes* when $n$ is huge and $p$ is tiny but the average
   count $\lambda = np$ stays moderate. It models **rare events over a continuum** of time or space —
   calls per minute, typos per page — where there's no fixed "number of trials."

So they're a family, not three unrelated objects: Bernoulli is one Binomial trial, and Poisson is the
Binomial's limit for "many chances, each unlikely."

## Play with it

Pick a family and reshape it. For the **Binomial**, slide $n$ and $p$: raising $p$ slides the hump right;
raising $n$ widens and smooths it. For **Poisson**, slide $\lambda$ and watch a right-skewed count
distribution stretch out. The reported $\mathbb{E}[X]$ and $\mathrm{Var}(X)$ track the shape.

## The formal definition

A single **Bernoulli** trial:

$$
P(X = 1) = p, \qquad P(X = 0) = 1 - p, \qquad \mathbb{E}[X] = p, \quad \mathrm{Var}(X) = p(1-p).
$$

The **Binomial** counts successes in $n$ independent Bernoulli trials. The binomial coefficient
$\binom{n}{k}$ counts the ways to arrange $k$ successes among $n$ slots:

$$
P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}, \qquad \mathbb{E}[X] = np, \quad \mathrm{Var}(X) = np(1-p).
$$

The **Poisson** models the count of independent rare events in a fixed interval, with average rate
$\lambda$:

$$
P(X = k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}, \qquad \mathbb{E}[X] = \lambda, \quad \mathrm{Var}(X) = \lambda.
$$

That last identity — *mean equals variance* — is the Poisson's fingerprint. If real count data has
variance much larger than its mean, it's "overdispersed" and Poisson is the wrong recipe.

<Callout type="why">
These pmfs are the **likelihoods** at the heart of classifiers. Logistic regression models each label as
a Bernoulli whose $p$ depends on the input; its loss is exactly the negative log-likelihood of that
Bernoulli. Poisson regression predicts counts (clicks, purchases, defects). Knowing the distribution
*is* knowing the loss function.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Aviation" icon="✈️">
Each of 190 booked passengers shows up independently with probability $p = 0.92$. The number who arrive
is **Binomial(190, 0.92)**, with mean $\approx 175$. The airline overbooks 10 seats because
$P(\text{more than 180 show}) $ is small — a Binomial tail calculation worth millions.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Call centers" icon="☎️">
Calls arrive randomly and independently at about 7 per minute. The count in any given minute is
**Poisson(7)**. Staffing asks "what's $P(\text{more than 12 calls})$ so we're not swamped?" — a Poisson
tail. No fixed number of trials exists, which is exactly when Poisson, not Binomial, is the right tool.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Manufacturing" icon="🔧">
A single weld either holds or fails — a **Bernoulli** trial with failure probability $p = 0.001$. Across
a bridge with 5,000 welds, the number of failures is Binomial(5000, 0.001), well approximated by
**Poisson(5)**. Rare-event-over-many-chances is the Poisson sweet spot.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Genetics" icon="🧬">
Each of a couple's 4 children inherits a recessive condition independently with probability $\tfrac14$.
The number affected is **Binomial(4, 0.25)**, mean $= 1$. The chance that *none* are affected is
$(0.75)^4 \approx 0.32$ — a direct binomial term genetic counselors actually quote.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The decision tree: a fixed number of independent yes/no trials → <strong>Binomial</strong>; a single
trial → <strong>Bernoulli</strong>; rare events over continuous time or space with no fixed trial count
→ <strong>Poisson</strong>.
</Callout>
