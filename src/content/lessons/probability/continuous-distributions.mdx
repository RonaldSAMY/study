## The hook: how long until the next earthquake?

Ask a seismologist *"what's the probability the next quake is exactly 4.7 years away?"* and the honest
answer is **zero** — because there are infinitely many possible wait times and no single one can carry
positive probability. Yet *"within the next 5 years"* has a perfectly good answer. That shift — from
asking about a single value to asking about a **range** — is what separates continuous randomness from
the counts of the last lesson, and it's why probability becomes **area under a curve**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
For a continuous variable, the **density** $f(x)$ has no probability *at* a point; probability is the
**area** under $f$ over an interval, and the total area is always $1$.

</Callout>

## Intuition: bars become a smooth curve

Take the bar charts from discrete distributions and imagine slicing the values ever finer — minutes into
seconds, seconds into milliseconds. The bars shrink to slivers and their tops trace a smooth curve: the
**probability density function** (pdf). The height $f(x)$ is *not* a probability — it can even exceed 1 —
it's a probability *per unit of $x$*. To get an actual probability you multiply height by width, i.e.
take **area**:

$$
P(a < X < b) = \text{area under } f \text{ from } a \text{ to } b.
$$

Three densities cover most of the continuous world:

- **Uniform($a,b$)** — every value in $[a,b]$ equally likely; a flat rectangle. "I know the range and
  nothing more."
- **Exponential($\lambda$)** — the **wait time** until the next random event (the continuous partner of
  the Poisson). It's **memoryless**: having waited 10 minutes already tells you nothing about how much
  longer you'll wait.
- **Gaussian / Normal($\mu,\sigma$)** — the bell curve. It appears whenever many small independent
  effects add up (the reason why is the Central Limit Theorem, two lessons ahead).

## The formal definition

A pdf $f(x)$ must be non-negative and integrate to 1; probabilities are integrals, and the running area
from the left is the **cumulative distribution function** (cdf):

$$
P(a < X < b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx, \qquad F(x) = P(X \le x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t)\,dt.
$$

The three families:

$$
\text{Uniform: } f(x) = \frac{1}{b-a} \text{ on } [a,b]; \qquad
\text{Exponential: } f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x},\; x \ge 0.
$$

$$
\text{Gaussian: } f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\, \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).
$$

The Gaussian's $\mu$ is its center (mean) and $\sigma$ its spread (standard deviation). Because computing
its area has no elementary formula, we use the cdf — exactly the area the demo shades for you.

<Callout type="why">
The Gaussian is the workhorse of ML. Least-squares regression secretly assumes Gaussian noise; its
squared-error loss <em>is</em> the negative log-likelihood of a Normal. Variational autoencoders,
diffusion models, and Gaussian processes all place Normal densities at their core. The Exponential,
meanwhile, models the survival and waiting times behind reliability and queueing models.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Ride-hailing" icon="🚕">
The wait for the next available driver in a busy area follows an **Exponential** distribution. Its
memorylessness is the surprising part: after waiting 4 minutes, your expected *remaining* wait is the
same as when you started. Useful and counterintuitive — past waiting earns you no "credit."
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Manufacturing tolerance" icon="📏">
The diameter of a machined bolt clusters around its target with **Gaussian** scatter. A part passes if
it's within μ ± 2σ — about 95% of output. Tightening σ (a better machine) shrinks the reject rate,
which is why "Six Sigma" literally refers to keeping defects beyond 6 standard deviations.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Computer simulation" icon="🎰">
A random-number generator returns a value **Uniform** on $[0,1]$ — the flat density, every value equally
likely. It's the raw material of all simulation: feed uniform numbers through the right transform and you
can manufacture exponential wait times, Gaussian noise, or any distribution you need.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Finance" icon="💹">
Daily returns of a stock are often modeled as **Gaussian** around a small mean. The area in the left
tail, $P(X < -0.05)$, estimates the chance of a 5% drop — the basis of "Value at Risk." (Markets have
fatter tails than a true Normal, a caveat that has caused real-world disasters.)
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="warning">
For a continuous variable, $P(X = c) = 0$ for <em>every</em> single value c — a zero-width slice has zero
area. So $P(X \le c)$ and $P(X < c)$ are equal, and only <em>ranges</em> carry probability. This trips up
everyone at first.
</Callout>
