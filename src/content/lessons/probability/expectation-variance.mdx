## The hook: would you take this bet?

A casino game pays you \$10 if you win and costs \$1 to play, but you only win 8% of the time. Should
you play? You can't know any single outcome — but you *can* compute what happens **on average** over
thousands of plays, and whether the results swing wildly or stay tame. Those two questions —
*where does it center?* and *how much does it scatter?* — are answered by **expectation** and
**variance**, the two numbers that compress an entire distribution into a usable summary.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The **expectation** $\mathbb{E}[X]$ is the distribution's balance point (its long-run average), and the
**variance** is the average squared distance from that point — how widely outcomes scatter.
</Callout>

## Intuition: balance point and spread

Picture the distribution's probabilities as weights placed along a ruler. The **expectation** is the
single spot where the ruler balances on a fingertip — heavier values pull the balance point toward
themselves. It need not be a value the variable can actually take (the average roll of a die is 3.5).

Once you know the center, the natural next question is *how far from it do outcomes usually land?* That's
**spread**. We measure it with the **variance**: the probability-weighted average of the *squared*
distance from the mean. Why squared? Squaring makes every deviation positive (so left and right don't
cancel) and punishes big misses more than small ones. Because the variance is in *squared* units, we
usually report its square root — the **standard deviation** $\sigma$ — which lives in the same units as
$X$ and is far easier to interpret.

A crucial early warning: **two distributions can share the same mean yet feel totally different.** A
steady index fund and a wild meme stock might both average 7% a year — but their variances, and therefore
your sleep, are worlds apart.

## The formal definition

For a discrete variable, the **expectation** is the probability-weighted sum of values; for a continuous
one, the sum becomes an integral:

$$
\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_x x\, p(x), \qquad \mathbb{E}[X] = \int x\, f(x)\, dx.
$$

Expectation is **linear** — the single most-used property in all of probability — and this holds *even
when variables are dependent*:

$$
\mathbb{E}[aX + b] = a\,\mathbb{E}[X] + b, \qquad \mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y].
$$

The **variance** is the expected squared deviation from the mean, with a handy shortcut formula:

$$
\mathrm{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}\big[(X - \mu)^2\big] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \big(\mathbb{E}[X]\big)^2, \qquad \sigma = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X)}.
$$

Variance scales by the **square** of a constant, and for **independent** variables it simply adds:

$$
\mathrm{Var}(aX + b) = a^2\,\mathrm{Var}(X), \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X + Y) = \mathrm{Var}(X) + \mathrm{Var}(Y) \;\text{ (independent)}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
Expectation and variance *are* the language of ML evaluation. A model's expected loss is the quantity
training minimizes. The famous **bias–variance tradeoff** literally decomposes prediction error into a
(bias)² term and a variance term. And every "mean ± std" you've ever seen on a benchmark is these two
numbers doing their job.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Casino & gambling" icon="🎲">
That \$10-or-nothing game: $\mathbb{E}[\text{payout}] = 0.08 \times 10 = 0.80$ dollars, but it costs \$1, so
the expected *profit* is $-0.20$ dollars per play. Negative expectation is the house edge — play long enough
and the average guarantees you lose. The casino's profit is just the law of large numbers collecting.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Insurance" icon="🏠">
An insurer charges a premium a bit above the **expected** payout per policy. Any single house may burn
down (huge variance) — but across a million policies the variance of the *average* shrinks, so the total
is predictable. Insurance is a business built on taming variance through pooling.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Investing" icon="📊">
Two portfolios both expect a 7% return, but one has σ = 4% and the other σ = 20%. Same mean, wildly
different risk. Modern portfolio theory treats variance *as* risk and seeks the highest expected return
per unit of standard deviation — the Sharpe ratio.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Logistics" icon="📦">
A courier's deliveries average 40 minutes, but the *variance* decides how big a buffer to promise.
Quoting the mean alone means you're late half the time; quoting mean + 2σ keeps ~97% of deliveries on
time. Spread, not center, sets the service-level guarantee.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Linearity of expectation is almost magical: 𝔼[X + Y] = 𝔼[X] + 𝔼[Y] <em>always</em>, even for dependent
variables. Variance has no such free pass — it only adds cleanly when the variables are
<strong>independent</strong>, which is exactly what the next lesson explores.
</Callout>
