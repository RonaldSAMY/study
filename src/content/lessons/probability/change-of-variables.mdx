## The hook: from a flat random number to anything you want

Every simulator, game, and Monte-Carlo engine starts with one humble tool: a generator that spits out a
**uniform** random number between $0$ and $1$ — every value equally likely. Yet the world you want to
model is rarely flat. Wait-times decay. Asset prices drift and skew. Noise is bell-shaped. So how do you
turn that featureless uniform into a richly-shaped distribution?

You push it through a **function**. Feed $x \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ into $y = -\ln(1 - x)$ and out comes
a perfectly **exponential** random variable. But here's the subtlety: when you bend the variable, the
*probability density* does **not** just come along for the ride unchanged. Stretch one region and the
density there must **thin out**; compress another and it must **pile up** — so that the total probability
stays exactly $1$. The bookkeeping that enforces this is the **change-of-variables formula**, and its
star is the **Jacobian**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
When you transform a random variable with $y = g(x)$, the density must be **rescaled by how much $g$
stretches or squeezes space** — that local stretch factor is the Jacobian, and without it probability
would leak away.
</Callout>

## Intuition: probability is conserved, density is not

A density $f_X(x)$ is **probability per unit length** — area, not height, is what counts. The chance of
landing in a tiny interval is $f_X(x)\,dx$. Now apply a smooth, one-to-one map $y = g(x)$. That same
chunk of probability must survive the trip, just relabeled:

$$
f_Y(y)\,|dy| = f_X(x)\,|dx|.
$$

Probability in the little box on the $x$ side equals probability in its image on the $y$ side — outcomes
were merely renamed, not created or destroyed. Rearranging gives the density on the new variable:

$$
f_Y(y) = f_X(x)\left|\frac{dx}{dy}\right| = \frac{f_X\big(g^{-1}(y)\big)}{\big|\,g'\big(g^{-1}(y)\big)\big|}.
$$

That fraction is the whole story. Where $g$ is **steep** ($|g'|$ large), it spreads a small $x$-interval
across a wide $y$-range, so the density is **divided down** — it thins out. Where $g$ is **flat**, it
crams a wide $x$-interval into a narrow $y$-range, so the density **spikes up**. The total area stays $1$
because we paid the toll: dividing by the local stretch $|g'|$.

A quick sanity check with squaring, $y = x^2$ on $x \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$: near $x = 0$ the function is
nearly flat, so equal $x$-steps barely move $y$ — probability bunches up, and indeed
$f_Y(y) = \tfrac{1}{2\sqrt{y}}$ blows up as $y \to 0$. Watch it happen in the demo.

## The formal definition

Let $X$ have density $f_X$, and let $y = g(x)$ be **differentiable and strictly monotonic** (so its
inverse $g^{-1}$ exists). The density of $Y = g(X)$ is

$$
f_Y(y) = f_X\big(g^{-1}(y)\big)\,\left|\frac{d}{dy}\,g^{-1}(y)\right|.
$$

The derivative factor — borrowed straight from the **chain rule** you met in differentiation — is the
one-dimensional **Jacobian**. In higher dimensions, $\mathbf{x} \mapsto \mathbf{y}$, the single
derivative becomes the absolute value of the determinant of the **Jacobian matrix** of partial
derivatives:

$$
f_{\mathbf{Y}}(\mathbf{y}) = f_{\mathbf{X}}\big(g^{-1}(\mathbf{y})\big)\,
\left|\det \mathbf{J}_{g^{-1}}(\mathbf{y})\right|,
\qquad
\big(\mathbf{J}\big)_{ij} = \frac{\partial x_i}{\partial y_j}.
$$

The determinant measures how the map scales **volume** locally — the exact multidimensional version of
"how much does $g$ stretch space here?"

<Callout type="why">
This formula is the literal engine of <strong>normalizing flows</strong>, a class of deep generative
models. They learn an invertible neural network $g$ that warps a simple Gaussian into the complicated
distribution of real images or audio. Training maximizes the log-likelihood
$\log f_X(g^{-1}(y)) + \log|\det \mathbf{J}|$ — so the log-Jacobian term is not a footnote, it's half the
loss. The same factor appears in the reparameterization trick behind variational autoencoders.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Reshape a variable, and its density reshapes with it — always paying the Jacobian toll.

<ExampleCard domain="Finance" icon="📈">
Daily <strong>log-returns</strong> $r$ are modeled as Gaussian (symmetric, can be negative). The price
multiplier is $M = e^{r}$. Pushing the bell curve through the exponential gives the
<strong>lognormal</strong> density $f_M(m) = f_r(\ln m)/m$ — that $1/m$ is the Jacobian, and it's why
modeled prices are skewed and never go negative.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Simulation" icon="🎲">
<strong>Inverse-transform sampling</strong> turns a uniform $U$ into any distribution with CDF $F$ via
$X = F^{-1}(U)$. The Jacobian of $F^{-1}$ is $1/f(x)$, which neatly cancels to reproduce exactly $f$.
This is how libraries generate exponential, Cauchy, or custom random variables from a single uniform RNG.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Audio engineering" icon="🔊">
Loudness is measured in <strong>decibels</strong>, $L = 10\log_{10}(P/P_0)$ — a logarithmic squashing of
sound power $P$. If power fluctuates with some density, the decibel reading's density is reshaped by the
Jacobian of the log, $|dP/dL|$. The compression is why a whisper and a jet engine fit on the same tidy
scale.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Generative AI" icon="🧠">
A <strong>normalizing flow</strong> samples $z$ from a plain Gaussian and applies a learned invertible
map $x = g(z)$ to produce a realistic image. To score how likely a real image is, it runs $g^{-1}$ and
adds the log-Jacobian term — the change-of-variables formula, evaluated millions of times per training
run.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Forward (build a sample) or backward (score a sample), every one of these relies on the same identity:
density on the new variable equals density on the old, divided by the local stretch.
</Callout>
