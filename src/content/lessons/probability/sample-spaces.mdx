## The hook: how does a lottery promise *exact* odds?

When a state lottery prints *"1 in 13,983,816 chance to win the jackpot,"* nobody flipped a coin to
get that number. They **listed every possible draw**, counted how many of them win, and divided. That
is the entire machinery of probability in one move: write down everything that *could* happen, mark the
slice you care about, and measure its size. Before we can talk about random variables, distributions,
or any model an AI learns, we need this foundation — the **sample space** and the **events** inside it.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **sample space** is the set of *all* possible outcomes; an **event** is any subset of them, and its
**probability** is just the share of the sample space it occupies.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the universe of outcomes, and slices of it

Start with a single experiment — anything with an uncertain result. The **sample space** $\Omega$ is the
complete list of outcomes that experiment could produce, with two rules: the outcomes must be
**mutually exclusive** (only one happens) and **exhaustive** (one of them definitely happens).

- Drawing a lottery ball numbered 1–36: $\Omega = \{1, 2, \dots, 36\}$.
- A weekend's weather (sun/rain) over two days: $\Omega = \{(S,S), (S,R), (R,S), (R,R)\}$.

An **event** is any subset of $\Omega$ — a question you can ask whose answer is yes or no for each
outcome. *"The ball is a multiple of 3"* picks out $\{3, 6, 9, \dots, 33\}$. When every outcome is
**equally likely**, probability is beautifully simple: count the favorable outcomes and divide by the
total.

$$
P(A) = \frac{\#\,\text{outcomes in } A}{\#\,\text{outcomes in } \Omega}.
$$

Events are sets, so we combine them with set operations: **union** ($A \cup B$, "$A$ or $B$"),
**intersection** ($A \cap B$, "$A$ and $B$"), and **complement** ($A^c$, "not $A$"). Those three are
all you need to build any question out of simpler ones.

## The formal definition

A **probability** is a function $P$ that assigns each event a number, obeying three axioms (due to
Kolmogorov):

$$
P(A) \ge 0, \qquad P(\Omega) = 1, \qquad P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) \;\text{ if } A \cap B = \varnothing.
$$

Everything else follows. The **complement rule** drops out immediately, because $A$ and $A^c$ together
fill $\Omega$ and never overlap:

$$
P(A^c) = 1 - P(A).
$$

When two events *can* overlap, naively adding them double-counts the shared part, so we subtract it
back — the **inclusion–exclusion** rule:

$$
P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B).
$$

<Callout type="why">
Machine-learning models are scored by how much probability they place on what actually happened. A
language model assigns a probability to every possible next token — its own sample space — and good
training pushes mass onto the real continuation. Getting the sample space and these add/subtract rules
right is the bedrock everything sits on.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The "list everything, measure a slice" pattern is everywhere uncertainty lives.

<ExampleCard domain="Genetics" icon="🧬">
Two brown-eyed parents each carry one brown (B) and one blue (b) gene. A child gets one gene from each
parent, so $\Omega = \{BB, Bb, bB, bb\}$ — four equally likely outcomes. The event *"blue eyes"* needs
two recessive genes: just $\{bb\}$, so $P = \tfrac14$. Three of the four outcomes show brown, giving the
famous 3:1 ratio.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Weather forecasting" icon="⛅">
A meteorologist's sample space for a weekend is $\{(S,S), (S,R), (R,S), (R,R)\}$ for sun/rain across
two days. The event *"at least one rainy day"* is everything except $(S,S)$ — so it's easiest to use the
complement: $P(\text{rain at least once}) = 1 - P(S,S)$. Complements turn an awkward "at least one" into
a single subtraction.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Card games" icon="🃏">
Draw one card from a 52-card deck: $\Omega$ has 52 equally likely outcomes. Let $A$ = "a heart" (13
cards) and $B$ = "a face card" (12 cards). They overlap in the three heart-faces, so
$P(A \cup B) = \tfrac{13}{52} + \tfrac{12}{52} - \tfrac{3}{52} = \tfrac{22}{52}$. Inclusion–exclusion
keeps you from counting the jack of hearts twice.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Quality control" icon="🏭">
A factory pulls one phone off the line; the sample space is $\{\text{works},\ \text{scratched screen},\ \text{dead battery},\ \text{both faults}\}$. The event *"ships to a customer"* is the single "works" outcome. Counting the defect
outcomes and subtracting from 1 gives the yield — the same complement trick, now driving a business
decision.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the recurring move: when an event is hard to count directly ("at least one defect," "rain on
some day"), count its <em>opposite</em> and subtract from 1. The complement is your most reliable
shortcut.
</Callout>
