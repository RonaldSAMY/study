## The hook: your test came back positive — should you panic?

A screening test for a rare disease is *"99% accurate."* Yours comes back **positive**. It feels like a
99% chance you're sick. Astonishingly, the true chance can be **under 10%**. Doctors get this wrong all
the time, and the reason is a single missing ingredient: *how rare the disease was in the first place.*
Learning the test result should **update** your belief — but by exactly how much is the job of
**conditional probability** and **Bayes' theorem**, the most important idea in applied probability.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Conditional probability** $P(A \mid B)$ rescales the world to only the outcomes where $B$ happened,
then asks how much of *that* smaller world is also $A$.
</Callout>

## Intuition: shrink the world to what you know

Before any evidence, the whole sample space is in play. The moment you learn that event $B$ occurred,
every outcome outside $B$ is impossible — so you **throw them away** and renormalize over what's left.
The conditional probability of $A$ *given* $B$ is the fraction of the surviving $B$-world that is also
$A$:

$$
P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}.
$$

The cleanest way to *feel* this is with **natural frequencies**: don't think in percentages, think in
counts of people. Out of 1,000 people, how many are sick *and* test positive? How many are healthy *but*
test positive anyway? Compare those two piles and the "paradox" evaporates. That's exactly what the demo
draws.

A crucial sanity check: $P(A \mid B)$ and $P(B \mid A)$ are **not** the same. *"Positive given sick"*
(the test's sensitivity) is huge; *"sick given positive"* (what you actually care about) can be small.
Bayes' theorem is the bridge that flips one into the other.

## The formal definition

Rearranging the definition of conditional probability gives the **multiplication rule**:

$$
P(A \cap B) = P(A \mid B)\,P(B) = P(B \mid A)\,P(A).
$$

Setting the two right-hand sides equal and solving yields **Bayes' theorem** — the formula for updating
a belief in light of evidence:

$$
P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A)\,P(A)}{P(B)}.
$$

Read it as: **posterior** $=$ **likelihood** $\times$ **prior** $/$ **evidence**. The *prior* $P(A)$ is
the prevalence you'd guess before testing; the *likelihood* $P(B \mid A)$ is how strongly the evidence
points at $A$; the denominator $P(B)$ is found with the **law of total probability**:

$$
P(B) = P(B \mid A)\,P(A) + P(B \mid A^c)\,P(A^c).
$$

Two events are **independent** when learning one tells you nothing about the other —
$P(A \mid B) = P(A)$, equivalently $P(A \cap B) = P(A)\,P(B)$.

<Callout type="why">
Bayes' rule *is* machine learning in miniature. A spam filter holds a prior belief about how much mail
is junk, sees evidence (the words in an email), and computes the posterior $P(\text{spam} \mid
\text{words})$. Training a model is the same loop at scale: start with a prior over parameters, observe
data, and update toward the parameters that best explain it.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Updating a belief with evidence shows up far beyond medicine.

<ExampleCard domain="Email security" icon="📨">
A filter knows 20% of mail is spam (prior). The word *"invoice"* appears in 60% of spam but only 4% of
real mail. Seeing it, Bayes updates the spam belief upward dramatically — yet a single innocent word is
never decisive, because the 80% prior of "real mail" still pulls back. The posterior balances both.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Courtroom reasoning" icon="⚖️">
A DNA match has a "1-in-a-million" random-match probability. That is $P(\text{match} \mid \text{innocent})$
— *not* $P(\text{innocent} \mid \text{match})$. Confusing the two is the famous **prosecutor's fallacy**.
With a large suspect pool, several innocent people can match, so the match alone is far from proof.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Search & rescue" icon="🚁">
A lost hiker is most likely on the north trail (prior). A team sweeps it and finds nothing. Rather than
giving up, rescuers run Bayes: the failed search *lowers* the north-trail posterior and *raises* the
others, redirecting the next sweep. Every absence of evidence is itself evidence that updates the map.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Insurance" icon="🛡️">
An insurer's prior is the base crash rate for all drivers. A driver's three claim-free years are
evidence with high likelihood under "safe driver," so the posterior shifts toward safe — and the
premium drops. Pricing risk *is* continually updating $P(\text{risky} \mid \text{record})$.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="warning">
The number-one Bayesian error is ignoring the <strong>prior</strong> (the base rate). A 99%-accurate
test for a 1-in-10,000 disease still produces mostly false alarms, because there are so few true cases
to find. Always ask "how common was it to begin with?"
</Callout>
