## The hook: how does a 3D world fit on a flat screen?

Your monitor is flat, yet the world in your game has depth — things are near, things are far, and the far ones look smaller. Somewhere between the 3D scene and the 2D grid of pixels, a piece of math throws away one dimension *on purpose*, in just the way your eyes do. That math is **projection**, and the virtual eye doing it is the **camera**. Choose its settings well and the world feels real; choose badly and everything looks warped, cramped, or eerily flat.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **camera** defines a box of visible space — the **view frustum** — and **projection** is the matrix that squashes everything in that box onto the flat screen, optionally making far things smaller.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the box you can see

In the **matrices and transforms** lesson you learned that a single matrix can move, rotate, and scale points. The camera is built from exactly those tools, chained together.

Picture standing in a dark room with a flashlight. You only see what falls inside the cone of light: too close and it is behind you, too far and it fades out, too far to the side and it misses the beam. A camera is the same. It can see only a bounded region of space called the **view frustum**, fenced in by:

- a **near plane** (closer than this is clipped away),
- a **far plane** (farther than this is invisible),
- and the sides, set by the **field of view (FOV)** — how wide the cone opens.

There are two flavors of projection. **Perspective** mimics eyes and lenses: parallel rails appear to converge and distant objects shrink, so the frustum is a truncated pyramid. **Orthographic** keeps sizes constant no matter the distance, so the frustum is a plain box — perfect for blueprints, isometric strategy games, and 2D UI.

## Play with it

This is a top-down slice of a camera. Slide the **FOV**, **near**, and **far** planes and watch the frustum reshape; objects inside it project down to the screen strip, and in perspective mode the far ones shrink. Switch to **orthographic** to see depth stop mattering.

<Callout type="tip">
Crank the FOV very wide and watch the projected objects stretch toward the edges — that is the "fish-eye" look. Narrow it and the scene flattens and zooms, like a sniper scope. Game FOV settings are a direct handle on this trade-off.
</Callout>

## The formal definition

A vertex's journey to the screen is a chain of matrix multiplies — the **Model-View-Projection (MVP)** chain:

$$
\mathbf{p}_{\text{clip}} = M_{\text{proj}} \, M_{\text{view}} \, M_{\text{model}} \, \mathbf{p}_{\text{local}}.
$$

The model matrix places an object in the world, the view matrix moves the world in front of the camera, and the projection matrix maps the frustum into a standard cube. A perspective projection (with vertical field of view $\theta$, aspect ratio $a$, and near/far $n, f$) has the form

$$
M_{\text{proj}} =
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{1}{a \tan(\theta/2)} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & \frac{1}{\tan(\theta/2)} & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & \frac{-(f+n)}{f-n} & \frac{-2fn}{f-n} \\
0 & 0 & -1 & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The magic is in that bottom row: it copies $-z$ into the output's $w$ component. After the **perspective divide** (dividing by $w$), an object's screen size scales like $1/z$ — far things get smaller, automatically.

<Callout type="why">
Because depth ends up encoded as $1/z$, precision bunches up near the camera. Setting the near plane too small spreads that precision thinly and causes **z-fighting** (flickering overlapping surfaces). Knowing the projection math tells you exactly which slider to fix.
</Callout>

## The same idea, across very different games

Every camera uses projection, but the right choice is genre-specific.

<ExampleCard domain="Isometric city builder" icon="🏙️">
A city builder uses an **orthographic** camera so a building looks the same size whether it sits at the front or back of the map. Constant scale lets players compare structures and place them on a clean grid — perspective would make far districts deceptively tiny.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Competitive shooter" icon="🎯">
Competitive players raise their **FOV** to see more around them, accepting edge distortion for awareness. Lower it and enemies appear larger and closer — effectively a zoom. The FOV slider is one of the most-tweaked settings in the genre.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Mobile endless runner" icon="📱">
A runner pulls the **near plane** forward and the **far plane** in to a tight band, so the GPU never processes scenery the player will never reach. A shallow frustum is free performance on a battery-limited phone.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Flight simulator" icon="✈️">
A flight sim needs a huge **far plane** to render the horizon, but that risks z-fighting on cockpit gauges inches away. Sims solve it by rendering the cockpit and the distant world with separate projections, each with its own near/far range.
</ExampleCard>
