## The hook: why does a ball look round?

A sphere is just a smooth surface, yet on screen it reads instantly as round — bright where it faces the light, dark where it turns away, with a tight white glint where the light reflects straight at you. None of that is painted on; it is *computed*, per pixel, from a single geometric fact: how much each tiny patch of surface tilts toward the light. Lighting is what turns flat-shaded triangles into objects that feel solid, and it all rests on a quantity you already know — the **dot product**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A surface looks bright in proportion to how directly it faces the light, and that "how directly" is exactly the **dot product** of the surface **normal** and the **light direction**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the normal does all the work

The key object is the **normal**: a unit vector sticking straight out of the surface at a point. In the **dot product** lesson you learned that for two unit vectors, their dot product is the cosine of the angle between them — $1$ when aligned, $0$ when perpendicular, negative when opposed.

Now point one vector at the light (call it $\mathbf{L}$) and let $\mathbf{N}$ be the surface normal. Their dot product $\mathbf{N} \cdot \mathbf{L}$ measures how squarely the surface faces the light:

- facing the light → close to $1$ → fully lit,
- side-on → near $0$ → dim,
- facing away → negative → in shadow (clamp it to $0$).

That single term is **diffuse** (Lambertian) lighting, the matte response of chalk or cloth. Real materials add two more pieces: a **specular** highlight, the mirror-like glint that appears when the reflected light points near your eye, and an **ambient** term, a small constant so shadowed areas are not pure black. Sum them and a sphere comes alive.

## The formal definition

With unit normal $\mathbf{N}$, unit light direction $\mathbf{L}$, unit view direction $\mathbf{V}$, and half-vector $\mathbf{H} = \frac{\mathbf{L} + \mathbf{V}}{\lVert \mathbf{L} + \mathbf{V} \rVert}$, a classic shading model sums three terms:

$$
\text{color} = \underbrace{k_a}_{\text{ambient}} + \underbrace{k_d \max(0, \mathbf{N} \cdot \mathbf{L})}_{\text{diffuse}} + \underbrace{k_s \,\big(\max(0, \mathbf{N} \cdot \mathbf{H})\big)^{\alpha}}_{\text{specular}}.
$$

The diffuse term is pure $\mathbf{N} \cdot \mathbf{L}$. The specular exponent $\alpha$ (the *shininess*) sharpens the highlight: large $\alpha$ gives a tiny, mirror-like glint, small $\alpha$ a broad sheen.

**Physically based rendering (PBR)** keeps this spirit but enforces *energy conservation* — a surface cannot reflect more light than it receives — and parameterizes materials by intuitive properties like **roughness** and **metalness** instead of arbitrary constants. The same dot products appear inside, just wrapped in formulas tuned to match real measured materials.

<Callout type="why">
Lighting is where the dot product stops being abstract. The moment you see a sphere shade smoothly from a single $\mathbf{N} \cdot \mathbf{L}$, you understand why graphics programmers care so much about vectors, angles, and unit length.
</Callout>

## The same idea, across very different games

Every lit game uses normals and dot products, but the style differs.

<ExampleCard domain="Horror game flashlight" icon="🔦">
A horror game leans on a low **ambient** term so unlit areas are nearly black, then a single moving flashlight provides the diffuse $\mathbf{N} \cdot \mathbf{L}$. The dread comes from how little of the scene clears the lighting threshold at any moment.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Racing game car paint" icon="🏎️">
Glossy car paint is almost all **specular** with a high shininess exponent, so the highlight is a sharp streak that slides across the body as the car turns. PBR's metalness and roughness let the same shader render both chrome trim and matte tires.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Stylized mobile RPG" icon="📱">
A mobile RPG bakes a **toon** look by snapping the diffuse $\mathbf{N} \cdot \mathbf{L}$ into two flat bands — lit and shadowed — instead of a smooth gradient. Cheap to compute and instantly readable on a small screen.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sci-fi space sim" icon="🛰️">
In open space there is no sky to bounce light, so the **ambient** term is essentially zero and a ship's dark side is genuinely black. A single harsh sun gives crisp diffuse-and-specular shading, selling the vacuum.
</ExampleCard>
