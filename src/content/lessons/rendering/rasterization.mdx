## The hook: the moment geometry becomes an image

In the pipeline lesson you watched a triangle travel from raw corners down to the screen. There is one exact instant where it stops being *math about shapes* and becomes *light you can see*: the GPU looks at the triangle, looks at the grid of pixels, and decides — for every single pixel — "are you inside this triangle or not?" That decision, repeated billions of times a second, is **rasterization**. Get it right and a crisp character appears; get it wrong and you get gaps, jaggies, or holes between triangles.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Rasterization** asks, for each pixel, "is your center inside this triangle?" — and the answer comes from three **edge functions** whose signs tell you which side of each edge you are on.
</Callout>

## Intuition: which side of the line are you on?

You already met the tool you need in the **dot and cross product** lesson. The 2D cross product of two vectors tells you their *turning direction* — positive for a left turn, negative for a right turn. Rasterization uses exactly that.

Take one edge of the triangle, say from corner $A$ to corner $B$. For any pixel center $P$, the quantity

$$
E_{AB}(P) = (B_x - A_x)(P_y - A_y) - (B_y - A_y)(P_x - A_x)
$$

is just the 2D cross product of the edge vector and the vector to the pixel. Its **sign** says whether $P$ is to the left or right of that edge. A point is inside the triangle when it is on the *same side* of all three edges at once. Three sign checks — that is the whole test.

The magnitudes of those three edge functions are a bonus: rescaled, they become the **barycentric coordinates**, the weights that say how much of each corner a pixel "belongs to." Those weights are how color, depth, and texture coordinates get smoothly blended across the triangle.

## The formal definition

For triangle corners $V_0, V_1, V_2$ and a pixel point $P$, define the three edge functions:

$$
w_0 = E_{V_1 V_2}(P), \quad w_1 = E_{V_2 V_0}(P), \quad w_2 = E_{V_0 V_1}(P).
$$

The pixel is **inside** when $w_0, w_1, w_2$ all share the sign of the triangle's signed area. The normalized weights

$$
\lambda_0 = \frac{w_0}{w_0 + w_1 + w_2}, \quad \lambda_1 = \frac{w_1}{w_0 + w_1 + w_2}, \quad \lambda_2 = \frac{w_2}{w_0 + w_1 + w_2}
$$

are the **barycentric coordinates**, with $\lambda_0 + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = 1$. Any per-vertex value $a$ (color, depth, UV) is interpolated as $a_P = \lambda_0 a_0 + \lambda_1 a_1 + \lambda_2 a_2$.

<Callout type="why">
Edge functions are *incremental*: stepping one pixel right just adds a constant to each $w$. That turns the inside test into a few additions per pixel, which is exactly why GPUs can fill millions of triangles per frame.
</Callout>

## The same idea, across very different games

The triangle test is universal, but each genre leans on a different corner of it.

<ExampleCard domain="Retro pixel platformer" icon="🕹️">
A deliberately low-resolution platformer keeps a coarse pixel grid on purpose. Because each pixel is large, the inside/outside decision is starkly visible — sprites have chunky, hard edges. The art style *is* the rasterizer's sampling grid, left unsmoothed.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Fighting game replays" icon="🥋">
Hit-detection overlays in a fighting game draw translucent triangular hit-boxes. The same barycentric weights that shade a triangle also let the engine find *where* on a limb a strike landed, blending damage values stored at each vertex of the collision mesh.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Racing game minimap" icon="🏁">
A track minimap fills thousands of tiny road triangles each frame. At speed, distant track segments shrink below a pixel and their centers fall between samples — so the minimap shimmers unless the engine adds extra sample points (multisampling) to catch those slivers.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Strategy fog of war" icon="🗺️">
A strategy game paints fog of war as a mesh of triangles whose corner values are "explored" or "hidden." Barycentric interpolation produces the soft gradient at the edge of your vision — a smooth fade computed per pixel from three corner numbers.
</ExampleCard>
