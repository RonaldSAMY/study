## The hook: the fastest triangle is the one you never draw

A modern game world might hold millions of objects, but your screen shows a few thousand at most. Naively sending everything to the GPU every frame would melt the budget. So the real art of real-time rendering is *avoiding work*: figuring out, as cheaply as possible, what the player cannot see and refusing to draw it. The frame you do not render is the cheapest frame of all.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Culling** removes objects the camera cannot see before they cost anything, while **batching** and **LOD** shrink the cost of the objects that remain.

</Callout>

## Intuition: see less, draw cheaper, ask less often

Three families of trick, in order of impact:

**1. Culling — skip the invisible.** From the **projection and cameras** lesson you know the camera sees only its **frustum**. **Frustum culling** tests each object against that wedge and discards anything outside it — usually with a fast bounding-sphere check, not the full mesh. **Occlusion culling** goes further: an object inside the frustum but fully hidden behind a wall is also skipped.

**2. Batching — fewer, bigger asks.** Every time you tell the GPU "draw this," that **draw call** carries fixed overhead from the CPU. A thousand identical crates as a thousand calls is slow; merging them into one call (instancing or atlasing) is fast. The draw-call count is often the true bottleneck — more than the triangle count.

**3. Level of detail (LOD) — match effort to importance.** A distant object covers a handful of pixels, so rendering its million-triangle mesh is wasteful. **LOD** swaps in simpler meshes (and cheaper shaders) as things recede, spending detail only where it shows.

## Play with it

Aim the camera by dragging in the scene and adjust its **FOV**. Objects inside the wedge draw solid; the rest are **culled** and shown faint. Toggle culling off to see everything drawn, and toggle **batching** to watch the draw-call counter collapse. Far objects drop to low **LOD** automatically.

## The formal definition

Frustum culling tests an object's bounding sphere (center $\mathbf{c}$, radius $r$) against each of the frustum's six planes. A plane with unit normal $\mathbf{n}$ and offset $d$ gives a signed distance $\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{c} + d$; the object is culled if, for any plane,

$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{c} + d < -r,
$$

meaning the whole sphere sits on the outside. One dot product per plane rejects an entire object.

The payoff is measured in **draw calls**. If $N$ objects share $T$ distinct meshes/materials, naive rendering issues $N$ calls, frustum culling cuts it to the visible count $V$, and batching by type brings it down toward the number of distinct visible types:

$$
\text{calls}_{\text{naive}} = N \;\longrightarrow\; \text{calls}_{\text{culled}} = V \;\longrightarrow\; \text{calls}_{\text{batched}} \approx T_{\text{visible}}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
These optimizations are pure dot products and bookkeeping, yet they often decide whether a game hits 60 FPS. Mastering them is how you turn a beautiful scene that stutters into one that runs — the bridge from rendering theory to a shippable engine.
</Callout>

## The same idea, across very different games

Every shipped game fights the frame budget its own way.

<ExampleCard domain="Battle royale" icon="🪂">
Dropping from the plane, a battle-royale player sees a huge map at once, so it leans hard on **LOD** and distance fog: far buildings are crude blocks that swap to detailed meshes as you parachute down, keeping the draw budget sane.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Corridor horror" icon="🚪">
A claustrophobic horror game is mostly walls, so **occlusion culling** shines: rooms beyond a closed door are entirely skipped. Designers even use doorways as natural culling portals to guarantee only one or two rooms render at a time.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Tower defense swarm" icon="🐛">
A tower-defense game spawns hundreds of identical enemies. **Instanced batching** draws the whole swarm in one call with a per-instance position, so a horde costs barely more than a single creature on the CPU.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="VR experience" icon="🥽">
A VR app must render twice per eye at high refresh, leaving almost no budget to waste. Aggressive **frustum culling** per eye plus tight LOD are non-negotiable — a missed frame budget literally makes players sick.
</ExampleCard>
