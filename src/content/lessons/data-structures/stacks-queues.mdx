## The hook: hitting Ctrl-Z

You place a wall in your base-builder, rotate it, paint it, then mash **Ctrl-Z** three times. The game
undoes them in *exactly the reverse order*: unpaint, unrotate, unplace. It never undoes the wall before
the paint. Meanwhile, across town, your multiplayer match has a lobby: the first player to click "ready"
is the first one dropped into the game. Two systems, two opposite rules about *what comes out first* — and
both are built from the dynamic arrays you already know, just with strict discipline about **which end** you
touch.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **stack** removes the <strong>most recently added</strong> item (last-in, first-out); a **queue**
removes the <strong>oldest</strong> item (first-in, first-out).
</Callout>

## Intuition: which end do you take from?

A dynamic array lets you add and remove anywhere, but unrestricted access invites bugs. Stacks and queues
deliberately *limit* you to make behavior predictable.

**Stack — LIFO (Last In, First Out).** Think of a stack of plates: you add to the top and take from the
top. The last plate on is the first plate off. The two operations are **push** (add on top) and **pop**
(remove the top). This naturally models *undo*: the last thing you did is the first thing reversed.

**Queue — FIFO (First In, First Out).** Think of a line at a ticket booth: people join at the back and are
served from the front. The first to arrive is the first served. The operations are **enqueue** (add at the
back) and **dequeue** (remove from the front). This models fairness and order: matchmaking, print jobs,
turn order.

Both are usually just a dynamic array underneath — the magic is the *rule* about which end you use.

## Play with it

Switch between **Stack** and **Queue** and watch the difference. In stack mode, items pile upward and
removal always takes the top. In queue mode, items line up and removal always takes the front. Same
elements, opposite exit.

## The formal definition

Both are **abstract data types**: defined by their operations, not their storage. Each operation is
$O(1)$ — constant time — because it only ever touches one end.

**Stack** (LIFO):

$$
\textbf{push}(x), \qquad \textbf{pop}() \rightarrow \text{last pushed}, \qquad \textbf{peek}() \rightarrow \text{top without removing}.
$$

**Queue** (FIFO):

$$
\textbf{enqueue}(x), \qquad \textbf{dequeue}() \rightarrow \text{oldest element}, \qquad \textbf{front}() \rightarrow \text{next to leave}.
$$

The defining contrast: a stack returns elements in **reverse** insertion order, a queue in the **same**
insertion order.

<Callout type="why">
These two appear constantly under the hood of intelligent systems. The **call stack** runs every program
and every recursive function. And the search lesson ahead uses a **queue** for breadth-first search (BFS)
and a **stack** for depth-first search (DFS) — swapping the container literally changes how an AI explores
a level.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Web browsing" icon="🌐">
Your browser's <strong>back button</strong> is a stack. Each page you visit is pushed; pressing back pops
the most recent one. That's why back always returns you to where you <em>just</em> were, never to page one.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Cafeteria" icon="🍽️">
A spring-loaded tray dispenser is a literal stack: staff push clean trays on top, diners take from the
top. The tray sitting longest at the bottom is the last to be used — pure LIFO.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Customer support" icon="🎧">
A support hotline is a queue. Callers are answered in the order they dialed in — first in line, first
helped. Jumping the queue would feel unfair, which is exactly why FIFO is the right rule here.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Concert tickets" icon="🎟️">
An online ticket waiting room holds buyers in a queue. The site admits people front-first as capacity
frees up, so early arrivals get first pick — order in equals order served.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
A handy mental test: if reversing the order is the <em>feature</em> (undo, back-button, evaluating nested
brackets), reach for a <strong>stack</strong>. If preserving order is the feature (fairness, scheduling,
breadth-first exploration), reach for a <strong>queue</strong>.
</Callout>
