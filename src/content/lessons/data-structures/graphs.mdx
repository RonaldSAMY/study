## The hook: every door in the dungeon

Picture a dungeon level: a Spawn room with a door to a Hall, the Hall opening into a Vault and a Lab, the
Lab leading to the Boss. The map isn't a neat hierarchy — rooms loop back, shortcuts connect distant areas,
a single room may have three exits. A tree can't capture this; it forbids loops and allows only one parent.
What you need is the most flexible structure in all of computing: the **graph**. It's how a game finds a
path to the player, how a map app routes around traffic, and how a social network knows who your friends'
friends are.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **graph** is just a set of <strong>nodes</strong> connected by <strong>edges</strong> — with no rules
about parents, loops, or direction, which makes it general enough to model almost any relationship.
</Callout>

## Intuition: trees with the rules removed

A graph keeps the *nodes* of a tree but throws away its restrictions. There is no single root, a node can
connect to any number of others, and **cycles** (loops back to where you started) are allowed. Edges can be
**undirected** (a two-way door between rooms) or **directed** (a one-way drop, a "follows" link).

To store a graph we need to record who connects to whom. Two layouts dominate:

- **Adjacency list** — for each node, keep a list of its neighbors. Compact when edges are few (most real
  graphs), and the natural choice for level maps and social networks.
- **Adjacency matrix** — an $n \times n$ grid where cell $(i, j)$ is 1 if an edge connects $i$ and $j$.
  Fast to check a specific pair, but uses $n^2$ space even when edges are sparse.

Once stored, the key operation is **exploration**: starting from one node, systematically visit everything
reachable. Because graphs have cycles, we must remember which nodes we've already **visited** — otherwise we
loop forever. There are two classic strategies, and you already met both containers behind them.

## The formal definition

A graph is a pair $G = (V, E)$ where $V$ is the set of nodes (vertices) and $E$ is the set of edges, each
edge joining two nodes. The two ways to explore it from a start node:

- **Breadth-First Search (BFS)** uses a **queue**. It visits the start, then all its neighbors, then *their*
  unvisited neighbors — expanding outward in rings of distance. On an unweighted graph, BFS finds the
  **shortest path** (fewest edges).
- **Depth-First Search (DFS)** uses a **stack** (often the recursion call stack). It follows one path as
  deep as possible, then backtracks to the last branch and tries another.

Both visit every reachable node once and look at every edge once, so each runs in $O(V + E)$ time. Both rely
on a **visited** set to avoid revisiting nodes in cycles.

<Callout type="why">
Graph search is the skeleton of game AI and beyond. Pathfinding (A* is BFS with a smart priority queue)
moves enemies through a level; DFS detects cycles and explores state spaces; and a neural network itself is
a <strong>directed graph</strong> of operations that autodiff traverses to compute gradients. Master BFS and
DFS and you've met the core of countless algorithms.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Social networks" icon="👥">
People are nodes; friendships are edges. "People you may know" runs a breadth-first search two hops out:
your friends, then their friends. The famous "six degrees of separation" is just a short BFS path length.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Navigation" icon="🗺️">
Cities are nodes and roads are weighted edges. A route planner searches this graph for the lowest-cost path,
re-running the search when traffic changes an edge's weight. Your GPS is graph search in your pocket.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Chemistry" icon="⚗️">
A molecule is a graph: atoms are nodes, bonds are edges. Software compares molecules by checking whether
their graphs match, and rings in the structure are literally cycles in the graph.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="The web" icon="🔗">
Web pages are nodes; hyperlinks are directed edges. Search engines crawl this graph with BFS and rank pages
by how the link graph is connected — the original idea behind PageRank.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
A tree is simply a special graph: connected, undirected, with no cycles and exactly one path between any two
nodes. So everything you learned about traversal carries over — a graph just adds the bookkeeping to handle
loops.
</Callout>
