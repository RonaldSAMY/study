## The hook: how wide is the canyon you can't cross?

A survey crew needs the distance across a canyon, but they can't stretch a tape over the gap. So they do
something clever: they pace out a 200 m baseline along their own rim, then measure the angles from each end of
that baseline to a lone tree on the far side. Three numbers — one length and two angles — and a triangle with
no right angle anywhere. The **Law of Sines** turns those numbers into the canyon's width exactly. Right
triangles got us this far; these two laws break the right-angle restriction and let us solve *any* triangle.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
In **any** triangle, sides and the angles opposite them are linked: the **Law of Sines** ties each side to
its opposite angle, and the **Law of Cosines** is the Pythagorean theorem generalised to any angle.
</Callout>

## Intuition: every triangle, not just the right ones

SOH-CAH-TOA only works when there's a $90°$ corner. But most real triangles — survey sightlines, flight paths,
truss members — are *oblique*: no right angle in sight. Two laws rescue us.

Label a triangle so that side $a$ is opposite angle $A$, side $b$ opposite $B$, and side $c$ opposite $C$
(the standard convention). Then:

**Law of Sines** — a bigger angle faces a longer side, in exact proportion:

$$
\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}.
$$

If you know an angle and its opposite side, you know that common ratio, and any other angle-or-side unlocks
the rest. It's the tool when you have an angle paired with its opposite side.

**Law of Cosines** — Pythagoras with a correction term for the angle:

$$
c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C.
$$

When $C = 90°$, $\cos C = 0$ and this collapses to $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$ — ordinary Pythagoras. For any other
angle, the $-2ab\cos C$ term accounts for how "open" the corner is. Use it when you know two sides and the
angle *between* them, or all three sides and want an angle.

## The formal definition

For a triangle with sides $a, b, c$ opposite angles $A, B, C$:

$$
\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C} = 2R,
$$

where $R$ is the radius of the triangle's circumscribed circle. And the Law of Cosines, in all three forms:

$$
a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A, \quad
b^2 = a^2 + c^2 - 2ac\cos B, \quad
c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C.
$$

**Which to use?** Match what you're given:

- **ASA / AAS** (an angle with its opposite side, plus more): Law of Sines.
- **SAS** (two sides + the included angle) or **SSS** (all three sides): Law of Cosines.

One caution — the **ambiguous case (SSA)**: knowing two sides and a non-included angle can fit zero, one, or
two triangles, so check whether a second solution exists before trusting one.

<Callout type="why">
These laws are **triangulation**, the geometric heart of positioning. GPS, indoor beacons, robot
localisation (SLAM), and 3D reconstruction from multiple camera views all recover unknown distances from
measured angles and baselines using exactly this reasoning. The Law of Cosines also reappears as the
algebra behind the distance between two vectors, which underlies similarity scores throughout machine
learning.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Wherever you must find a distance or angle you cannot measure directly, you build a triangle and apply a law.

<ExampleCard domain="Land surveying" icon="🏞️">
From a 200 m baseline along a canyon rim, the angles to a tree across the gap are $65°$ and $78°$. The third
angle is $180° - 65° - 78° = 37°$, then the Law of Sines gives the sightline to the tree —
$\frac{200}{\sin 37°} = \frac{d}{\sin 78°}$ — and from there the canyon's width, no tape required.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Air navigation" icon="🛩️">
A pilot flies 120 km, turns through an interior angle, then flies 90 km on a new heading. The straight-line
distance back to the start is one Law of Cosines step: $d^2 = 120^2 + 90^2 - 2(120)(90)\cos(\text{turn})$.
Flight computers chain these to convert a series of legs into a direct return path.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sailing" icon="⛵">
A boat can't sail straight into the wind, so it **tacks** in a zigzag. Each tack and the desired course form
an oblique triangle; the Law of Sines tells the skipper how far to sail on each leg to make good a target
distance upwind, trading extra distance for a workable angle.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Soccer" icon="⚽">
The "shooting angle" — how wide the goal looks from where a striker stands — is the angle at the player's
vertex of the triangle to the two goalposts. With distances to each post known, the Law of Cosines returns
that angle, quantifying how good the chance really is.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Canyon, flight path, tacking course, shot on goal: each is an oblique triangle where a couple of measurements
plus one law unlock everything else. Triangulation is just these two formulas, applied with care.
</Callout>
