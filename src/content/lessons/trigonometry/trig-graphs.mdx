## The hook: predicting the tide six hours from now

A harbour master needs to know whether a cargo ship will clear the seabed at 3 p.m. The water rises and
falls about twice a day in a smooth, repeating swell — and a single equation predicts it weeks ahead:
$h(t) = A\sin(Bt + C) + D$. Pick the right four knobs and the curve hugs the real tide chart almost
perfectly. Those knobs — **amplitude, period, phase and midline** — are the entire vocabulary for shaping a
wave, and once you can read them you can model anything that cycles.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Every sine wave is the plain $\sin x$ after three edits: **A** stretches it tall, **B** squeezes it
sideways (sets the period), and **C** slides it left or right (the phase).
</Callout>

## Intuition: one wave, three knobs

From the unit circle you know that plotting the height of a spinning point against its angle gives the
endless wave $y = \sin x$: it oscillates between $-1$ and $1$ and repeats every $2\pi$. Real-world cycles are
rarely that tidy — they can be taller, faster, and shifted in time. We reshape the basic wave with three
parameters in

$$
y = A\,\sin(Bx + C).
$$

- **$A$ — amplitude.** Multiplying the output stretches the wave vertically. It now swings between $-A$ and
  $+A$. Bigger $A$, taller wave.
- **$B$ — controls the period.** Multiplying the *input* squeezes the wave horizontally. It completes a full
  cycle when $Bx$ advances by $2\pi$, so the **period** is $\frac{2\pi}{B}$. Bigger $B$, more cycles crammed in.
- **$C$ — phase.** Adding to the input shifts the whole wave sideways by $-\frac{C}{B}$. Positive $C$ slides it
  *left* (the wave starts "ahead").

Add a constant $D$ at the end and you also lift the whole curve to a new midline — handy for tides that
oscillate around mean sea level rather than zero.

## The formal definition

For $y = A\sin(Bx + C) + D$ with $B > 0$:

$$
\text{amplitude} = |A|, \qquad
\text{period} = \frac{2\pi}{B}, \qquad
\text{phase shift} = -\frac{C}{B}, \qquad
\text{midline } y = D.
$$

Cosine is the same wave shifted by a quarter period: $\cos x = \sin\!\left(x + \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$, so
everything above applies to $A\cos(Bx + C)$ too. The **frequency** — cycles per unit of $x$ — is the
reciprocal of the period, $f = \frac{B}{2\pi}$, and $B$ itself is called the **angular frequency**.

<Callout type="why">
Decomposing a signal into sines of different amplitudes and frequencies is the **Fourier transform**, the
workhorse behind audio models, image compression, and the spectral features fed to many networks. The three
knobs here — amplitude, frequency, phase — are exactly the numbers a Fourier analysis reports for each
component. Reading a wave fluently is reading the language of signals.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Wherever a quantity rises and falls on a steady cycle, these three knobs describe it.

<ExampleCard domain="Oceanography" icon="🌊">
A semidiurnal tide is modelled as $h(t) = A\sin(Bt) + D$ with a period of about 12.4 hours, so
$B = \frac{2\pi}{12.4} \approx 0.51$ per hour. The amplitude $A$ is half the tidal range and $D$ is mean sea
level. Harbour software solves for these from past readings, then predicts safe docking windows.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Human physiology" icon="🫁">
Quiet breathing is nearly sinusoidal: lung volume cycles roughly every 4 seconds, so $B \approx \frac{2\pi}{4}
\approx 1.57$ per second. The amplitude is your tidal volume (how deep each breath is). Ventilators and
sleep-tracking apps fit exactly this $A\sin(Bt)$ shape to detect irregular breaths.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Electrical power" icon="⚡">
Wall-socket voltage is a pure sine: in Europe $v(t) = 325\sin(100\pi t)$ volts. Here $A = 325$ V (the peak),
and $B = 100\pi$ gives a period of $\frac{2\pi}{100\pi} = 0.02$ s — that is the 50 Hz mains frequency. Phase
differences between voltage and current are central to how power is delivered.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Maritime safety" icon="🗼">
A lighthouse beam sweeping at a constant rate makes its apparent brightness rise and fall like
$A\sin(Bt) + D$. Each lighthouse has a signature **period** between flashes; sailors identify it by timing
the cycle — reading $B$ straight off the wave to know which light they are seeing.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Tides, breaths, mains voltage and a lighthouse flash are governed by completely different physics, yet each
is captured by the same $A\sin(Bx + C) + D$. To model a cycle you only ever need: how tall, how fast, how
shifted, around what midline.
</Callout>
