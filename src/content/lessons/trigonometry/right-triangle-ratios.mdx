## The hook: measuring a building you can't climb

A surveyor stands 50 metres from a skyscraper, points a small sighting tool called a clinometer at the roof,
and reads one number: the **angle** up to the top, say 58°. That single angle, plus the distance she already
paced out, is enough to know the building's height to the metre — no ladder, no drone, no tape measure
flung skyward. The secret is that in any right triangle, an **angle locks the ratios of the sides**. Learn
those ratios and you can turn an angle into a length, or a length into an angle, anywhere.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
In a right triangle, each angle fixes the **ratios** of the sides: **sine**, **cosine** and **tangent** are
just three of those ratios, captured by the mnemonic **SOH-CAH-TOA**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: same angle, same shape

You met angles in radians and degrees in the previous lesson. Now drop one into a right triangle — a triangle
with a 90° corner. Pick one of the *other* two angles and call it $\theta$. Relative to $\theta$, the three
sides get names:

- the **opposite** side (across from $\theta$),
- the **adjacent** side (next to $\theta$, not the long one),
- the **hypotenuse** (the long side, across from the right angle).

Here is the key fact, which comes straight from *similar triangles*: if two right triangles share the same
angle $\theta$, they are scaled copies of each other, so their side **ratios** are identical — even though
the triangles can be wildly different sizes. A ratio that depends only on the angle, not the size, deserves
its own name. We get three of them:

$$
\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}}, \qquad
\cos\theta = \frac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}}, \qquad
\tan\theta = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}.
$$

The mnemonic **SOH-CAH-TOA** packs all three: **S**ine = **O**pp/**H**yp, **C**osine = **A**dj/**H**yp,
**T**angent = **O**pp/**A**dj.

Drag the apex to reshape the right triangle. Watch the three ratios update — then try to **keep the angle θ
fixed** while changing the size, and notice the ratios stay put. That invariance is the whole point.

## The formal definition

For a right triangle with the angle $\theta$ (one of the two non-right angles):

$$
\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}, \quad
\cos\theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}, \quad
\tan\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}.
$$

Because the Pythagorean theorem says $\text{opp}^2 + \text{adj}^2 = \text{hyp}^2$, dividing through by
$\text{hyp}^2$ gives a relation you will see again and again:

$$
\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1.
$$

To solve the surveyor's problem: the height is the **opposite** side and the 50 m is **adjacent**, so use
tangent — $\tan(58°) = \frac{h}{50}$, giving $h = 50\tan(58°) \approx 80$ m.

<Callout type="why">
These ratios are the bridge between **angles and coordinates**, and machine learning lives on that bridge:
rotating data or images, decomposing a vector into components, and the attention "rotations" in modern
Transformers all reduce to multiplying by sines and cosines of an angle. Master the right triangle and the
geometry of high-dimensional models becomes far less mysterious.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Any time you know an angle and one length — or two lengths and want the angle — these ratios do the work.

<ExampleCard domain="Surveying" icon="📐">
A surveyor 50 m from a tower measures the angle to its top as 58°. Height is opposite, distance is adjacent,
so $h = 50\tan(58°) \approx 80$ m. This "angle of elevation" trick is how heights of mountains, trees and
chimneys are found without touching them.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Skateboarding" icon="🛹">
A skate ramp rises 1.2 m over a horizontal run of 2.0 m. The launch angle satisfies
$\tan\theta = \frac{1.2}{2.0} = 0.6$, and the ramp surface (the part you actually roll up) is the hypotenuse,
$\sqrt{1.2^2 + 2.0^2} \approx 2.33$ m of plywood to cut.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Ladder safety" icon="🪜">
Safety codes want a ladder at about 75° to the ground. For a 6 m ladder, the base should sit
$6\cos(75°) \approx 1.55$ m from the wall (adjacent = hyp·cos θ), and it reaches
$6\sin(75°) \approx 5.8$ m up (opposite = hyp·sin θ). Too shallow and it slides; too steep and it tips.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Aviation" icon="✈️">
On final approach a jet follows a 3° glide slope. To lose 900 m of altitude (the opposite side), the ground
distance is $\frac{900}{\tan(3°)} \approx 17.2$ km. Pilots and autoland systems treat the descent as one
long, thin right triangle.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Every example is the same triangle wearing a costume: an angle plus one side hands you the others through
sin, cos and tan. Tower, ramp, ladder, flight path — one set of ratios.
</Callout>
