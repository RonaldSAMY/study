## The hook: where do I point the telescope?

An amateur astronomer knows a planet is sitting 0.7 of the way up toward the zenith — a ratio her star chart
gave her — but the telescope mount wants an **angle** in degrees. She needs to run the trig *backwards*: not
"what's the sine of this angle," but "what angle has this sine?" That reverse operation is the **inverse trig
function**, and it is how every device that measures a ratio — a sensor, a controller, a star tracker — turns
that ratio back into an angle it can aim with.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Inverse trig functions undo sine, cosine and tangent: given a **ratio**, $\arcsin$, $\arccos$ and $\arctan$
hand you back the **angle** that produced it.

</Callout>

## Intuition: running the machine in reverse

You know how to go *forward*: feed an angle into $\sin$ and get a ratio out, like $\sin(30°) = 0.5$. The
inverse asks the reverse question — *"which angle gives a sine of 0.5?"* — and the answer is written
$\arcsin(0.5) = 30°$ (also spelled $\sin^{-1}$).

There's a catch, and it's the whole subtlety of this lesson. Many angles share the same sine: $30°$, $150°$,
$390°$, … all have sine $0.5$. If the inverse handed back *all* of them it wouldn't be a function. So we
**restrict the range** and agree on one canonical answer, the **principal value**:

- $\arcsin$ returns an angle in $\left[-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right]$ (i.e. $-90°$ to $90°$),
- $\arccos$ returns an angle in $\left[0, \pi\right]$ ($0°$ to $180°$),
- $\arctan$ returns an angle in $\left(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)$.

Those windows are chosen so each ratio maps to exactly one angle. When a real problem needs a different angle
(say one in the second quadrant), you take the principal value and adjust using symmetry.

## The formal definition

The inverse trig functions are defined as the inverses of sine, cosine and tangent **restricted** to the
ranges above:

$$
\theta = \arcsin(x) \iff \sin\theta = x \text{ and } \theta \in \left[-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right],
$$
$$
\theta = \arccos(x) \iff \cos\theta = x \text{ and } \theta \in [0, \pi],
$$
$$
\theta = \arctan(x) \iff \tan\theta = x \text{ and } \theta \in \left(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right).
$$

The domains: $\arcsin$ and $\arccos$ accept $x \in [-1, 1]$ (a sine or cosine can never exceed 1), while
$\arctan$ accepts any real $x$. They invert in the expected way on the principal range:
$\arcsin(\sin\theta) = \theta$ holds **only** when $\theta$ already lies in that range — a common trap.

<Callout type="why">
Wherever a model produces a ratio and you need a direction, you call an inverse. In robotics, **inverse
kinematics** uses $\arctan$ and $\arccos$ to turn a desired hand position into joint angles. The
two-argument $\operatorname{atan2}(y, x)$ — arctan that also tracks the quadrant — appears throughout
graphics, navigation, and the angle features engineered for ML models from sensor data.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Any device that *measures a ratio* and *needs an angle* runs an inverse trig function.

<ExampleCard domain="Astronomy" icon="🔭">
To aim at an object 0.7 of the way to the zenith, the mount needs elevation
$\arcsin(0.7) \approx 44.4°$. Computerised "go-to" telescopes evaluate exactly this inverse to convert chart
coordinates into motor angles for the altitude axis.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Renewable energy" icon="☀️">
A solar panel collects the most energy when tilted to face the sun. If the optimal setup makes the panel's
rise-to-run ratio $\tan\theta = 0.58$, the installer sets the tilt to $\arctan(0.58) \approx 30°$. Sun-tracking
mounts recompute this angle through the day.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Basketball" icon="🏀">
A coaching app films a free throw and measures the ball leaving with horizontal speed $v_x$ and vertical
speed $v_y$. The launch angle is $\arctan\!\left(\frac{v_y}{v_x}\right)$ — the app turns two measured speeds back
into the release angle to compare against the ideal ~52°.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Robotics" icon="🦾">
For a two-link arm to place its gripper at a target, inverse kinematics solves for the elbow angle with
$\arccos$ (from the law of cosines) and the shoulder angle with $\arctan$. The desired *position* (a pair of
ratios) becomes the *joint angles* the motors actually turn to.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The common move: a measurement gives you a ratio, but a motor, mount or model needs an angle. Inverse trig is
the one-way door from ratio space back to angle space — telescope, solar panel, basketball, robot arm alike.
</Callout>
