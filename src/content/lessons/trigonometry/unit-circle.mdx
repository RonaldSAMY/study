## The hook: how high are you on the Ferris wheel?

Step into a Ferris-wheel cabin and start to rise. Your **height** climbs, slows near the top, falls,
bottoms out, and climbs again — smoothly, forever, as long as the wheel turns. Plot that height against
time and you get one of the most important shapes in all of science: a **sine wave**. The astonishing
part is that this wave is hiding inside a single, simple picture — a point going around a circle of
radius 1. Learn that picture and you have learned the heart of trigonometry.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
On a circle of radius **1**, the point at angle $\theta$ has coordinates $(\cos\theta,\ \sin\theta)$.
That single fact *is* trigonometry — everything else is a consequence of it.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from a triangle to a spinning point

You may have met sine and cosine through **SOH-CAH-TOA** — ratios of the sides of a right triangle:

$$
\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}, \qquad
\cos\theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}.
$$

Now do something clever: make the **hypotenuse equal to 1**. Dividing by 1 changes nothing, so the
opposite side is *exactly* $\sin\theta$ and the adjacent side is *exactly* $\cos\theta$. The triangle no
longer just *contains* the ratios — its legs **are** the ratios.

Drop that triangle inside a circle of radius 1, with one corner at the centre. The far corner sits on
the circle at the point $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$. As you increase $\theta$, that point **spins around
the circle**. Watch only its height and you see it rise and fall and rise again — the same value comes
back every full turn. Spinning in a circle and repeating heights is *why* trig functions make **waves**.

## The formal definition

Place a point on the **unit circle** — the circle of radius $1$ centred at the origin — at angle
$\theta$ measured counter-clockwise from the positive $x$-axis. We *define*:

$$
\cos\theta = x\text{-coordinate}, \qquad \sin\theta = y\text{-coordinate}.
$$

Because every point on the circle satisfies $x^2 + y^2 = 1$, substituting immediately gives the most
famous identity in trigonometry, the **Pythagorean identity**:

$$
\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1.
$$

The **tangent** is their ratio (the slope of the radius line):

$$
\tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}.
$$

We measure the angle in **radians**, where the angle equals the arc length travelled around the unit
circle. A full trip around is the circumference $2\pi$, so $360° = 2\pi$ radians. Because going once
around returns you to the same point, sine and cosine are **periodic**:

$$
\sin(\theta + 2\pi) = \sin\theta, \qquad \cos(\theta + 2\pi) = \cos\theta.
$$

<Callout type="why">
Periodic functions are everywhere in machine learning and signals. **Fourier features** rewrite any
signal as a sum of sines and cosines; the **positional encodings** inside Transformers use
$\sin$ and $\cos$ of different frequencies to tell the model *where* each token sits; models of audio,
weather **seasonality**, and electrical signals all lean on the unit circle. Master this picture and a
huge amount of applied math suddenly rhymes.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The unit circle isn't just geometry homework. The same rise-and-fall pattern shows up wherever
something **goes around** or **swings back and forth**.

<ExampleCard domain="Amusement parks" icon="🎡">
A Ferris wheel turns at a steady rate, so your **height** above the ground is
$h(t) = h_0 + R\,\sin(\omega t)$ — the radius $R$ scales the circle's $\sin\theta$, and $\omega$ sets how
fast you go around. Your height is highest at the top, zero-change at the very top and bottom, and
changing fastest as you pass the middle — exactly the sine wave from the demo.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Audio & music" icon="🎵">
A **pure tone** is literally a sine wave of air pressure, $p(t) = A\,\sin(2\pi f t)$. The **frequency**
$f$ (cycles per second) is the **pitch** — concert A is $440$ Hz — and the amplitude $A$ is the loudness.
Every richer sound is a sum of such sine waves, which is exactly what a Fourier transform pulls apart.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Physics" icon="🔧">
A mass on a spring, or a gently swinging pendulum, traces **simple harmonic motion**: its position is
$x(t) = A\,\cos(\omega t)$. The bob sweeps back and forth just as the unit-circle point sweeps around,
and its shadow on the wall moves in a perfect cosine — the horizontal leg of the triangle, animated.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Earth & seasons" icon="🌅">
The length of **daylight** over a year rises and falls smoothly — long days at the solstice, short days
half a year later. To a good approximation it follows a sine, $D(t) \approx \bar{D} + A\,\sin\!\big(\tfrac{2\pi}{365}\,t\big)$,
which is why "seasonality" terms in forecasting models are built from sines and cosines.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the through-line: a wheel turning, air vibrating, a spring bouncing, the Earth tilting. Different
physics, identical math — a point circling the unit circle, its coordinates singing out $\cos\theta$ and
$\sin\theta$.
</Callout>
