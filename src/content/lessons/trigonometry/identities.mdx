## The hook: spinning a sprite without it wobbling

A game studio rotates thousands of sprites every frame. Naively, each rotation recomputes sines and cosines
from scratch — expensive, and over many frames tiny errors creep in and the sprite slowly stretches. The fix
is an **identity**: a rule guaranteeing that the rotation's sine and cosine combine to keep length *exactly*
1, no matter the angle. Identities are the equations of trigonometry that hold for **every** input, and they
are what let engineers rearrange, simplify, and trust their trig.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A trig **identity** is an equation true for *every* angle — like $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ — so you
can substitute one side for the other anytime, anywhere.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a true equation vs. an equation to solve

There is a quiet but crucial difference. An *equation* like $\sin\theta = 0.5$ is a question — it's true only
for special angles ($30°$, $150°$, …). An **identity** like $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ is a
*statement of fact* — it's true for **all** $\theta$. Identities are tools you apply; equations are problems
you solve.

The most important identity falls straight out of the unit circle. A point at angle $\theta$ has coordinates
$(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$, and every point on the unit circle satisfies $x^2 + y^2 = 1$. Substitute and you
have the **Pythagorean identity**:

$$
\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1.
$$

The other workhorses are the **angle-addition formulas**, which say what happens when you rotate by a sum of
two angles:

$$
\sin(\alpha + \beta) = \sin\alpha\cos\beta + \cos\alpha\sin\beta,
$$
$$
\cos(\alpha + \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta.
$$

Set $\alpha = \beta = \theta$ and these collapse into the **double-angle formulas**
$\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$ and $\cos 2\theta = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta$. From these few rules,
almost every other identity can be derived.

## The formal definition

The core identities you should know:

**Pythagorean (and its two cousins, dividing by $\cos^2$ or $\sin^2$):**

$$
\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1, \qquad 1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta.
$$

**Angle addition:**

$$
\sin(\alpha \pm \beta) = \sin\alpha\cos\beta \pm \cos\alpha\sin\beta,
$$
$$
\cos(\alpha \pm \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta \mp \sin\alpha\sin\beta.
$$

**Double angle** (special case $\alpha=\beta$):

$$
\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta, \qquad
\cos 2\theta = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta.
$$

**Co-function** (the "co" in cosine): $\sin\theta = \cos\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{2} - \theta\right)$.

<Callout type="why">
Angle-addition is exactly **2D rotation**: rotating a point by $\alpha$ then $\beta$ is rotating by
$\alpha+\beta$, and writing that out *is* the addition formula. Rotation matrices in computer graphics,
robotics and the rotary positional embeddings (RoPE) inside large language models all encode these
identities. The Pythagorean identity is also why a rotation never changes a vector's length — it keeps the
geometry of a model's representations intact.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Identities are not classroom curiosities — they keep real systems exact and efficient.

<ExampleCard domain="Computer graphics" icon="🎮">
To rotate a sprite by $\alpha + \beta$, the angle-addition formulas combine the two rotations into one,
saving repeated trig calls each frame. And because $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$, the rotation preserves every
sprite's size — no creeping distortion across thousands of frames.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Electrical power" icon="🔌">
Instantaneous AC power is $v\,i = V I\sin^2(\omega t)$, and the identity $\sin^2\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 - \cos 2\theta)$
splits it into a steady average plus a wobble at *twice* the line frequency. That algebraic split is how
engineers compute the real "average power" a device draws.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Optics" icon="🕶️">
Polarised sunglasses pass light by a factor of $\cos^2\theta$ (Malus's law), where $\theta$ is the angle
between filter and light. Using $\cos^2\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2\theta)$, designers predict exactly how
much glare two stacked polarisers cut as you rotate one.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Carpentry" icon="🪚">
Framing a roof, a carpenter often needs $\sin$ of a doubled pitch angle. Rather than re-measuring, they use
$\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$ to get the new cut from numbers already on the rafter square — an
identity doing arithmetic on the job site.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Across games, power grids, lenses and rooftops, the move is the same: replace a messy trig expression with an
equal, friendlier one. Identities are licensed substitutions that are always safe to make.
</Callout>
