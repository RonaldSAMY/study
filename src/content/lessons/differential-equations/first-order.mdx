## The hook: why a lump of carbon is a clock

A fragment of charcoal from an ancient fire is quietly losing carbon-14 atoms. The rule is breathtakingly
simple: *the number that decay each year is a fixed fraction of however many are left.* That one sentence
is a first-order differential equation, and solving it turns the charcoal into a clock accurate across
tens of thousands of years. Last lesson we learned to *read* such rules from a slope field. Now we learn
to **solve** them — to get an honest formula for the answer.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **first-order ODE** involves only the first derivative. The two kinds you can almost always crack are
**separable** (group $y$ on one side, $x$ on the other, integrate) and **linear** (a tidy
$y' + p(x)y = q(x)$ form with a standard recipe).
</Callout>

## Intuition: separate, then integrate

The most common first-order equation in nature is

$$
\frac{dy}{dt} = k\,y,
$$

"change in proportion to current amount." It is **separable**: shuffle all the $y$'s left and all the
$t$'s right, then integrate both sides — using exactly the integration skills from Calculus 1:

$$
\frac{1}{y}\,dy = k\,dt \;\Longrightarrow\; \int \frac{1}{y}\,dy = \int k\,dt \;\Longrightarrow\; \ln|y| = kt + C.
$$

Exponentiating gives the famous answer $y = y_0 e^{kt}$. The sign of $k$ decides the destiny: $k>0$ is
explosive growth, $k<0$ is decay toward zero. The constant $k$ is the single dial that controls how
sharply the whole family bends.

A close cousin, the **linear** first-order ODE $\frac{dy}{dt} = k(C - y)$, describes something *filling
toward a ceiling* — a capacitor charging, a cup warming, a drug saturating. Its solutions all curve up
and flatten out at the target $C$, fast when $k$ is large.

positive $k$ flares upward and negative $k$ melts toward zero; switch to charging mode to see curves race
up to a shared ceiling. The green curve is one highlighted member so you can track it.

## The formal definition

A first-order ODE is **separable** if it can be written

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = g(x)\,h(y) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \int \frac{dy}{h(y)} = \int g(x)\,dx.
$$

It is **linear** if it fits the standard form

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)\,y = q(x),
$$

which is solved by multiplying through by the **integrating factor** $\mu(x) = e^{\int p(x)\,dx}$, turning
the left side into the derivative of a product:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\big[\mu(x)\,y\big] = \mu(x)\,q(x) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad y = \frac{1}{\mu(x)}\int \mu(x)\,q(x)\,dx.
$$

For the pure-decay case $y' = -ky$ both methods agree on $y = y_0 e^{-kt}$.

<Callout type="why">
Exponential decay is everywhere in ML: learning-rate schedules decay as $\eta_t = \eta_0 e^{-\lambda t}$,
weight decay shrinks parameters toward zero, and exponential moving averages (the backbone of Adam and
momentum) are the discrete echo of $\frac{dy}{dt} = k(\text{new} - y)$.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Archaeology" icon="🪨">
Carbon-14 decays by $\frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N$, giving $N = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$ with a half-life of
5,730 years. Measure how much $^{14}\!C$ remains in a bone or charcoal, invert the formula, and you read
off its age. A first-order ODE is literally a clock.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Electronics" icon="⚡">
Charging a capacitor through a resistor obeys $\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{1}{RC}(V_s - V)$. The voltage rises
and levels off at the supply $V_s$, with the product $RC$ setting the pace — small $RC$ snaps full almost
instantly, large $RC$ eases up gently. This is the "charging" curve in the demo.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Pharmacology" icon="💊">
After an injection, a drug clears at $\frac{dc}{dt} = -k_e\,c$, an exponential decay. The clearance rate
$k_e$ tells doctors how often to redose so the concentration stays inside the safe-yet-effective window —
dosing schedules are first-order ODEs solved on a whiteboard.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Chemistry" icon="🧪">
Brine flows into a stirred tank and the well-mixed solution flows out. The salt mass follows the *linear*
ODE $\frac{dS}{dt} = r_{\text{in}} - \frac{r_{\text{out}}}{V}S$. The integrating factor handles it cleanly,
and the concentration drifts toward the inflow concentration — a classic mixing problem.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Decay, charging, dosing, mixing — different units, one mathematical skeleton. Spotting that a problem is
"separable" or "linear first-order" is half the battle; the integral recipes do the rest.
</Callout>
