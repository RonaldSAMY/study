## The hook: one toolkit, a thousand stories

A disease tearing through a city. Foxes and rabbits locked in an endless boom-and-bust. A skydiver
reaching a speed she cannot exceed. A pill rising and fading in your bloodstream. These look like four
unrelated stories — yet each is captured by writing down *how its quantities change* and letting the
differential equation tell the rest. **Modeling** is the art of translating a situation into an ODE; this
lesson is where that art pays off.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
To model anything, write one differential equation per quantity that says "rate of change = what adds in −
what leaves" — then solve (or simulate) to watch the story unfold.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the bookkeeping of change

Every model in this lesson is built by the same move: for each quantity, balance the **inflows** against
the **outflows**.

- **Decay/clearance:** something leaves in proportion to how much is there → $-k\,y$.
- **Interaction:** two populations meet at a rate proportional to the *product* of their sizes → $\pm b\,xy$.
- **Saturation:** a force or rate that grows until it cancels another → terminal velocity, charging.

When two quantities influence each other, you get a **system** of coupled ODEs. The predator equation
needs the prey count; the prey equation needs the predator count. They must be solved *together*, and the
result is often a rhythm no single equation could produce — like the eternal cycle of foxes chasing rabbits.

## The formal definition

A **system** of first-order ODEs evolves a state vector $\mathbf{y}(t)$ by a rule $\mathbf{y}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{y})$.
Four classics:

**Exponential / logistic growth** (single quantity, saturating):
$$
\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right).
$$

**SIR epidemic** (three coupled compartments):
$$
S' = -\beta S I, \qquad I' = \beta S I - \gamma I, \qquad R' = \gamma I.
$$

**Lotka–Volterra predator–prey** (two coupled populations):
$$
R' = aR - bRF, \qquad F' = -cF + dRF.
$$

**Newtonian motion with drag** (terminal velocity):
$$
m\frac{dv}{dt} = mg - k v^2.
$$

Each is just "rate of change = inflow − outflow," written once per quantity. The **equilibrium** of any
model is where every rate is zero — the epidemic's burnout, the skydiver's terminal speed, the
predator-prey balance point.

<Callout type="why">
These exact systems are training data and architecture in modern ML. Epidemic and ecological models are
fit to data with **neural ODEs**; reinforcement-learning agents are trained inside physics simulators that
integrate equations of motion; pharmacokinetic ODEs power AI dosing recommendations.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Epidemiology" icon="🤒">
The **SIR** model splits a population into Susceptible, Infected, and Recovered. Infections occur when the
two groups mix ($\beta S I$), recoveries drain the infected pool ($\gamma I$). The ratio
$R_0 = \beta S_0/\gamma$ predicts whether an outbreak explodes or fizzles — the number every public-health
agency watched in 2020.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Ecology" icon="🦊">
Foxes and rabbits follow Lotka–Volterra. Rabbits breed freely but are eaten; foxes starve without rabbits
but thrive when prey is plentiful. The coupling produces self-sustaining **cycles** — a population boom is
always followed, with a lag, by a predator boom and then a crash.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Environmental science" icon="🌊">
A pollutant entering a lake and flushing out with the outflow obeys $\frac{dC}{dt} = \frac{Q}{V}(C_{\text{in}} - C)$.
Stop the source ($C_{\text{in}}=0$) and the concentration decays exponentially with time constant $V/Q$ —
the lake's natural flushing time, which tells regulators how long a cleanup will take.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Aerospace" icon="🚀">
A rocket or falling body with quadratic drag obeys $m\dot v = mg - kv^2$. Set $\dot v = 0$ and you get
terminal velocity $v_\infty = \sqrt{mg/k}$ straight from the equation — no solving required. Equilibrium
analysis reveals the ending before you ever integrate.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Every scenario was built by the same recipe: name the quantities, write a rate equation for each, identify
inflows and outflows, then find equilibria. Master that recipe and you can model almost anything that
changes over time.
</Callout>
