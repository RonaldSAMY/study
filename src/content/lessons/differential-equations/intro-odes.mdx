## The hook: the rule that doesn't tell you *where*, only *how fast*

Imagine a skydiver leaping from a plane. Nobody hands you a formula for her speed at every second.
What you *do* know is the rule the speed must follow: *gravity speeds her up, air resistance slows her
down, and the faster she falls the harder the air pushes back.* That single sentence — a statement
about the **rate of change** — is a **differential equation**. The whole field is the art of turning
"here's the rule for change" into "here's what actually happens."

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **differential equation** is an equation whose unknown is a *function*, and which relates that
function to its own **derivative** — it tells you the slope the solution must have at every point.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from a rate to a path

In calculus you learned that the derivative $\frac{dy}{dx}$ is the **slope** of a curve. An ordinary
differential equation (**ODE**) flips the script: instead of being handed $y$ and asked for its slope,
you are handed the *slope rule* and asked to find $y$.

The simplest ODE you already solved without naming it. When you computed an integral
$y = \int f(x)\,dx$, you were solving

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x).
$$

That is a differential equation! The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus *is* a recipe for solving the
easiest kind. Real ODEs are richer because the rate can depend on the current value too, as in
$\frac{dy}{dx} = y$ — "grow in proportion to how big you already are."

Because integration always leaves a "+ C", a single ODE has a whole **family** of solutions. Pin down
one specific curve by giving a starting point, called an **initial condition** like $y(0) = 2$.

## The formal definition

An **ordinary differential equation** is an equation involving an unknown function $y(x)$ and its
derivatives. Its **order** is the highest derivative that appears. A general first-order ODE looks like

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x, y),
$$

and a **solution** is any function $y(x)$ that makes the equation true on an interval. The **general
solution** carries arbitrary constants (one per order); an **initial value problem** adds a condition
such as $y(x_0) = y_0$ to select a single curve:

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x, y), \qquad y(x_0) = y_0.
$$

For example, $\frac{dy}{dx} = y$ has general solution $y = Ce^{x}$. The condition $y(0) = 2$ forces
$C = 2$, so the one true curve is $y = 2e^{x}$.

<Callout type="why">
Machine learning is differential equations in disguise. Training a network with gradient descent solves
$\frac{d\theta}{dt} = -\nabla L(\theta)$ — "move the parameters opposite the loss gradient." Diffusion
image generators, neural ODEs, and physics-informed networks are all built on exactly this language.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Skydiving" icon="🪂">
A diver's speed obeys $\frac{dv}{dt} = g - \tfrac{k}{m}v^2$: gravity adds speed, drag removes it in
proportion to $v^2$. You can read the ending off the equation without solving it — when the two terms
cancel, $\frac{dv}{dt}=0$ and she stops accelerating. That balance speed is **terminal velocity**.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Personal finance" icon="💰">
Continuously compounded savings follow $\frac{dB}{dt} = rB$: the balance grows at a rate proportional to
the balance itself. More money earns more interest, which earns more interest — the slope field tilts
ever steeper as you climb, exactly the runaway shape of compound growth.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Microbiology" icon="🦠">
A young bacterial colony with unlimited food doubles on a schedule: $\frac{dN}{dt} = \mu N$. Each cell
divides independently, so total births scale with the current count. The same equation as the bank
account — biology and finance share one differential rule.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Engineering" icon="🪣">
Water draining from a tank obeys Torricelli's law, $\frac{dh}{dt} = -c\sqrt{h}$: a deeper tank pushes
water out faster, so the level falls quickly at first and then crawls. The rate depends on the current
height — you can't know $h(t)$ until you solve the ODE.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice none of these gave a formula for the quantity directly. Each gave a *rule for its rate*. That's
the signature of a differential equation — and why slope fields are such a powerful way to see them.
</Callout>
