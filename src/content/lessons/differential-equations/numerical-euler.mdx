## The hook: the equation no one can solve

The predator-prey and epidemic models you just played with have a dirty secret: most of them have **no
closed-form solution**. There is no neat formula to plug $t$ into. Yet weather services, game engines, and
spacecraft navigate by these equations every second. How? They cheat — beautifully. Instead of solving for
all time, they take the slope you *do* know right now and step a tiny bit forward, then repeat. That is
**Euler's method**, the humble idea behind nearly every simulation on Earth.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Euler's method** turns an ODE into a recipe: from where you are, follow the current slope for one small
step $h$, land at a new point, and repeat — trading a perfect formula for a good-enough chain of straight lines.
</Callout>

## Intuition: walking along the slope field

Remember the slope field from the very first lesson? At every point the ODE $y' = f(x, y)$ hands you a
slope. Euler's method literally *walks* that field:

1. Start at the known point $(x_0, y_0)$.
2. Read the slope $f(x_0, y_0)$ there.
3. Step forward by $h$ in $x$, rising by slope $\times h$: $y_1 = y_0 + h\,f(x_0, y_0)$.
4. You're at a new point. Repeat.

Each step is a tiny straight-line tangent guess. String them together and you get a polyline that *shadows*
the true curve. The catch: each step drifts a little off, and errors accumulate. The fix is intuitive — take
**smaller steps**. Halve $h$ and you roughly halve the error, at the cost of twice the work. That tradeoff,
accuracy versus computation, is the central tension of all numerical computing.

## The formal definition

For the initial value problem $y' = f(x, y),\; y(x_0) = y_0$, **Euler's method** generates approximations
$y_n \approx y(x_n)$ at points $x_n = x_0 + nh$ via the update

$$
y_{n+1} = y_n + h\,f(x_n, y_n), \qquad x_{n+1} = x_n + h.
$$

This is just the first two terms of the Taylor expansion $y(x+h) = y(x) + h\,y'(x) + \tfrac{h^2}{2}y''(\xi)$.
The dropped term makes the **local error** per step $O(h^2)$; accumulated over $\sim 1/h$ steps, the **global
error** is $O(h)$ — Euler is a **first-order** method. Halving $h$ halves the error:

$$
\text{global error} \approx C\,h.
$$

Smarter schemes (Runge–Kutta 4, the method powering the demos in earlier lessons) reuse several slope samples
per step to reach global error $O(h^4)$ — vastly more accurate for the same $h$.

<Callout type="why">
Euler's update *is* gradient descent. Training a model solves $\frac{d\theta}{dt} = -\nabla L(\theta)$ by
Euler steps $\theta_{n+1} = \theta_n - \eta\,\nabla L(\theta_n)$, where the learning rate $\eta$ is exactly
the step size $h$. Too large and you overshoot and diverge; too small and training crawls — the very same
tradeoff you are dragging on the slider. That is your bridge to the next track.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Meteorology" icon="🌦️">
Weather models step the fluid-dynamics equations forward over a grid of the atmosphere, a few minutes of
simulated time per step. Too large a step and the simulation becomes unstable and blows up; that stability
limit is why forecasts demand supercomputers churning through billions of tiny Euler-like updates.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Game development" icon="🎮">
Every game physics engine integrates motion each frame: $v \mathrel{+}= a\,\Delta t$, then $x \mathrel{+}= v\,\Delta t$.
That's Euler (its "semi-implicit" cousin) with $h$ = the frame time. Low frame rates mean big steps, which is
why physics can glitch — characters tunneling through walls — when a game stutters.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Spaceflight" icon="🛰️">
Spacecraft trajectories integrate gravitational equations of motion numerically — no closed form exists for
three or more bodies. Mission planners use tiny, carefully controlled steps (and higher-order methods) so
errors stay small over months of coasting between planets.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Robotics" icon="🤖">
A robot's controller predicts where its arm will be a moment from now by Euler-stepping its dynamics, then
corrects. The control loop runs at a fixed rate, so its step size $h$ is the loop period — faster loops mean
smaller steps and steadier, more precise motion.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
From thunderstorms to spaceflight, the recipe never changes: read the slope, take a small step, repeat. The
only knob is $h$ — and choosing it well is the difference between a stable simulation and a crash.
</Callout>
