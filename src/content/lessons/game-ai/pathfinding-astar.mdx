## The hook: the unit that walks around the wall

You right-click across the map in a strategy game and your soldier glides smoothly around a lake, through
a gap in the trees, and up to the spot you clicked. It never bumps the wall, never doubles back, never
takes the long way. Behind that one click is a tiny piece of brilliance that runs millions of times a day
in games, robots and map apps: a **graph search** that finds the *shortest* path without checking every
possible route. The crown jewel of those searches is **A\***.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A\* finds the shortest path by always expanding the spot that looks most promising — the one whose
**cost so far plus an optimistic guess of the cost remaining** is smallest.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a smarter flood fill

From the **graphs** lesson you know a map is just **nodes** (places you can stand) joined by **edges**
(moves you can make), each edge carrying a **cost**. Pathfinding is the question: what is the cheapest
chain of edges from start to goal?

The simplest correct answer is **Dijkstra's algorithm**: flood outward from the start, always growing the
frontier at the cheapest-known node first. It is guaranteed to find the shortest path — but it spreads in
*every* direction equally, like ripples in a pond, exploring tons of cells that point away from the goal.

A\* adds one ingredient: a **heuristic**, a cheap guess of how far each cell still is from the goal. By
preferring cells that are both cheap to reach *and* estimated to be close to the goal, A\* aims the flood
like a flashlight instead of a floodlight. Same shortest path, a fraction of the work.

We score every candidate cell $n$ with:

$$
f(n) = g(n) + h(n)
$$

where $g(n)$ is the real cost to reach $n$ from the start, and $h(n)$ is the heuristic estimate of the
cost from $n$ to the goal. Dijkstra is just A\* with $h(n) = 0$ everywhere.

## The formal definition

On a graph with non-negative edge costs, A\* keeps a priority queue (the **open set**) ordered by $f(n)$.
Each step it pops the lowest-$f$ node, marks it closed, and **relaxes** its neighbours: if going through
the current node reaches a neighbour more cheaply than any path found so far, record the new cost and
push it.

$$
g(\text{nb}) \leftarrow g(\text{cur}) + \text{cost}(\text{cur}, \text{nb}), \qquad
f(\text{nb}) = g(\text{nb}) + h(\text{nb}).
$$

```python
for nb in neighbors(current):
    tentative = g[current] + cost(current, nb)
    if tentative < g[nb]:          # found a cheaper way to nb
        g[nb] = tentative
        f[nb] = tentative + heuristic(nb, goal)
        came_from[nb] = current     # remember the path
```

On a 4-connected grid the natural heuristic is the **Manhattan distance** (you can only step along axes):

$$
h(n) = |n_x - \text{goal}_x| + |n_y - \text{goal}_y|.
$$

The magic property is **admissibility**: as long as $h$ never *overestimates* the true remaining cost,
A\* is guaranteed to return the genuinely shortest path. Manhattan distance is admissible on a grid
because the real route can only be longer once walls get in the way.

<Callout type="why">
This is the search at the heart of game AI, and the same template runs over <em>any</em> graph — road
networks, dialogue trees, even the state space of a puzzle. Pick the right heuristic and an
exponential-looking problem becomes interactive-fast. Pick one that overestimates and you trade a little
optimality for even more speed (that is what "weighted A\*" does on purpose).
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Real-time strategy" icon="⚔️">
When you order a squad across an RTS map, each unit runs A\* over a navigation grid. The terrain cost of
a swamp cell can be set high so units *prefer* the road — A\* naturally trades a longer-but-faster route
for a shorter-but-slower one, because cost, not raw distance, is what it minimises.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Warehouse robotics" icon="📦">
A fulfilment-centre robot must reach a shelf without crashing into racks or other robots. The floor is a
grid graph; A\* plans the route, and the heuristic (straight-line distance to the bay) keeps it from
wandering down the wrong aisle. Thousands of robots replan continuously as the warehouse changes.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sliding-tile puzzles" icon="🧩">
The 15-puzzle isn't a map at all — each *arrangement* of tiles is a node, and a move is an edge. A\*
solves it by treating "number of tiles out of place" (or summed Manhattan distances of every tile) as the
heuristic, slicing a search space of billions down to something solvable in milliseconds.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Ride-hailing dispatch" icon="🚕">
Matching a driver to a rider means searching a city road graph where edges are streets weighted by live
traffic. A\* with a distance-based heuristic finds the quickest pickup route far faster than flooding the
whole map — and reruns the instant a road jams up.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the leap: the first two examples are literally maps, but the puzzle is a graph of <em>game
states</em> with no geometry at all. A\* doesn't care — it only needs nodes, edge costs, and an
admissible guess of the distance remaining.
</Callout>
