## The hook: how does a Sim decide to eat?

A character in *The Sims* is hungry, tired, bored, and a little bit lonely all at once. No tree of yes/no questions captures the *trade-off* — eat now, or sleep first, or grab a quick chat? The game's answer is refreshingly numeric: every possible action rates how good an idea it is right now, as a single score, and the character just does whatever scores highest. This is **utility AI**, and it shines exactly where behavior trees feel rigid: messy situations with many competing priorities.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Utility AI** scores each candidate action with a number computed from the agent's current needs, then picks the action with the **highest score** — turning decision-making into "evaluate and take the max".
</Callout>

## Intuition: from yes/no to how-much

A behavior tree asks **boolean** questions: is the enemy visible? do I have ammo? Great for clear priorities, awkward when everything is a matter of degree. Utility AI replaces *whether* with *how much*.

The key tool is the **utility curve** (also called a *response curve*): a function that maps a need, measured on a 0-to-1 scale, to a desirability, also 0 to 1. The shape encodes your design intent:

- A **linear** curve says desirability rises steadily with the need.
- A **quadratic** curve (the need squared) stays low until the need gets high, then shoots up — perfect for "only panic when threat is *really* high".
- An **inverse** curve (one minus the need) makes an action attractive when a need is *low* — you only Rest when you are *not* hungry.

Each action combines a few such curves into one score, often by multiplying them so that *any* disqualifying factor (a high threat while eating) drags the whole score down. Then the agent compares all scores and takes the maximum. Change the inputs and the winner can change smoothly, which is why utility agents feel less robotic than rule-based ones.

## The formal definition

Each action $a$ has a score built from utility curves $u_1, u_2, \dots$ evaluated on the relevant inputs $x_i$. The most common scheme multiplies them:

$$
\text{Score}(a) = \prod_i u_i(x_i),
$$

and the agent chooses

$$
a^\star = \arg\max_{a \in A}\; \text{Score}(a).
$$

A curve is just a function $u : [0,1] \to [0,1]$. Three handy shapes:

$$
u_{\text{lin}}(x) = x, \qquad
u_{\text{quad}}(x) = x^2, \qquad
u_{\text{inv}}(x) = 1 - x.
$$

So "eat" might score $\text{hunger} \times (1 - \text{threat})$ — appealing when you are hungry **and** safe — while "flee" scores $\text{threat}^2$, ignoring hunger entirely. Multiplication acts like a soft AND: a single near-zero factor vetoes the action.

<Callout type="why">
Utility AI degrades gracefully. A behavior tree must have an explicit branch for every situation, but a utility agent simply scores whatever actions exist — add a new action with its own curve and it competes automatically, no rewiring. This is also the conceptual bridge to machine learning: a learned policy is, in the end, a function that scores actions and takes the best one, exactly like a utility curve you didn't have to hand-author.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

"Score the options, take the best" is one of the most reused ideas in all of computing.

<ExampleCard domain="Personal finance" icon="💳">
A budgeting app ranks where your next dollar should go: paying high-interest debt scores higher as the rate climbs, topping up savings scores higher when the balance is low. You follow the highest-scoring suggestion — a utility decision.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Healthcare logistics" icon="🏥">
An organ-transplant system scores each waiting patient on urgency, match quality, and wait time, then offers the organ to the top score. The curves are debated for years precisely because their *shapes* encode ethical priorities.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Streaming infrastructure" icon="📶">
A video player constantly scores quality levels: higher resolution is desirable, but its score collapses as the buffer drains. Each second it picks the bitrate with the best score — smooth playback from a running max.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports strategy" icon="⚽">
A football analytics model scores every pass option by chance of success times value gained. Coaches use it to ask whether a player consistently chose the lower-utility play — the same argmax, applied to humans.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The danger of utility AI is *tuning*: dozens of curves interact, and a bad shape gives weird choices that are hard to trace. That is the flip side of its flexibility — there are no explicit branches to read, only numbers to balance.
</Callout>
