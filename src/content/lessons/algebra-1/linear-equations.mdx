## The hook: how many weeks until the trip?

You have \$3 saved and you add \$2 to a jar every week. The road trip needs \$11. How many weeks, $x$, until you can
go? In symbols that's $2x + 3 = 11$. You don't have to guess — there's a reliable procedure that *unwinds* the equation
and hands you the answer. The trick is to picture the equals sign as the pivot of a **balance scale**: as long as you do
the same thing to both pans, it never tips, and the truth is preserved at every step.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
An **equation** says two expressions are equal; you **solve** it by applying the same operation to both sides until the
unknown stands alone — like keeping a scale balanced while you remove weights.

</Callout>

## Intuition: an expression set equal to something

Last lesson we built and simplified expressions like $2x + 3$. An **equation** takes such an expression and asserts it
equals another value: $2x + 3 = 11$. Solving means finding the variable value that makes the statement true.

The golden rule is **balance**. The two sides are equal *right now*, so any operation you apply to one side you must apply
to the other to keep them equal:

1. **Undo addition** by subtracting the same amount from both sides.
2. **Undo multiplication** by dividing both sides by the same number.

You peel operations off in reverse PEMDAS order — strip the added constant first, then divide out the coefficient — until
only $x$ remains.

An **inequality** ($<, >, \le, \ge$) follows the same moves, with one twist: multiplying or dividing both sides by a
**negative** number flips the direction of the sign.

## The formal definition

A **linear equation** in one variable has the form

$$
a\,x + b = c, \qquad a \neq 0,
$$

and its solution is found by isolating $x$:

$$
a\,x + b = c \;\Longrightarrow\; a\,x = c - b \;\Longrightarrow\; x = \frac{c - b}{a}.
$$

For our example, $a = 2,\ b = 3,\ c = 11$ gives

$$
x = \frac{11 - 3}{2} = \frac{8}{2} = 4.
$$

An **inequality** replaces $=$ with a comparison, e.g.

$$
a\,x + b \le c,
$$

and the same isolation works — but **reverse the sign** when you multiply or divide by a negative:

$$
-2x \le 6 \;\Longrightarrow\; x \ge -3.
$$

<Callout type="why">
Training a model means solving for the values that make an equation (the gradient set to zero) hold. The balance
principle — do the same thing to both sides — is the bedrock of every algebraic manipulation that derivation relies on.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Setting two quantities equal and solving for the unknown is one of the most useful moves in all of practical math.

<ExampleCard domain="Personal budgeting" icon="🏦">
Saving for a trip: $2x + 3 = 11$, where $x$ is weeks. Subtract the \$3 you already have, divide by the \$2 weekly
deposit, and $x = 4$ weeks. The balance scale *is* your savings jar.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Road trips" icon="⛽">
A tank holds 12 gallons and you burn 0.4 gallons per mile of hills. How many hill-miles $m$ empties it from full?
$12 - 0.4m = 0$ gives $m = 30$. Solving tells you when to refuel *before* you're stranded.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Fitness" icon="🏃">
You burn 300 calories at the start plus 8 per minute. To hit a 700-calorie workout: $300 + 8t = 700$, so $t = 50$ minutes.
Change the goal and the same equation re-solves instantly.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Cooking" icon="🧁">
A bakery needs *at least* 48 cupcakes; each tray holds 12. Trays $t$ must satisfy $12t \ge 48$, so $t \ge 4$. The
inequality gives a whole **range** — 4 trays or more — not a single number.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Equations pin down one exact value; inequalities describe a band of acceptable values. Both are solved by the same
balance moves — just keep the sign honest when negatives appear.
</Callout>
