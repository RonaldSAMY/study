## The hook: where exactly is the tomato plant?

You're mapping a vegetable garden so you can tell a friend where to water. "Near the fence" is hopeless. But "3 beds to the
right of the gate and 2 beds up" pins it exactly. Two numbers, in a fixed order, name a single spot with zero ambiguity.
That pair is a **coordinate**, and the grid it lives on — the **coordinate plane** — is how algebra turns numbers into
pictures and pictures back into numbers.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The **coordinate plane** locates any point with an ordered pair $(x, y)$: how far across, then how far up — and that link
between numbers and positions is what lets us *draw* relationships.
</Callout>

## Intuition: two number lines, crossed

You already solved equations to get single numbers on a number line. Now cross **two** number lines at right angles: a
horizontal **x-axis** and a vertical **y-axis**, meeting at the **origin** $(0, 0)$. Every point is named by an **ordered
pair** $(x, y)$:

- the **x-coordinate** says how far *right* (positive) or *left* (negative) from the origin,
- the **y-coordinate** says how far *up* (positive) or *down* (negative).

Order matters: $(3, 2)$ and $(2, 3)$ are different spots — always read **x first**, like reading before climbing.

The two axes slice the plane into four **quadrants**, numbered counter-clockwise starting top-right (I), where both
coordinates are positive, around to bottom-right (IV), where $x>0$ and $y<0$.

## The formal definition

The **Cartesian plane** is the set of all ordered pairs of real numbers:

$$
\mathbb{R}^2 = \{\,(x, y) \mid x \in \mathbb{R},\ y \in \mathbb{R}\,\}.
$$

The point $(x, y)$ sits $x$ units along the horizontal axis and $y$ units along the vertical axis from the origin
$(0,0)$. The signs of the coordinates fix the quadrant:

$$
\text{I}: (+,+), \quad \text{II}: (-,+), \quad \text{III}: (-,-), \quad \text{IV}: (+,-).
$$

The straight-line distance between two points comes from the Pythagorean theorem:

$$
d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
A data point with two features — say a house's size and price — *is* a point in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Machine learning plots
thousands of such points and looks for patterns among them. The coordinate plane is the canvas the whole field draws on.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Pairs of numbers naming positions appear far beyond graph paper.

<ExampleCard domain="Gardening" icon="🥕">
A garden map: the tomato is at $(3, 2)$ — 3 beds right of the gate, 2 beds up. Anyone with the same grid finds the exact
plant. The ordered pair removes all guesswork.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Video games" icon="🎮">
Every sprite on screen has a position $(x, y)$ in pixels from a corner. Moving a character is just changing its
coordinates each frame; a collision is two points landing close together.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports analytics" icon="🏀">
A shot chart records where each attempt was taken as a point on the court. Clusters of made shots in one region reveal a
player's hot zone — pure coordinate data turned into strategy.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Weather tracking" icon="🌡️">
Plot the day number across and the temperature up: each reading is a point. Connect them and a week of weather becomes a
visible trend line — numbers made into a picture.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Whether it's beds, pixels, court positions, or temperatures, the move is identical: pick an origin and two directions, and
every location becomes a pair of numbers you can compute with.
</Callout>
