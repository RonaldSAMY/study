## The hook: how fast is the trip ticking by?

On a road trip your odometer reads 60 miles after 1 hour, 120 after 2 hours, 180 after 3. Every hour adds the same 60
miles. Plot distance against time and the points fall on a perfectly straight line — and its **steepness is your speed**.
That constant "amount of change per step" is the single most useful number about a relationship, and it has a name:
**slope**. Master it here and you've met the workhorse of all of applied math.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **linear function** changes by a constant amount per step; that rate is the **slope** $m$, and the line is described
completely by $y = mx + b$, where $b$ is its starting height.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the function whose rate never changes

You just met functions as input → output machines. A **linear** function is the simplest kind: each step in $x$ changes
$y$ by the *same* amount. That fixed amount is the **slope**

$$
m = \frac{\text{change in } y}{\text{change in } x} = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}.
$$

A slope of $60$ means "$y$ goes up 60 for every 1 step right." Positive slope climbs, negative slope falls, and zero slope
is flat.

The other number you need is where the line *starts* — its height when $x = 0$. That's the **y-intercept** $b$, the point
$(0, b)$ where the line crosses the vertical axis. Together they give the famous **slope-intercept form**

$$
y = m x + b,
$$

which you've actually been using all along: the phone bill $20 + 5g$ is a line with intercept $20$ and slope $5$.

## The formal definition

A **linear function** has the form

$$
f(x) = m x + b,
$$

where $m$ is the **slope** and $b$ the **y-intercept**. Given any two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ on the line, the
slope is

$$
m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}.
$$

For the road trip, two readings $(1, 60)$ and $(3, 180)$ give

$$
m = \frac{180 - 60}{3 - 1} = \frac{120}{2} = 60 \text{ mph}, \qquad b = 0,
$$

so $y = 60x$. The defining feature of a line is that this ratio is **the same no matter which two points you choose** — the
rate of change is constant.

<Callout type="why">
Linear regression — fitting the best line $y = mx + b$ through data — is the "hello world" of machine learning. The slope
$m$ is exactly a model's *weight*: how strongly the output responds to the input. Gradient descent's whole job is to find
good values of $m$ and $b$.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

A constant rate of change plus a starting value describes an enormous range of everyday situations.

<ExampleCard domain="Road trips" icon="🛣️">
Distance vs. time at steady speed: $y = 60x$. Slope $60$ is your speed in mph, intercept $0$ because you start at mile
zero. Double the slope and you've doubled your pace.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Phone data plans" icon="📱">
The bill $C = 20 + 5g$ is a line: intercept \$20 (the flat fee, paid even at $g=0$) and slope \$5 per gigabyte. The
slope is the price of one more GB.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Gym membership" icon="🏋️">
A \$30 sign-up plus \$10 per visit: $\text{cost} = 30 + 10v$. Intercept $30$ is the joining fee; slope $10$ is the
per-visit rate. Comparing two gyms is just comparing two lines.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Gardening" icon="🌿">
A seedling 4 cm tall growing 2 cm per week: $h = 4 + 2w$. Intercept $4$ is its height today, slope $2$ is the weekly
growth rate. The line predicts when it reaches the trellis.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
In every case the intercept $b$ is "the value when nothing has happened yet" and the slope $m$ is "how much it changes per
step." Spot those two numbers and you've understood the whole relationship.
</Callout>
