## The hook: one formula, every month's bill

Your phone plan charges a flat \$20 plus \$5 for every extra gigabyte you use. In March you used 3 GB, in April 7 GB,
in May 2 GB. You *could* write a fresh sum each month — but it's the same shape every time. So you write it **once**, with a
letter standing in for "however many gigabytes": $20 + 5g$. Now one expression covers every month, past and future. That
letter is a **variable**, and learning to write and tidy such expressions is the real start of algebra.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **variable** is a letter holding a number you don't know yet (or that can change); an **expression** combines variables
and numbers with operations, and **simplifying** means writing the same expression in its shortest equal form.
</Callout>

## Intuition: building on the order of operations

In the last lesson every quantity was a fixed number and PEMDAS told you how to combine them. Nothing about those rules
changes here — we just allow some of the numbers to be **letters**. The expression $20 + 5g$ still means "multiply $g$ by
5 first, then add 20," exactly as PEMDAS demands.

Two pieces of vocabulary make the rest easy:

- A **term** is a chunk separated by $+$ or $-$. In $2x + 3x + 4y - 5$ the terms are $2x$, $3x$, $4y$, and $-5$.
- The **coefficient** is the number multiplying a variable. In $5g$ the coefficient is $5$.

**Like terms** carry the *same* variable, so they can be merged: $2x + 3x = 5x$, just as 2 apples plus 3 apples is 5
apples. You cannot merge $5x$ with $4y$ any more than you can add apples to oranges — different variables stay separate.

## The formal definition

An **expression** is a finite combination of numbers, variables, and operations, for example

$$
2x + 3x + 4y - 5.
$$

Each additive piece is a **term**; a term's numeric multiplier is its **coefficient**. Two terms are **like terms** when
they have identical variable parts. Combining them uses the **distributive law** in reverse:

$$
2x + 3x = (2 + 3)\,x = 5x.
$$

More generally, for any like terms with the same variable,

$$
a\,x + b\,x = (a + b)\,x.
$$

The distributive law also expands a factor over a sum:

$$
k\,(x + y) = k\,x + k\,y.
$$

<Callout type="why">
A machine-learning model is, at heart, a giant expression in many variables (the weights). Simplifying and combining like
terms is exactly what libraries do to evaluate those expressions efficiently — fewer operations, same value, faster
training.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

A variable is just a placeholder for "the amount that can change." That shows up everywhere.

<ExampleCard domain="Phone data plans" icon="📱">
A plan costs $20 + 5g$ dollars, where $g$ is gigabytes of overage. The variable lets one formula price *every* customer
and every month. Plug in $g = 3$ to get \$35; plug in $g = 7$ to get \$55.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Road trips" icon="🚗">
Fuel for a trip of $d$ miles in a car doing 30 mpg, with gas at \$4/gallon, is $\tfrac{4}{30}\,d$ dollars. Drive twice as
far and the expression doubles — the variable $d$ carries the "how far."
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Gardening" icon="🌻">
Fertilizer for $r$ rows at 2 scoops each, plus 3 scoops for the herb pots, is $2r + 3$. Add a second batch of $4r$ and you
collect like terms: $2r + 4r + 3 = 6r + 3$ — same scoops, tidier formula.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Music streaming" icon="🎧">
A playlist's total minutes is $3.5s + 4a$, where $s$ is the number of short songs (3.5 min each) and $a$ the number of
long ones (4 min each). Different variables, so the terms stay separate — you can't merge songs of different length.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
In every case the variable is "the part that varies." Writing the relationship once, with a letter, beats rewriting a new
sum for every situation.
</Callout>
