## The hook: a recipe that doubles itself

You are doubling a soup recipe. It calls for *"3 cups of stock plus 2 cans of beans, where each can is 1.5 cups."* Do you write
$3 + 2 \times 1.5$ and read it left to right to get $7.5$? Or do you multiply first to get $3 + 3 = 6$? Those are
**different amounts of soup** — and only one is right. The number isn't ambiguous; the *order you combine* the numbers is.
Before algebra can use letters, we have to agree on exactly how numbers combine. That agreement is this lesson.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Arithmetic has a fixed **order of operations** — parentheses, then multiplication and division, then addition and
subtraction — so that every person (and every computer) reads the same expression the same way.
</Callout>

## Intuition: numbers, then the rules that bind them

You already count, add, and split things into fractions every day. Algebra just makes the rules explicit so they never
get fuzzy. Three things are worth naming clearly:

1. **Integers** — the whole numbers and their negatives: $\dots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \dots$. Negatives are just "the other
   direction" (owing money, below sea level, below zero).
2. **Fractions** — a part of a whole, $\tfrac{3}{4}$, which is the same as the division $3 \div 4 = 0.75$.
3. **Order of operations** — when several operations sit in one expression, we don't read strictly left to right. We do
   **P**arentheses, then **E**xponents, then **M**ultiplication/**D**ivision, then **A**ddition/**S**ubtraction (PEMDAS).

Parentheses are the override switch: anything inside them happens *first*, no matter what. That single power is what lets
the same digits and signs mean different things.

## The formal definition

An **integer** is any number in the set

$$
\mathbb{Z} = \{\,\dots, -2,\, -1,\, 0,\, 1,\, 2,\, \dots\,\}.
$$

A **rational number** (fraction) is any ratio of two integers with a non-zero denominator:

$$
\frac{p}{q}, \qquad p, q \in \mathbb{Z}, \quad q \neq 0.
$$

The **order of operations** says that to evaluate an expression you resolve, in this priority:

$$
\text{Parentheses} \;\to\; \text{Exponents} \;\to\; \text{Multiply / Divide} \;\to\; \text{Add / Subtract},
$$

working left to right within each equal-priority level. So

$$
3 + 2 \times 1.5 = 3 + 3 = 6, \qquad \text{but} \qquad (3 + 2) \times 1.5 = 5 \times 1.5 = 7.5.
$$

<Callout type="why">
Computers evaluate billions of expressions exactly this way. Every line of machine-learning code — a loss like
$\tfrac{1}{n}\sum (y - \hat{y})^2$ — relies on the interpreter applying these precedence rules consistently. Get the order
wrong and the model "learns" the wrong thing.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The order of operations isn't a school ritual — it is how careful totals get computed everywhere.

<ExampleCard domain="Cooking" icon="🍲">
Scaling a recipe: *base 3 cups of stock, plus 2 cans at 1.5 cups each.* Written
$3 + 2 \times 1.5$, multiplication binds the cans to their size first, giving $6$ cups. Reading it left-to-right
($7.5$) would over-fill the pot. The math protects the dish.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Personal budgeting" icon="💰">
Your monthly leftover is $\text{income} - (\text{rent} + \text{food} + \text{transport})$. The parentheses say *add up all
the bills first, then subtract.* Drop them — $2000 - 800 + 400 + 200$ — and you'd compute $1800$ instead of $600$, a very
expensive typo.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports statistics" icon="⚾">
A batting average is $\tfrac{\text{hits}}{\text{at-bats}}$ — a fraction, an integer over an integer. 45 hits in 150 at-bats
is $\tfrac{45}{150} = 0.300$. The division *is* the operation, and it must finish before you compare two players.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Gardening" icon="🌱">
Planting a bed: 4 rows of 6 seedlings, plus a spare 5 in a pot, is $4 \times 6 + 5 = 29$. Multiplication (rows of plants)
resolves before the lone addition. Mis-order it and you'd order the wrong number of seedlings.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice negatives quietly at work in budgeting (spending is subtraction) and fractions in batting averages and recipes.
The same handful of rules covers all of them.
</Callout>
