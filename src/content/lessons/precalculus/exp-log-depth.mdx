## The hook: when nine cases become a thousand

In the first week of an outbreak, a clinic logs **9** cases. Nobody panics — nine is small. But each
sick person infects a couple more, who infect a couple more. By week eight the count is in the
**hundreds**, and the curve is screaming upward. Nothing about the *rule* changed week to week; the
same multiplication just kept compounding. That runaway behavior — and its mirror image, things that
fade away by a constant fraction — is the world of **exponentials**. To *measure* and *tame* such
quantities, we'll need their inverse: the **logarithm**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
An **exponential** multiplies by the same factor over and over (so it explodes or decays), and a
**logarithm** is the question "how many times did we multiply?" — the exact tool that undoes it.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from "powers" to "rates of change"

You already met **exponents** in algebra ($2^3 = 8$) and the **rational functions** of the previous
lesson, whose graphs bend toward asymptotes. An exponential function pushes that bending to the
extreme: instead of adding a fixed amount each step (a straight line), it **multiplies by a fixed
factor** each step.

- A linear quantity grows by *the same amount*: $+5, +5, +5, \dots$
- An exponential quantity grows by *the same ratio*: $\times 1.5, \times 1.5, \times 1.5, \dots$

That single change — addition to multiplication — is why exponentials feel slow at first and then
violently fast. The **logarithm** runs the movie backward: given the result, it recovers the exponent.
Just as subtraction undoes addition and division undoes multiplication, **logs undo exponentials**.

## The formal definition

An **exponential model** is

$$
y = a\,e^{kt},
$$

where $a$ is the starting amount (the value at $t = 0$), and $k$ is the **continuous rate**. If
$k > 0$ the quantity **grows**; if $k < 0$ it **decays**. The base $e \approx 2.71828$ is **Euler's
number**, the natural choice because it makes the rate of change equal to the quantity itself —
$\frac{d}{dt}e^{t} = e^{t}$ — which is why it appears everywhere in calculus and modeling.

The **natural logarithm** $\ln$ is the inverse of $e^x$. They cancel cleanly:

$$
\ln(e^x) = x \qquad\text{and}\qquad e^{\ln x} = x \quad (x > 0).
$$

Any base can be rewritten in terms of $e$, which is why $e$ and $\ln$ are enough on their own:

$$
a^x = e^{x \ln a}.
$$

Two quantities fall straight out of the model. The **doubling time** (when $k>0$) and the
**half-life** (when $k<0$) come from asking when $y$ reaches twice or half its start:

$$
t_{\text{double}} = \frac{\ln 2}{k}, \qquad t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{|k|}.
$$

Finally, the defining superpower of logs: they turn **multiplication into addition**.

$$
\ln(xy) = \ln x + \ln y, \qquad \ln(x^p) = p\,\ln x.
$$

This is exactly why a **log scale** spaces $1, 10, 100, 1000$ evenly — each step is "$\times 10$,"
which becomes "$+1$" once you take the log.

<Callout type="why">
Logs and $e$ are the native language of machine learning. We minimize **log-loss** (cross-entropy)
instead of raw probabilities because multiplying thousands of tiny likelihoods underflows to zero —
but **adding their logs** is stable. The **log-likelihood** turns a product over a dataset into a sum
we can optimize, and **exponential learning-rate decay**, $\eta_t = \eta_0\,e^{-\lambda t}$, gently
shrinks the step size as training settles.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The pair "multiply repeatedly / ask how many times" shows up far beyond epidemics.

<ExampleCard domain="Epidemiology" icon="🦠">
Early in an outbreak, cases follow $y = a\,e^{kt}$. A measured **doubling time** of 3 days means
$k = \frac{\ln 2}{3} \approx 0.23$ per day. Public-health teams watch the *slope on a log plot*: if it
flattens, interventions are working — the multiplicative engine is slowing even while totals still rise.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Finance" icon="💰">
Money compounded **continuously** grows as $A = P\,e^{rt}$. Invest \$1000 at $r = 5\%$ and after
$t = 10$ years you have $1000\,e^{0.05 \cdot 10} = 1000\,e^{0.5} \approx 1648$ dollars. The same $\ln 2 / r$
formula gives the famous "rule of 70": your money doubles in about $70/5 = 14$ years.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Archaeology" icon="🦴">
Carbon-14 **decays** with a half-life of about 5730 years, so $k = -\ln 2 / 5730 \approx -0.000121$
per year. Measure how much $^{14}\!C$ remains in a bone, then solve $e^{kt}$ for $t$ with a logarithm:
$t = \frac{\ln(\text{fraction left})}{k}$. That single log turns a leftover fraction into an age.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Seismology" icon="🌋">
The **Richter scale** is a base-10 log of wave amplitude, so each whole number is a $\times 10$ jump in
shaking (and roughly $\times 32$ in energy). A magnitude 7 isn't "a bit worse" than a 5 — it's
$10^{2} = 100$ times the amplitude. Logs let a single small number stand in for a vast range, the same
trick **decibels** use for sound.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the symmetry: growth ($k>0$) and decay ($k<0$) are the <em>same</em> equation with the sign
flipped, and the log is what lets you <em>solve</em> for the unknown time in either one.
</Callout>
