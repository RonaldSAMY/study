## The hook: how do you draw a loop-the-loop?

Picture a rider on a **Ferris wheel**. As the wheel turns, their seat sweeps out a perfect circle.
But here's the catch: at the very top and the very bottom they are at the *same* horizontal spot — two
different heights for one value of $x$. A normal function $y = f(x)$ can't do that; it only allows **one
$y$ for each $x$**. So how do we describe a path that doubles back, loops, or spirals?

The trick is to stop asking *"what is $y$ when $x$ is here?"* and start asking *"where is the point
**right now**?"* We let a hidden helper variable — **time** $t$, or an **angle** $\theta$ — drive both
coordinates at once. That single idea unlocks circles, spirals, flower petals, and the swirling art of
a spirograph.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Instead of tying $y$ to $x$, let a **parameter** (a clock $t$ or an angle $\theta$) move a point around,
and read off **both** $x$ and $y$ from it — so the curve can loop, cross, and spiral freely.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a moving point, not a fixed graph

You already know two things that make this click:

1. **Functions** map one input to one output. Great for "height vs. distance," but helpless when a curve
   revisits the same $x$. The Ferris wheel breaks the one-$y$-per-$x$ rule.
2. **The unit circle** from trigonometry. You learned that as the angle $\theta$ sweeps around,
   $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ traces a circle. That was your *first parametric curve* — you just didn't
   call it that. The angle was the hidden driver doing all the work.

There are two flavors of this idea:

- **Parametric form** — a clock $t$ feeds **two separate functions**: $x = f(t)$ and $y = g(t)$. As $t$
  ticks forward, the point $(x, y)$ moves and paints a path.
- **Polar form** — instead of left/right and up/down, you steer by **angle $\theta$** and **distance from
  the origin $r$**. Curves that are "round" or "petal-shaped" become astonishingly simple this way.

## The formal definition

**Parametric form.** A curve in the plane is given by two functions of one parameter $t$:

$$
x = f(t), \qquad y = g(t), \qquad t \in [a, b].
$$

As $t$ runs over its interval, the point $\big(f(t), g(t)\big)$ sweeps out the curve. The classic example
is the **circle of radius 1**:

$$
x = \cos t, \qquad y = \sin t, \qquad t \in [0, 2\pi].
$$

A check: at $t = 0$ we are at $(1, 0)$; at $t = \tfrac{\pi}{2}$ we are at $(0, 1)$ — a quarter turn
counterclockwise, exactly as the unit circle promised.

**Polar coordinates.** Instead of $(x, y)$, locate a point by its distance from the origin $r$ and its
angle $\theta$ from the positive $x$-axis. The two systems convert with plain trigonometry:

$$
x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta, \qquad r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{y}{x}.
$$

**Polar curves.** Let the radius itself depend on the angle, $r = f(\theta)$, and beautiful shapes fall
out almost for free:

$$
\underbrace{r = a\theta}_{\text{spiral}}, \qquad
\underbrace{r = \cos(k\theta)}_{\text{rose}}, \qquad
\underbrace{r = 1 + \cos\theta}_{\text{cardioid}}.
$$

The rose has $k$ petals when $k$ is odd and $2k$ petals when $k$ is even — a whole flower from one tiny
formula.

<Callout type="why">
Machine learning loves this trick. A robot arm or self-driving planner describes its **path as $x(t),
y(t)$** so it can reason about position *and* timing together. Periodic signals (hour-of-day,
day-of-year) are fed to models as **angle features** $\big(\sin\theta, \cos\theta\big)$ so that 23:59 and
00:01 sit next to each other instead of at opposite ends of a line. And radial, polar-style encodings
turn "round" data into coordinates a model can actually learn from.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The same "let a parameter move the point" recipe shows up far from the math classroom.

<ExampleCard domain="Amusement parks" icon="🎡">
A Ferris-wheel seat of radius $R$ turning at rate $\omega$ has position
$x(t) = R\cos(\omega t)$, $y(t) = R\sin(\omega t) + R$ (lifted so the bottom sits at the ground). One
parameter $t$ — time — drives both coordinates, so the seat can be at the same $x$ twice (top and bottom)
without breaking any rule. That's something $y = f(x)$ simply cannot express.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports" icon="⛲">
Water arcing from a fountain (or a thrown ball) is parametric in time:
$x(t) = v_x\,t$ and $y(t) = v_y\,t - \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2$. The horizontal part marches steadily while the
vertical part rises and falls, and together they trace the graceful arc. Eliminating $t$ would hide the
timing — but the *splash moment* is exactly when $y(t) = 0$.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Radar & sonar" icon="📡">
A radar sweep is pure polar. The dish points at angle $\theta$ and reports the distance $r$ to whatever
it pings, giving a point $(r, \theta)$. The whole screen is a polar plot: angle = direction, radius =
range. Converting with $x = r\cos\theta,\ y = r\sin\theta$ drops each blip onto a flat map.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Art & toys" icon="🌀">
A **spirograph** gear rolling inside a ring traces a hypotrochoid — a parametric curve like
$x(t) = (R - r)\cos t + d\cos\!\big(\tfrac{R - r}{r}t\big)$ with a matching $y(t)$. Tiny changes to the
gear ratio give wildly different looping flowers. Lissajous figures (in the demo) are the same spirit:
$x = \sin(3t),\ y = \sin(2t)$ weaves an over-under braid.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the pattern: the first two examples use <strong>time</strong> as the parameter, the radar uses an
<strong>angle</strong>, and the spirograph uses an abstract dial. Same machinery, different driver.
</Callout>
