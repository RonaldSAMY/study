## The hook: where is the function *heading*?

Point a phone camera at a faraway sign and tap zoom. 2×, 5×, 20× — with every step the blur
sharpens and the letters settle toward something definite, even before you can read them perfectly.
You're not asking *"what is the picture AT infinite zoom?"* (you never get there). You're asking
*"what is it clearly **heading toward**?"*

That single shift in question — from *"what is the value here?"* to *"what value is the function
**approaching**?"* — is the seed of all of calculus. The tool for asking it precisely is the
**limit**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **limit** is the value a function **heads toward** as its input creeps toward some point — and that
target can exist even when the function has no value (or a different value) exactly at that point.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a value can have a destination it never reaches

You already know that a **function** is a machine: feed in $x$, get out $f(x)$. And from rational
functions you've met two kinds of trouble spots:

- a **hole**, where a factor cancels — like $\dfrac{x^2-1}{x-1}$, which is just $x+1$ *everywhere
  except* $x=1$, where plugging in gives the undefined $\tfrac{0}{0}$;
- an **asymptote**, where the graph shoots off as $x$ nears a forbidden input.

At a hole the graph looks like an unbroken line with one point *poked out*. Walk along it from the
left and you sail toward the missing point's height; walk in from the right and you arrive at the
same height. The function never actually *lands* on that point — but its **destination** is crystal
clear. A limit is just a careful name for that destination.

The key mental move: **the value the function HEADS toward is not necessarily its value AT the
point.** Limits care about the journey approaching $x=c$, not about what (if anything) sits exactly
at $c$.

## The formal definition

We write the statement *"as $x$ approaches $c$, $f(x)$ approaches $L$"* like this:

$$
\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L.
$$

Read it aloud as *"the limit of $f$ of $x$, as $x$ goes to $c$, equals $L$."* It says nothing about
$f(c)$ itself — only about the **approach**.

Often we sneak up from one side at a time. The **one-sided limits** are:

$$
\lim_{x \to c^-} f(x) \quad\text{(from the left)} \qquad\text{and}\qquad \lim_{x \to c^+} f(x) \quad\text{(from the right)}.
$$

The full (two-sided) limit exists **only when both sides agree**:

$$
\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L \iff \lim_{x \to c^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to c^+} f(x) = L.
$$

We can also let the input run off to the horizon. A **limit at infinity** asks where the function
settles as $x$ grows without bound:

$$
\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L,
$$

meaning $f(x)$ levels off toward $L$ as $x$ gets huge (think of a graph flattening into a
horizontal asymptote).

Back to the demo's **removable hole**. Even though substitution gives the undefined $\tfrac{0}{0}$ at
$x=1$, cancelling the shared factor reveals the destination:

$$
\frac{x^2-1}{x-1} = \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1} = x+1 \;\longrightarrow\; 2 \quad\text{as } x \to 1.
$$

So $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{x^2-1}{x-1} = 2$, cleanly — the limit fills in the hole that the
formula left empty.

<Callout type="why">
This is the foundation of machine learning. A **derivative** — the slope that tells a model which way
to nudge its weights — is *defined* as a limit: $f'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$. Every
**gradient** in gradient descent, the engine that trains neural networks, is built from limits like
this. Master "approaching," and optimization stops being magic.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

Limits aren't a graph-paper curiosity. The "value something heads toward" shows up everywhere.

<ExampleCard domain="Photography" icon="📷">
Digital zoom keeps magnifying a point in the frame. With each step the region homes in on one spot,
the way the marker homes in on $x=1$. You never reach "infinite zoom," but the framing clearly
**approaches a target** — exactly $\lim$ behavior, lens edition.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Driving" icon="🚗">
A speedometer shows your **instantaneous speed** — but speed is distance over time, and at a single
instant the time window is zero. The trick: average your speed over a shrinking window of seconds.
As the window $\to 0$, the average **approaches** the number the needle shows. That's
$\lim_{\Delta t \to 0}$, and it's literally a derivative.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Skydiving" icon="🪂">
A falling skydiver speeds up, but air resistance grows with speed until it cancels gravity. Velocity
then **levels off** at a **terminal velocity** $v_T$ it gets ever closer to but never quite exceeds:
$\lim_{t\to\infty} v(t) = v_T$. A limit at infinity describing a real, physical ceiling.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Displays" icon="🖥️">
Cram more and more pixels into the same screen and the staircased edges smooth out. As pixel density
$\to\infty$, the rendered image **approaches** a perfectly continuous picture. No finite screen is
truly smooth, but the sequence of ever-denser screens has a clear limit: the ideal continuous image.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Two flavors hide in these examples: approaching a <em>finite point</em> (zoom, speedometer) and
approaching <em>infinity</em> (terminal velocity, pixel density). Same question every time — "what is
it heading toward?"
</Callout>
