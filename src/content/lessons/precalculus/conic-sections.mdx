## The hook: one cut of a cone, four different shapes

Point a flashlight straight at a wall and you get a **circle** of light. Tilt it a little — the
circle stretches into an **ellipse**. Tilt it until the cone of light runs parallel to the wall and
the edge never closes up: that's a **parabola**. Tilt it further still and you slice *two* pieces out
of the beam — a **hyperbola**. You just made all four **conic sections** with a single light, simply
by changing the angle. Planets, satellite dishes, GPS, and even the contour lines a neural network
descends all turn out to be these same four curves.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The four conics — circle, ellipse, parabola, hyperbola — are really **one family**, and a single dial
called the **eccentricity** $e$ morphs smoothly from one to the next.
</Callout>

## Intuition: curves you already half-know

You have met pieces of this before. In **quadratics** you graphed $y = x^2$ and called it a parabola —
that's a conic. In the **parametric and polar** lesson you learned to trace a curve by sweeping an
angle $\theta$ and reading off a radius $r$; the slickest description of *all* conics is exactly such a
polar equation. And every circle you have ever drawn, $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$, is the simplest conic of all.

So instead of memorizing four unrelated formulas, picture **one cone of light** and a knob you turn.
At one extreme the slice is a tidy closed loop; turn the knob and the loop stretches, then bursts open.
That knob is the eccentricity $e$, and it is the through-line of this whole lesson.

## The formal definition

Each conic has a clean **Cartesian** equation when it sits nicely on the axes.

A **circle** of radius $r$ centred at the origin:

$$
x^2 + y^2 = r^2.
$$

An **ellipse** with semi-axes $a$ and $b$ — a circle stretched by different amounts in $x$ and $y$:

$$
\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1.
$$

A **parabola** opening upward, with focal distance $p$:

$$
y = \frac{x^2}{4p}.
$$

A **hyperbola**, the "minus" cousin of the ellipse, with two opening branches:

$$
\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1.
$$

**The unifying idea — eccentricity.** Every conic can be described by one point (the **focus**), one
line (the **directrix**), and a single number $e \ge 0$: the curve is the set of points whose distance
to the focus is $e$ times the distance to the directrix. That one rule produces all four shapes:

$$
e = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{circle}, \quad
0 < e < 1 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{ellipse}, \quad
e = 1 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{parabola}, \quad
e > 1 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{hyperbola}.
$$

In **polar** form, with the focus at the origin, the whole family collapses into the single equation the
demo draws:

$$
r = \frac{l}{1 + e\cos\theta},
$$

where $l$ is the **semi-latus rectum** (the curve's size). Geometrically, this is the **slicing-a-cone**
picture: tilt the cutting plane and you sweep $e$ from $0$ (a level cut → circle) through $1$ (parallel
to the cone's side → parabola) and beyond (steep cut through both nappes → hyperbola).

<Callout type="why">
Conics are everywhere in science and machine learning. Newton proved orbits are conics: bound planets
trace ellipses, one-shot comets trace parabolas or hyperbolas. In **optimization**, the level-sets
(contours) of a quadratic loss are **ellipses**, and their eccentricity tells you how badly conditioned
the problem is — fat round contours mean fast gradient descent, thin stretched ones mean slow zig-zags.
The contours of a 2-D **Gaussian** are ellipses too, with the covariance matrix setting their shape.
Reading a conic is reading the geometry of learning.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The conics aren't a textbook curiosity — each shape earns its keep because of a special **focusing**
property.

<ExampleCard domain="Astronomy" icon="🪐">
Every body orbiting the Sun follows a conic with the Sun at one **focus**. Earth's orbit is an ellipse
with eccentricity $e \approx 0.017$ — nearly a circle. Halley's Comet is a long, thin ellipse,
$e \approx 0.97$. Some interstellar visitors arrive with $e > 1$: they whip once around the Sun on a
**hyperbolic flyby** and leave forever. Same equation, different $e$.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Engineering" icon="📡">
A satellite dish and a car headlight are both **parabolas** (rotated into a paraboloid). A parabola has
the magic property that every ray arriving parallel to its axis bounces to a single **focus** — so the
dish concentrates a faint signal onto the receiver. Run it backwards: put a bulb at the focus of a
headlight reflector and every ray leaves in a tight parallel beam.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Navigation" icon="🛰️">
Older **LORAN** systems (and the math inside **GPS**) locate you using **hyperbolas**. If two stations
send a pulse and you measure the *difference* in arrival times, that fixes your distance-difference to
the pair — and "constant difference of distances to two foci" is exactly the definition of a hyperbola.
Cross two such hyperbolas and the intersection is your position.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Medicine" icon="🩺">
A **lithotripter** destroys kidney stones without surgery using the two **foci** of an **ellipse**. An
ellipse reflects any ray from one focus straight to the *other* focus. Doctors place a shock-wave source
at one focus and the patient's stone at the other; the scattered energy reconverges precisely on the
stone. The same acoustic trick makes "whispering galleries" carry a murmur across a dome.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the pattern: the parabola focuses *parallel rays to a point*, the ellipse focuses *one focus to
the other*, and the hyperbola encodes a *difference of distances*. Each application is just a focusing
property of one conic, picked for the job.
</Callout>
