## The hook: one knob to turn the volume up

Picture the volume slider on your phone. The song doesn't change — the *shape* of the
sound wave stays exactly the same — but every value gets bigger or smaller together.
Slide it up and the quiet parts and loud parts both grow in proportion. That single
control is doing math: it takes a function (the sound wave) and **stretches it
vertically**. Reach for a different knob — say, a delay that nudges the whole track a
beat later — and you've **shifted** the function sideways.

Almost every "knob" in the world works like this. Instead of inventing a brand-new
function each time, we take a function we already understand and **bend, slide, flip, or
chain** it. That is the entire idea of *transformations and composition*.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **transformation** takes a function you already know and slides, stretches, or flips it
into a new one; **composition** feeds one function's output into another so their effects
stack up.
</Callout>

## Intuition: start from a shape you already know

You already met some "parent" shapes. From **quadratics** you know the parabola
$y = x^2$, and from **trig graphs** you know the wave $y = \sin x$. Transformations let
you describe a *whole family* of curves by saying how they differ from that parent shape.

There are four moves, and each maps to one knob:

- **Vertical shift** — $f(x) + k$ lifts the graph up by $k$ (or down if $k$ is negative).
  *Everything gets $k$ added to its height.*
- **Horizontal shift** — $f(x - h)$ slides the graph right by $h$. (Yes, *minus* moves it
  *right* — the graph has to "wait" until $x$ catches up to $h$ before it does what it did at $0$.)
- **Stretch / shrink** — $a\,f(x)$ scales the height by $a$ (your volume knob), and $f(bx)$
  squeezes the graph horizontally by a factor of $b$ (it happens faster).
- **Reflection** — $-f(x)$ flips it upside down across the $x$-axis; $f(-x)$ mirrors it
  left-to-right across the $y$-axis.

These are exactly the "$a$, $h$, and $k$" knobs you turned on the quadratic
$y = a(x - h)^2 + k$ — only now they work on *any* function, not just parabolas.

## The formal definition

Start with any base function $f$. We can build a new function $g$ by applying all four
moves at once:

$$
g(x) = a\,f\big(b\,(x - h)\big) + k.
$$

Reading it from the inside out tells you the order things happen to the input and output:

- **Horizontal:** $x - h$ shifts right by $h$; multiplying by $b$ stretches/squeezes the
  $x$-axis. (Inside the parentheses, effects feel *backwards*: bigger $b$ makes it narrower.)
- **Vertical:** multiplying by $a$ stretches the height (and flips it if $a < 0$); adding
  $k$ lifts it.

**Reflections** are just the sign choices $a < 0$ (flip top-to-bottom) and $b < 0$ (flip
left-to-right).

**Composition** is a different operation: instead of bending one function, you feed the
output of one into the input of another. We write it $f \circ g$, meaning

$$
(f \circ g)(x) = f\big(g(x)\big).
$$

The inner function $g$ runs **first**, then $f$ acts on its result. Order matters:

$$
f\big(g(x)\big) \neq g\big(f(x)\big) \quad \text{in general.}
$$

For example, with $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ and $g(x) = x^2$, we get $f(g(x)) = \sqrt{x^2} = |x|$,
but $g(f(x)) = (\sqrt{x})^2 = x$ (only for $x \ge 0$). Same two functions, different result.

<Callout type="why">
Composition is the skeleton of every neural network. A network is literally a long chain
$f_L(\dots f_2(f_1(x)))$ — each layer is a function fed the previous layer's output. And
training nudges the per-layer versions of $a$ (weights, the stretch) and $k$ (biases, the
shift). Learning these transformations *is* what "learning" means.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

The same shifts, stretches, and chains show up far from any math class.

<ExampleCard domain="Audio synthesis" icon="🎹">
A synthesizer starts from a base wave like $\sin x$. The **volume** knob is the vertical
stretch $a$: louder means a taller wave, $a\,\sin x$. **Pitch** is the horizontal squeeze
$b$: a higher note is $\sin(bx)$, the same wave cycling faster. Add a constant offset and
you've shifted the wave's baseline — exactly $a\,f(b(x-h)) + k$ with sound coming out.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Photography" icon="📷">
A camera's **brightness curve** maps incoming light to pixel values. Sliding the
*exposure* slider is a vertical shift $f(x) + k$ — every pixel gets brighter. Cranking
*contrast* is a stretch $a\,f(x)$ that pushes lights lighter and darks darker around the
midpoint. A "negative" film look is the reflection $-f(x)$: bright becomes dark.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Smart home" icon="🌡️">
Your thermostat follows a daily temperature schedule $T(t)$. Travel across one time zone
and you don't redesign the schedule — you just shift it: $T(t - 1)$ runs the *same* curve
one hour later. Want it a couple degrees warmer all day? That's the vertical shift
$T(t) + 2$. One curve, slid around to fit your life.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Currency exchange" icon="💱">
Converting money is pure **composition**. Let $g$ turn dollars into euros and $f$ turn
euros into yen. To go straight from USD to JPY you chain them: $f(g(x))$ — euros are the
intermediate output handed to the next step. Reverse the order and you'd get a completely
different (wrong) number, which is why $f \circ g \neq g \circ f$.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice the pattern: a transformation reshapes *one* function with knobs, while composition
*links* functions in a chain. Real systems use both at once — a synth stretches a wave and
then pipes it through a filter (another function).
</Callout>
