## The hook: why does the same prompt give different answers?

Ask a chatbot the same question twice and you may get two different replies — sometimes a safe, predictable
one, sometimes a creative surprise. That variation isn't a bug; it's a **dial** you can turn. After all the
attention layers finish, the model holds a list of raw scores over the whole vocabulary. Turning that list
into one actual word is its own small science, and it decides whether your model sounds like a careful
encyclopedia or a freewheeling poet.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
The model's final scores (**logits**) become a probability distribution via **softmax**; **temperature**
sharpens or flattens it, and a **sampling rule** (greedy, top-k, top-p) picks the next token from it.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from scores to a choice

The last layer outputs one **logit** per vocabulary token — an unbounded "how much I like this word" score.
You already met the fix in the [activation-functions lesson](/neural-networks/activation-functions):
**softmax** exponentiates the logits and normalizes them into probabilities that sum to 1.

Then comes the artistic part. We rarely just take the single highest-probability token (that's **greedy**
decoding, and it tends to be repetitive and dull). Instead we **sample**, and we shape the distribution first:

- **Temperature $T$** divides the logits before softmax. Low $T$ makes the distribution *spiky* (confident,
  near-greedy); high $T$ makes it *flat* (diverse, risky). This is literally lowering or raising the
  [entropy](/information-theory/entropy) of the next-token distribution.
- **Top-k** keeps only the $k$ most likely tokens and renormalizes — cutting off the long tail of nonsense.
- **Top-p (nucleus)** keeps the smallest set of tokens whose probabilities sum past $p$ — an *adaptive* cutoff
  that's wide when the model is unsure and narrow when it's confident.

ever selecting true garbage.

## The formal definition

Given logits $z_1, \dots, z_V$, the temperature-scaled softmax probability of token $j$ is

$$
p_j = \frac{\exp(z_j / T)}{\sum_{v=1}^{V} \exp(z_v / T)}.
$$

As $T \to 0$ the distribution concentrates on the top logit (greedy); as $T$ grows it approaches uniform.
The sampling rules then restrict the support before drawing:

$$
\text{top-}k:\ \text{keep the }k\text{ largest } p_j;\qquad
\text{top-}p:\ \text{keep the smallest set with } \sum p_j \ge p.
$$

After pruning, renormalize the survivors and draw one token at random from them.

```python
p = softmax(logits / T)            # temperature reshapes the distribution
p[p < threshold_from_topk_topp] = 0
p /= p.sum()                       # renormalize the survivors
next_token = sample(p)             # draw one token
```

<Callout type="why">
This is the knob behind "creativity" in every deployed LLM. A coding assistant runs near $T = 0$ for
correct, deterministic output; a brainstorming partner runs hotter. Same model weights — totally different
behavior — set entirely by how you turn logits into a choice.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Creative writing" icon="✍️">
A story generator runs at high temperature with top-p around 0.9: enough randomness for surprising imagery,
but the nucleus cutoff blocks incoherent words. Drop the temperature and the prose turns flat and formulaic.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Code generation" icon="💻">
A programming assistant wants the *one* correct continuation, so it decodes greedily or at very low
temperature. Randomness here means bugs — you don't want a creative semicolon.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Game dialogue" icon="🎮">
An NPC powered by an LLM samples at moderate temperature so repeated conversations feel fresh instead of
canned, while top-k keeps the character from blurting nonsense that breaks immersion.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Weather & forecasting copy" icon="🌦️">
A service that auto-writes forecast summaries keeps temperature low: it wants "rain likely this afternoon,"
not a poetic gamble. Predictability is the feature, so the distribution is kept sharp.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Temperature and entropy are two views of the same thing: raising $T$ raises the entropy (uncertainty) of the
next-token distribution, lowering it makes the choice more certain. The sampling rules then decide how much
of that uncertainty you're willing to act on.
</Callout>
