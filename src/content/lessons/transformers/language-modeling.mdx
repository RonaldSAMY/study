## The hook: the phone keyboard that learned every language

When your phone suggests the next word — in English, in Spanish, in Hindi — it is playing one simple
game: *given everything typed so far, guess what comes next.* An LLM plays the exact same game, just at
a vastly larger scale. There is no separate "understand grammar" or "learn facts" module. There is one
objective, repeated over trillions of tokens: **predict the next token**. Astonishingly, getting very
good at that one game is what produces translation, reasoning, and code.

We have a transformer that turns a sequence into scores for the next token (from the softmax lesson).
Now we give it a goal, a way to measure progress, and a score everyone reports: **perplexity**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Train the model to put **high probability on the token that actually comes next**; measure how well it
does with **cross-entropy** (average surprise), and report **perplexity** — roughly *how many tokens
the model is effectively choosing between* at each step.
</Callout>

## Intuition: turn the whole internet into a fill-in-the-blank quiz

Any text is automatically a supervised dataset. Take a sentence, hide the next word, ask the model to
predict it, and you already know the right answer — it's the next word. No human labeling needed. This
is why language models can train on the entire web: every sentence grades itself.

At each position the model outputs a **probability distribution** over the whole vocabulary. We reward
it for assigning high probability to the true next token and penalize it for being confidently wrong.
The penalty is **surprise**: $-\log p(\text{true token})$. If the model said the right token was almost
certain, surprise is near 0; if it gave the right token almost no chance, surprise is huge. Averaging
that surprise over all positions gives the **cross-entropy loss** — the very quantity you met in
information theory, now used as a training target.

**Perplexity** is just that loss, exponentiated, turned into an intuitive unit: a perplexity of 1 means
"I always knew the next token"; a perplexity of 50,000 means "I might as well have guessed uniformly
from the whole vocabulary."

## The formal definition

A language model factorizes the probability of a sequence using the chain rule of probability — each
token conditioned on all the ones before it:

$$
P(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_T) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} P(x_t \mid x_{\lt t}).
$$

Training minimizes the **cross-entropy** between the true next token and the model's predicted
distribution — equivalently, the average negative log-likelihood:

$$
\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log P\big(x_t \mid x_{\lt t}\big).
$$

Because the target is "one-hot" (the true token has probability 1), cross-entropy collapses to just
the surprise of the correct token at each step. **Perplexity** is the exponential of that loss:

$$
\mathrm{PPL} = \exp(\mathcal{L}) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log P(x_t \mid x_{\lt t})\right).
$$

```python
logits = model(tokens[:-1])               # score the next token at every position
loss   = cross_entropy(logits, tokens[1:])  # mean of -log p(true next token)
ppl    = loss.exp()                          # perplexity = exp(average loss)
loss.backward()                             # one objective trains the whole model
```

<Callout type="why">
Minimizing cross-entropy is the same as pushing the model's distribution toward the data's true
distribution (their KL divergence to zero). The irreducible part of the loss is the language's own
entropy — genuine unpredictability no model can beat. Everything an LLM "knows" is a side effect of
chasing this one number down.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Weather forecasting" icon="🌧️">
A forecaster who says "70% chance of rain" is graded by **log loss**: if it rains, the penalty is
$-\log(0.7)$; a confident-but-wrong forecast is punished far more. Good forecasters, like good language
models, are *calibrated* — their stated probabilities match reality, which is exactly what minimizing
cross-entropy rewards.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports betting" icon="🎲">
A bookmaker's odds are a probability distribution over outcomes. **Perplexity is the effective number
of outcomes** they're hedging against: tight odds on a clear favorite mean low perplexity; a wide-open
field means high perplexity. A model with perplexity 8 is as unsure as someone guessing between 8
equally likely choices.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Mobile autocomplete" icon="📱">
Your multilingual keyboard ranks next-word suggestions by probability. When it nails the word you
wanted, its surprise on your text is low — low perplexity, better suggestions. Train it on your own
messages and it adapts: the loss on *your* writing style goes down.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Game shows" icon="🎯">
Think of "Wheel of Fortune": guessing the next letter of a phrase. A contestant who knows English well
needs few guesses — low perplexity. A perplexity of 2 means "on average I'm down to a coin flip per
token," which is roughly how strong modern models are at ordinary English.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Perplexity in <strong>nats</strong> uses <em>exp</em>; in <strong>bits</strong> it uses
<em>2<sup>loss</sup></em>. Same idea, different log base — bits answer "how many yes/no questions, on
average, to pin down the next token."
</Callout>
