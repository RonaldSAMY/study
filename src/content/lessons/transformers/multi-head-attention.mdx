## The hook: reading a sentence with several pairs of eyes

When a careful editor reads *"The tired cat slept soundly,"* they track several things at once: which
adjective modifies which noun, what the subject of the verb is, and how the adverb attaches. One pass,
many simultaneous questions. A single [attention](/transformers/attention) operation can only form *one*
weighted blend per token — one question at a time. So transformers run several attention operations side by
side, each free to specialize. That's **multi-head attention**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Multi-head attention** runs several attention operations in parallel — each with its own Q, K, V — so the
model can attend to different relationships at once, then concatenates their outputs and mixes them.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a committee of specialists

You already know one attention head: query, key, value, softmax, blend. Now imagine $h$ copies of it
running together over the same input. Crucially, each head gets its **own** learned projection matrices, so
each one carves the embedding into a different, smaller subspace and asks a different question.

In practice, heads reliably discover distinct jobs. Studied transformers contain heads that:

- attend to the **previous token** (useful local context),
- lock onto the **syntactic head** of a phrase (the noun an adjective belongs to),
- track **long-range** dependencies like matching brackets or quotation marks.

No one programs these roles — they emerge from training, the same way the embedding geometry did. After
the heads finish, their outputs are **stacked together** and passed through one final linear layer that
decides how to combine the perspectives.

**backward**, one fixates on the **noun**, one looks **forward**. Toggle a head off to see what its slice

## The formal definition

Split the work into $h$ heads. Each head $i$ has its own projections $W_i^Q, W_i^K, W_i^V$ and runs ordinary
scaled dot-product attention:

$$
\text{head}_i = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{(X W_i^Q)(X W_i^K)^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) (X W_i^V).
$$

Then concatenate all heads and apply one output projection $W^O$:

$$
\text{MultiHead}(X) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \dots, \text{head}_h)\, W^O.
$$

Each head works in dimension $d_k = d / h$, so the total compute stays about the same as one full-width head
— you're slicing the same budget into specialists rather than paying extra.

```python
heads = [attention(X @ Wq[i], X @ Wk[i], X @ Wv[i]) for i in range(h)]
out = concat(heads, axis=-1) @ Wo     # mix the perspectives back together
```

<Callout type="why">
A single head is forced to average all relationships into one blend, blurring them together. Splitting into
heads lets the model keep separate "channels" for grammar, position, and meaning — a big reason transformers
capture language so well, at no extra cost.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Sports analytics" icon="⚽">
Tracking a play, one analyst watches the ball carrier, another watches off-ball runs, another watches the
defensive line. Each "head" attends to a different facet of the same moment; the coach fuses their reports
into one decision — exactly the concat-and-project step.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Legal review" icon="⚖️">
A contract analyzer needs one head tracking defined terms, one tracking cross-references ("as defined in
Section 4"), and one tracking dates and amounts. Different relationships, read in parallel over the same clause.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Music" icon="🎼">
Hearing a chord, your ear simultaneously tracks the bass line, the melody on top, and the harmony in between.
Multiple attention channels over the same notes — separate then combined into the sound you perceive.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Vision transformers" icon="🖼️">
Image models split a picture into patches and let different heads attend to different cues: one to edges, one
to color regions, one to far-apart patches that form an object. Concatenating them rebuilds a rich view of the scene.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
More heads is not automatically better. Each head is narrower (smaller $d_k$), so there's a balance: enough
heads to specialize, wide enough heads to still represent each subspace well.
</Callout>
