## The hook: how does a search box know "puppy" means "dog"?

You type *"affordable laptop for students"* and the results include pages that never use the word
"affordable" — they say *"budget"* and *"cheap."* The search engine isn't matching letters. It has
quietly turned every word into a **point in space**, and it found pages whose points sit *near* yours.
Meaning became geometry. That trick — representing a token as a vector of numbers — is the **embedding**,
and it is the very first thing that happens inside every large language model.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
An **embedding** maps each token to a vector, placed so that things with similar meaning end up
**close together** and meaningful relationships become **consistent directions**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from a name to a place

A word like "cat" is just a symbol — there's no arithmetic you can do with the letters. So a model keeps
a giant lookup table called the **embedding matrix**: one learnable row per token. Looking up a token is
just picking its row, which gives a vector like the ones from the [vectors lesson](/linear-algebra/vectors).

Why bother? Because once a word *is* a vector, all of linear algebra becomes available. You already know
two of the tools you need:

- **Distance / direction** tells you how related two words are (recall the dot product from the vectors lesson).
- A **weighted sum** of input vectors is exactly what a [neuron](/neural-networks/the-neuron) computes.

The model never *defines* meaning. It nudges these vectors during training until the geometry is useful —
and remarkably, structure appears on its own: synonyms cluster, and relationships like *gender* or
*plural* show up as a repeatable step you can add.

## The formal definition

Let the vocabulary have $V$ tokens. The embedding matrix is

$$
E \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d},
$$

where $d$ is the embedding dimension (often 768, 4096, or more). Token $i$ becomes the row $E_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$.

To compare two embeddings $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ we use **cosine similarity** — the cosine of the
angle between them, which ignores length and cares only about direction:

$$
\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert} \in [-1, 1].
$$

A value near $1$ means "point the same way" (similar); near $0$ means unrelated; near $-1$ means opposite.
The analogy magic is plain vector arithmetic on these rows:

$$
\mathbf{e}_{\text{king}} - \mathbf{e}_{\text{man}} + \mathbf{e}_{\text{woman}} \approx \mathbf{e}_{\text{queen}}.
$$

```python
v_king = E[token_id["king"]]              # row lookup in the embedding matrix
sim = (a @ b) / (norm(a) * norm(b))       # cosine similarity, ignores length
```

<Callout type="why">
Embeddings are why an LLM can generalize. Because "budget" and "cheap" sit close, a pattern learned for
one transfers to the other for free. The whole model that follows — attention and all — operates on these
vectors, never on the raw words.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Web search" icon="🔎">
A search engine embeds your query and every document into the same space, then ranks documents by
**cosine similarity** to the query. "How to fix a flat tire" surfaces a page titled "Repairing a punctured
wheel" because their vectors nearly coincide — no shared keywords required.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Music streaming" icon="🎧">
Each song becomes a vector learned from listening patterns. Songs you'd enjoy next are simply the **nearest
neighbors** of what you're playing. The "radio" feature is a walk through embedding space, hopping from one
nearby point to the next.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Genomics" icon="🧬">
Researchers embed proteins so that sequences with similar 3D structure or function land near each other.
A newly discovered protein can be understood by reading off its neighbors — guilt by geometric association.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Translation" icon="🌐">
Multilingual models embed "dog," "perro," and "chien" into nearly the same location. Meaning is shared
across languages, so the geometry — not a dictionary — is what lets the model bridge them.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
These spaces have hundreds of dimensions, far past what we can draw. But every intuition from the 2D demo
— closeness, direction, analogy — carries over unchanged. That is the gift of the list-of-numbers view.
</Callout>
