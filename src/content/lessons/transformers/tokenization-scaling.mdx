## The hook: how a search engine reads a query it has never seen

Type `antidisestablishmentarianism` or a brand-new product name into a search box and the system still
indexes it sensibly. It can't have a slot for every possible word — languages invent words faster than
any dictionary can grow. The trick is to break text into **reusable pieces**: common chunks stay whole,
rare words split into smaller parts, and *nothing* is ever truly out-of-vocabulary.

That's the first half of this lesson. The second half answers the question every lab obsesses over:
once you can train these models, **how much better do they get if you make them bigger?** The answer is
shockingly clean — a straight line on a log-log plot, the **scaling law**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Tokenization** chops text into subword pieces (via Byte-Pair Encoding) so any text maps to a fixed
vocabulary, and **scaling laws** say a model's loss drops as a smooth power law as you grow its
parameters, data, and compute — predictable, but with a hard floor it can never beat.
</Callout>

## Intuition: subword pieces, and the line that predicts the future

**Why not just use words?** A word vocabulary is enormous and still misses every typo, name, and
neologism. **Why not just use letters?** Then sequences become punishingly long and each token carries
almost no meaning. **Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)** finds the sweet spot. It starts from individual
characters and repeatedly merges the most frequent adjacent pair into a new token. Frequent strings
like `tion`, `ing`, or `the` become single tokens; a rare word gracefully falls back to a handful of
pieces. The vocabulary is fixed (say 50k–100k tokens), yet it can spell *anything*.

**Scaling laws** are the second surprise. If you plot a model's loss against the compute used to train
it — on **log-log axes** — you get a remarkably straight, downward line. That straight line is the
signature of a **power law**: every time you multiply compute by 10, the *reducible* part of the loss
shrinks by the same factor. It lets researchers predict the loss of a model they haven't trained yet,
and decide how to spend a fixed budget between a bigger model and more data.

But the line doesn't go to zero. There is an **irreducible loss** — the genuine entropy of language
from the last lesson — that no amount of scale can beat.

## The formal definition

**BPE**, in one loop: start with the characters, then greedily apply the highest-priority learned merge
that's present, until none remain.

```python
tokens = list(word)                  # start from characters
while True:
    pair = best_adjacent_merge(tokens, ranked_merges)
    if pair is None:
        break
    tokens = apply_merge(tokens, pair)   # e.g. ('t', 'ion') -> 'tion'
```

**Scaling laws.** Empirically, test loss follows a power law in model size $N$ (and, separately, in
dataset size $D$):

$$
L(N) = E + \frac{A}{N^{\alpha}},
$$

where $E$ is the **irreducible loss** (the entropy floor), and $\alpha$ is a small exponent (often
$\approx 0.05\text{–}0.1$). Taking logs of the *reducible* part turns it into a straight line — why
log-log plots look linear:

$$
\log\big(L - E\big) = \log A - \alpha \log N.
$$

Compute, parameters, and data are linked by $C \approx 6\,N\,D$ FLOPs. The **Chinchilla** finding is
that, for a fixed compute budget, $N$ and $D$ should grow *together* — roughly **20 training tokens per
parameter** — rather than building a giant model starved of data.

<Callout type="why">
Scaling laws turned deep learning from alchemy into forecasting. Before spending millions on a training
run, a lab fits the power law on small models and *extrapolates* the loss of the big one. The clean
exponent is why "just make it bigger" has been such a reliable bet — until you hit the floor $E$ or run
out of data.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Search & retrieval" icon="🔎">
A search index doesn't store every word — it breaks queries into subword pieces and stems so a never-
before-seen term still matches related documents. Subword tokens are why searching a misspelled or
freshly coined word still returns something useful instead of a blank.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Printing" icon="🅰️">
Gutenberg's movable type didn't carve a separate block for every word — it reused a small set of
letters and common ligatures (`fi`, `fl`) to print *any* text. BPE is movable type for neural nets:
a fixed set of reusable pieces that compose into the entire language.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Economics" icon="📉">
Manufacturing **learning curves** (Wright's law) say unit cost falls by a fixed percentage every time
cumulative production doubles — a power law, straight on a log-log plot. Solar panels and microchips
followed it for decades. Model loss versus compute is the same kind of curve, same kind of line.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Fitness training" icon="🏃">
A new runner improves fast, then gains shrink as they approach their physiological limit — diminishing
returns toward a hard floor. Scaling a model feels identical: early scale buys huge gains, later scale
buys less, and the irreducible loss $E$ is the limit you asymptote toward but never cross.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Tokenization quietly shapes a model's quirks: counting the letters in a word or doing digit arithmetic
is hard partly because numbers and rare words get chopped into odd pieces. Many "why can't the model
do X" puzzles trace back to the tokenizer.
</Callout>
