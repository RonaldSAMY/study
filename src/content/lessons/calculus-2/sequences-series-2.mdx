## The hook: an echo that adds up to a finite sound

Clap in a canyon and the echo bounces back, then bounces again, fainter each time — half as loud, then a
quarter, then an eighth. You're hearing an **infinite sum**: $\tfrac12 + \tfrac14 + \tfrac18 + \cdots$.
Infinitely many claps, yet the total is *finite* — it lands exactly on $1$. Add infinitely many things
and sometimes you get a finite answer, and sometimes you get infinity. The whole game is telling which.

This connects straight back to integration. An integral was an infinite sum of infinitely thin pieces.
A **series** is an infinite sum of discrete terms. Both ask the same question: *does adding endlessly
many things settle down?*

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A series **converges** if its running total (the partial sums) homes in on a single finite number, and
**diverges** if the total runs off to infinity or never settles — and the size of the terms is what
decides which.

</Callout>

## Intuition: watch the running total

A **sequence** is an ordered list of numbers $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$. A **series** is what you get by
adding them up. The key object is the **partial sum** — the total after $n$ terms:

$$
S_n = a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n.
$$

The infinite series $\sum a_n$ *converges* to $L$ if the partial sums $S_n$ approach $L$ as $n \to \infty$.
This is exactly the **limit** idea from Calculus 1, applied to a staircase of running totals.

A necessary first check: if the terms $a_n$ don't even shrink to zero, the sum has no hope — it diverges.
But shrinking to zero is *not enough*. The harmonic series $\sum \frac1n$ has terms going to zero, yet its
total grows without bound. We need sharper tests.

The most useful is the **ratio test**: look at how fast consecutive terms shrink,
$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|$. If $L < 1$ the terms collapse fast enough to
converge; if $L > 1$ they grow and it diverges; if $L = 1$ the test is silent and you need another tool.

## The formal definition

A series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n$ **converges** to $S$ if the sequence of partial sums has a finite limit:

$$
S = \lim_{n\to\infty} S_n, \qquad S_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k.
$$

The **geometric series** is the one we can sum exactly. For $|r| < 1$:

$$
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} r^{n} = \frac{1}{1-r}.
$$

The **ratio test**: with $L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|$,

$$
L < 1 \Rightarrow \text{converges}, \qquad L > 1 \Rightarrow \text{diverges}, \qquad L = 1 \Rightarrow \text{inconclusive}.
$$

A **power series** lets the terms carry a variable $x$:

$$
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n x^{n} = c_0 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + \cdots
$$

The ratio test tells you the **radius of convergence** — the window of $x$ for which the series adds up
to a finite value. Power series are how we'll represent whole functions in the next lesson.

<Callout type="why">
Series are the engine inside your devices. A computer can only add, subtract, and multiply — it cannot
"do" $\sin$, $e^x$, or $\ln$ directly. It evaluates them by summing a few terms of a **power series**.
Convergence tests guarantee that a handful of terms is enough to hit the accuracy you need.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Acoustics" icon="🔊">
A canyon echo at half-loudness each bounce sums to $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(\tfrac12)^n = 1$ — a finite total
from infinitely many echoes. Reverb units in audio software use this exact geometric decay to fake a room
without storing an endless tail of sound.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Estimating π" icon="🥧">
The Basel series $\sum \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}$ lets you approximate $\pi$ by adding reciprocals
of squares. It converges slowly — a vivid demonstration that "converges" and "converges *fast*" are very
different promises.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Finance" icon="💰">
The present value of a bond paying forever, discounted each year, is a geometric series. Because the
discount ratio is below 1, an *infinite* stream of payments has a *finite* price today — the entire
theory of perpetuities is one converging series.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Computing" icon="🖥️">
To evaluate $e^x$, a processor sums $\sum \frac{x^n}{n!}$. The ratio of consecutive terms is
$\frac{x}{n+1} \to 0$, so it converges blazingly fast — a dozen terms give all the digits a calculator
shows. (That's the bridge to the next lesson.)
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Beyond the ratio test there's a whole toolbox: the <strong>comparison</strong>, <strong>integral</strong>,
and <strong>alternating series</strong> tests. Each is a different lens for the same question — does the
infinite total settle down?
</Callout>
