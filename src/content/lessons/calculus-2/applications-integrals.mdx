## The hook: how do you find the volume of a wine glass?

A wine glass, a vase, a rocket nozzle, a turned wooden bowl — none of these have a tidy formula like
"length × width × height." But they all share a secret: they are made by spinning a **curve** around an
axis. If you can describe the profile with a function $y = f(x)$, calculus can hand you the exact volume,
no matter how curvy.

This is the payoff of the previous lesson. Once you can *evaluate* integrals, you can *measure* things
that have no elementary formula: the area trapped between two curves, the volume of a solid, the average
of a quantity that never stops changing.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
An integral is an **infinite sum of infinitely thin pieces** — thin strips give area, thin disks give
volume, and the integral divided by the width gives the average. Slice, sum, shrink.
</Callout>

## Intuition: slice it thin, then add it up

Every application here follows the same three-step recipe you already know from Riemann sums:

1. **Slice** the region into many thin pieces.
2. **Approximate** each piece by something simple (a rectangle, a disk).
3. **Sum and shrink** — let the slices get infinitely thin, and the sum becomes an integral.

For **area between two curves** $f$ (top) and $g$ (bottom), each thin strip has height $f(x) - g(x)$ and
width $dx$:

$$
A = \int_a^b \big(f(x) - g(x)\big)\,dx.
$$

For a **volume of revolution**, spin the curve $y = f(x)$ around the $x$-axis. Each thin slice is a
**disk** of radius $f(x)$ and thickness $dx$, so its volume is $\pi f(x)^2\,dx$. Add them up:

$$
V = \int_a^b \pi\,f(x)^2\,dx.
$$

## Play with it

Pick a profile curve and watch it spin into a solid. The slider sets how many **disks** approximate the
solid — each is a circle of radius $f(x_i)$, drawn edge-on. With few disks the estimate is rough; crank
it up and the disk-sum $\sum \pi f(x_i)^2\,\Delta x$ converges on the exact volume $\int \pi f(x)^2\,dx$.

## The formal definition

**Area between curves** (with $f \ge g$ on $[a,b]$):

$$
A = \int_a^b \big(f(x) - g(x)\big)\,dx.
$$

**Volume by disks** (revolving $y=f(x)$ about the $x$-axis):

$$
V = \int_a^b \pi\,\big[f(x)\big]^2\,dx.
$$

**Average value** of a function over $[a,b]$ — the constant height that would enclose the same area:

$$
\bar f = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx.
$$

<Callout type="why">
The **average value** formula is the continuous version of "add them up and divide by how many." In
machine learning, the *expected value* of a continuous random variable is exactly this idea: an integral
of the value weighted by its probability density. Volume integrals reappear whenever you integrate a
density over a region.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Glassblowing" icon="🍷">
A wine glass bowl is the curve $y=\sqrt{x}$ spun about its axis. Its volume is
$\int_0^4 \pi(\sqrt{x})^2\,dx = \int_0^4 \pi x\,dx = 8\pi \approx 25.1$. The glassmaker never integrates,
but the shape obeys the math exactly.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Estimating π" icon="🥧">
The area of a quarter circle of radius 1 is $\int_0^1 \sqrt{1-x^2}\,dx = \frac{\pi}{4}$. Multiply by 4
and you have **estimated π by measuring an area** — the same principle behind Monte-Carlo "throw darts at
a circle" methods used to benchmark computers.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Climate" icon="🌡️">
Yesterday's temperature changed every minute. The **average temperature** isn't found by listing values —
it's $\frac{1}{24}\int_0^{24} T(t)\,dt$, the area under the temperature curve divided by the length of
the day. Every "daily average" you read is secretly an integral.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Hydroelectric power" icon="🌊">
The energy a turbine harvests over a day is the area under its power curve, $\int_0^{24} P(t)\,dt$. Two
plants with the same *peak* output can deliver wildly different *total* energy — the integral, not the
peak, is what fills the grid.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The disk method has cousins: <strong>washers</strong> (a hole in the middle, $\pi(R^2 - r^2)$) and
<strong>shells</strong> (cylindrical layers). They all come from the same "slice, sum, shrink" idea —
only the shape of the slice changes.
</Callout>
