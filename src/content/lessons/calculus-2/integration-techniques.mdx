## The hook: how much work does a rocket do?

A rocket's engine doesn't push with a constant force. As it climbs, the thrust ramps up — say it grows
like $2t\,e^{t^2}$. The **work** done over the burn is the area under that curve, an integral. But you
can't just "read off" the antiderivative of $2t\,e^{t^2}$ from a table. You need a *technique* — a way
to massage the integral into something you already know.

That is what this lesson is about. The Fundamental Theorem told us that *an antiderivative computes
area*. It did **not** tell us how to find antiderivatives of messy expressions. Three techniques cover
the vast majority of cases: **u-substitution**, **integration by parts**, and **partial fractions**.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Integration techniques are pattern-matching tricks: each one **rewrites a hard integral as an easy one**
you already know how to do — substitution undoes the chain rule, by parts undoes the product rule, and
partial fractions splits one fraction into several simple ones.
</Callout>

## Intuition: run the derivative rules backwards

Every differentiation rule you learned has an integration twin, read in reverse:

- The **chain rule** $\frac{d}{dx}f(g(x)) = f'(g(x))\,g'(x)$ run backwards becomes **u-substitution**.
- The **product rule** $\frac{d}{dx}(uv) = u'v + uv'$ run backwards becomes **integration by parts**.
- Splitting a complicated fraction back into the pieces it "came from" is **partial fractions**.

The star of the show is **u-substitution**. The idea: if you spot a chunk $g(x)$ *and* its derivative
$g'(x)$ both sitting in the integrand, rename the chunk $u = g(x)$. Then $du = g'(x)\,dx$ — and the
$g'(x)\,dx$ already present silently turns into $du$. The integral collapses into the variable $u$.

## The formal definition

**u-substitution** (the reverse chain rule):

$$
\int f\big(g(x)\big)\,g'(x)\,dx = \int f(u)\,du, \qquad u = g(x),\; du = g'(x)\,dx.
$$

For our rocket, $u = t^2$ gives $du = 2t\,dt$, so $\int 2t\,e^{t^2}\,dt = \int e^{u}\,du = e^{u} + C = e^{t^2} + C.$

**Integration by parts** (the reverse product rule):

$$
\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du.
$$

You use it when the integrand is a *product* of two unrelated things — e.g. $\int x\,e^{x}\,dx$. Choose
$u = x$ (gets simpler when differentiated) and $dv = e^x\,dx$, then $\int x e^x dx = x e^x - \int e^x dx = (x-1)e^x + C.$

**Partial fractions** splits a rational function into a sum of simple fractions:

$$
\frac{1}{x^2 - 1} = \frac{1}{(x-1)(x+1)} = \frac{1/2}{x-1} - \frac{1/2}{x+1},
$$

and each piece integrates to a logarithm.

<Callout type="why">
In machine learning these aren't academic. Computing the **normalizing constant** of a probability
distribution, the **expected value** of a continuous variable, or the area under an ROC curve all reduce
to integrals — and substitution is the workhorse that makes them solvable in closed form.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Spaceflight" icon="🚀">
The **work** done by a variable thrust $F(t) = 2t\,e^{t^2}$ over a burn from $t=0$ to $t=1$ is
$\int_0^1 2t\,e^{t^2}\,dt$. Substituting $u = t^2$ turns it into $\int_0^1 e^{u}\,du = e - 1 \approx 1.72$.
A wall of notation becomes a one-line answer.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Environmental science" icon="🏭">
A pollutant enters a lake at rate $\frac{2t}{1+t^2}$. The total accumulated over time is
$\int \frac{2t}{1+t^2}\,dt$. Let $u = 1 + t^2$, so $du = 2t\,dt$ — the numerator *is* $du$. The integral
becomes $\int \frac{du}{u} = \ln(1+t^2) + C$. Buildup that looked intractable is just a logarithm.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Pharmacology" icon="💊">
A drug's concentration decays while the body absorbs more, modelled by a product like $t\,e^{-t}$.
The total exposure $\int t\,e^{-t}\,dt$ needs **integration by parts** ($u=t$, $dv=e^{-t}dt$), giving
$-(t+1)e^{-t} + C$ — the basis of the "area under the curve" dosing that pharmacists actually use.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Electronics" icon="🔌">
Filter and circuit responses constantly produce rational functions like $\frac{1}{x^2-1}$. **Partial
fractions** breaks them into $\frac{1/2}{x-1} - \frac{1/2}{x+1}$, each a clean logarithm — exactly how
engineers invert Laplace transforms to find a circuit's behavior over time.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
There's an art to it: the same integral can sometimes be cracked by more than one technique, and some
need two in sequence. Fluency comes from recognizing the *shape* of the integrand quickly — the demo
above trains exactly that reflex.
</Callout>
