## The hook: watching a machine learn, live

You have now collected every piece. **Vectors** gave us a way to represent data and parameters as
lists of numbers. **Gradients** told us which way is uphill on a multivariable surface. **Gradient
descent** turned that into a rule for rolling downhill. **Convexity** told us when that roll is
guaranteed to find the best answer. **Probability** gave us a principled way to say what "best" even
means. And **regression** gave us a model to fit.

In this capstone we assemble them into the simplest complete learning system there is: a straight line
that **learns its own slope and intercept** from data, purely by descending a loss surface. This is, in
miniature, exactly what training a neural network does — same loop, just more parameters.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Learning is optimization**: define a loss that measures error, then run gradient descent on the
model's parameters until the loss is as small as it gets — and the model has "learned."
</Callout>

## Intuition: the four ideas, working as one

Here is how each earlier idea shows up in one training run.

1. **Vectors (linear algebra).** The data points are vectors, and the model's parameters
   $\mathbf{w} = (w, b)$ — slope and intercept — are a vector too. Prediction is the affine map
   $\hat{y} = w x + b$. Stacking many points turns this into a single matrix–vector equation, the
   bridge to everything in linear algebra.

2. **Probability.** We assume each observation is the true line plus **Gaussian noise**. Asking "which
   line makes the data most likely?" (maximum likelihood) turns out to be *identical* to "which line
   has the smallest squared error." So the loss is not arbitrary — it falls out of a probability model.

3. **Gradients (calculus).** To improve the line we need to know how the loss changes as we nudge $w$
   and $b$. That is the gradient $\nabla L = (\partial L/\partial w,\; \partial L/\partial b)$ — the
   steepest-uphill direction in parameter space.

4. **Gradient descent + convexity.** We step opposite the gradient, over and over. Because the
   squared-error loss for a line is **convex** (a single bowl), this is guaranteed to converge to the
   one best fit — no local-minimum traps.

## The formal definition

**The model (vectors).** For each input $x_i$ the prediction is

$$
\hat{y}_i = w\,x_i + b.
$$

**The loss (probability).** Assume targets are the line plus Gaussian noise,
$y_i = w x_i + b + \varepsilon_i$ with $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. The likelihood of
all the data is a product of Gaussians; taking $-\log$ turns the product into a sum and the exponent
into a square. The **negative log-likelihood** is, up to constants,

$$
L(w, b) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \big(w x_i + b - y_i\big)^2,
$$

the **mean squared error**. So *minimizing MSE is maximizing the Gaussian likelihood* — that is the
probability thread made concrete.

**The gradient (calculus).** Differentiate $L$ with respect to each parameter:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial w} = \frac{2}{n}\sum_i (w x_i + b - y_i)\,x_i, \qquad
\frac{\partial L}{\partial b} = \frac{2}{n}\sum_i (w x_i + b - y_i).
$$

**The update (gradient descent).** Step both parameters opposite the gradient:

$$
w \leftarrow w - \eta\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}, \qquad
b \leftarrow b - \eta\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial b}.
$$

Repeat until $\nabla L \approx \mathbf{0}$. Because $L$ is a convex quadratic in $(w, b)$, this single
loop is *guaranteed* to land at the global best-fit line — the same answer the closed-form normal
equations give, but reached by rolling downhill.

<Callout type="why">
This loop — predict, measure loss, compute gradient, step — is the <strong>entire</strong> training
recipe for modern ML. A deep network swaps the line $wx + b$ for millions of stacked nonlinear layers
and computes the gradient by <strong>backpropagation</strong>, but the outer loop is exactly what you
just watched. Master this and you understand how every model learns.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Last-mile logistics" icon="📦">
Fit delivery time as a function of distance from the depot to set realistic customer ETAs. Each
day's deliveries are data points; gradient descent learns the slope (minutes per km) and intercept
(fixed handling time) that minimize squared prediction error.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports analytics" icon="🏀">
Model how a player's points scored relate to minutes played. Training a line by gradient descent
yields a per-minute scoring rate; the residuals flag games where a player wildly over- or
under-performed their usual pace.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Climate science" icon="🌡️">
Regress average regional temperature against year to estimate a warming trend. The learned slope is
the degrees-per-decade rate, and viewing it as maximum likelihood under Gaussian noise gives an
honest sense of how confident that trend really is.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Neural networks" icon="🧠">
The very same loop scales up: replace the single line with stacked layers of weights (giant parameter
vectors), keep the predict → loss → gradient → step cycle, and you are training a deep net. Linear
regression is the one-neuron, one-layer special case of everything that follows.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Step back and see the unity: a <em>vector</em> of parameters, a <em>probabilistic</em> loss, its
<em>gradient</em>, and a <em>descent</em> step on a <em>convex</em> surface. Five tracks of this course
collapse into one elegant idea — <strong>learning is just optimization</strong>.
</Callout>
