## The hook: the best you can do with a fixed budget

So far we have let the ball roll anywhere downhill. But real decisions come with **rules you cannot
break**. You have exactly \$10,000 to split between two investments — not a penny more. A drone has
a fixed battery, so the route length is capped. A factory has a quota it must hit exactly. The question
is no longer "what is the best point anywhere?" but "what is the best point **on this line / curve /
surface** I am confined to?"

This is **constrained optimization**, and its central tool — the **Lagrange multiplier** — is one of
the most elegant ideas in all of applied math. It turns "optimize while obeying a rule" back into the
familiar language of gradients.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
At the best point on a constraint, the goal's contour line is exactly **tangent** to the constraint —
so the two gradients point along the same line: $\nabla f = \lambda \, \nabla g$.
</Callout>

## Intuition: walk the fence until you can't climb higher

Picture the objective $f(x, y)$ as a hill, drawn with **contour lines** (rings of equal height, like a
topographic map). The constraint $g(x, y) = c$ is a fence you must walk along — you cannot leave it.

As you walk the fence, you cross contour lines, going up or down in $f$. You are at the best point when
walking *either* direction along the fence no longer increases $f$. Geometrically, that happens exactly
where the fence runs **tangent** to a contour — where it just *kisses* one ring without crossing it.

At that kiss point, the contour and the fence share the same direction, so their **perpendiculars**
(the gradients $\nabla f$ and $\nabla g$) are **parallel**. "Parallel" means one is a scalar multiple of
the other, and that scalar is the **Lagrange multiplier** $\lambda$. That single picture is the whole
method.

## The formal definition

To optimize $f(\mathbf{x})$ subject to a constraint $g(\mathbf{x}) = c$, form the **Lagrangian**:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = f(\mathbf{x}) - \lambda\,\big(g(\mathbf{x}) - c\big).
$$

Setting all its partial derivatives to zero recovers the tangency condition plus the constraint itself:

$$
\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \lambda \, \nabla g(\mathbf{x}), \qquad g(\mathbf{x}) = c.
$$

The first equation says the gradients are parallel (the geometric "kiss"); the second keeps you on the
fence. For our demo, $f = xy$ gives $\nabla f = (y, x)$ and $g = x + y$ gives $\nabla g = (1, 1)$, so
$\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$ forces $y = x$. Combined with $x + y = B$ that yields the balanced
optimum $x = y = B/2$ — exactly what the slider shows.

<Callout type="why">
Constraints are everywhere in machine learning. <strong>Regularization</strong> is "minimize the loss
subject to keeping the weights small." <strong>Support vector machines</strong> maximize a margin
subject to classifying points correctly. The multiplier $\lambda$ even has a meaning: it is the
<em>shadow price</em> — how much the optimal value would improve if you loosened the constraint by one unit.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Investment allocation" icon="💰">
Maximize a portfolio's expected return subject to spending exactly your capital and not exceeding a
risk ceiling. The optimum sits where the return contour is tangent to the budget plane — and the
Lagrange multiplier tells you the extra return one more dollar of risk budget would buy.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Manufacturing" icon="🏭">
A plant minimizes production cost while meeting an exact output quota. The cost contours kiss the
quota surface at the cheapest feasible production mix; the multiplier is the marginal cost of raising
the quota by one unit — gold for pricing decisions.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Nutrition planning" icon="🥗">
Design the cheapest meal plan that hits exact calorie and protein targets. Minimizing cost subject to
those equality constraints is a classic Lagrange problem; the optimal plan is where the cost contour is
tangent to the constraint set, balancing price against the binding requirements.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Spaceflight" icon="🛰️">
Plan a satellite maneuver that reaches a target orbit using the least fuel, subject to fixed
start and end conditions. The fuel-optimal trajectory is found where the cost's gradient aligns with
the constraint gradients — Lagrange multipliers underpin the whole field of optimal control.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The leap from unconstrained to constrained optimization is small but profound: instead of seeking
$\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$ (flat everywhere), we seek $\nabla f$ <em>parallel to</em> $\nabla g$ (flat
<em>along the constraint</em>). The fence absorbs the leftover slope.
</Callout>
