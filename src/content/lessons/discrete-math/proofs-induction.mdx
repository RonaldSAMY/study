## The hook: how do you check infinitely many cases?

Suppose someone claims that the first $n$ whole numbers always add up to $n(n+1)/2$. You could test it:
$1 = 1$, $1+2 = 3$, $1+2+3 = 6$… and it keeps working. But "I checked a thousand cases" is not the same
as "it is true for *all* numbers" — and there are infinitely many. A **proof** is an argument that
settles the question forever, with no exceptions left to test. Building proofs is the moment math turns
from *computing answers* into *knowing why they are right* — the exact skill behind every theorem you
will trust later.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **proof** is a chain of logical implications from things we accept to the thing we want; **induction**
is the special chain that topples an infinite row of dominoes — prove the first falls, and prove each
one knocks over the next.

</Callout>

## Intuition: three ways to be certain

Using the implications from the last lesson, there are three workhorse proof styles:

1. **Direct proof.** Assume the hypothesis and march straight to the conclusion via $p \to q$ steps.
   *"If $n$ is even then $n^2$ is even"*: write $n = 2k$, so $n^2 = 4k^2 = 2(2k^2)$, which is even. Done.
2. **Proof by contradiction.** Assume the statement is **false**, then derive something impossible. The
   false assumption must have been the problem. This is how we show $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.
3. **Proof by induction.** For statements about *every* natural number $n$. Show it holds for the
   starting case, then show that *if* it holds for some $k$, it must hold for $k+1$. Like dominoes:
   knock the first, guarantee each topples the next, and the whole infinite line falls.

Induction is the star of this lesson because it is how we prove things about loops, recursion, and
sums — the structures that fill computer science and machine learning.

<Callout type="tip">
A chain needs <em>both</em> parts. If the first domino never falls (no base case), nothing happens —
even if every domino *would* knock over its neighbor. And if some domino fails to reach the next (a
broken inductive step), the chain stops dead. Miss either and the proof collapses.
</Callout>

## The formal definition

To prove a statement $P(n)$ holds for every integer $n \ge 1$ by **mathematical induction**:

$$
\underbrace{P(1)}_{\text{base case}}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\underbrace{\big(P(k) \to P(k+1)\big)\ \text{for all } k \ge 1}_{\text{inductive step}}
\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; P(n)\ \text{for all } n \ge 1.
$$

The assumption $P(k)$ inside the step is the **inductive hypothesis** — you get to *assume* the claim
at $k$ in order to prove it at $k+1$. Here is the running example, fully proved:

**Claim.** $\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^{n} i = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$.

**Base case.** For $n = 1$: the left side is $1$, the right side is $\tfrac{1 \cdot 2}{2} = 1$. ✓

**Inductive step.** Assume $\sum_{i=1}^{k} i = \tfrac{k(k+1)}{2}$. Then

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{k+1} i = \underbrace{\frac{k(k+1)}{2}}_{\text{hypothesis}} + (k+1)
= \frac{k(k+1) + 2(k+1)}{2} = \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}.
$$

That is exactly $P(k+1)$. By induction the formula holds for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="why">
Induction is how we prove **algorithms are correct** and how recurrences are solved. Convergence
arguments for gradient descent, correctness of recursive code, and bounds in learning theory all lean
on "it holds at step $k$, therefore at step $k+1$." Master the domino and you can reason about any
process that builds on itself.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Computer science" icon="💻">
A recursive function works for an input of size $n$ because it works for the **base case** (say
$n = 0$) and each call correctly reduces to a smaller one. That argument *is* induction — it is the
standard way to prove a recursive algorithm returns the right answer for every input size.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Finance" icon="💰">
Compound interest says a balance grows by a factor $(1 + r)$ each year. Prove by induction that after
$n$ years the balance is $P(1+r)^{n}$: it holds at year $1$, and if it holds at year $k$, multiplying
by $(1+r)$ once more gives year $k+1$. Each year topples the next.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Climbing a ladder" icon="🪜">
To argue you can reach *every* rung: you can reach the **first** rung (base case), and from any rung you
can always step to the **next** (inductive step). Together these guarantee you reach arbitrarily high —
the most physical picture of induction there is.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Epidemiology" icon="🦠">
A rumor (or a virus) that starts with one person and where every informed person tells someone new each
day reaches a predictable count. Showing "if $k$ people know on day $k$, then more know on day $k+1$"
is an inductive step — the same logic models cascades in networks.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="warning">
The inductive hypothesis is a tool, not cheating: you assume $P(k)$ only to derive $P(k+1)$, never the
final claim outright. And never skip the base case — without a first domino, a perfect chain of
implications proves nothing at all.
</Callout>
