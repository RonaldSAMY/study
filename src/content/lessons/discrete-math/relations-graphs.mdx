## The hook: the web is just dots and lines

Open any social app and you are staring at a **graph**: people are dots, friendships are lines between
them. Google ranks pages by how web pages link to one another — dots and lines again. Map apps find
routes through a graph of intersections and roads. Every one of these is built from a simpler idea: a
**relation**, a precise statement of *which things are connected to which*. Relations, the special ones
we call **functions**, and the **graphs** that draw them are the structures discrete math hands directly
to machine learning.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A **relation** says which elements of one set are linked to which of another; a **function** is a
relation where every input has exactly **one** output; and a **graph** is just a relation drawn as dots
(nodes) joined by lines (edges).
</Callout>

## Intuition: from pairs to pictures

Take two sets — say users and movies. A relation picks out **ordered pairs**: *(Ana, Inception)* if Ana
watched Inception. Formally a relation is any subset of the **Cartesian product** $A \times B$, the set
of all possible pairs. That is the bridge from the last lessons: a relation is a *set* of pairs,
selected by a true/false *condition*.

A **function** is the disciplined kind of relation: each input is paired with **exactly one** output.
"Each user has one birth year" is a function; "each user watched some movies" is a relation but not a
function (one user, many movies). Functions are the input–output machines you have used since algebra,
now seen as special relations.

When both sets are the *same* set — people related to people, cities to cities — drawing the relation
gives a **graph**: a node for each element, and an edge for each related pair. If the relation is
symmetric (friendship), edges are undirected lines; if it has direction (follows, links), they are
arrows.

## The formal definition

The **Cartesian product** of sets $A$ and $B$ is every ordered pair:

$$
A \times B = \{\, (a, b) : a \in A,\ b \in B \,\}.
$$

A **relation** $R$ from $A$ to $B$ is any subset $R \subseteq A \times B$; we write $a\,R\,b$ when the
pair is in it. A **function** $f : A \to B$ is a relation in which every $a \in A$ appears in **exactly
one** pair — one output per input:

$$
\big(a, b_1\big) \in f \ \text{and}\ \big(a, b_2\big) \in f \;\Longrightarrow\; b_1 = b_2.
$$

A **graph** is a pair $G = (V, E)$ of **vertices** $V$ and **edges** $E$, where $E$ is a relation on
$V$. Its **adjacency matrix** $A$ encodes the edges:

$$
A_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } (i, j) \in E, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
$$

A handy fact you can prove by induction: entry $(i, j)$ of the matrix power $A^{k}$ counts the number of
length-$k$ paths from node $i$ to node $j$ — turning a graph question into matrix multiplication.

<Callout type="why">
This is where discrete math meets the rest of the course. **Graph neural networks** pass information
along edges; a recommendation engine is a graph of users and items; and that adjacency matrix is a
**matrix** in the linear-algebra sense — multiply it and you propagate influence through the network.
Relations are also exactly the tables of a relational database.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Social networks" icon="🧑‍🤝‍🧑">
People are nodes; friendships are undirected edges, follows are directed ones. "People you may know" is
the engine finding nodes two hops away in the graph — friends of your friends who are not yet your
friends, a set difference over the adjacency structure.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Family trees" icon="🌳">
A family tree is a **directed graph**: an arrow from parent to child. "Is an ancestor of" is the
relation you get by following arrows repeatedly — a *transitive* relation, since an ancestor of your
ancestor is also your ancestor.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Flight routes" icon="✈️">
Airports are nodes; a direct flight is an edge, often **weighted** by price or time. Finding the
cheapest trip with one connection is asking for the best length-2 path — the matrix-power idea, made
into your travel itinerary.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Search engines" icon="🔎">
The web is a giant directed graph: pages are nodes, hyperlinks are arrows. PageRank treats a link as a
*vote* and ranks pages by the structure of this graph — a relation over billions of nodes that decides
what you see first.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Every example reuses one move: write down which pairs are related, then either reason about the pairs
(relation), demand one output each (function), or draw the dots and lines (graph). Same object, three
costumes.
</Callout>
