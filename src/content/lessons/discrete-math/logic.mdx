## The hook: the two-symbol language behind every computer

Strip a computer down to its core and you find one astonishingly simple idea repeated billions of
times: a wire is either **on** or **off**, **true** or **false**, $1$ or $0$. Stack a few rules for
combining those wires — *and*, *or*, *not* — and you can add numbers, store memories, and eventually
train neural networks. Those rules are **propositional logic**, and they are the direct cousins of the
set operations you just learned.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**Logic** is the algebra of statements that are either true or false, built from three operations —
**AND**, **OR**, **NOT** — whose behavior is captured completely by a **truth table**.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from sets to statements

In the last lesson, *and / or / not* combined **collections**. Here they combine **statements**. A
**proposition** is any sentence that is definitely true or false: *"it is raining,"* *"x > 5."* Call
them $p$ and $q$.

- $p \land q$ (**AND**) is true only when **both** are true — the cousin of intersection.
- $p \lor q$ (**OR**) is true when **at least one** is true — the cousin of union.
- $\lnot p$ (**NOT**) flips true to false — the cousin of complement.

The link is exact: if $p$ means *"x is in A"* and $q$ means *"x is in B,"* then $p \land q$ is precisely
the membership test for $A \cap B$. Logic and sets are two views of one structure.

There is one new operation with no everyday set-name: **implication** $p \to q$, read *"if p then q."*
It is the engine of every proof, and it has one famous quirk we will meet below.

## The formal definition

A **truth table** lists every combination of inputs and the resulting output. For two inputs there are
$2^2 = 4$ rows; for $n$ inputs, $2^n$. The basic connectives:

$$
\begin{array}{cc|ccc}
p & q & p \land q & p \lor q & p \to q \\\hline
\text{T} & \text{T} & \text{T} & \text{T} & \text{T} \\
\text{T} & \text{F} & \text{F} & \text{T} & \text{F} \\
\text{F} & \text{T} & \text{F} & \text{T} & \text{T} \\
\text{F} & \text{F} & \text{F} & \text{F} & \text{T}
\end{array}
$$

Two statements are **logically equivalent** ($\equiv$) when their truth tables match column-for-column.
The most useful equivalences are **De Morgan's laws**, which connect logic right back to set
complements:

$$
\lnot (p \land q) \equiv \lnot p \lor \lnot q, \qquad
\lnot (p \lor q) \equiv \lnot p \land \lnot q.
$$

Implication can be rewritten purely with the basics: $p \to q \equiv \lnot p \lor q$. A statement that
is true in *every* row is a **tautology**; one false in every row is a **contradiction**.

<Callout type="why">
Every comparison in a model — `if loss < threshold`, a ReLU's `max(0, x)`, a decision-tree split — is a
proposition. Loss functions for classification (cross-entropy) come straight from logic and
information; and the boolean masks that power attention and padding in transformers are literally
tables of true/false.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Circuit logic gates" icon="⚡">
A physical **AND gate** outputs current only when both inputs carry current; an **OR gate** needs just
one. A **NAND gate** ($\lnot(p \land q)$) is special: you can build *every* other gate from NANDs
alone. The truth table in the demo is the exact spec a chip designer wires into silicon.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Search engines" icon="🔎">
Advanced search uses **boolean operators**: `cats AND dogs` requires both terms, `cats OR dogs` accepts
either, and `cats -dogs` is `cats AND NOT dogs`. The engine evaluates a logical expression over each
page to decide *true (show it)* or *false (hide it)*.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Legal contracts" icon="⚖️">
A clause like *"if payment is late, then a fee applies"* is an implication $p \to q$. Lawyers exploit
the vacuous-truth rule: if payment is never late ($p$ false), the clause is satisfied regardless. Whole
contracts are conjunctions of conditions that must *all* hold.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Smart home" icon="🏠">
An automation rule reads *"turn on the porch light if it is dark AND motion is detected."* The hub
evaluates $p \land q$ every second. Change it to OR and the light triggers far more often — the same
toggle you feel in the demo, deciding your electricity bill.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
De Morgan's laws are the bridge back to the previous lesson: <em>not (A and B)</em> equals
<em>(not A) or (not B)</em>. The complement of an intersection is the union of the complements — set
theory and logic telling the same story.
</Callout>
