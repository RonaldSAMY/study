## The hook: how do banks price risk they cannot calculate?

A bank holds a tangle of thousands of contracts, and someone asks a simple question: *"How much
could we lose on a really bad day?"* There is no neat formula — the payoff depends on interest rates,
exchange rates, and defaults all moving together. So the bank does something almost cheeky: it
**simulates tomorrow** a million times with random market moves, records the loss in each imaginary
tomorrow, and reads off the answer from the pile of results. No integral solved, just **a lot of dice
rolled**. That is the Monte Carlo method, and it quietly powers finance, physics, graphics, and every
generative model you will meet next.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
To compute an average (or an area, or an integral) you cannot do by hand, **take many random samples
and average them** — the more samples, the closer the average gets to the true value.
</Callout>

## Intuition: averages instead of formulas

You already know that the **expected value** of a random variable is its long-run average, $\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_x x\,p(x)$
or $\int x\,f(x)\,dx$. Monte Carlo turns that definition on its head. Instead of *computing* the
expectation, it **estimates** it: draw samples $x_1, \dots, x_N$ from the distribution and take their
plain average.

The deep reason this works is the **law of large numbers** you met with the dice simulator: as the
number of samples grows, the sample average converges to the true expectation. Monte Carlo is simply
that law, used on purpose, as a calculator.

The same trick computes areas and integrals. Want the area of a weird blob? Throw darts at a square you
*can* measure, count the fraction that land inside the blob, and multiply. A hard geometry problem
becomes a counting problem — which is exactly how we will estimate $\pi$ in a moment.

## The formal definition

We want the expectation of some quantity $g(X)$ under a distribution $p$:

$$
\mu = \mathbb{E}_{X \sim p}\big[g(X)\big] = \int g(x)\, p(x)\, dx.
$$

The **Monte Carlo estimator** draws $N$ independent samples $x_i \sim p$ and averages:

$$
\hat{\mu}_N = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} g(x_i).
$$

This estimator is **unbiased** — on average it equals the truth, $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\mu}_N] = \mu$ — and
the law of large numbers guarantees $\hat{\mu}_N \to \mu$ as $N \to \infty$. How fast? Its standard
error is

$$
\operatorname{sd}(\hat{\mu}_N) = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}, \qquad \sigma^2 = \operatorname{Var}\big(g(X)\big).
$$

That $\sqrt{N}$ in the denominator is the headline: to halve the error you need **four times** as many
samples. Crucially, this rate does *not* depend on the dimension — which is why Monte Carlo beats grid
methods for the high-dimensional integrals all over machine learning.

```python
# Estimate E[g(X)] for X ~ p by averaging samples
samples = p.sample(N)          # draw N independent draws
estimate = g(samples).mean()   # the Monte Carlo estimate
stderr   = g(samples).std() / N**0.5   # shrinks like 1/sqrt(N)
```

<Callout type="why">
Generative models are built on expectations no one can integrate: the evidence in a VAE, the loss in a
diffusion model, the policy gradient in RL. Every one is approximated by averaging a handful of random
samples. Monte Carlo is the engine under the whole track.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Quantitative finance" icon="💹">
To price a complex option, a desk simulates thousands of random price paths for the underlying asset,
computes the payoff on each, and **averages** them (discounted to today). The spread of those payoffs
also gives the risk. No closed-form Black–Scholes needed — just sampled futures.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Video-game AI" icon="🎮">
A Go or chess engine cannot search every line. Instead it plays out many **random rollouts** from the
current position and averages the win rates to score each move. This Monte Carlo tree search is what let
AlphaGo evaluate positions no formula could.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Computer graphics" icon="🎬">
A photorealistic frame asks: how much light reaches each pixel, summed over every path light could take?
That integral is monstrous, so renderers fire **random rays** into the scene and average the light they
gather. The grainy "noise" in a half-rendered image *is* Monte Carlo error shrinking.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Epidemiology" icon="🦠">
To forecast an outbreak, modelers run a disease through a population thousands of times with random
contacts and recoveries. Averaging the runs gives the expected case curve; the range across runs gives
the uncertainty band you see on public dashboards.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Four very different integrals — a payoff, a win rate, a light field, a case count — all attacked the
same way: sample, evaluate, average. That uniformity is what makes Monte Carlo so beloved.
</Callout>
