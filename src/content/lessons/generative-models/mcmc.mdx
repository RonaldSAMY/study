## The hook: drawing samples when you only know "how likely," not "how often"

Plain Monte Carlo needs you to **sample** from a distribution. But the most useful distributions in
science arrive in a frustrating form: you can compute how *relatively* likely any single state is, yet
you have no idea how to draw fresh examples, and you cannot even add up all the states to normalize it.
A protein has more configurations than atoms in the universe; a Bayesian posterior hides an integral no
one can solve. You can *score* a state — but not *sample* one.

Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is the escape hatch. Instead of sampling directly, it takes a **random
walk** through the space, nudged so that it spends time in each region exactly in proportion to that
region's probability. Let the walk run, and the places it visits *are* your samples.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Build a random walk whose long-run visiting frequency equals the target distribution — then the path it
traces gives you samples, even from a distribution you can only score up to a constant.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a guided wander

Recall from Bayes that a posterior is $p(\theta \mid \text{data}) \propto p(\text{data} \mid \theta)\,p(\theta)$.
The annoying part is the "$\propto$": we know the shape but not the normalizing constant, so we cannot
sample directly. MCMC only ever needs **ratios** of the target, and ratios make the unknown constant
cancel.

The **Metropolis** recipe is almost suspiciously simple. From your current spot:

1. **Propose** a small random step to a nearby point.
2. Compare densities. If the proposal is **more** probable, **always go**.
3. If it is **less** probable, go anyway with probability equal to the ratio — otherwise stay put.

Always climbing toward higher probability, but *sometimes* tolerating a downhill step, is the magic.
Pure greed would get stuck on the first peak; the occasional downhill move lets the walk explore the
whole landscape while still lingering where probability is high.

## The formal definition

We want samples from a target $\pi(x)$ known only up to a constant: $\pi(x) = \tilde{\pi}(x)/Z$ with
$Z$ unknown. Metropolis–Hastings builds a Markov chain using a proposal $q(x' \mid x)$. Given the
current state $x$, propose $x' \sim q(\cdot \mid x)$ and accept it with probability

$$
A(x \to x') = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x')\, q(x \mid x')}{\tilde{\pi}(x)\, q(x' \mid x)}\right).
$$

Because only the **ratio** $\tilde{\pi}(x')/\tilde{\pi}(x)$ appears, the unknown $Z$ cancels — that is
the whole point. With a symmetric proposal (like a Gaussian step, where $q(x'\mid x) = q(x \mid x')$)
the $q$ terms cancel too, leaving the plain Metropolis rule $\min(1, \tilde{\pi}(x')/\tilde{\pi}(x))$.

```python
x = x0
for _ in range(steps):
    x_prop = x + sigma * randn()              # symmetric Gaussian step
    ratio  = target(x_prop) / target(x)       # unknown Z cancels
    if rand() < min(1, ratio):
        x = x_prop                            # accept
    chain.append(x)                            # keep it either way
```

This chain is built to satisfy **detailed balance**, $\pi(x)\,T(x\to x') = \pi(x')\,T(x'\to x)$, which
guarantees $\pi$ is its stationary distribution. After an initial **burn-in** (while the walk forgets
its starting point), the collected states behave like samples from $\pi$ — though consecutive ones are
**correlated**, not independent.

<Callout type="why">
MCMC is how Bayesian models are actually fit and how energy-based generative models draw samples. The
same accept/reject logic underlies physics simulations and, conceptually, the iterative denoising you
will meet in diffusion models. When a distribution is too tangled to sample, MCMC is the workhorse.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Molecular simulation" icon="🧬">
A molecule's states follow the Boltzmann distribution, $p(\text{config}) \propto e^{-E/kT}$, with an
impossible normalizer. Metropolis Monte Carlo — its original 1953 use — proposes jiggling the atoms and
accepts moves by the energy ratio, sampling realistic shapes to predict how a drug binds its target.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Cosmology" icon="🔭">
To infer the universe's parameters (its age, its dark-energy density) from telescope data, astronomers
run MCMC over the Bayesian posterior. The cloud of accepted points becomes the "corner plot" of
credible regions reported in papers — direct output of a guided random walk.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Image denoising" icon="🖼️">
Treat a clean image as a high-probability configuration of pixels under a model that rewards smoothness
and agreement with the noisy input. Sampling that posterior by flipping pixels and accepting by the
probability ratio — a Gibbs/Metropolis sweep — reconstructs the underlying picture.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Codebreaking" icon="🔐">
To crack a substitution cipher, define a "score" for how English-like a decryption looks. MCMC proposes
swapping two letters in the key and accepts by the score ratio. Within thousands of steps the chain
wanders into the one mapping that turns gibberish into readable text.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Atoms, galaxies, pixels, and ciphers — each is a space with a probability you can only *score*. The same
propose-and-accept loop draws samples from all of them without ever computing the normalizer.
</Callout>
