## The hook: how do you take the derivative of a coin flip?

Training a neural network means following gradients downhill. But generative models want a **random**
step in the middle: "sample a latent code, then decode it." The moment you write `z = sample(...)`, the
chain rule hits a wall — there is no smooth path from the network's parameters, through a dice roll, to
the loss. Randomness is not differentiable.

The reparameterization trick is a beautifully simple workaround. It splits the randomness off into a
fixed, parameter-free noise source, and leaves behind a smooth, deterministic function that gradients
*can* climb back through. You still get a genuine random sample — but now you can also differentiate it.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Instead of sampling $z$ directly from a distribution that depends on your parameters, sample fixed noise
$\varepsilon$ and **transform** it: $z = \mu + \sigma\,\varepsilon$. The randomness lives in
$\varepsilon$; the parameters live in a differentiable formula.

</Callout>

## Intuition: move the randomness out of the way

Suppose you want a sample from $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are produced by
your network. Sampling that distribution *directly* gives a number with no usable derivative with
respect to $\mu$ or $\sigma$ — the operation is a black box of randomness.

Now recall the **change of variables** idea: stretching and shifting a random variable reshapes its
distribution in a predictable way. So draw a standard normal $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ — pure
noise, depending on *nothing* — and form

$$
z = \mu + \sigma\,\varepsilon.
$$

The result is still distributed exactly as $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$. But now $z$ is a plain,
differentiable function of $\mu$ and $\sigma$: $\partial z / \partial \mu = 1$ and
$\partial z / \partial \sigma = \varepsilon$. The chain rule sails right through, because the only
random thing, $\varepsilon$, is a constant as far as the gradient is concerned.

## The formal definition

We want the gradient of an expectation whose distribution $q_\theta$ depends on parameters $\theta$:

$$
\nabla_\theta\, \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_\theta}\big[f(z)\big].
$$

The problem: the thing we sample from *is* what depends on $\theta$, so we cannot push $\nabla_\theta$
inside the expectation. The fix is to express $z$ as a deterministic, differentiable transform of
parameter-free noise, $z = g_\theta(\varepsilon)$ with $\varepsilon \sim p(\varepsilon)$. For a Gaussian,
$g_\theta(\varepsilon) = \mu + \sigma\,\varepsilon$. Now the distribution we average over no longer
depends on $\theta$, so the gradient moves inside:

$$
\nabla_\theta\, \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_\theta}\big[f(z)\big]
= \mathbb{E}_{\varepsilon \sim p(\varepsilon)}\big[\nabla_\theta\, f(g_\theta(\varepsilon))\big].
$$

The right-hand side is an ordinary Monte Carlo estimate of a gradient — sample a few $\varepsilon$,
backpropagate through $f$ and $g_\theta$, average. This **pathwise** estimator usually has far lower
variance than the alternative score-function (REINFORCE) estimator, which is why it dominates wherever
the latent variable is continuous.

```python
eps   = torch.randn_like(sigma)   # noise: requires_grad = False
z     = mu + sigma * eps          # differentiable in mu, sigma
loss  = f(z).mean()
loss.backward()                   # gradients flow into mu and sigma
```

<Callout type="why">
This single trick is what makes the **VAE** trainable end to end: the encoder outputs $\mu$ and
$\sigma$, the trick samples a latent $z$, the decoder reconstructs the input, and the gradient flows all
the way back through the sampling step. No reparameterization, no VAE.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Robotics" icon="🤖">
A robot's policy outputs a *distribution* over actions and adds exploration noise: $a = \mu_\theta(s) + \sigma\,\varepsilon$.
Reparameterizing lets the learning signal flow back into $\mu_\theta$ and $\sigma$, so the controller
learns not just an action but how much to explore — the backbone of algorithms like SAC.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Bayesian deep learning" icon="🩺">
To express uncertainty, a medical model treats each weight as a distribution, $w = \mu + \sigma\,\varepsilon$,
rather than a fixed number ("Bayes by Backprop"). The trick trains $\mu$ and $\sigma$ by gradient
descent, so the network can say "I am unsure" on an unfamiliar scan instead of guessing confidently.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Language models" icon="🔤">
Picking a discrete token is non-differentiable, so the **Gumbel-softmax** trick reparameterizes a
categorical draw: add fixed Gumbel noise to the logits, then soften with a temperature. Gradients flow
through the relaxed choice, letting models learn to make near-discrete selections.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Supply-chain planning" icon="📦">
Optimizing inventory under random demand means minimizing an expected cost. Writing demand as a fixed
noise source pushed through a parameterized model turns the noisy objective into a differentiable one, so
gradient methods can tune reorder points directly against the sampled scenarios.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Actions, weights, tokens, demand — in each case a random quantity sits in the middle of an optimization.
The same move (fixed noise, deterministic transform) restores the gradient path every time.
</Callout>
