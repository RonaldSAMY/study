## The hook: grading on a curve

A chemistry professor gives a brutal midterm: the raw scores cluster around 42 out of 100. Panic. Then the
professor **curves** them — shifting and rescaling so the class centers on a B with a sensible spread.
Suddenly the scores are comparable, the outliers are clear, and everyone can be ranked fairly. Nothing about
who-knew-what changed; the *distribution* was just standardized.

Deep networks need the same treatment. As signals pass through layers, their distributions drift — getting
larger, smaller, off-center — which makes the layers above chase a moving target and slows training to a
crawl. **Normalization** layers curve the activations on the fly: recenter to mean 0, rescale to variance 1,
every step.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Normalization standardizes a layer's activations to **mean 0, variance 1**, then lets the network rescale
them with two learnable knobs — so every layer always receives a clean, predictable input.
</Callout>

## Intuition: keep the inputs civilized

From **descriptive statistics** you know the two numbers that summarize any batch of values: the **mean**
(center) and the **standard deviation** (spread). From **regularization** you know we want training to be
stable and well-behaved. Normalization combines both ideas.

The problem it solves is *internal covariate shift*: as the weights below change during training, the
distribution of activations feeding the next layer keeps shifting. Each layer must constantly re-adapt to a
new input distribution — like trying to learn while someone keeps changing the units. Normalization pins that
distribution in place: standardize first, *then* learn.

There are two flavors, differing only in **which direction** they compute the statistics:

- **Batch norm**: for each feature, use the mean and variance **across the batch** of examples.
- **Layer norm**: for each example, use the mean and variance **across that example's own features**.

## The formal definition

For a set of activations $x_1, \dots, x_m$, compute the mean and variance, standardize, then apply the
learnable scale $\gamma$ and shift $\beta$:

$$
\mu = \frac{1}{m}\sum_i x_i, \qquad \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{m}\sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2,
$$

$$
\hat{x}_i = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \epsilon}}, \qquad y_i = \gamma\,\hat{x}_i + \beta.
$$

The tiny $\epsilon$ avoids division by zero. The genius of $\gamma$ and $\beta$: the network can *undo* the
normalization if it wants ($\gamma = \sigma$, $\beta = \mu$), so it never loses expressive power — it just
gets to *choose* the scale rather than have it drift.

```python
mu  = x.mean(axis=0)                  # batch norm: stats per feature, over the batch
var = x.var(axis=0)
x_hat = (x - mu) / np.sqrt(var + eps)
y = gamma * x_hat + beta              # learnable scale and shift
```

<Callout type="why">
Batch norm depends on the whole batch, so it struggles with tiny batches and sequence models. Layer norm
normalizes each example on its own — independent of batch size — which is why <strong>transformers</strong>
and RNNs use layer norm almost universally.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Education" icon="🎓">
Curving exam scores is literal standardization: subtract the class mean, divide by the spread, and a raw 42
becomes a z-score you can compare across exams. Batch norm curves each neuron's outputs the same way, every
training step.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Audio streaming" icon="🔊">
Music platforms apply **loudness normalization** (LUFS): a whisper-quiet jazz track and a brick-walled pop
song are rescaled to the same perceived volume so you are not lunging for the dial between songs. Layer norm
does this per "song" (per example); batch norm does it per "channel" across the playlist.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Finance" icon="💱">
To compare a coffee's price in Tokyo, London, and New York, you convert everything to one currency and adjust
for local cost of living. Only after putting values on a common scale can you reason about them — exactly
what normalization gives the next layer.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Photography" icon="📷">
Auto **white balance** rescales a photo's color channels so a sheet of paper reads as neutral white under
warm or cold light. The scene's content is unchanged; its distribution is recentered. A normalization layer
white-balances the activations before the next layer looks at them.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
A pleasant side effect: batch norm's batch statistics inject a little noise each step, which acts as mild
<em>regularization</em> — sometimes reducing the need for dropout. Stability and generalization, two for one.
</Callout>
