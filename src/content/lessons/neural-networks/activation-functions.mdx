## The hook: why stacking linear layers is a waste

Imagine wiring three audio mixers in a row, each just scaling and adding signals. No matter how many you
chain, the result is still one big scaling-and-adding — a single linear pass. Stack a hundred linear
neuron layers and you get the algebraic power of... one linear layer. Something has to **bend** the signal
between layers, or depth buys you nothing.

That bend is the **activation function**. It is the small nonlinear twist after each neuron's weighted sum
that lets networks carve curved decision boundaries, model "if-then" behavior, and ultimately approximate
almost any function.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
An activation function is a **nonlinear** squash applied to each neuron's output — without it a deep
network collapses into a single linear layer.
</Callout>

## Intuition: shape matters, and so does the slope

A neuron's pre-activation $z = \mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x} + b$ is a straight-line score. The activation
reshapes it:

- **Sigmoid** squashes anything into $(0,1)$ — great for a probability, but it *saturates*: far from zero
  the curve goes flat.
- **Tanh** is a recentered sigmoid landing in $(-1,1)$, so its outputs are zero-centered.
- **ReLU** simply zeroes out negatives, $\max(0, z)$ — cheap, and it does not saturate for positive
  inputs, which keeps gradients alive in deep networks.
- **GELU** is a smooth, modern cousin of ReLU used throughout transformers; it lets a little negative
  signal leak through.
- **Softmax** is special: it acts on a whole vector of scores and turns it into a probability
  distribution that sums to 1 — the standard final layer for classification.

The second thing that matters is the **derivative**. Learning happens by following slopes (the chain rule,
two lessons ahead). Where an activation is flat, its derivative is near zero and the neuron stops learning
— the dreaded *vanishing gradient*. So we judge an activation by both its shape **and** its slope.

## The formal definition

The common activations and their derivatives:

$$
\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}}, \qquad \sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)\,\bigl(1 - \sigma(z)\bigr).
$$

$$
\tanh(z), \qquad \frac{d}{dz}\tanh(z) = 1 - \tanh^2(z).
$$

$$
\operatorname{ReLU}(z) = \max(0, z), \qquad \operatorname{ReLU}'(z) = \begin{cases} 1 & z > 0 \\ 0 & z < 0 \end{cases}.
$$

GELU uses the standard normal CDF $\Phi$: $\operatorname{GELU}(z) = z\,\Phi(z)$. For a vector of scores
$\mathbf{z} = (z_1, \dots, z_K)$, **softmax** produces a distribution:

$$
\operatorname{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} e^{z_j}}, \qquad \sum_{i} \operatorname{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_i = 1.
$$

```python
relu = np.maximum(0, z)
gelu = z * 0.5 * (1 + erf(z / np.sqrt(2)))
probs = np.exp(z - z.max()) / np.exp(z - z.max()).sum()  # stable softmax
```

<Callout type="why">
The choice of activation is not cosmetic. Replacing sigmoid with ReLU in hidden layers was one of the
changes that made very deep networks trainable, precisely because ReLU's derivative does not vanish for
positive inputs.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Language models" icon="💬">
The final layer of a chatbot predicts the next token with <strong>softmax</strong> over the whole
vocabulary: tens of thousands of scores become a probability distribution. The model then samples its next
word from that distribution.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Computer vision" icon="📷">
Convolutional image networks lean on <strong>ReLU</strong> after almost every layer. It is fast to compute
on millions of pixels and keeps gradients flowing through dozens of layers so the network can learn edges,
textures, then objects.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Game AI" icon="🎮">
A reinforcement-learning agent's policy head often uses <strong>tanh</strong> to bound a continuous action
(like a steering angle) into a safe range such as -1 to 1, with outputs naturally centered at zero.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Genomics" icon="🧬">
A model scoring whether a DNA segment is a binding site ends in a single <strong>sigmoid</strong>, turning
the raw score into a probability a biologist can threshold — say, flag anything above 0.9.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Rule of thumb: ReLU or GELU for hidden layers (keep gradients alive), sigmoid for a single
yes/no probability, softmax for picking one of many classes.
</Callout>
