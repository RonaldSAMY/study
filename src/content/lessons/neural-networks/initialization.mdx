## The hook: turning the amp up to eleven

Picture a live band running through a chain of effects pedals into an amplifier. If each pedal is set
too quiet, by the time the signal reaches the speaker it has faded into a whisper. If each pedal is set
too loud, the signal clips, screeches, and feeds back into an unusable roar. Sound engineers call the
sweet spot **gain staging**: every stage should pass the signal along at roughly the same healthy level.

A deep neural network is exactly this kind of chain — except the "stages" are layers, and the "gain" is
set by the **scale of the random starting weights**. Get that scale wrong and a 30-layer network is dead
before training even begins. This is the problem of **vanishing and exploding gradients**, and the fix is
a careful choice of *initialization*.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
Initialize weights so each layer multiplies the **variance** of its signal by about **1** — then activations
(and the gradients that flow back) stay alive all the way through a deep network.
</Callout>

## Intuition: variance is the signal's volume

You already know an **activation function** decides what each neuron outputs, and from
**expectation & variance** you know that variance measures spread — how loud or quiet a set of numbers is.

Now stack the layers. A neuron computes a weighted sum of its $n_{\text{in}}$ inputs. If the inputs and the
weights are independent and roughly zero-mean, the variance of that sum is

$$
\operatorname{Var}(z) = n_{\text{in}} \cdot \operatorname{Var}(W) \cdot \operatorname{Var}(x).
$$

So each layer **multiplies** the signal's variance by the factor $n_{\text{in}} \cdot \operatorname{Var}(W)$.
Multiply by a number less than 1 layer after layer and the variance decays geometrically toward zero —
the signal **vanishes**. Multiply by a number greater than 1 and it **explodes**. Only when that factor is
about 1 does the signal keep a steady volume through the whole stack. The exact same multiplication happens
to gradients on the way back, which is why a vanished signal means a network that cannot learn.

## The formal definition

We want $n_{\text{in}} \cdot \operatorname{Var}(W) \approx 1$, which fixes the weight variance:

$$
\operatorname{Var}(W) = \frac{1}{n_{\text{in}}}.
$$

This is **Xavier (Glorot)** initialization, designed for activations like $\tanh$ that are roughly linear
near zero. A common symmetric form also accounts for the backward pass:

$$
\operatorname{Var}(W) = \frac{2}{n_{\text{in}} + n_{\text{out}}}.
$$

**ReLU** is different: it zeroes out half its inputs, cutting the variance in half. To compensate, **He**
initialization doubles the weight variance:

$$
\operatorname{Var}(W) = \frac{2}{n_{\text{in}}}.
$$

```python
# He initialization for a ReLU layer (PyTorch/NumPy style)
W = np.random.randn(n_out, n_in) * np.sqrt(2.0 / n_in)
b = np.zeros(n_out)
```

<Callout type="why">
This is why you never initialize a deep net with, say, <code>randn() * 0.01</code> for everything. That
fixed scale ignores $n_{\text{in}}$, so wide layers vanish and the network stalls. Matching the scale to the
fan-in is what made training networks with dozens of layers practical.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Audio engineering" icon="🎚️">
A guitar signal through five pedals: if every pedal cuts the level by half, the amp receives
$(1/2)^5 \approx 3\%$ of the original — a vanishing whisper. If every pedal doubles it, you get clipping and
feedback — an exploding roar. Unity gain at each stage keeps the tone intact, exactly like a per-layer
variance factor of 1.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Finance" icon="📈">
Compound growth is the same geometric multiplication. A return factor of $0.97$ per year quietly erodes a
fund toward zero; $1.03$ per year compounds it upward. Across 30 "layers" (years) a factor microscopically
off from 1 produces wildly different ends — which is why deep nets are so sensitive to the weight scale.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Media & art" icon="🖨️">
Photocopy a photo, then photocopy the copy, again and again. Faint details fade to white (vanishing) while
specks of toner can smear into black blotches (exploding). Each copy is a layer that scales the "signal";
only a perfect 1× copier would preserve the picture through many generations.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Public health" icon="🦠">
Epidemiologists track the reproduction number $R$: each infected person passes the disease to $R$ others.
$R < 1$ and the outbreak dies out over generations; $R > 1$ and it grows exponentially. A neural network's
per-layer variance factor is the $R$ of its signal — and we engineer it to sit right at the critical value.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Initialization only sets the <em>starting</em> point. It cannot fix a network on its own — but a bad start
can make a good architecture untrainable. The next lessons (normalization especially) add machinery that
keeps the signal healthy <em>during</em> training, not just at step zero.
</Callout>
