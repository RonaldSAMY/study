## The hook: a network needs a grade, not a vibe

A photo app claims a picture is a cat with 55% confidence. Is that good? The picture *is* a cat — so the
model was right but timid. Tomorrow it says 99% on a picture of a dog. Same "right/wrong" tally, wildly
different mistakes. To improve, the network needs more than a pass/fail mark; it needs a **continuous
grade** that gets worse smoothly as predictions drift from the truth.

That grade is the **loss function**. It compresses "how wrong was this prediction?" into one number, and
the entire job of training is to make that number small.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A loss function measures how far a prediction is from the truth as a single number — the quantity the
network minimizes during training.
</Callout>

## Intuition: match the loss to the task

You met **cross-entropy** in information theory as the cost of encoding the true distribution using your
model's predicted one, and you saw in **maximum likelihood** that minimizing it is the same as maximizing
the probability the model assigns to the correct answers. Two losses cover most of deep learning:

- **Mean squared error (MSE)** for predicting *numbers*. The penalty grows with the square of the gap, so
  big misses hurt much more than small ones.
- **Cross-entropy** for predicting *probabilities* of classes. It punishes confident wrong answers
  brutally — being 99% sure and wrong costs far more than being 60% sure and wrong.

The contrast matters. If you use MSE on a classifier, a confidently-wrong prediction is barely penalized,
and the gradients that drive learning go weak exactly when they should be loud. Cross-entropy fixes that:
its penalty heads toward infinity as a confident prediction turns out wrong.

## The formal definition

For regression with predictions $\hat{y}_i$ and targets $y_i$ over $N$ examples, **mean squared error**:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(\hat{y}_i - y_i\right)^2.
$$

For binary classification with predicted probability $\hat{y}$ and label $y \in \{0, 1\}$, **binary
cross-entropy**:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}} = -\Bigl[\, y \log \hat{y} + (1 - y)\log(1 - \hat{y}) \,\Bigr].
$$

For $K$ classes with a one-hot target and softmax probabilities $\hat{y}_k$, the **categorical
cross-entropy** (equivalently the negative log-likelihood, NLL) keeps only the true class term:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} = -\sum_{k=1}^{K} y_k \log \hat{y}_k = -\log \hat{y}_{\text{true}}.
$$

```python
mse = np.mean((y_hat - y) ** 2)
bce = -np.mean(y * np.log(y_hat) + (1 - y) * np.log(1 - y_hat))
```

<Callout type="why">
Minimizing cross-entropy is exactly <strong>maximum likelihood</strong>: the parameters that make the
observed labels most probable are the ones that minimize the negative log-likelihood. The loss is not
arbitrary — it falls straight out of probability theory.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Weather forecasting" icon="🌦️">
Predicting tomorrow's temperature is regression, so models train with <strong>MSE</strong>. Squaring means
a forecast off by 10°C is penalized 100× more than one off by 1°C — exactly the asymmetry meteorologists
want.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Medical screening" icon="🩺">
A diagnostic model outputs the probability of disease and trains with <strong>cross-entropy</strong>. A
confident "all clear" on a sick patient incurs a huge loss, pushing the model to stay calibrated rather
than recklessly certain.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="E-commerce" icon="🛒">
A click-through model predicts the probability a user taps an ad. <strong>Binary cross-entropy</strong>
rewards well-calibrated probabilities, which matters because the predicted number is later multiplied by a
bid — over-confidence literally costs money.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Robotics" icon="🤖">
A robot arm learning to reach a target minimizes the <strong>squared distance</strong> between its
fingertip and the goal — MSE in physical space. Tiny errors are tolerated; large ones dominate the loss
and get corrected first.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Pick the loss from the output: real-valued predictions → MSE; a probability or class → cross-entropy. The
output activation and the loss are chosen together (sigmoid with BCE, softmax with categorical CE).
</Callout>
