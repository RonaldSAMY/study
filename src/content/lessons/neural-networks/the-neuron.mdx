## The hook: a triage decision in a single number

A radiologist looks at a scan and weighs a handful of clues: *how large is the mass? how irregular are
the cell borders? how dense is the tissue?* Some clues matter more than others, so they get more
**weight**. Add the weighted clues together, lean toward caution with a baseline **bias**, and squash
the total into a single confidence: *"85% likely malignant."*

That entire reasoning — weigh, sum, shift, squash — is exactly what one artificial **neuron** does. It is
the smallest decision-making unit in a neural network, and it is nothing more than a tiny linear model
with a knob on the end.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A neuron takes a **dot product** of its inputs with learned **weights**, adds a **bias**, and passes the
result through an **activation function** to produce one output.
</Callout>

## Intuition: a dot product that learned what to care about

You already know the **dot product** as a similarity score: $\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}$ is large when the
input $\mathbf{x}$ points the same way as the weight vector $\mathbf{w}$. A neuron uses exactly this. The
weights $\mathbf{w}$ are a *learned template* of "what this neuron is looking for," and the dot product
measures how strongly the current input matches that template.

Two more pieces finish the unit:

- The **bias** $b$ shifts the decision threshold. With a big negative bias the neuron stays quiet unless
  the evidence is strong; with a positive bias it fires easily.
- The **activation** $\sigma$ bends the straight-line score into something useful — for example squashing
  it into a probability between 0 and 1.

Because the inputs $\mathbf{x}$ live in a **tensor** and the weights in another, a real layer computes
thousands of these dot products at once. But the logic of *one* neuron is the whole story.

## The formal definition

Given an input vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, a weight vector $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, and a
scalar bias $b$, a neuron computes a **pre-activation** (often called the logit):

$$
z = \mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i + b.
$$

It then applies an **activation function** $\sigma$ to get the output:

$$
a = \sigma(z) = \sigma\!\left(\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b\right).
$$

With the logistic activation $\sigma(z) = \dfrac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}$, the output is a number in $(0, 1)$ you can
read as a probability. The set of points where $z = 0$ is a straight line (in 2D) or a **hyperplane** (in
higher dimensions) — the neuron's decision boundary.

```python
z = w @ x + b          # weighted sum (dot product) plus bias
a = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-z))  # logistic activation -> "firing rate" in (0, 1)
```

<Callout type="why">
Stack many of these and let the weights be learned from data, and you have a neural network. Every
parameter a giant language model tunes is just a $w_i$ or a $b$ inside one of billions of these units.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Email & NLP" icon="📧">
A spam filter is a neuron. The input is word counts; the weights are large and positive for tokens like
"free" or "wire transfer," negative for words common in your real mail. The dot product is a spam score,
the bias sets how aggressive the filter is, and the activation turns it into "probability this is spam."
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Finance" icon="💳">
A credit model weighs income, debt ratio, and payment history. Each applicant is a vector; the learned
weights say how much each factor counts. A negative bias keeps approvals conservative, and the sigmoid
output is the estimated probability of repayment.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Sports analytics" icon="⚽">
Scouts score a prospect from speed, accuracy, and stamina. Give each attribute a weight, sum them, and
add a bias for the position's baseline difficulty. One neuron turns a stat line into a single "draft this
player?" signal.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Smart agriculture" icon="🌾">
A field sensor decides when to irrigate from soil moisture, temperature, and humidity. The weights encode
which readings matter for this crop; a high bias means "water often." The neuron fires when the weighted
evidence crosses its threshold.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
In every case the math is identical — a dot product plus a bias plus a squash. Only the meaning of the
weights changes, and the weights are exactly what learning will discover.
</Callout>
