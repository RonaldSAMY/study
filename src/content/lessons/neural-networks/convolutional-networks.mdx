## The hook: a magnifying glass that hunts for edges

Imagine sweeping a tiny stencil across a photograph, and every time the patch underneath looks like a
vertical edge, the stencil lights up. Slide it over the whole image and you get a new picture — a map of
*where the vertical edges are*. Swap the stencil for one tuned to corners, or skin texture, or the curve of
an eye, and you get a different map each time.

That sliding stencil is a **convolution**, the stencil is a **kernel**, and stacking layers of them is a
**convolutional neural network** (CNN) — the architecture that taught computers to see. Instead of wiring
every pixel to every neuron (millions of weights, no sense of geometry), a CNN reuses one small kernel
everywhere, baking in the truth that a cat is a cat in any corner of the frame.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
A convolution slides one small set of **shared weights** (a kernel) across an image, producing a **feature
map** that says where that kernel's pattern appears.
</Callout>

## Intuition: weight sharing is the big idea

From **backpropagation** you know a network learns its weights by gradient descent. A fully connected layer
on a modest $200 \times 200$ image would need 40,000 weights *per neuron* — and it would have to relearn what
an edge looks like separately for every location. Wasteful and fragile.

Convolution fixes both problems with **weight sharing**: a single $3 \times 3$ kernel (just 9 numbers) slides
across every position. Two consequences fall out:

1. **Translation equivariance** — because the *same* kernel scans everywhere, a feature detected in the
   top-left is detected identically in the bottom-right.
2. **Massive parameter savings** — 9 shared weights instead of millions, so the network learns from far less
   data and generalizes better.

After convolution, a **pooling** step (e.g. take the max of each $2 \times 2$ block) shrinks the map, keeping
the strongest responses while granting a little tolerance to *where* exactly a feature sat.

## The formal definition

For an image $I$ and a kernel $K$ of size $k \times k$, the (cross-correlation) feature map is

$$
S(i, j) = \sum_{m}\sum_{n} I(i + m,\; j + n)\, K(m, n).
$$

Each output pixel is a dot product of the kernel with the patch beneath it.

```python
for i in range(H - 2):
    for j in range(W - 2):
        out[i, j] = (img[i:i+3, j:j+3] * kernel).sum()   # slide and dot
```

Here is the beautiful connection to the **convolution theorem** you already met in the Fourier track:
convolution in the spatial domain is plain *multiplication* in the frequency domain.

$$
\mathcal{F}\{I * K\} = \mathcal{F}\{I\} \cdot \mathcal{F}\{K\}.
$$

<Callout type="why">
This is not just elegant — it is practical. A kernel is a frequency filter: a blur kernel keeps low
frequencies (smooth regions) and an edge kernel keeps high frequencies (sharp transitions). And for large
kernels, libraries literally compute the convolution via the FFT because multiplying in frequency space is
faster. The math you learned for signals is the math a CNN runs on images.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Medical imaging" icon="🩻">
Radiology software runs edge-detecting kernels over X-rays and MRIs to outline bone fractures or tumor
boundaries that are hard for the eye to catch. The same convolution that finds a square's edge in the demo
traces the edge of a hairline crack.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Remote sensing" icon="🛰️">
Satellites photograph farmland; convolutions detect the straight boundaries between fields and the texture of
healthy versus stressed crops. Because the kernel is shared, a field in one corner of the image is analyzed
exactly like a field in another — translation equivariance over square kilometers.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Photo editing" icon="🖼️">
Every "sharpen," "blur," and "emboss" filter in your photo app is a convolution with a fixed kernel — the
same operation a CNN performs, except the CNN *learns* its kernels from data instead of having an artist
hand-design them.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Document scanning" icon="📄">
Before recognizing text, an OCR pipeline convolves the page with stroke-detecting kernels to find the
vertical and horizontal pen strokes that make up letters. Pooling then tolerates the slight wobble of
handwriting so the same letter is recognized whether it leans left or right.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Stack convolutions and a hierarchy emerges on its own: early layers learn edges, middle layers combine edges
into textures and parts (an eye, a wheel), deep layers assemble parts into whole objects. Each level is built
from the level below — pattern detectors all the way up.
</Callout>
