## The hook: a model that can write, but not behave

After pre-training, a language model is a dazzling autocomplete: it knows grammar, facts, and style, but it
has no idea what *you* want. Ask it for help and it might ramble, dodge, or confidently make things up.
There is no "loss function for being helpful, honest, and harmless" — those are human judgments, not labels
in a dataset.

So we ask humans. Show people two model answers and let them pick the better one. Do this thousands of times,
distill those choices into a **reward model**, and then use the reinforcement-learning machinery from this
whole track to push the language model toward answers humans prefer. That pipeline is **RLHF** —
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback — the step that turned raw language models into usable assistants.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
**RLHF** learns a reward function from human *comparisons*, then runs policy-gradient ascent so the language
model produces answers that score high under that learned reward.
</Callout>

## Intuition: from "which is better?" to a steering signal

Asking a human to *rate* an answer 7.5/10 is hopeless — nobody is consistent. But asking "is A better than
B?" is easy and reliable. RLHF is built entirely on these pairwise preferences.

The trick is the **Bradley–Terry** model: assume each response has a hidden scalar reward, and the chance a
human prefers $A$ over $B$ grows with the *difference* in their rewards, squashed through a sigmoid. Fit a
reward model $r_\phi$ to predict the observed choices and you've converted messy human taste into a single
differentiable number per response.

Now the language model is just a **policy** (lesson 4): given a prompt, it outputs a distribution over the
next tokens. We do policy-gradient ascent to raise the reward — but with a leash. A **KL penalty** keeps the
tuned model close to the original so it doesn't "reward-hack" into bizarre, degenerate text that games the
reward model. The algorithm of choice for this constrained ascent is **PPO**, which clips each update so the
policy never lurches too far in one step.

**DPO** (Direct Preference Optimization) is the elegant shortcut: with some algebra you can skip training a
separate reward model *and* the RL loop, optimizing the policy on the preference pairs directly with a single
classification-style loss. Same goal, far less machinery.

## The formal definition

**Step 1 — reward model.** Given a prompt $x$ with a preferred answer $y_w$ and a rejected one $y_l$, fit
$r_\phi$ by maximizing the Bradley–Terry likelihood:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\phi) = -\,\mathbb{E}\big[\, \log \sigma\big(r_\phi(x, y_w) - r_\phi(x, y_l)\big) \,\big].
$$

**Step 2 — PPO.** Optimize the policy $\pi_\theta$ to maximize reward while staying near the reference model
$\pi_{\text{ref}}$:

$$
\max_{\theta}\; \mathbb{E}\big[\, r_\phi(x, y) \,\big] \;-\; \beta\, \mathrm{KL}\big(\pi_\theta \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}\big).
$$

**DPO** folds both steps into one loss directly on the preference pairs — no separate reward model, no RL loop:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}} = -\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[\, \log \sigma\!\left( \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w\mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w\mid x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l\mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l\mid x)} \right) \right].
$$

```python
# DPO loss: raise preferred logprobs, lower rejected ones, vs a frozen reference
logp_w = logprob(policy, x, y_win) - logprob(ref, x, y_win)
logp_l = logprob(policy, x, y_lose) - logprob(ref, x, y_lose)
loss = -F.logsigmoid(beta * (logp_w - logp_l)).mean()
```

<Callout type="why">
This is how the assistants people use every day are shaped. Pre-training gives a model knowledge; RLHF (or DPO)
gives it manners, helpfulness, and refusals. The same Bellman-to-policy-gradient ideas you learned on a tiny
grid-world scale all the way up to aligning models with billions of parameters.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Chat assistants" icon="💬">
Instruction-tuned chatbots are aligned by collecting preference pairs over their own answers, training a
reward model, and running PPO. The leap from "plausible text" to "actually helpful reply" is almost entirely
this preference-learning step.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Content moderation" icon="🛡️">
A summarizer can be steered away from sensational or unsafe phrasing by rewarding outputs that human raters
mark as faithful and neutral. The reward model encodes a policy team's judgment far more flexibly than a
keyword blocklist.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Code assistants" icon="💻">
Developers can express preferences over code completions — does it run, is it idiomatic, is it documented?
A reward model trained on those comparisons nudges a coding model toward suggestions engineers actually accept.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Image generation" icon="🎨">
Text-to-image models are tuned the same way: humans pick the nicer of two generations, a reward model learns
aesthetic and prompt-faithfulness preferences, and the generator is optimized to score higher — RLHF beyond
language entirely.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
The KL leash matters. Without it, the policy chases the reward model into adversarial nonsense it happens to
score highly — "reward hacking." Staying close to the reference model keeps the outputs fluent and trustworthy
while still improving on human preference.
</Callout>
