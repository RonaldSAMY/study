## The hook: a robot in a room with no instructions

Drop a cleaning robot into an unfamiliar room. Nobody tells it the floor plan. All it can do is
*try* something — roll forward, turn, bump a wall — and notice what happens: sometimes it reaches the
charging dock (good!), sometimes it tumbles down the stairs (very bad). Over time it should learn a
*habit*: from any spot, what is the smartest move?

To make "smartest move" a math problem we need a precise way to describe the world, the choices, and
what counts as good. That description is the **Markov Decision Process (MDP)** — the contract that
every reinforcement-learning algorithm is written against.

<Callout type="intuition" title="The one-sentence idea">
An **MDP** says: you are in a **state**, you pick an **action**, the world rolls dice to send you to a
new state and hands you a **reward** — and the future depends only on where you are *now*, not how you
got there.
</Callout>

## Intuition: the agent–environment loop

Reinforcement learning is a conversation that repeats forever:

1. The **agent** observes the current **state** $s_t$.
2. It chooses an **action** $a_t$.
3. The **environment** responds with a **reward** $r_{t+1}$ and a **next state** $s_{t+1}$.
4. Repeat.

The single most important assumption is the **Markov property**: the next state depends only on the
*current* state and action — the past is already baked into "where you are now." This is exactly the
conditional-probability thinking from Bayes, but pointed forward in time: $P(s_{t+1}\mid s_t, a_t)$ needs
no earlier history.

Two more pieces complete the picture. A **policy** $\pi$ is the agent's strategy — its rule for picking
actions. And the **discount factor** $\gamma$ (a number between 0 and 1) decides how much a reward in the
future is worth *today*: a reward $t$ steps away is multiplied by $\gamma^t$, so far-off rewards fade.

## The formal definition

An MDP is the five-tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, \gamma)$:

- $\mathcal{S}$ — the set of **states**.
- $\mathcal{A}$ — the set of **actions**.
- $P(s' \mid s, a)$ — the **transition probability** of landing in $s'$ after doing $a$ in $s$.
- $R(s, a)$ — the **reward** for that step.
- $\gamma \in [0, 1)$ — the **discount factor**.

The agent's goal is to maximize the **expected return**: the discounted sum of all future rewards.

$$
G_t = r_{t+1} + \gamma\, r_{t+2} + \gamma^2 r_{t+3} + \cdots = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^{k}\, r_{t+k+1}.
$$

A **policy** maps states to action probabilities, $\pi(a \mid s)$, and the whole game is to find the policy
that makes $G_t$ as large as possible on average.

```python
# one turn of the agent-environment loop
state = env.reset()
for t in range(T):
    action = policy(state)              # agent decides
    next_state, reward, done = env.step(action)  # environment responds
    state = next_state
    if done: break
```

<Callout type="why">
Almost every modern AI that *acts* — game engines, robots, ad bidders, and the fine-tuning step behind
chatbots — is trained by casting its problem as an MDP. Get the states, actions, and rewards right and
the learning algorithms in the next lessons do the rest.
</Callout>

## The same idea, four different worlds

<ExampleCard domain="Video games" icon="🎮">
In Pac-Man the **state** is the maze layout plus everyone's positions; the **actions** are up/down/left/right;
the **reward** is points for pellets and a big penalty for being caught. The Markov property roughly holds —
the current screen tells you almost everything you need to decide your next move.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Robotics" icon="🦾">
A warehouse robot's **state** is its location and battery level; **actions** are drive, turn, or pick up a box.
Motors slip and wheels skid, so the same action gives different outcomes — that randomness is exactly the
transition probability $P(s'\mid s,a)$, not a bug.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Smart-home control" icon="🌡️">
A learning thermostat sits in a **state** of (room temperature, time of day). Its **action** is how hard to
heat. The **reward** trades comfort against energy cost. A patient agent (high $\gamma$) pre-heats before a
cold morning instead of reacting only once you shiver.
</ExampleCard>

<ExampleCard domain="Logistics" icon="📦">
A store's restocking problem is an MDP: the **state** is current inventory, the **action** is how much to
reorder, transitions come from random customer demand, and the **reward** subtracts holding costs and
stock-outs. Discounting captures that money saved now beats money saved next quarter.
</ExampleCard>

<Callout type="note">
Notice what *isn't* in the tuple: a map, a plan, or any labelled "right answers." The agent is handed only
rewards, and must discover good behavior itself. That is what makes reinforcement learning different from
the supervised learning in earlier tracks.
</Callout>
